# Top 20 Research Summaries for Differentiated PBL in a Robotics Workshop
## For students with EBD (ADHD, ODD, anxiety, depression, OCD, PTSD) in groups of 4-8 with individual projects

Selection criteria: direct applicability to designing robotics projects where each student works on a different project and must sustain independent work while the teacher cycles between them.

---

## 1. Taylor, Allen, Van & Moohr (2024)
**"The Effects of Project-Based Learning on Student Behavior and Teacher Burnout in an Emotional/Behavioral Support Classroom"**
*Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders (SAGE)*

**What they did:** This is the only published empirical study that placed PBL directly inside an EBD support classroom and measured both student behavioral outcomes and teacher burnout/efficacy. The researchers implemented PBL units in a self-contained classroom for students classified with emotional disturbance and tracked changes in prosocial behavior, disruptive behavior, and teacher-reported stress.

**Key findings:**
- PBL reduced disruptive behavior during project periods compared to traditional instruction.
- Teacher feelings of efficacy increased, and self-reported burnout decreased.
- The primary mechanism: PBL provides *continuous engaged activity* at all times, which eliminates the trigger-rich dead time (transitions, waiting, unstructured moments) that causes the majority of behavioral incidents in EBD classrooms.
- PBL's inherent flexibility allowed teachers to manage cognitive load — students could process information at their own pace rather than keeping up with whole-class instruction.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** Your model — individual projects, hands-on building — is *precisely* the type of PBL that works. The robotics context is ideal because a student always has a next physical action (attach a motor, test a sensor, modify a line of code). The research says: when you eliminate dead time, you eliminate most behavioral incidents. Your design priority should therefore be ensuring every student always has a visible, concrete next action — even a default low-demand task — so no student is ever sitting with nothing to do while you're helping someone else.

## 2. Boardman (2025)
**"High School Students' Experiences With Scaffolding in Project-Based Learning"**
*Journal of Adolescent & Adult Literacy (Wiley/ILA)*

**What they did:** Qualitative study of high school students navigating PBL units, examining which types of scaffolding actually helped students work independently and which were ignored or found unhelpful. Focused specifically on the student experience rather than teacher implementation.

**Key findings:**
- The scaffolds that most reliably enabled independent work were: **(a)** a small number of connected task cards that linked each activity to the next — not thick instruction packets, but 3-5 cards per phase; **(b)** interactional scaffolds — structured prompts that told students *how* to ask for help, how to collaborate, and what to do when stuck; **(c)** flexible materials that could be adjusted mid-project without starting over.
- Scaffolding that students rejected: overly detailed written instructions (felt like worksheets), scaffolds that couldn't be modified once started, and scaffolds that required reading large amounts of text before acting.
- Peer collaboration worked best when structured (specific roles, specific prompts), not when students were just told to "work together."

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** Design each project phase as a physical "mission card" — a laminated card or simple printout with: a clear goal, 2-3 concrete steps, a done-condition ("You know you're done when..."), and a "stuck protocol" ("If you're stuck for 3 minutes: re-read step 2, try once more, then put your red flag up and move to your backup task"). Keep instructions visual and short. Thick written manuals will be ignored. The stuck protocol is the single highest-leverage scaffold for enabling independence — it gives anxious and impulsive students an alternative to calling out or shutting down when you're busy with another student.

## 3. Belland, Walker, Kim & Lefler (2017)
**"Synthesizing Results From Empirical Research on Computer-Based Scaffolding in STEM Education: A Meta-Analysis"**
*Review of Educational Research, 87(2), 309-344. Cited by 400+.*

**What they did:** Gold-standard meta-analysis of 144 experimental studies (333 outcomes) examining the effects of computer-based scaffolding across all STEM disciplines and age groups (primary through adult education) in problem-centered curricula.

**Key findings:**
- Overall effect of scaffolding: g = 0.46 (moderate, consistently positive across all contexts).
- **For students with learning disabilities specifically: g = 3.13** — an extraordinarily large effect, meaning scaffolding had roughly 7 times more impact on students with LD than on the general population.
- Strategic and metacognitive scaffolding (helping students plan and monitor their work) outperformed purely conceptual scaffolding (just giving content information).
- The effect was substantial and significant across all age groups.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** This is the strongest quantitative evidence you will find that scaffolding — specifically the kind that helps students plan, monitor, and self-check — is disproportionately beneficial for exactly your population. Your EBD students have executive function challenges that functionally mirror learning disabilities in how they interfere with task performance. This means every dollar of effort you invest in creating structured scaffolds (mission cards, checklists, self-monitoring sheets) pays back at a dramatically higher rate for your students than for neurotypical peers. This isn't a nice-to-have; it's the single most evidence-backed intervention you can build into your projects.

## 4. Cumming, Qiu, Oblath, Frazier et al. (2025)
**"Perceived Stress, Executive Function, and Stress Regulation: Implications for Middle Schoolers' Emotional and Behavioral Well-being"**
*Remedial and Special Education (SAGE)*

**What they did:** Examined the relationships between perceived stress, executive function capacity, stress regulation strategies, and emotional/behavioral outcomes in middle school students. Measured executive function with both performance-based and teacher-reported measures.

**Key findings:**
- Executive function is the neurological bridge between a student's stress level and their behavioral outcomes. When perceived stress rises above a personal threshold, EF degrades rapidly — working memory shrinks, planning ability drops, cognitive flexibility disappears.
- This degradation is not willful misbehavior. It is a capacity failure. The same student who works well independently at 9:00am may be completely unable to at 11:00am after a stressful morning event.
- Students who had learned explicit stress regulation strategies showed less EF degradation under stress.
- The continuum of behavioral competence is closely linked to EF capacity, perceived stress, and ability to navigate stressful situations.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** Independence capacity is not fixed per student — it fluctuates daily with their stress state. Practically: build a brief low-demand "warm-up" task at the start of each session (e.g., tidy the build station, check component inventory, sketch today's plan) that lets you do a quick visual read of each student's regulation level before you assign independent work. A student who arrives dysregulated goes on a more concrete, hands-on task with fewer decisions (assembling components by rote). A student who's regulated that day can take on a more open-ended phase (designing, problem-solving). You are dynamically differentiating by real-time executive function capacity, not just by diagnosis.

## 5. Harrison, Soares, Rudzinski & Johnson (2019)
**"ADHD and Classroom-Based Interventions: Evidence-Based Status, Effectiveness, and Moderators of Effects in Single-Case Design Research"**
*Review of Educational Research (SAGE). Cited by 100+.*

**What they did:** Systematic review of 147 single-case design studies evaluating classroom-based interventions for students with ADHD, using What Works Clearinghouse standards to determine evidence-based status.

**Key findings:**
- The two most effective intervention categories for ADHD in classrooms were: **(1) self-management interventions** — where students monitor and record their own behavior or task completion — and **(2) instructional modifications** — breaking tasks into smaller chunks, using concrete materials, and building in frequent low-stakes success points.
- Both categories were *more effective than consequence-based systems alone* (reward charts, token economies without self-monitoring).
- Classroom interventions reduced off-task and disruptive behavior with largest effects for consequence-based + self-regulation combinations.
- The review classified instructional and self-management interventions as meeting evidence-based standards.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** For your ADHD students specifically, add a self-monitoring layer to each project: a simple paper or whiteboard tracker where the student marks off each completed step themselves. This does two things — it externalizes working memory (they don't have to hold the plan in their head) and it creates a visible record of progress that feels rewarding. Design projects so the first 10 minutes always produce something tangible (a part assembled, a code segment running), because early success dramatically reduces avoidance and off-task behavior in ADHD students. Consequence-based systems alone (sticker charts, points) are weaker than self-management; combine them or lead with self-monitoring.

## 6. Moohr, Balint-Langel, Taylor & Rizzo (2021)
**"Practicing Academic Independence: Self-Regulation Strategies for Students with Emotional and Behavioral Disorders"**
*Beyond Behavior (SAGE), 30(1)*

**What they did:** Practitioner-focused article providing step-by-step procedures for implementing three specific evidence-based self-regulation strategies with EBD students: self-regulated strategy development (SRSD), self-monitoring, and strategy instruction.

**Key findings:**
- Self-regulation is defined as "a set of specific cognitive skills necessary for students to independently manage, monitor, and assess their own academic learning and behavior."
- Students with EBD often lack these skills — but they can be *explicitly taught*.
- **Self-monitoring** involves teaching students to: (a) self-determine performance goals, (b) self-observe and record progress, (c) reflect on their target behavior, and (d) self-administer reinforcers when goals are met.
- The I Control curriculum showed improvements in teacher-reported executive function, externalizing behavior, emotional control, and social problem-solving.
- The article provides reproducible implementation steps, not just theory.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** This is the most directly actionable article for your independence problem. Before a student begins a project phase, they set a micro-goal for that session ("I will complete milestone 3"). They have a simple tracking sheet where they mark steps as done and rate their own focus/effort at the end. Over time, students internalize the plan-do-check cycle and need fewer teacher prompts. Start with very small goals (one 10-minute milestone) and expand as students demonstrate success. The key insight: self-regulation is a *teachable skill*, not a personality trait — and your workshop is an ideal context to teach it because the project milestones create natural self-monitoring checkpoints.

## 7. Smale-Jacobse, Meijer, Helms-Lorenz et al. (2019)
**"Differentiated Instruction in Secondary Education: A Systematic Review of Research Evidence"**
*Frontiers in Psychology, 10, 2366. Cited by 771.*

**What they did:** The most-cited systematic review of differentiated instruction in secondary education. Reviewed all peer-reviewed empirical studies examining how DI was applied, what student characteristics it targeted, and what outcomes it produced.

**Key findings:**
- Effective differentiation works along **three axes simultaneously**: *readiness* (current skill level), *interest* (what motivates the student), and *learning profile* (how the student processes information best — visual, kinesthetic, auditory, etc.).
- **Single-axis differentiation (e.g., only by difficulty level) has much weaker effects.** Multi-axis differentiation — adjusting readiness AND interest AND learning profile — produced the strongest outcomes.
- Forms of DI studied included peer tutoring, flexible grouping, tiered assignments, choice boards, and project-based approaches.
- DI in secondary education is under-researched relative to elementary; the studies that do exist show positive but modest effects.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** When designing each student's project, explicitly address all three axes. Ask: *What is this student ready to build right now?* (readiness — a student who can't solder yet gets snap-together circuits), *What theme or real-world application will make them care?* (interest — a student obsessed with cars builds a wheeled robot; a student into music builds a sound-sensing device), *Does this student need to start by doing, by reading, or by watching a demo?* (learning profile — provide the same milestone via a hands-on kit, a visual diagram, or a 2-minute video). For ODD students, the interest axis is the highest-leverage one — a project imposed on them will be resisted; a project they helped choose is treated as *theirs to defend*.

## 8. Billingsley, Thomas et al. (2018)
**"Effects of Student Choice of Instructional Method on the Learning Outcomes of Students with Comorbid Learning and Emotional/Behavioral Disabilities"**
*Learning Disability Quarterly (SAGE). Cited by 44.*

**What they did:** Experimental study in which students with comorbid LD and EBD were given the opportunity to choose *how* they received instruction (not just what they learned). Compared outcomes between choice and no-choice conditions.

**Key findings:**
- When students with EBD/LD chose their instructional method, both engagement and task completion improved significantly compared to teacher-assigned conditions.
- The act of choosing *itself* reduced problem behavior — because it transferred partial ownership of the learning experience to the student. This reduced the adversarial dynamic that is common with EBD students, especially those with ODD.
- The choices were structured (2-3 options), not open-ended. Open-ended choice can be paralyzing for anxious or dysregulated students.
- Student choice was associated with higher motivation and lower rates of refusal behavior.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** Build a "how do you want to start?" moment into the beginning of each project phase. Offer 2-3 concrete options — e.g., "Do you want to watch a 2-minute video, look at the diagram, or have me show you once?" This is not open-ended ("What do you want to do?") — that can paralyze anxious students or trigger ODD through perceived demand. It's *bounded choice*: the student must complete the milestone, but they choose the path. For ODD students especially, this small transfer of agency is often the difference between buy-in and a power struggle before you even start. Extend the principle to project selection itself: let students choose from 3-4 pre-designed projects rather than assigning one.

## 9. Riden, Kumm & Maggin (2022)
**"Evidence-Based Behavior Management Strategies for Students With or at Risk of EBD: A Mega Review of the Literature"**
*Remedial and Special Education (SAGE)*

**What they did:** A "mega review" — a review of reviews — that synthesized 50 separate meta-analyses and systematic reviews encompassing 20 different intervention types for students with EBD. The goal was to identify which interventions have the strongest cumulative evidence base.

**Key findings:**
The review identified **eight interventions classified as evidence-based practices** for EBD students:
1. Self-monitoring / self-management
2. Token economies (when combined with other strategies)
3. Functional behavior assessment
4. Social skills instruction
5. Cognitive-behavioral interventions
6. Peer-mediated interventions
7. Teacher praise and positive reinforcement
8. Group contingencies

Notably absent from the evidence-based list: punishment-based systems, rigid behavioral contracts without student input, and purely consequence-driven approaches without a self-management component.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** This is your reference checklist. When you design your workshop management system, embed as many of these eight as structurally possible:
- *Self-monitoring:* milestone tracking sheets students fill in themselves.
- *Token economies:* completion of project milestones earns build-time credits or component choices (combine with self-monitoring, not standalone).
- *Social skills instruction:* brief structured collaboration moments between individual projects (e.g., peer demos).
- *Teacher praise:* specific behavioral praise at each check-in ("You stayed on your milestone for 12 minutes straight — that's your longest stretch this week").
- *Group contingencies:* whole-workshop rewards when all students hit their daily milestone targets.
This gives you eight evidence-backed levers, not just intuition.

## 10. Cibrian & Lakes (2021)
**"The Potential for Emerging Technologies to Support Self-Regulation in Children with ADHD: A Literature Review"**
*International Journal of Child-Computer Interaction*

**What they did:** Comprehensive literature review examining how technology-based interventions — including robotics, tangible computing, wearables, and apps — can support self-regulation in children with ADHD. Focused specifically on the mechanisms by which technology helps, not just whether it helps.

**Key findings:**
- Hands-on technological interventions provide **immediate, tangible feedback** — and it is this immediacy that helps ADHD children self-regulate. The feedback loop is faster than what a teacher can provide, and it is non-social (no judgment, no emotional charge).
- Tangible computing (building physical things that respond — exactly like robotics) was particularly effective because the *physical act of building* provides proprioceptive feedback that anchors attention.
- Technology-based interventions allowed children to self-regulate and stay on task **without teacher intervention** — critically important in settings where the teacher is managing multiple students.
- Key design principle: the technology must provide clear, immediate signals of success and failure. Ambiguous feedback increased frustration; clear feedback reduced it.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** Robotics is not just a topic for your workshop — it is itself a therapeutic medium for ADHD self-regulation. When a student connects a motor and it spins, or writes code and a light turns on, that immediate tangible feedback does what no verbal instruction can: it confirms success instantly and non-judgmentally. Design projects so that each milestone produces a visible, testable result (the motor moves, the sensor reads a value, the LED lights up). Avoid milestones where success is invisible or abstract (e.g., "plan your design" with nothing to test). Every milestone should *do something the student can see, hear, or feel*. This is the mechanism that sustains independent work without you.

## 11. Berrezueta-Guzman & Pau (2021)
**"Assessment of a Robotic Assistant for Supporting Homework Activities of Children With ADHD"**
*IEEE Access*

**What they did:** Developed and tested a robotic assistant called "Atent@" designed to support homework activities of children with ADHD. The robot guided students through tasks using visual milestones and digital checklists, with AI-based algorithms developed by therapists to help children refocus.

**Key findings:**
- When a robotic/technological system (not a person) guided students through tasks via visual milestones, **task adherence dramatically increased** compared to when a teacher gave verbal commands.
- The non-human mediator eliminated the social-emotional friction that often triggers oppositional or avoidant behavior in ADHD/ODD children.
- The robot detected poor habits and inappropriate behavior and used corrective prompts — but because these came from a neutral technological system rather than an authority figure, they were accepted rather than resisted.
- The system was designed by therapists to break tasks into micro-steps with visual confirmation at each step.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** This validates the principle that an external, non-human system guiding students step-by-step produces better task adherence than teacher verbal commands. You don't need an AI robot — your mission cards, visual checklists, and physical prototype examples serve the same function. The key insight: when an ODD student refuses a teacher's instruction, they are refusing a *social demand from an authority figure*. When the same instruction comes from a card on their desk, the social charge is removed. Design your scaffolding materials to function as the neutral mediator. Students should be able to look at their card and know what to do next without asking you — and when they do, they're following "their project plan," not "the teacher's orders."

## 12. Makerspace EBD Study (2019)
**"The Effects of Makerspace Learning on the Social Interactions Among Students with Emotional or Behavioral Disorder"**
*Doctoral Dissertation, University of Texas at San Antonio. ERIC: ED604859.*

**What they did:** Single-case experiment with multielement design. Four middle school students with EBD (with social skill goals in their IEPs) participated in both makerspace learning sessions and traditional classroom sessions. Researchers measured the frequency and quality of social interactions in each setting.

**Key findings:**
- Makerspace learning (hands-on building based on constructionist principles) **significantly improved social interactions** compared to traditional classroom instruction.
- Social-emotional engagement was the most visible learning dimension across all makerspace stations.
- The makerspace environment reduced the social demand of interaction — students could interact *around the task* (passing materials, showing builds, discussing designs) rather than through forced social exercises.
- Individualized instruction within a constructivist framework supported students in building social-emotional competencies organically.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** Your workshop is essentially a makerspace. This study says that the hands-on building context naturally generates positive social interaction among EBD students — you don't need to force social skills exercises. When students are each building their own project at adjacent stations, organic social moments emerge: "Hey look at this," "Can you hand me that servo?", "How did you get yours to turn?" Design your physical layout to encourage these moments — adjacent workstations rather than isolated cubicles, shared component bins that require brief cooperative moments, and occasional structured peer demos ("Show the person next to you what your robot does right now"). The social interaction comes from the task context, not from you mandating it.

## 13. Watson et al. (2025)
**"A Critical Review of Empirical Support for Trauma-Informed Approaches in Schools"**
*Review of Education (Wiley/BERA)*

**What they did:** The most rigorous current review of the evidence base for trauma-informed approaches in schools. Examined studies from the US, UK, Australia, and Canada to determine what actually works versus what is assumed to work.

**Key findings:**
- Interest in trauma-informed schools is high internationally, but the empirical evidence for whole-school trauma-informed models is *limited*.
- What the evidence *does* support: the three structural pillars that consistently appear in successful programs are **predictability, relational safety, and low-demand re-entry**.
- Isolated trauma-informed teacher training (knowledge alone) does not change student outcomes. What changes outcomes is when trauma-informed principles are **embedded into the physical and instructional design** of the learning environment.
- The review calls for integrating trauma-informed approaches with existing evidence-based frameworks (DI, SEL, PBIS) rather than treating them as separate add-ons.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** This tells you that trauma-informed practice is not a training you attend and then "know" — it is a set of design decisions you build into the session structure itself:
- **Predictability:** Every session starts and ends the same way (same greeting, same warm-up task, same cool-down, same physical layout). Students with PTSD need to know exactly what will happen and when.
- **Relational safety:** Students need to know what happens when they make a mistake. Design a visible, non-punitive "restart protocol" for when builds fail or code breaks. Frame it as engineering ("failure is data").
- **Low-demand re-entry:** Have a default "soft start" task for any student who arrives dysregulated (sorting components, sketching, reviewing their build plan) — something that requires zero decision-making and lets them settle before engaging their project.

## 14. Smith, Z.R. & Langberg (2020)
**"Academic Motivation Deficits in Adolescents with ADHD and Associations with Academic Functioning"**
*Research on Child and Adolescent Psychopathology (Springer)*

**What they did:** Compared academic motivation (intrinsic, extrinsic, and amotivation) between adolescents with ADHD and without, and examined how motivation predicted academic functioning across subjects.

**Key findings:**
- Adolescents with ADHD showed **significant motivational deficits** compared to peers across all areas: intrinsic motivation (d = 0.49), extrinsic motivation (d = 0.43), and amotivation (d = 0.42).
- Higher intrinsic motivation for knowledge was associated with higher reading scores — even after controlling for ADHD severity.
- Extrinsic motivation for external motivators (grades, rewards) was the only motivation type associated with GPA.
- Amotivation (feeling "nothing I do matters") was negatively associated with multiple areas of academic impairment.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** Your ADHD students arrive with a measurable motivational deficit that is not their fault. This means you cannot rely on the inherent appeal of robotics alone — you must actively design motivation into the project structure. Two levers: (1) *Intrinsic motivation* — connect each project to the student's genuine interests. A student fascinated by animals builds a robot that responds to sound; a student into gaming builds a controller. The interest-project match must be real, not cosmetic. (2) *Combat amotivation* — students who believe nothing they do matters need early, undeniable proof of success. Design the first milestone of every project to be completable in 10 minutes with a guaranteed visible result (LED lights up, motor spins). This breaks the "nothing I do works" belief with physical evidence.

## 15. Morsink, Van der Oord, Antrop, Danckaerts & Scheres (2022)
**"Studying Motivation in ADHD: The Role of Internal Motives and the Relevance of Self Determination Theory"**
*Journal of Attention Disorders (SAGE)*

**What they did:** Theoretical review applying Self-Determination Theory (SDT) to ADHD, examining how the three basic psychological needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness) interact with ADHD symptoms to predict motivation and behavior.

**Key findings:**
- Children with ADHD were equally motivated as typically developing peers when tasks had these characteristics: **rewarded, competitive, socially evaluated, and containing predictable elements.** When these features were absent, motivation dropped dramatically.
- External reinforcers (rewards, tokens) can actually *undermine* intrinsic motivation in ADHD students if used carelessly — the "overjustification effect." Once a reward is removed, motivation drops below the original baseline.
- The capacity to experience "flow" or "hyperfocus" — time passing quickly when deeply engaged — exists in ADHD but requires high personal interest and low perceived coercion.
- ADHD students show particular aversion to the slow passage of time. Boredom is not just unpleasant — it is functionally intolerable.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** This refines how you use rewards and structure engagement. Don't use token economies as your primary motivator — use them to bridge gaps, not to drive engagement. Instead, design projects so the *task itself* is rewarding: build with visible results, frequent testable milestones, and genuine personal interest alignment. The finding about time perception is critical: for ADHD students, 15 minutes of boring waiting feels like an hour, but 15 minutes of engaged building feels like 2 minutes. Your 15-minute rotation cycle works — but only if the student is engaged during those 15 minutes. If they're stuck and waiting for you, those 15 minutes will feel eternal and trigger behavioral breakdown. This is why the stuck protocol matters so much.

## 16. Visual Activity Schedule Systematic Review (2022)
**"The Efficacy of Visual Activity Schedule Intervention in Reducing Problem Behaviors in Children with ADHD"**
*Published in PubMed; systematic review.*

**What they did:** Systematic review of studies using visual activity schedules (VAS) — physical or digital visual sequences showing tasks in order — specifically for children with ADHD aged 5-12. Examined effects on on-task behavior, problem behaviors, and task completion.

**Key findings:**
- Visual activity schedules produced increased on-task and on-schedule behavior across academic settings (math, language arts, transitions).
- Visual timers were associated with **significant reductions in inattentive and motor instability behaviors**, with effects particularly pronounced for children at higher risk for ADHD.
- Both students and teachers reported they enjoyed the visual schedules and found them helpful.
- The mechanisms: VAS externalize sequence memory (students don't have to remember what comes next), reduce the cognitive load of transitions, and provide a non-verbal behavioral cue that students can consult independently.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** This is the direct empirical validation of your mission card system. A visual activity schedule for each student's session — showing the 3-4 milestones for that day in order, with checkboxes — is not just helpful; it is one of the most evidence-backed interventions available for ADHD. Add a visual timer (physical or digital) showing how long the current work block lasts. The combination of visual schedule + visual timer addresses two of the biggest ADHD challenges simultaneously: "What do I do now?" (schedule) and "How long do I have to do it?" (timer). Both reduce the need to call the teacher.

## 17. Smith, Thompson, Maynard & Kim (2025)
**"Effects of Self-Management Interventions on Challenging Behavior: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis"**
*Research on Social Work Practice (SAGE)*

**What they did:** The most comprehensive meta-analysis of self-management interventions in schools. Analyzed 75 single-case design studies (236 participants, 456 effects) and 4 comparison group studies (422 participants, 11 effects).

**Key findings:**
- Self-management interventions **positively impacted challenging behaviors and academic outcomes** across the full range of studies.
- Core elements of effective self-management: (a) self-determining performance goals, (b) self-observing and recording progress, (c) reflecting on target behavior, (d) self-administering reinforcers when goals are met.
- The STARS program (Self-Management Training and Regulation Strategies) decreased teacher ratings of disruptive behavior.
- Self-management interventions worked across ages, settings, and disability categories.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** This confirms what Harrison (2019) and Moohr (2021) found from different angles: self-management is the single strongest evidence-based strategy for enabling independent work in students with behavioral challenges. The practical implementation: at the start of each session, each student sets one concrete goal for that session ("I will finish wiring milestone 4" or "I will test my code and fix one bug"). They track their own progress on a simple sheet. At the end of the session, they self-rate how they did. Over weeks, students build the metacognitive habit of planning, doing, and reviewing — which is the exact executive function skill that ADHD, EBD, and trauma compromise. You are teaching self-regulation through the project structure, not as a separate lesson.

## 18. Culclasure, Longest et al. (2019)
**"Project-Based Learning (PjBL) in Three Southeastern Public Schools: Academic, Behavioral, and Social-Emotional Outcomes"**
*Interdisciplinary Journal of Problem-Based Learning, 13(2). Cited by 119.*

**What they did:** Large-scale federally funded evaluation (2015-2017) of PBL implementation in three New Tech Network schools. Compared 9th graders in PBL schools with demographically matched control students in traditional schools across academic (test scores), behavioral (disciplinary incidents), and social-emotional (SEL survey) outcomes.

**Key findings:**
- PBL students outperformed control students on end-of-year academic assessments.
- PBL students showed *fewer* disciplinary incidents than control students.
- PBL students showed higher social-emotional competencies.
- The critical design feature that distinguished *high-engagement* PBL from low-engagement busywork: projects had a **real audience and a real product** — something that existed beyond the classroom and was shared with others. This "authentic purpose" was the key driver of engagement, not the project methodology alone.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** Every project needs an endpoint visible to someone outside the workshop — even minimally. This could be: a demonstration to another class, a 30-second video of the robot functioning posted to a school display, a brief presentation to a parent or administrator, or simply showing a visiting teacher. For EBD students who have often failed publicly, the prospect of an audience is motivating *and* anxiety-inducing — so scaffold toward it gradually (demo to you first, then to one peer, then to a small external group). The endpoint gives the project meaning beyond "the teacher told me to build this" and tells the student what "done" actually looks like.

## 19. OCD Educational Burden Study (2024)
**"The Psychosocial and Educational Burden of Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder in Youth"**
*Journal of Affective Disorders (Elsevier)*

**What they did:** Examined the specific ways OCD interferes with school functioning in children and adolescents. Collected data from both parent and child perspectives on which academic domains were most affected.

**Key findings:**
- 47% of parents and 44% of children reported OCD caused **significant problems in school/academic functioning**.
- The two most significant school problems: **(1) difficulties concentrating on schoolwork** (obsessive thoughts intrude during tasks) and **(2) difficulties doing homework** (compulsions and perfectionism delay completion).
- OCD symptoms **wax and wane with stress** — a student may be fine one week and severely impaired the next, without any visible external cause.
- Among those with OCD, symptom severity correlated with likelihood of needing school accommodations (48% received remedial help, 40% were in special education).
- Teachers generally have very limited knowledge of OCD and often mistake compulsive behaviors for defiance or attention-seeking.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** For your OCD students, the robotics context has a specific advantage and a specific risk. The advantage: building a physical thing provides a concrete, testable endpoint ("the motor moves") that bypasses perfectionism about abstract quality ("is my essay good enough?"). The risk: OCD students may get trapped in repetitive checking loops (re-tightening a screw 20 times, re-running the same code test endlessly). Design project milestones with explicit "done conditions" that are objective and binary: "The motor turns when you press the button. Yes or no. If yes, move to the next card." Also: OCD symptoms fluctuate unpredictably. A student who was highly independent last session may be deeply stuck today. Build flexibility into your expectations — have a "lighter load" version of each milestone that a struggling student can complete instead of the full version.

## 20. Sciacca (2025)
**"Experiences of Teachers Using Project-Based Learning with Students with Emotional and Behavioral Disorders"**
*Doctoral Dissertation, ProQuest*

**What they did:** The most recent study specifically examining what it is actually like for teachers to run PBL with EBD students. Qualitative interviews with teachers who had implemented PBL in EBD settings, examining what worked, what didn't, and what they wished they had known.

**Key findings:**
- PBL worked best when: **(a)** projects were broken into very short phases with clear, unambiguous completion criteria — not "work on your project" but "complete steps 1-3 on your milestone card"; **(b)** behavioral expectations were embedded into the project structure itself, not enforced as a separate overlay — the project *is* the behavior management tool; **(c)** teachers accepted from the start that different students would be at completely different stages simultaneously, and designed for asynchronous progress rather than trying to synchronize the group.
- Teachers who tried to keep all EBD students at the same project stage reported higher stress, more behavioral incidents, and lower satisfaction.
- Teachers who designed for asynchronous individual progress — exactly your model — reported the opposite: lower stress, fewer incidents, higher satisfaction.
- High-leverage practices identified: differentiation, explicit instruction embedded within projects, structured peer interaction, and continuous progress monitoring.

**What this means for your robotics workshop:** This is the most directly validating study for your exact model. You are already running asynchronous individual projects — and the research says this is *the correct architecture for EBD students*, not a compromise forced by limited resources. The practical implication: **never design a robotics session where all students need to be at the same stage at the same time.** Your independent-work window opens when three conditions are met: (1) each student has a concrete next action they understand; (2) the task at that action requires hands-on doing rather than planning or deciding; (3) you have a visible signal system (sticky-note flag, whiteboard help queue, red cup) so students who get stuck can signal you without disrupting others or waiting in anxiety.

## Synthesis: What the Top 20 Converge On

Reading across all 20 studies, five design principles emerge consistently:

**1. Externalize everything.** Working memory, planning, sequencing, and self-monitoring — everything that executive function normally handles internally must be made physical and visible. Mission cards, checklists, visual timers, self-tracking sheets. This is the #1 evidence-backed intervention (Belland g=3.13, Harrison, Moohr, Smith, visual schedules review).

**2. Design for fluctuating capacity, not fixed diagnoses.** A student's ability to work independently changes daily based on stress, sleep, external events. Build warm-ups that let you read the room, and have lighter/heavier versions of each milestone ready (Cumming, Watson, OCD study).

**3. Make the task itself the reward.** Tangible, immediate, testable results from building sustain attention better than any token economy. Every milestone should *do something visible* (Cibrian, Berrezueta-Guzman, Morsink, Smith/Langberg motivation).

**4. Transfer ownership through bounded choice.** Let students choose *how* to approach each milestone (2-3 options), *which* order to tackle sub-tasks, and *what* project to build — but always within structured boundaries. This reduces ODD resistance, increases engagement, and builds self-determination (Billingsley, Smale-Jacobse, Sciacca).

**5. Asynchronous progress is the architecture, not the exception.** Each student at a different stage on a different project is the optimal design for EBD populations. Trying to synchronize makes everything worse (Taylor, Sciacca, Culclasure).
