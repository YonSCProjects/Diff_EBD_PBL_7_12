ABSTRACT

Objective: Although front-line doctors recommend medications, this kind of treatment has limited efficacy in improving executive functions (EFs) in children and adolescents with attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD). This study explored the effects of non-pharmacological intervention on EFs in children and adolescents with ADHD.

Methods: In accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Review and Meta-analyses guidelines, we searched seven electronic databases: APA PsycINFO, CINAHL Complete, EMBASE, ERIC, Medline, Pubmed, and Web of Science, from inception to March 2022. Two authors independently screened studies for eligibility, extracted data, and assessed bias risk using the Physiotherapy Evidence Database scale. Our analyses included randomized controlled trials and non-randomized comparison studies of non-pharmacological interventions and assessed EFs through neurocognitive tasks in children and adolescents between 5 and 18 years.

Results: Sixty-seven studies with 3147 participants met the inclusion criteria. The final meta-analysis included 74 independent interventions categorized into six categories: cognitive training, EF-specific curriculum, game-based training, mindfulness practice, neurofeedback training, and physical exercise. Overall, non-pharmacological interventions (combined) produced significant moderate to large effects on overall EFs in children and adolescents with ADHD (g=0.673). Physical exercise had a large positive effect on domain-specific EFs, including inhibitory control (g=0.900) and cognitive flexibility (g=1.377). Cognitive training had a large training effect on working memory (g=0.907), and an EF-specific curriculum had a small to moderate beneficial effect on planning performance (g=0.532).

Conclusion: Non-pharmacological interventions, particularly physical exercise, cognitive training, and an EF-specific curriculum, appear to have beneficial effects on EFs in children and adolescents with ADHD.

BACKGROUND AND INTRODUCTION

Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a common neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. Executive functions (EFs) are a set of higher-order cognitive processes essential for goal-directed behavior, comprising three core components: inhibitory control, working memory, and cognitive flexibility. Children and adolescents with ADHD typically show significant deficits in EFs, which contribute to academic difficulties, social challenges, and reduced quality of life.

While pharmacological treatments (primarily stimulant medications) remain the first-line treatment for ADHD, their efficacy in improving EFs is limited, and concerns about side effects and long-term safety have led to growing interest in non-pharmacological alternatives.

Non-pharmacological interventions for EFs in ADHD include:
1. Cognitive training programs (computerized working memory training)
2. EF-specific curriculum (school-based programs targeting EF skills)
3. Game-based training (video games and interactive technologies designed to train EFs)
4. Mindfulness-based interventions (meditation and mindfulness practices)
5. Neurofeedback training (EEG-based biofeedback targeting brain activity)
6. Physical exercise (aerobic and structured physical activity programs)

---

METHODS

Search Strategy

Seven electronic databases were searched from inception to March 2022:
- APA PsycINFO
- CINAHL Complete
- EMBASE
- ERIC
- Medline
- PubMed
- Web of Science

Search terms combined ADHD, executive function domains (inhibitory control, working memory, cognitive flexibility, planning), and the six intervention types.

Inclusion Criteria:
- Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) or non-randomized comparison studies
- Participants: children and adolescents with ADHD, ages 5-18 years
- Non-pharmacological intervention targeting EFs
- EFs assessed through neurocognitive tasks (not only behavioral rating scales)
- Comparison condition (active or passive control)

Data Extraction and Quality Assessment:
- Two authors independently screened studies and extracted data
- Bias risk assessed using the Physiotherapy Evidence Database (PEDro) scale
- Discrepancies resolved through consensus

Statistical Analysis:
- Effect sizes calculated using Hedges' g (standardized mean difference)
- Random effects model used due to expected heterogeneity
- Subgroup analyses by intervention type and EF domain
- Publication bias assessed

---

RESULTS

Study Selection:
- Initial database search identified: >2,000 articles
- After deduplication and screening: 67 studies with 3,147 participants included
- Total of 74 independent interventions coded (some studies included multiple interventions)

Intervention Categories and Sample Sizes:
1. Cognitive training: 15 studies, 727 participants
2. EF-specific curriculum: 16 studies, 744 participants  
3. Game-based training: 12 studies, 598 participants
4. Mindfulness practice: studies addressing mindfulness
5. Neurofeedback training: 13 studies, 444 participants
6. Physical exercise: 17 studies, 558 participants

Overall Effects:

Combined non-pharmacological interventions:
- Overall EF: g = 0.673 (moderate to large effect; statistically significant)
- Evidence of publication bias; trim-and-fill adjustment increased estimated effect size

Effects by Intervention Type on Domain-Specific EFs:

Physical Exercise:
- Inhibitory control: g = 0.900 (large effect)
- Cognitive flexibility: g = 1.377 (very large effect)
- Note: Very high heterogeneity across studies

Cognitive Training:
- Working memory: g = 0.907 (large training effect)
- Near-transfer effects documented
- Questions remain about far-transfer to broader EF domains

EF-Specific Curriculum:
- Planning performance: g = 0.532 (small to moderate effect)
- Lower heterogeneity compared to other interventions
- Good evidence of transferability to real-world EF skills

Game-Based Training:
- Medium effect size on overall EF
- Promising for engagement and motivation
- High heterogeneity

Neurofeedback Training:
- Near-large effect on overall EF
- High heterogeneity
- Long training durations required

Mindfulness Practice:
- Moderate effects on attention-related EF components
- Benefits for self-regulation

---

DISCUSSION

Key Discussion Points:

1. Physical exercise shows the largest effects on EFs, particularly inhibitory control and cognitive flexibility. The neurobiological mechanisms include increased cerebral blood flow, neurotransmitter activity (dopamine and norepinephrine), and neuroplasticity. However, the very high heterogeneity across physical exercise studies limits conclusions about optimal exercise parameters.

2. Cognitive training shows strong near-transfer effects (improvement on trained tasks) but evidence for far-transfer (improvement on untrained EF tasks and real-world functioning) remains mixed.

3. EF-specific curriculum offers promise as a school-based approach that can be integrated into existing educational programs, showing consistent effects on planning with lower heterogeneity.

4. Methodological limitations: The meta-analysis mixed studies of substantially different interventions, limiting comparability. High heterogeneity was found across most intervention types. Many studies lacked adequate control conditions.

5. Publication bias: Evidence of publication bias was found, suggesting reported effect sizes may be inflated.

6. Ages and severity: Effects may differ across age groups and ADHD subtypes/severity levels; subgroup analyses by these factors were limited.

---

CONCLUSIONS

Non-pharmacological interventions, particularly physical exercise, cognitive training, and an EF-specific curriculum, appear to have beneficial effects on EFs in children and adolescents with ADHD. Physical exercise demonstrated the largest effects on inhibitory control and cognitive flexibility, while cognitive training showed strong effects on working memory. The EF-specific curriculum approach showed consistent, lower-heterogeneity effects on planning. These findings support the use of non-pharmacological approaches as adjuncts or alternatives to medication for improving EFs in children and adolescents with ADHD, though more rigorous research with adequate control groups and evaluation of far-transfer effects is needed.
