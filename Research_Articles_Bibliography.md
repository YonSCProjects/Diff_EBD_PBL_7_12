# Comprehensive Research Bibliography
## Differentiated PBL for Students with EBD in STEM/Robotics Settings
*Only verified, real research with identifiable authors, journals, and DOIs/links*

---

## A. FOUNDATIONAL TEXTS & FRAMEWORKS

1. **Tomlinson, C.A. (2014).** *The Differentiated Classroom: Responding to the Needs of All Learners* (2nd Ed.). ASCD.
   - The leading framework for differentiating content, process, and product by readiness, interest, and learning profile.

2. **CAST (2018).** *Universal Design for Learning Guidelines version 2.2.*
   - The definitive UDL framework: multiple means of engagement, representation, and action/expression.

3. **Barkley, R.A. (2015).** *Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder: A Handbook for Diagnosis and Treatment* (4th Ed.). Guilford Press.
   - ADHD as a disorder of executive functioning and self-regulation; the case for externalized scaffolding.

4. **Minahan, J. & Rappaport, N. (2012).** *The Behavior Code: A Practical Guide to Understanding and Teaching the Most Challenging Students.* Harvard Education Press.
   - Breaks down internalizing (anxiety, depression) and externalizing (ODD, ADHD) behaviors with step-by-step intervention plans.

5. **Greene, R.W. (2014).** *Lost at School: Why Our Kids with Behavioral Challenges are Falling Through the Cracks and How We Can Help Them.* Scribner.
   - The Collaborative & Proactive Solutions (CPS) model for ODD and conduct challenges.

6. **Condliffe, B., et al. (2017).** *Project-Based Learning: A Literature Review.* MDRC.
   - Comprehensive PBL implementation review; notes structural challenges and necessary supports for diverse learners.

## B. PBL + EMOTIONAL/BEHAVIORAL DISORDERS (DIRECT INTERSECTION)

7. **Taylor, J.C., Allen, L.M., Van, J., & Moohr, M. (2024).** "The Effects of Project-Based Learning on Student Behavior and Teacher Burnout in an Emotional/Behavioral Support Classroom." *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders* (SAGE).
   - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/10634266241235933
   - **The only published empirical study placing PBL directly inside an EBD classroom.** PBL reduced disruptive behavior and increased teacher efficacy. Key mechanism: continuous engaged activity eliminates trigger-rich dead time.

8. **Sciacca, J.L. (2025).** "Experiences of Teachers Using Project-Based Learning with Students with Emotional and Behavioral Disorders." Doctoral Dissertation, ProQuest.
   - Link: https://search.proquest.com/openview/f3284aea35dd8d14dd8429e421c14110/1
   - Most recent PBL + EBD study. Teachers reported: short phases with clear completion criteria work best; behavioral expectations embedded in project structure outperform external enforcement; asynchronous progress across students is the right model, not a compromise.

9. **Smith, D.F. (2022).** "Supporting Students with ADHD in Project-Based Learning." Doctoral Dissertation, University of Pittsburgh.
   - PDF: https://d-scholarship.pitt.edu/downloads/cd3ad931-a841-4eed-b597-a48038922ad7
   - Examines what supports enable ADHD students to succeed in PBL environments.

10. **Culclasure, B.T., Longest, K.C., et al. (2019).** "Project-Based Learning (PjBL) in Three Southeastern Public Schools: Academic, Behavioral, and Social-Emotional Outcomes." *Interdisciplinary Journal of Problem-Based Learning*, 13(2). **Cited by 119.**
    - PDF: https://docs.lib.purdue.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1842&context=ijpbl
    - Large-scale federally funded study (2015-2017). PBL improved academic, behavioral, AND social-emotional outcomes. Critical design feature: real audience and real product drove engagement.

11. **Lambie, K. (2020).** "Project-Based Learning (PBL) in STEM: Perspectives of Students with Special Education Needs (SENs)." Doctoral Dissertation, Western University.
    - Focuses on SEN students (primarily anxiety) in STEM PBL; explores differentiation for multiple special needs.

12. **Boardman, A.G. (2025).** "High School Students' Experiences With Scaffolding in Project-Based Learning." *Journal of Adolescent & Adult Literacy* (Wiley/ILA).
    - DOI: https://ila.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/jaal.70018
    - Key finding: the scaffolds that best enabled independent work were a small number of connected task cards, interactional scaffolds (structured prompts for asking for help/collaborating), and flexible materials adjustable without restarting.

## C. ROBOTICS, MAKERSPACES & STEM FOR SPECIAL EDUCATION

13. **Berrezueta-Guzman, J. & Robles-Bykbaev, V. (2021).** "Robotic Technologies in ADHD Care: Literature Review." *IEEE Access.*
    - DOI: 10.1109/access.2021.3137082
    - Exhaustive review confirming robotic assistants support executive function, attention span, and task-completion in children with ADHD.

14. **Berrezueta-Guzman, J. & Pau, I. (2021).** "Assessment of a Robotic Assistant for Supporting Homework Activities of Children With ADHD." *IEEE Access.*
    - DOI: 10.1109/access.2021.3093233
    - When a robotic system guides students through tasks via visual milestones, task adherence dramatically increases vs. teacher verbal commands.

15. **Papakostas, G.A. & Sidiropoulos, G. (2021).** "Social Robots in Special Education: A Systematic Review." *Electronics.*
    - DOI: 10.3390/electronics10121398
    - Robots provide predictable, non-judgmental interaction that reduces anxiety and oppositional behavior compared to human-to-human instruction.

16. **Cibrian, F.L. & Lakes, K.D. (2021).** "The Potential for Emerging Technologies to Support Self-Regulation in Children with ADHD: A Literature Review." *International Journal of Child-Computer Interaction.*
    - DOI: 10.1016/j.ijcci.2021.100421
    - Hands-on technological interventions provide immediate tangible feedback that helps ADHD children self-regulate and stay on task without teacher intervention.

17. **"Robots for Inclusive Classrooms: A Scoping Review." (2023).** *Universal Access in the Information Society* (Springer).
    - DOI: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10209-023-01065-z
    - Scoping review of 33 studies. LEGO Mindstorms kits (WeDo, NXT, EV3) were the most-used robots (13 studies). Only 2 robots were specifically developed for children with special needs; the rest are off-the-shelf.

18. **"Study of Divergent Thinking and Task Performance in Children with ADHD Through a Modular Robotic Task." (2025).** *Australasian Journal of Special and Inclusive Education* (Taylor & Francis).
    - DOI: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/19404158.2025.2567849
    - Children with ADHD (aged 9-14) using the CreaCube modular robotic task showed distinct exploratory patterns; demonstrates potential of robotics-based tasks for creative problem-solving in ADHD.

19. **"Education Technology for Students with ADHD in Secondary School: Preliminary Results of a Single-Case Study." (~2024).** *Springer.*
    - DOI: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-032-01632-4_27
    - Single-case study: inclusive learning through education technology for secondary students with ADHD to mitigate challenging behaviors and enhance attention, collaboration, and self-control.

20. **O'Connell, A. & Banga, A. (2024).** "Design and Evaluation of a Socially Assistive Robot Schoolwork Companion for College Students with ADHD." *ACM.*
    - DOI: 10.1145/3610977.3634929
    - 9 out of 10 students with ADHD felt the robotic companion helped them regain focus. Applies "body doubling" and externalized executive function concepts.

21. **"The Effects of Makerspace Learning on the Social Interactions Among Students with Emotional or Behavioral Disorder." (2019).** Doctoral Dissertation, University of Texas at San Antonio. *ERIC: ED604859.*
    - Link: https://eric.ed.gov/?id=ED604859
    - Single-case experiment with four middle school EBD students. Makerspace learning (constructionist principles) significantly improved social interactions compared to traditional classroom settings.

22. **Hwang, J. & Taylor, J.C. (2016).** "Stemming on STEM: A STEM Education Framework for Students with Disabilities." *Journal of Science Education for Students with Disabilities.*
    - Pedagogical adjustments for accessible, emotionally manageable STEM education for special education populations.

23. **Israel, M., et al. (2015).** "Supporting All Learners in School-Wide Computational Thinking: A Cross-Case Qualitative Analysis." *Computers & Education.*
    - How to integrate students with disabilities into computer science and robotics; highlights need for explicit instruction within open-ended tasks.

24. **Basham, J.D. & Marino, M.T. (2013).** "Understanding STEM Education and Supporting Students through Universal Design for Learning." *Teaching Exceptional Children.*
    - Applying UDL guidelines specifically to STEM environments for cognitive and neurodivergent diversity.

25. **"A Systematic Literature Review of Research-Based Interventions and Strategies for Students with Disabilities in STEM and STEAM Education." (2025).** *International Journal of Science and Mathematics Education* (Springer).
    - DOI: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10763-025-10544-z
    - Most current comprehensive review of STEM interventions for students with disabilities.

## D. SCAFFOLDING, EXECUTIVE FUNCTION & COGNITIVE LOAD

26. **Belland, B.R., Walker, A.E., Kim, N.J., & Lefler, M. (2017).** "Synthesizing Results From Empirical Research on Computer-Based Scaffolding in STEM Education: A Meta-Analysis." *Review of Educational Research*, 87(2), 309-344. **Cited by 400+.**
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/0034654316670999
    - Meta-analysis of 144 studies (333 outcomes). Computer-based scaffolding showed consistently positive effect (g=0.46) across all ages. **Scaffolding among students with learning disabilities showed a particularly large effect size (g=3.13).**

27. **Belland, B.R., Walker, A.E., & Kim, N.J. (2017).** "A Bayesian Network Meta-Analysis to Synthesize the Influence of Contexts of Scaffolding Use on Cognitive Outcomes in STEM Education." *Review of Educational Research* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.3102/0034654317723009
    - Metacognitive and strategic scaffolding produced small-to-moderate effect sizes (g=0.384 and g=0.345); conceptual scaffolding effect was smaller (g=0.126).

28. **Cumming, M.M., Qiu, Y., Oblath, R., Frazier, S.L., et al. (2025).** "Perceived Stress, Executive Function, and Stress Regulation: Implications for Middle Schoolers' Emotional and Behavioral Well-being." *Remedial and Special Education* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/07419325241265973
    - Executive function degrades rapidly when perceived stress exceeds threshold, leading to behavioral dysregulation. This is capacity failure, not willful misbehavior. Independence capacity fluctuates with daily stress state.

29. **"Efficacy of Non-Pharmacological Interventions on Executive Functions in Children and Adolescents with ADHD: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." (2023).** *Psychiatry Research* (Elsevier).
    - DOI: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1876201823002484
    - Significant moderate-to-large effects on overall executive functions; physical exercise, cognitive training, and EF-specific curricula were particularly beneficial.

30. **"The Efficacy of Visual Activity Schedule Intervention in Reducing Problem Behaviors in Children with ADHD Between the Age of 5 and 12 Years: A Systematic Review." (2022).** *PubMed.*
    - Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35035237/
    - Visual timers associated with significant reductions in inattentive and motor instability behaviors. Effects pronounced for children at higher risk for ADHD.

31. **"Scaffolding Executive Function Processes for Students." (2017).** *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders.* ERIC: EJ1168865.
    - PDF: https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1168865.pdf
    - Strategic note-taking intervention served as scaffold opening new pathways for struggling students to access executive functions and flexibly deploy cognitive strategies.

## E. DIFFERENTIATED INSTRUCTION — SYSTEMATIC REVIEWS & FRAMEWORKS

32. **Smale-Jacobse, A.E., Meijer, A., Helms-Lorenz, M., et al. (2019).** "Differentiated Instruction in Secondary Education: A Systematic Review of Research Evidence." *Frontiers in Psychology*, 10, 2366. **Cited by 771.**
    - Link: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2019.02366/full
    - Gold-standard review. Effective DI works along three axes: readiness, interest, and learning profile. Single-axis differentiation has much weaker effects.

33. **Billingsley, G.M., Thomas, C.N., et al. (2018).** "Effects of Student Choice of Instructional Method on the Learning Outcomes of Students with Comorbid Learning and Emotional/Behavioral Disabilities." *Learning Disability Quarterly* (SAGE). **Cited by 44.**
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0731948718768512
    - When EBD/LD students chose *how* they received instruction (not just what), engagement and task completion improved. Choice itself reduced problem behavior by transferring ownership.

34. **Ziernwald, L., Hillmayr, D., & Holzberger, D. (2022).** "Promoting High-Achieving Students Through Differentiated Instruction in Mixed-Ability Classrooms — A Systematic Review." *Journal for the Education of the Gifted* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1932202X221112931
    - DI in mixed-ability secondary classrooms along readiness, interest, and profile axes.

35. **"Australian Teachers and School Leaders' Use of Differentiated Learning Experiences as Responsive Teaching for Students with ADHD." (2023).** *ResearchGate.*
    - Link: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/369490306
    - Implications for teacher competency in autonomy-supportive differentiation for ADHD students.

## F. EBD — META-ANALYSES, STATE-OF-THE-FIELD REVIEWS

36. **Mitchell, B.S., Kern, L., & Conroy, M.A. (2019).** "Supporting Students with Emotional or Behavioral Disorders: State of the Field." *Behavioral Disorders* (SAGE). **Cited by 346.**
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0198742918816518
    - Definitive overview of the EBD research landscape: nomenclature, terminology, and state of supports.

37. **Soares, D.A., Harrison, J.R., Peltier, C., & Press, K. (2025).** "Academic and Behavioral Strategies in Inclusive Settings for Students With EBD: A Meta-Analysis." *Behavioral Disorders* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/01987429241261382
    - Most current meta-analysis on strategy effectiveness for EBD students in K-12 inclusive settings. Dearth of empirical support, though studied strategies showed moderate-to-large effects.

38. **Gersib, J.A. & Mason, S. (2023).** "A Meta-Analysis of Behavior Interventions for Students With Emotional-Behavioral Disorders in Self-Contained Settings." *Behavioral Disorders* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/01987429231160285
    - 15 studies with 20 effect sizes. Examines generalizability of behavior interventions from general education to self-contained EBD settings.

39. **Riden, B.S., Kumm, S., & Maggin, D.M. (2022).** "Evidence-Based Behavior Management Strategies for Students With or at Risk of EBD: A Mega Review of the Literature." *Remedial and Special Education* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/07419325211047947
    - Mega review of 50 meta-analytic/systematic reviews encompassing 20 interventions. Identified **eight interventions classified as evidence-based practices** for EBD students.

40. **Harrison, J.R., Soares, D.A., & Joyce, J. (2019).** "Inclusion of Students with Emotional and Behavioural Disorders in General Education Settings: A Scoping Review." *International Journal of Inclusive Education* (T&F). **Cited by 60.**
    - DOI: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/13603116.2018.1444107
    - Scoping review examining efficacy of academic and behavior interventions for at-risk students in inclusive settings.

41. **Maggin, D.M., Wehby, J.H., & Gilmour, A.F. (2016).** "Intensive Academic Interventions for Students With Emotional and Behavioral Disorders." *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1063426616649162
    - EBD students underperform due to instructional opportunity loss. Most effective recovery: increased engaged time, high-frequency low-stakes response opportunities, immediate specific feedback.

## G. SELF-REGULATION & SELF-MANAGEMENT INTERVENTIONS

42. **Smith, T.E., Thompson, A.M., Maynard, B.R., & Kim, A.M. (2025).** "Effects of Self-Management Interventions on Challenging Behavior: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." *Research on Social Work Practice* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/10497315231200351
    - 75 single-case studies (236 participants) + 4 comparison group studies (422 participants). Self-management interventions positively impacted challenging behaviors and academic outcomes.

43. **Moohr, M.L., Balint-Langel, K., Taylor, J.C., & Rizzo, K.L. (2021).** "Practicing Academic Independence: Self-Regulation Strategies for Students with Emotional and Behavioral Disorders." *Beyond Behavior* (SAGE), 30(1).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/10742956211020666
    - Provides step-by-step procedures for three evidence-based SR strategies: self-regulated strategy development, self-monitoring, and strategy instruction for EBD students.

44. **Harrison, J.R., Soares, D.A., Rudzinski, S., & Johnson, R. (2019).** "ADHD and Classroom-Based Interventions: Evidence-Based Status, Effectiveness, and Moderators of Effects in Single-Case Design Research." *Review of Educational Research* (SAGE). **Cited by 100+.**
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/0034654319857038
    - Systematic review of 147 studies. Most effective for ADHD: (1) self-management interventions and (2) instructional modifications (smaller chunks, concrete materials, frequent success points). Both outperformed consequence-based systems.

45. **Del Vecchio, M.L., Crosland, K., & Fuller, A. (2025).** "The Effects of Self Monitoring With I-Connect to Increase On Task Behavior of Students With or at Risk for Emotional and Behavioral Disorders." *Journal of Positive Behavior Interventions* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/10983007241268784
    - Technology-based self-monitoring (I-Connect app) plus reinforcement significantly increased on-task behavior for EBD students.

## H. ADHD — MOTIVATION, INTEREST & ENGAGEMENT

46. **Morsink, S., Van der Oord, S., Antrop, I., Danckaerts, M., & Scheres, A. (2022).** "Studying Motivation in ADHD: The Role of Internal Motives and the Relevance of Self Determination Theory." *Journal of Attention Disorders* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/10870547211050948
    - ADHD research needs to include internal motives as mediators; warns of negative effects when external reinforcers undermine intrinsic motivation.

47. **Smith, Z.R. & Langberg, J.M. (2020).** "Academic Motivation Deficits in Adolescents with ADHD and Associations with Academic Functioning." *Research on Child and Adolescent Psychopathology* (Springer).
    - DOI: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10802-019-00601-x
    - Adolescents with ADHD showed significant motivational deficits across intrinsic motivation (d=0.49), extrinsic motivation (d=0.43), and amotivation (d=0.42). Higher intrinsic motivation predicted higher reading scores.

48. **"Academic Motivation Decreases Across Adolescence for Youth With and Without ADHD: Effects of Motivation on Academic Success." Smith et al. (2023).** *Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry* (Wiley).
    - DOI: https://acamh.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jcpp.13815
    - Motivation declines across adolescence for all youth but more steeply for ADHD; motivation is a key mediating variable.

## J. OCD IN SCHOOLS

52. **Colbert, E., Powell, L., Weighall, A., & Gibson-Miller, J. (2025).** "Current Levels of Knowledge and the Impact of Psychoeducational Interventions on Understandings of Paediatric OCD Among School Staff: A Systematic Narrative Review." *SAGE.*
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/27546330251348341
    - 6 studies, 952 participants. School staff have limited OCD knowledge, particularly regarding accommodations; psychoeducation shows promise.

53. **"The Impact of Pediatric Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder on School Attendance and School Functioning: A Case for Supported Education." (2025).** *Child Psychiatry & Human Development* (Springer).
    - DOI: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10578-025-01846-y
    - 48% received remedial help, 40% were in special education, 21.6% experienced partial/no school attendance. Symptom severity predicted accommodation need.

54. **"The Psychosocial and Educational Burden of Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder in Youth." (2024).** *Journal of Affective Disorders* (Elsevier).
    - DOI: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0165032724015088
    - 47% of parents and 44% of children reported OCD caused significant school problems. Main issues: concentrating on schoolwork and doing homework. Symptoms wax and wane with stress.

## K. TRAUMA-INFORMED APPROACHES IN SCHOOLS

55. **Watson, S., et al. (2025).** "A Critical Review of Empirical Support for Trauma-Informed Approaches in Schools and a Call for Conceptual, Empirical and Practice Integration." *Review of Education* (Wiley/BERA).
    - DOI: https://bera-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/rev3.70025
    - Most rigorous current review. Interest in trauma-informed schools is high across US, UK, Australia, Canada, but empirical evidence for whole-school models is still limited. Calls for integration with existing frameworks (DI, SEL).

56. **"A Scoping Review of School-Based Efforts to Support Students Who Have Experienced Trauma." (2020).** *School Mental Health* (Springer).
    - DOI: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12310-020-09368-9
    - Identifies key components: counseling, skill development, psychoeducation, parent engagement.

57. **"Multi-Tiered Approaches to Trauma-Informed Care in Schools: A Systematic Review." (2019).** *School Mental Health* (Springer).
    - DOI: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12310-019-09326-0
    - Maps trauma-informed care onto MTSS/PBIS tiered frameworks.

58. **"Childhood Posttraumatic Stress and School-Related Behaviors: A Scoping Review." (2024).** *School Mental Health* (Springer).
    - DOI: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12310-024-09729-8
    - Childhood trauma adversely impacts academic performance, classroom behavior, and relationships.

59. **"Understanding and Responding to the Effects of Trauma in the Classroom: A Primer for Educators." (2023).** *Elsevier.*
    - DOI: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2773233923000049
    - Practical primer connecting trauma exposure to classroom behavior; nearly half of US students have experienced a potentially traumatic event.

60. **"Adverse Childhood Experiences and Internalizing and Externalizing Problems of Students with EBD: The Role of PTSD Symptoms and Executive Functioning." (2025).** *International Journal of School & Educational Psychology* (Taylor & Francis).
    - DOI: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/21683603.2025.2473897
    - ACEs interact with PTSD symptoms and executive functioning to worsen outcomes in EBD students.

## L. ANXIETY & DEPRESSION IN SCHOOL SETTINGS

61. **"School Anxiety Accommodation in Youth: Prevalence and Patterns Among Teachers." (2025).** *Child Psychiatry & Human Development* (Springer).
    - DOI: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10578-025-01853-z
    - Every teacher reported at least one anxiety-accommodating behavior; 71% used them frequently. Accommodation can promote avoidance as an anxiety maintenance factor.

62. **"School-Based Depression and Anxiety Prevention Programs: An Updated Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." (2021).** *Clinical Psychology Review* (Elsevier).
    - DOI: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0272735821001227
    - 130 articles, 118 trials, 45,924 participants. School-based prevention programs are effective; targeted programs may outperform universal ones.

63. **"School-Based Mental Health Interventions Targeting Depression or Anxiety: A Meta-Analysis of Rigorous Randomized Controlled Trials." (2022).** *Journal of Youth and Adolescence* (Springer).
    - DOI: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10964-022-01684-4
    - School-based interventions effective at reducing depression and anxiety in K-12.

64. **"Adolescent Depression in School: Risk Factors and Consequences on School Functioning." (2024).** *Zeitschrift fur Bildungsforschung* (Springer).
    - DOI: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s35834-024-00458-1
    - High depressive symptoms elevate risk of disorder onset and reduce school performance.

65. **McCarthy, A.E., Young, J.F., Benas, J.S., & Gallop, R.J. (2018).** "School-Related Outcomes From a Randomized Controlled Trial of Adolescent Depression Prevention Programs." *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1063426617717739
    - RCT showing depression prevention programs improve school-related outcomes.

## M. COOPERATIVE LEARNING, SOCIAL SKILLS & SEL FOR EBD

66. **Wattanawongwan, S., Smith, S.D., & Vannest, K.J. (2021).** "Cooperative Learning Strategies for Building Relationship Skills in Students With Emotional and Behavioral Disorders." *Beyond Behavior* (SAGE), 30(1).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1074295621997599
    - EBD students benefit in academic achievement, social skills, and relationship skills through cooperative activities — when groups are well-structured with clear expectations and explicit instruction.

67. **Daunic, A.P., Aydin, B., Corbett, N.L., Smith, S.W., et al. (2023).** "Social-Emotional Learning Intervention for K-1 Students at Risk for Emotional and Behavioral Disorders: Mediation Effects." *Behavioral Disorders* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/01987429231185098
    - SEL programs significantly enhance social-emotional skills and reduce conduct problems; effects larger for high-risk students.

## N. CHECK-IN/CHECK-OUT (CICO) & STRUCTURED BEHAVIORAL SUPPORT

68. **Kleinsmith, C.E. & Maggin, D.M. (2025).** "Practical Implementation of Evidence-Based Practices: Check-in Check-out for Students With and at Risk for Emotional and Behavioral Disorders." *Teaching Exceptional Children* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/00400599251326459
    - CICO is an evidence-based Tier 2 intervention for EBD; practical implementation guide.

69. **Flannery, K.B., McGrath Kato, M., Kittelman, A., et al. (2024).** "Check-In/Check-Out (CICO) in High School: Results From a Small Randomized Controlled Trial." *Behavioral Disorders* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/01987429231225461
    - RCT of high-school-adapted CICO. Small-to-moderate effect sizes on attendance (tardies, absences) and teacher ratings of academic/social behaviors.

70. **Kittelman, A., et al. (2024).** "CICO-Secondary: A Single-Case Experimental Study in High School." *Journal of Positive Behavior Interventions* (SAGE).
    - DOI: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/10983007231200542
    - Adapted CICO for adolescents proved implementable with fidelity and improved outcomes.

## O. UDL — REVIEWS & SECONDARY EDUCATION
