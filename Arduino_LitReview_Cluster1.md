*Phase A working notes. Source: deep reads of 11 verified articles + skim of 15 more, all in `articles_full_texts/`. Feeds Phase B principle distillation.*

## Deep-read summaries

### Harrison, Soares, Rudzinski, & Johnson (2019) — ADHD classroom-based interventions
**Citation:** *Review of Educational Research*, 89(4), 569–611. DOI: 10.3102/0034654319857038. Source file: `harrison_2019_adhd.txt`.

**Headline:** Classroom-based interventions for ADHD are moderately effective; **self-management and instructional interventions** meet WWC evidence-based standards. Effectiveness depends on (a) FBA-informed selection, (b) setting (special ed > general ed for instructional effects), and (c) implementer (researcher > teacher for behavioral effects).

**Key quotes:**
- "Classroom-based interventions for students with ADHD were moderately effective."
- Self-management and instructional interventions "deemed evidence based by What Works Clearinghouse standards."
- Behavioral interventions "most effective when selected through functional behavior analysis and implemented by researchers in secondary settings."
- Instructional interventions "more effective when implemented in special education settings targeting academic outcomes."

**Candidate principles:**
- Every classroom intervention for ADHD should be informed by a functional behavior analysis (FBA).
- Self-management and instructional scaffolding are the two strongest evidence-based classroom intervention categories for ADHD.

**Caveats:** Full text partially paywalled; effect sizes by moderator not extracted.

### Mitchell, Kern, & Conroy (2019) — EBD state of the field
**Citation:** *Behavioral Disorders*, 44(2), 70–84. DOI: 10.1177/0198742918816518. Source file: `mitchell_2019.txt`.

**Headline:** EBD students are at high risk for long-term negative outcomes (social, academic, dropout). Field recommendations: revised IDEA terminology, universal screening, multitiered systems of support (MTSS), integrated service delivery.

**Key quote:** "Students who experience ED are at greater risk for a host of negative long-term outcomes that include poor social, behavioral, and academic achievement in school, which in turn may impact graduation and postsecondary outcomes."

**Candidate principles:**
- MTSS with preventive interventions is foundational — Arduino PBL can function as a Tier 1 engagement/skills activity with Tier 2/3 layered supports.
- Early, universal screening for SEB problems.

### Cumming et al. (2024) — Perceived stress, executive function, EBD
**Citation:** *Remedial and Special Education*, 45(2), 85–100. Source file: `cumming_2024.txt`.

**Headline:** Students with/at-risk for EBD have significantly lower **working memory** and **cognitive flexibility** than peers. **Perceived stress directly degrades EF**, which in turn mediates academic performance (ELA β=.61; math β=.65). EF is not a trait — it's a state that collapses under stress.

**Key quotes:**
- "Students with or at-risk for EBD scored lower on EF and academic tasks."
- "Perceived stress was directly and negatively related with EF and indirectly associated with academic outcomes via EF."
- "Students with or at-risk for EBD had lower working memory and cognitive flexibility compared to typical peers."

**Candidate principles:**
- Every learning activity must include **external cognitive scaffolds** (visual checklists, step cards, reference aids) to bypass working memory and flexibility deficits.
- Stress reduction (predictable routines, safe peers, present adults) is not optional — it's load-bearing for EF and therefore for learning.
- **Independence capacity fluctuates with daily stress.** Tier assignment should allow fluid movement between tiers across sessions.

**Caveats:** Small sample (n=118); correlational/structural, not intervention-mechanistic.

### Harrison, Soares, & Joyce (2019) — EBD inclusion scoping review
**Citation:** *International Journal of Inclusive Education*, 23(12), 1258–1277. DOI: 10.1080/13603116.2018.1444107. Source file: `harrison_soares_joyce_2019.txt`.

**Headline:** Policies support EBD inclusion but the empirical literature is fragmented across six themes (professional experience, strategy effectiveness, policy, influencing factors, teacher training, teacher knowledge). Gaps dominate.

**Candidate principle:** Inclusion requires alignment across policy, teacher training, strategy effectiveness — policy mandates alone do not translate to effective practice.

**Caveats:** Abstract/metadata only; scoping review, not outcomes synthesis.

### Barker & de Lugt (2022) — ODD evidence-based practices
**Citation:** *International Journal of Special Education*, 37(1), 85–98. Source file: `barker_2022.txt`.

**Headline:** **ODD is an interactional disorder** — symptoms occur within relationships and social contexts. Three evidence-based classroom interventions: functional behavior analysis, group contingency, and self-monitoring.

**Key quotes:**
- "ODD should be best considered to be an interactional disorder... the symptoms of ODD largely occur within a relationship between the child and another person, within a social context."
- "Children with ODD rebel against any attempt to modify or influence their behavior, both positively (e.g., using rewards) or negatively (e.g., using punishments). They will see this as a manipulation, and will act in a way that counters the person trying to modify their behavior."
- "Most cases of ODD do not appear in all settings... modifications and differences in the environment can be effective in the remediation of ODD."

**What to avoid:** Direct reward/punishment systems (perceived as manipulation), verbal coercion, power struggles.

**Candidate principles:**
- ODD interventions must be **environmentally sensitive and relationship-centered**; avoid perceived coercion.
- Group contingencies and self-monitoring work because they **reduce adult–child confrontation** — the task is "the boss," not the teacher.
- **Environmental modification > compliance enforcement.**

### Abramovitch et al. (2024) — Pediatric OCD educational burden
**Citation:** *Journal of Affective Disorders*, 368, 97–106. Source file: `abramovitch_2024.txt`.

**Headline:** ~90% of youth with OCD report functional impairment in at least one domain; **school is the most-affected domain**. Academic impairment ranges 32–47% in parent/child reports. Social interpersonal problems affect up to three-quarters of youth.

**Key quotes:**
- "Nearly 90% of children with OCD report impairment in at least one domain and almost 50% endorse impairment in at least three domains."
- "One of the most common child- and parent-reported OCD-related impairments are related to school work and homework, with endorsement ranging from 32% to 47%."
- "Children with OCD reported being most affected by their symptoms in the school domain."

**What to avoid:** Public visibility of rituals; misattribution of compulsions as "perfectionism" or "diligence"; exposure to compulsion-triggering situations without clinical support.

**Candidate principles:**
- School staff need OCD-specific recognition training (general anxiety knowledge doesn't transfer).
- Accommodations for OCD should **allow flexible time and reduced public visibility** for rituals — in Arduino PBL, this means no forced public demos, time-neutral task cards, solo-work options at every step.

### Colbert, Powell, Weighall, & Gibson-Miller (2025) — School staff OCD interventions
**Citation:** *Neurodiversity*, 2025. Source file: `colbert_2025.txt`.

**Headline:** School staff OCD knowledge is limited (46–58% recognize OCD as a disorder). **Brief written psychoeducation (fact sheets, info packs) shows moderate effectiveness; longer workshops (2+ hours) do not.** OCD symptoms are systematically under-recognized compared to general mental health concerns.

**Key quotes:**
- "Schools identified 58% of youth as above OCD threshold, compared to 83% by parents. This suggests that general signs of mental health difficulties are more consistently observed across settings, however OCD-specific indicators may be under-recognised within educational settings."
- "Brief psychoeducational interventions appear to show moderate effectiveness for improving OCD knowledge in school staff. Paper-based informational materials [...] both showed significant improvements in knowledge, while a workshop format [...] did not."

**Candidate principle:** For staff training, brief written materials > long workshops. Implication for the Ministry proposal: include a 2-page OCD fact sheet as a deliverable, not a multi-hour training module.

**Caveats:** Only 6 studies reviewed; quality fair to poor; no follow-up on student outcomes.

### Smith et al. (2024) — Self-management interventions meta-analysis
**Citation:** *Research on Social Work Practice*, 2024. DOI: 10.1177/10497315231200351. Source file: `smith_2024.txt` (149K — file exceeded deep-read token budget; principles inferred from abstract and corpus triangulation).

**Inferred headline (from corpus triangulation):** Self-management interventions (self-monitoring, self-evaluation, self-reinforcement, self-instruction) are evidence-based for challenging behavior across a meta-analysis.

**Candidate principle:** Self-management routines should be embedded in every Arduino PBL task card (self-check step, self-rate step, self-reward step).

### Del Vecchio, Crosland, & Blair (2025) — I-Connect self-monitoring app
**Citation:** *Journal of Positive Behavior Interventions*, 27(1). Source file: `del_vecchio_2025.txt`.

**Headline:** Technology-based self-monitoring (I-Connect app) + reinforcement produced significant on-task behavior gains in upper-elementary EBD students. 100% treatment integrity, strong social validity.

**Candidate principles:**
- Digital self-monitoring tools are feasible in classrooms and work when paired with reinforcement.
- Student and teacher buy-in is a prerequisite for implementation fidelity — students must want to use the tool.

**Caveats:** Small single-case sample (n=3); on-task measured only, not academic skills.

### Rosen, Boyle, Cariss, & Forchelli (2014) — Scaffolding executive function via strategic note-taking
**Citation:** *Learning Disabilities: A Multidisciplinary Journal*, 20(4), 165–176. ERIC EJ1168865. Source file: `meltzer_2014.txt` (NOTE: filename is mis-leading — the file contains the Rosen et al. paper, not a Meltzer paper. The Verification_Log P2 entry incorrectly "corrected" the authors to Meltzer, Reddy, Sales Pollica & Roditi; that correction is itself wrong and needs to be re-corrected. See CORRECTIONS NEEDED section in Arduino_LitReview_Notes.md.)

**Headline:** A two-column strategic note-taking scaffold **opens pathways for students with LD to access and flexibly deploy executive function**. Critically: *how* each student uses the scaffold and *what* they learn about themselves is individualized by LD profile.

**Key quotes:**
- "The strategic note-taking intervention served as a scaffold, opening up new pathways for struggling students to access executive functions and flexibly deploy cognitive strategies."
- "Importantly, 'how' each student did so and 'what' each student learned about her/himself as a learner in the process were dependent on the specific challenges presented by her/his learning disability."
- "Cognitive scaffolding can help students with learning disabilities actively improve their own executive functioning."

**Candidate principles:**
- Scaffolds must be **embedded in authentic tasks** (not taught as separate skills).
- Scaffolds must be **adaptable to individual profiles** — one-size-fits-all fails.
- **External supports enable EF practice without requiring intact EF first** — you don't have to have working memory to USE a working-memory scaffold, and using the scaffold is how you improve your own EF over time.

**Caveats:** n=3 case studies; LD (not EBD) population, though mechanisms likely transfer.

### Gibbs (2023) — Australian teachers' DI for students with ADHD
**Citation:** *Emotional and Behavioural Difficulties*, 28, 18–31. DOI: 10.1080/13632752.2023.2194131. Source file: `whitaker_2023.txt` (NOTE: filename is misleading — the file contains the Gibbs paper, not a Whitaker paper. Single author: Kathy Gibbs, Griffith University. The Verification_Log P11 entry incorrectly "corrected" the authors to Whitaker et al.; that correction is itself wrong and needs to be re-corrected.)

**Headline:** Australian teachers use DI effectively for ADHD and create safe, secure classroom climates, **but do not differentiate assessments**. School leaders unclear on differentiation practices.

**Key quotes:**
- "Teachers and school leaders have a good understanding of ADHD, teachers use DI as an effective teaching practice to enhance learning for this student group and ensure the classroom environment is safe and secure. However, they do not adjust assessments for students with ADHD."
- "School leaders are not clear how teachers differentiate assessments or adapt the classroom environment."

**Candidate principles:**
- Differentiated instruction requires differentiated **assessment** for validity — Arduino PBL assessment rubrics must offer multiple proficiency pathways.
- School leader buy-in is not automatic; the Ministry proposal should include administrator-facing materials.

**Caveats:** Small qualitative study, Australian context; abstract-only.

## Skim summaries

- **Gersib & Mason (2023)** — Meta-analysis: behavior interventions in self-contained EBD classrooms yield large effects (g=0.93), with relational components (parent communication, peer support, adult mentoring) as the strongest moderator (B=1.26, p<.05). Implication: structure + relationships > structure alone.
- **Riden, Kumm, & Maggin (2022)** — Mega review of 50 systematic reviews, 20 practices; identifies 8 with sufficient evidence: CICO, FABI, Group Contingency, Peer-Mediated, Self-Management, SRSD (writing), Token Economies, Video Modeling. Match intensity to need.
- **Maggin, Wehby, & Gilmour (2016)** — Framework for brief experimental analyses (BEA) within MTSS; positions individualization as ongoing, data-driven, embedded in instruction — not post-hoc.
- **Soares, Harrison, Peltier, & Press (2024)** — Meta-analysis of academic/behavioral strategies in inclusive settings for EBD: moderate-to-large effects, but sparse general-ed implementation evidence; requires adaptation.
- **Moohr, Balint-Langel, Taylor, & Rizzo (2021)** — Self-regulation strategies for EBD: SRSD, self-monitoring, strategy instruction. 64% delivered 1:1 or small-group. Deliberate practice → self-efficacy. Relevant to small-group Arduino workshops.
- **Gilmour, Sandilos, Pilny, Schwartz, & Wehby (2022)** — Teacher burnout profiles predict classroom management quality. Struggling teachers use fewer effective strategies and give less autonomy. **Teacher well-being is a first-class design constraint for the curriculum.**
- **Morsink et al. (2022)** — [File exceeded token budget; likely addresses external vs. intrinsic motivation in ADHD. Warrants targeted re-read for Phase B.]
- **Smith, Langberg, Cusick, Green, & Becker (2020)** — 8th-grade ADHD students show significantly lower intrinsic motivation (d=0.49), extrinsic motivation (d=0.43), and higher amotivation (d=0.42). **Extrinsic motivation paradoxically supports homework/GPA for ADHD** — structured external supports are enabling, not undermining.
- **Qiu et al. (2023)** — Meta-analysis of 67 studies: non-pharmacological EF interventions for ADHD show moderate-to-large effects (g=0.673). **Physical exercise has the largest effects on inhibitory control (g=0.900) and cognitive flexibility (g=1.377).** Implication: build movement breaks into the session routine.
- **Jones (2018)** — Brief ODD practitioner guide; emphasizes environmental modification, clear expectations, avoiding power struggles. Applicable to Arduino facilitation.
- **Kaur, Floyd, & Balta (2022)** — Evidence review of seven ODD behavioral treatment programs; underlying principle: non-confrontational, transparent problem-solving > coercive control. CPS (Collaborative Problem Solving) and modified group-contingency most transferable to school.
- **Fernández de la Cruz et al. (2025)** — [File exceeded deep-read token budget. Title: impact of pediatric OCD on school attendance and functioning.]
- **Thomas & Karuppali (2022)** — Visual Activity Schedules for ADHD children 5–12: reduced task-initiation latency, reduced off-task during transitions. Work best when paired with clear task structure. Directly applicable to Arduino task cards with picture-cue steps.
- **Gage, Kramer, & Ellis (2021)** — 350K+ high school students; EBD students report significantly more negative perceptions of school climate across all dimensions. **EBD students with IEP services perceived more positive climates than EBD students without IEP services** in some domains — formal, visible supports improve sense of belonging.

## Top candidate design principles from Cluster 1

1. **FBA-informed intervention selection.** Every scaffold, reinforcement, or behavioral routine in the Arduino workshop should be picked based on the individual student's antecedents and function, not a one-size-fits-all protocol. (Harrison 2019; Barker 2022; Riden 2022)
2. **Embedded, individualized scaffolding.** External cognitive supports (task cards, visual schedules, reference sheets) embedded in authentic tasks and adapted to individual profiles. (Rosen 2014; Cumming 2024)
3. **Stress reduction and EF support are inseparable.** Predictable routines, safe peer dynamics, present adults — EF is a state that collapses under stress. (Cumming 2024; Harrison 2019)
4. **Relational moderators > structure alone.** Peer collaboration structure, teacher mentoring, family connection where possible. (Gersib 2023; Gage 2021)
5. **ODD → environmental modification, not coercion.** Group contingencies and self-monitoring reduce adult–child confrontation. The checklist is the boss. (Barker 2022)
6. **Self-monitoring + reinforcement for engagement.** Digital or paper self-monitoring with interval check-ins and visible progress. (Del Vecchio 2025; Moohr 2021)
7. **Movement breaks for ADHD inhibitory control and flexibility.** Physical exercise has the largest EF effect sizes. Sessions must include built-in movement. (Qiu 2023)
8. **External structure is enabling, not undermining, for ADHD.** Explicit task breakdowns, structured reinforcement, clear contingencies. Intrinsic motivation through competence + autonomy within structure. (Smith 2020; Rosen 2014)
9. **OCD-specific staff knowledge via brief written materials.** 2-page fact sheet > 2-hour workshop. (Colbert 2025; Abramovitch 2024)
10. **Teacher well-being is a first-class design constraint.** Burnout predicts reduced effective strategy use and less autonomy-giving. Arduino PBL must reduce teacher load (pre-prepared materials, self-directed task cards, peer co-facilitation). (Gilmour 2022)
11. **Tiered supports within a small-group workshop.** MTSS principles apply even at group-of-8 scale: Arduino PBL is the Tier 1 activity; differentiated scaffolding layers Tier 2/3 on top. (Mitchell 2019; Riden 2022)
12. **Visual schedules reduce on-task latency and transition friction.** Picture-cued task cards lower verbal-prompt demand. (Thomas & Karuppali 2022)

## Gaps in Cluster 1 for Arduino PBL

1. Limited evidence on peer-led or collaborative learning for EBD populations — most studies are teacher-led, whole-class, or 1:1.
2. No mechanistic studies of hands-on PBL × EF scaffolding interaction.
3. **Zero studies on Arduino/maker/STEM projects specifically for EBD students.**
4. No guidance on sustaining teacher facilitation without burnout in Arduino-PBL contexts.
5. Unclear how to balance differentiation for mixed ADHD/ODD/OCD/anxiety groups simultaneously.
6. OCD-specific accommodation in open-ended problem-solving contexts absent.
7. Longitudinal outcomes of scaffolded interventions unclear — most studies measure proximal on-task behavior.
8. Limited guidance on scaffold fading without creating learned helplessness.
9. Family engagement in school-based PBL for EBD not addressed.
10. Cost, resource, and fidelity barriers to implementation not studied.
