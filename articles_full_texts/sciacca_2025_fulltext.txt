                                                     Abstract
Students with emotional and behavioral disorders (EBD) are at risk for many adverse outcomes
due to their complex needs (National Center for Education Statistics, 2024; Office of Special
Education Programs, 2020; Smith et al., 2018). The difficulty teachers face in meeting those
needs adds an additional challenge (Smith et al., 2018; Sutherland et al., 2019). Project-based
learning is an instructional method that shares similarities with high-leverage practices (HLP)
(Wertz & Mulcahy, 2024) and has been successful with students without disabilities (Culclasure
et al., 2019; Duke et al., 2021; Eldiva & Azizah, 2019; Febriana, 2017; Gomez-Pablos et al.,
2017; Revelle et al., 2020; Samsudin et al., 2020; Sormunen et al., 2019); however, there is little
research on its use with students with EBD. This study aimed to understand the experiences and
perceptions of teachers using PjBL with students with EBD. A collective case study was
conducted using interviews and artifact data from three teachers using PjBL with students with
EBD. Data from three participant interviews and artifacts were analyzed using HLP and the
foundations of culturally inclusive pedagogies and practices as a theoretical framework. Findings
highlighted the importance of building relationships with students to adapt to their needs,
positive impacts across academic and non-academic domains, challenges related to planning and
flexible grouping, and an overall positive effect on teachers and students. These insights offer
valuable guidance for future research and practice focused on understanding and expanding the
use of PjBL to better serve students with EBD.

                                   Chapter I. Statement of the Problem
         Students with emotional and behavioral disabilities (EBD) face challenges in traditional
educational environments. They are challenged socially, emotionally, and behaviorally and
struggle academically (Chen et al., 2021; Smith et al., 2018). These challenges lead to adverse
outcomes, including negative impacts on academic achievement and behaviors (Chen et al.,
2021; Smith et al., 2018), increased rates of high school dropouts (National Center for Education
Statistics, 2024), and isolation from their typical, same-aged peers (Office of Special Education
Programs, 2020). Implementing evidence-based practices with these students can be difficult
because of their complex needs (Farley et al., 2023; Smith et al., 2018; Sutherland et al., 2019).
One evidence-based intervention might work for some students with EBD, but the multiple
variables that influence a student's behaviors and achievement require individualized adaptive
approaches (Farley et al., 2023).
         It is important to identify instructional approaches that teachers successfully implement
with a positive impact on their students. Project-based learning (PjBL) is an approach that has
had a positive impact on the academic and social-emotional needs of non-disabled students
(Culclasure et al., 2019; Febriana, 2017; Lee et al., 2015; Samsudin et al., 2020). While this
approach has limited research on students with EBD (Taylor et al., 2024), the positive impact it
has on students with learning disabilities (Berkeley et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2022; Sormunen et al.,
2019) and visually impaired, deaf, or hard of hearing (Eldiva & Azizah, 2019) may be replicated
across populations. This case study explored the experiences and perceptions of teachers who
have used PjBL with students with EBD, focusing on their views regarding the impact on
students and the implementation process.

Problem Statement
         The problem is that students with EBD have complex needs. The multifaceted challenges

students with EBD face have adverse outcomes related to academics and behavior (Chen et al.,
2021; Smith et al., 2018) and cause disengagement (Didion et al., 2018). While the national
average dropout rate for all students with disabilities in the 2021-2022 school year was 15%,
39% of students with emotional disturbance dropped out of high school (National Center for
Education Statistics, 2024), an increase from 32% in the 2017-2018 school year (Office of
Special Education Programs, 2020). High school dropouts are more likely to be unemployed or
receive low wages (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2024). Individuals without a high school
diploma have a 5.6% unemployment rate, compared with 3.9% for individuals with a high school
diploma. Dropouts earn a median weekly income of $708, compared with $899 for high school
graduates. (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2024). In 2023, the average monthly expenses in the
United States totaled $6,440 (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2023), nearly double the income of
a high school dropout. Earning an average annual salary of $36,816 (U.S. Bureau of Labor
Statistics, 2024) places them far below the average cost of living in Pennsylvania, which was
$87,500 per year for a family of four (Adams, 2024).

         Students with disabilities represent 17% of the K-12 population but account for 24% of
in-school suspensions, 29% of out-of-school suspensions, 21% of expulsions, and 28% of
school-related arrests (U.S. Department of Education Office of Civil Rights, 2023). Exclusionary
discipline practices lead to lower academic achievement (Wang et al., 2022), decreased student
engagement (Cohen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022), increases in disruptive behavior, adverse
impacts on social and emotional skills (Cohen et al., 2023), and an increase in arrests (Novak,
2022).
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  17

         Students with disabilities also accounted for 81% of physical restraints, 32% of
mechanical restraints, and 75% of seclusionary practices (U.S. Department of Education Office
of Civil Rights, 2023). Seclusionary practices encompass any "involuntary confinement of a
student alone in any room or area. It includes the use of any room or area in which the student is
alone and not free to leave (or believes they are not free to leave)" (U.S. Department of Justice,
2024, para. 1). Students with EBD had more than twice the number of disciplinary removals as
compared to other students with disabilities (Office of Special Education Programs, 2020).
Students with EBD are more likely to attend school in a separate setting, with 12% attending a
separate school as compared to 3% of all students with disabilities (Office of Special Education
Programs, 2020). Students with EBD are overrepresented in the juvenile justice system and more
likely to recidivate (Kim et al., 2022). Their academic, social, emotional, and behavioral
challenges can cause decreased student engagement and poor social skills (Smith et al., 2018)
that contribute to these adverse outcomes (National Center for Education Statistics, 2024; Office
of Special Education Programs, 2020; Smith et al., 2018).

         Though research-based practices are available, it has been difficult to implement those
practices in schools due to the complex needs of students with EBD (Farley et al., 2023; Smith et
al., 2018; Sutherland et al., 2019). Teachers often feel unprepared to meet the students' needs and
struggle to implement "effective instructional and classroom management strategies" (Gilmour et
al., 2022, p. 16). Lack of knowledge and negative attitudes toward students with EBD present an
additional challenge (Alkahtani, 2022; Gilmour et al., 2022) that reduces teachers' ability to
implement academic and non-academic strategies with fidelity (Gilmour et al., 2022). The
stresses of teaching students with EBD can lead to educator burnout (Gilmour et al., 2022;
Hirsch et al., 2023) and an increased risk of turnover for both special educators and non-special
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  18

educators (Gilmour & Wehby, 2020). Understanding teachers' experiences who use PjBL with
students with EBD can help inform practices relative to these students' complex academic,
behavioral, social, and emotional needs.
Purpose of the Study

         Identifying and implementing the most effective strategies to use when teaching students
with EBD remains a challenge based on the individual needs of each student (Criss et al., 2024;
Gilmour et al., 2022; Scheibel, 2023; Sutherland et al., 2019). Project-based learning (PjBL)
offers a way to engage students with EBD academically while addressing social-emotional
learning academically (Culclasure et al., 2019; Febriana, 2017; Lee et al., 2015; Samsudin et al.,
2020). PjBL is a constructivist approach (Jumaat et al., 2017) that promotes active learning
(Gomez-Pablos et al., 2017; Sormunen et al., 2019) with positive effects on social and emotional
learning (Culclasure et al., 2019; Febriana, 2017; Lee et al., 2015; Samsudin et al., 2020).
Studies have shown positive effects on students in areas of participation (Gomez-Pablos et al.,
2017; Sormunen et al., 2019), critical thinking, and other academic skills (Culclasure et al., 2019;
Duke et al., 2021; Eldiva & Azizah, 2019; Febriana, 2017; Revelle et al., 2020). PjBL is linked
to positive outcomes with cooperation (Shin, 2018; Sormunen et al., 2019), motivation (Dole et
al., 2017; Duke et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2015; Sari, 2018; Shin, 2018), and engagement (Dole et
al., 2017; Gomez-Pablos et al., 2017). Most research involves PjBL with non-disabled students
(Almulla, 2020; Dole et al., 2017; Gomez-Pablos et al., 2017; Sormunen et al., 2019), and
studies that did include students with disabilities focused on those with speech, reading, and
writing disabilities (Berkeley et al., 2019), learning disabilities (Lu et al., 2022; Sormunen et al.,
2019), and students who were blind, deaf, or intellectually disabled (Eldiva & Azizah, 2019).
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  19

         PjBL could be an educational practice teachers can implement that may have positive
effects academically, behaviorally, socially, and emotionally for students with EBD, based on the
research with students who were non-disabled (Almulla, 2020; Culclasure et al., 2019; Dole et
al., 2017; Duke et al., 2021; Febriana, 2017; Gomez-Pablos et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2015; Revelle
et al., 2020; Samsudin et al., 2020; Sari, 2018; Shin, 2018) in inclusive settings (Sormunen et al.,
2019), and self-contained classrooms (Taylor et al., 2024).

         The purpose of this study was to understand the experiences and perceptions of teachers
using PjBL with students with EBD using a collective case study design. The benefits of using
this approach were that it allowed me to understand and report the experiences of individual
teachers implementing PjBL with students with EBD. A collective case study design allowed me
to gain multiple perspectives (Creswell & Poth, 2018) around the implementation of PjBL with
this population. The population was selected through criterion sampling. Participants were
educators who taught using PjBL in classes with students with an IEP disability category of
emotional disturbance. This included a special educator in a self-contained setting, a special
educator who taught in both a self-contained setting and supported general education teachers,
and a general educator with inclusive classrooms. The grade levels targeted included students in
grades 6-12.

         This approach allowed me to gather relevant and timely data (Creswell & Poth, 2018) on
educators currently using PjBL. I collected multiple forms of data to understand their perceptions
through interviews and their implementation process through artifacts, and teacher-created
documents (Creswell & Poth, 2018). It was important to understand the specific characteristics
of the experiences and how they were implemented to analyze teachers' perceptions of how
PjBL impacted their students and their views on the effectiveness of employing it as an
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  20

instructional methodology. A collective case study focusing on the implementation of PjBL with
students with EBD offered the opportunity to understand teachers' insights regarding the
perceived effectiveness and feasibility of PjBL with students with EBD.
Research Question

         Considering the challenge teachers face in meeting the complex needs of students with
EBD, the research question was: What are the experiences and perceptions of teachers who use
project-based learning with students with emotional and behavioral disorders?
Theoretical Framework

         Teachers of students with disabilities have access to different research-based instructional
practices; however, it can be challenging to find the time to identify, study, and practice the
different research-based strategies (Brownell et al., 2021). In response to this challenge, the
Council for Exceptional Children (CEC) and the Collaboration for Effective Educator
Development, Accountability, and Reform (CEEDAR) Center synthesized 22 high-leverage
practices (HLPs) called the High Leverage Practices for Students with Disabilities (Aceves &
Kennedy, 2024). These practices can and should be used by any educator who works with
students with disabilities, including those teaching in general education classrooms (Aceves &
Kennedy, 2024).

         The HLPs are rooted in culturally inclusive pedagogies and practices (CIPP) (Taylor &
Monaco, 2024). Educators can more effectively address all students' academic, behavioral,
social, and emotional needs by being more culturally aware and inclusive. CIPP guides educators
to see students as "complex individuals" (Taylor & Monaco, 2024, p. 19) with "multilayered
identities, experiences, and cultures" (Taylor & Monaco, 2024, p. 19). These guiding ideas help
educators to understand and address the complex, individualized needs of students with EBD
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  21

(Smith et al., 2018; Sutherland et al., 2019) by understanding that the needs of these students are
also intertwined with their identities, cultures, and environments (Taylor & Monaco, 2024). HLP
and its theoretical underpinnings guided data collection and analysis by focusing on the teacher's
experiences, perceptions, and implementation of PjBL with students with EBD.
Significance of the Study

         This study sought to address the gap in research by investigating the use of PjBL with
students with EBD. There is minimal research on the use of PjBL and students with EBD. One
paper suggested using the arts with PjBL to support students with challenging behaviors, arguing
that art-based PjBL allows for more opportunities for student expression outside typical
academic contexts (Taylor et al., 2018). Other research found that PjBL positively affected
behaviors and teacher burnout, which is where the current research ends (Taylor et al., 2024).

         Researchers state the importance of investigating ways to engage students with EBD
(Didion et al., 2018; Taylor et al., 2024) and the effects of PjBL on students with disabilities
(Dole et al., 2017; Taylor et al., 2018; Taylor et al., 2024). It is important to understand whether
these positive effects carry over to students with EBD to address the challenge of effectively
educating them and reducing the likelihood of adverse outcomes (Bettini et al., 2016; Smith et
al., 2018; Sutherland et al., 2019). Students with EBD could benefit from this research as it may
lead to understanding how PjBL impacts their learning outcomes.

         Finding solutions to engagement and achievement for students with EBD is important for
both the students and the field of education (Bettini et al., 2016). The findings of this study could
influence general and special educators working with students with EBD. Identifying
instructional methods that can be used with high-leverage practices for the highest impact on
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  22

students with EBD would benefit teachers and the students in inclusive and alternative education
settings.

         Other stakeholders that may benefit from this research include educational leaders, such
as curriculum directors, special education directors, and building-level principals. If these
individuals are made aware of the possible benefits of PjBL for students with EBD and how
teachers implement PjBL, they can use this knowledge to ensure their teachers have the
appropriate support and knowledge to use it in their settings.
Definitions of Terms
The following terms are defined to help understand the content of this study.
Academic Needs--Areas in which students with EBD require support or intervention to attain
grade-level academic skills.
Asset-based approach--An approach to teaching that focuses on the strengths and talents a
student brings and how those strengths and talents can be utilized to support the learning of each
student (NYU Steinhardt, 2018).
Behavioral Needs--Areas in which students with EBD require support or intervention to attain
developmentally appropriate behaviors.
Culturally Inclusive Pedagogies and Practices (CIPP)--Teaching approaches that guide
educators to be aware of cultural differences, inclusive of the different backgrounds of their
students, and supporting these differences in their instructional practices and relationships with
students (Taylor & Monaco, 2024).
Culturally Responsive Pedagogy-- A teaching approach that embeds the awareness of students'
social and cultural backgrounds and how these factors impact their success (Ladson-Billings,
1995).
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  23

Culturally Relevant Pedagogy--A teaching approach that guides teachers to build awareness and
be supportive of the cultural identities of their students (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011).
Culturally Sustaining Pedagogy--A teaching approach that guides teachers to support students'
cultural and linguistic differences (Paris, 2012).
Emotional and Behavioral Disabilities (EBD)--"A variety of conditions and symptoms that
impair learning, social functioning, safety, and behavioral health of children or adolescents"
(Vanderbilt Kennedy Center, 2014, p. 1).
Emotional and Behavioral Disorders--"A variety of conditions and symptoms that impair the
learning, social functioning, safety, and behavioral health of children or adolescents" (Vanderbilt
Kennedy Center, 2014, p. 1).
Emotional Disturbance--

         (i) Emotional disturbance means a condition exhibiting one or more of the following
         characteristics over a long period of time and to a marked degree that adversely affects a
         child's educational performance:

                  (A) An inability to learn that cannot be explained by intellectual, sensory, or
                  health factors.
                  (B) An inability to build or maintain satisfactory interpersonal relationships with
                  peers and teachers.
                  (C) Inappropriate types of behavior or feelings under normal circumstances.
                  (D) A general pervasive mood of unhappiness or depression.
                  (E) A tendency to develop physical symptoms or fears associated with personal or
                  school problems.
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  24

         (ii) Emotional disturbance includes schizophrenia. The term does not apply to children
         who are socially maladjusted unless it is determined that they have an emotional
         disturbance. (Individuals with Disabilities Act, 2004, § 300.8)
Inclusive Classrooms--Classrooms in which students with disabilities are educated alongside
their non-disabled peers (Reading Rockets, 2024).
Individualized Education Program (IEP)--"A written plan for the provision of services for the
education of students who are disabled or gifted" (Commonwealth of Pennsylvania, 2024).
Intersectionality--A framework that focuses on the interconnectedness of multi-level identities
and how these identities shape individuals' experiences, including their privileges and
experiences with oppression (Garcia, 2024).
High Leverage Practices (HLP)--Practices identified as effective when used with students with
disabilities to improve their instruction and behavior (Brownell et al., 2021).
High Quality Project Based Learning (HQPBL)--A framework outlining six criteria that
identify what high quality project based learning experiences should include for students (The
Buck Institute of Education, 2018).
Multi-tiered system of supports (MTSS)--"A proactive and preventative approach that integrates
data and instruction to maximize student achievement and support students' social, emotional,
and behavioral needs from a strengths-based perspective" (American Institutes for Research,
2024a, para. 1).
Project-Based Learning (PjBL)--" a teaching method in which students gain knowledge and
skills by working for an extended period to investigate and respond to an authentic, engaging,
and complex question, problem, or challenge" (PBLWorks, 2024d, para. 3).

                                   Chapter II. Review of the Literature
         This collective case study examined the experiences and perspectives of teachers using
project-based learning (PjBL) with students with emotional and behavioral disorders (EBD). The
literature review begins by focusing on the background of students with EBD and current
strategies utilized to address their multifaceted needs. High-leverage practices were applied as a
framework as the review synthesizes the research on PjBL and its impact on students with EBD
and their teachers.
Students with EBD
         Emotional and behavioral disorders are defined as "a variety of conditions and symptoms
that impair the learning, social functioning, safety, and behavioral health of children or
adolescents" (Vanderbilt Kennedy Center, 2014, p. 1). Characteristics of individuals with EBD
can include hyperactivity, aggression, self-injurious behavior, withdrawal, immaturity, and
learning difficulties (Division for Emotional and Behavioral Health, 2024; Kauffman &
Landrum, 2018; Vanderbilt Kennedy Center, 2014). The most severe individuals "may exhibit
distorted thinking, excessive anxiety, bizarre motor acts, and abnormal mood swings" (Division
for Emotional and Behavioral Health, 2024, para. 8). For educational services, these students are
often identified as having an emotional disturbance (ED) (Individuals with Disabilities Act,
2004).
History of Emotional Disturbance Definition
         The term emotional disturbance was first formally used in the early 1900s for students
who were "incorrigible and truants" (Wills-Jackson, 2019, p. 82). The term seriously emotionally
disturbed was added in 1963 with the passing of Public Law 88.164 (Mental Retardation
Facilities and Community Health Centers Act, 1963) and again used in 1975 with the passage of
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  28

The Education for All Handicapped Children Act of 1975. This definition did not encompass the
socially maladjusted (Wills-Jackson, 2019). When this act was reauthorized in 1997 as IDEA,
the term was officially changed to emotional disturbance (ED) (Individuals with Disabilities
Education Act, 2004).

         In education today, students are diagnosed with emotional disturbance under the
Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, 2004).
Qualification for an educational diagnosis of emotional disturbance is determined by meeting
one or more criteria:

         (A) An inability to learn that cannot be explained by intellectual, sensory, or health
             factors.

         (B) An inability to build or maintain satisfactory interpersonal relationships with peers
             and teachers.

         (C) Inappropriate types of behaviors or feelings under normal circumstances.
         (D) A general pervasive mood of unhappiness or depression
         (E) A tendency to develop physical symptoms or fears associated with personal or school

             problems. (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, 2004)
Challenges with the Definition

         The federal definition of ED continues to create challenges when considering how to best
serve this population of students (Freeman et al., 2019). It excludes students with social
maladjustments and is not objective in identifying the amount of time a student must demonstrate
the criteria to be identified and what constitutes inappropriate behavior (Freeman et al., 2019;
Kavale et al., 2004). There are similar challenges for the definition of learning disability, which
also focuses on an inability to learn that "cannot be explained by intellectual, sensory, or health
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  29

factors" (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, 2004). Often, students with learning
disabilities also exhibit behavioral challenges (Kavale et al., 2004). As students with EBD often
are academically below grade level, the question becomes whether these deficits are related to a
learning disability or if their behaviors prevent them from learning at a typical pace. The
complex needs of students with EBD also present a challenge in diagnosis and treatment, as
comorbidities are often not recognized (Kavale et al., 2004).

         The criteria for identifying a student with ED have remained unchanged since it was
codified into law, unlike learning disabilities, which were updated to reflect current practice and
research (Hanchon & Allen, 2017). The IDEA definition outlines that all the criteria must be
met, but fails to outline the measurement of these criteria (Mitchell et al., 2019). This definition
has posed a problem for school psychologists attempting to identify students appropriately when
evaluating them for special education services, relying on the subjectivity of the evaluator. The
subjective nature of evaluation contributes disproportionality in identifying students with
externalized behaviors and gender and race inequities (Hanchon & Allen, 2017).

         The Council for Children with Behavioral Disorders advocated changing the label of
serious emotional disturbance, as it attached a stigma to individuals with these disabilities and
adopted "the terminology Emotional and Behavior Disorder" (Wills-Jackson, 2019, p. 83).
Fourteen states have or will officially change from emotional disturbance to emotional disability
to help mitigate this stigma (Arundel, 2024; Monserrate, 2024; Salhotra, 2021). Following their
work, the National Mental Health and Special Education Coalition further outlined a more
precise definition for EBD (Mitchell et al., 2019). They defined students with EBD as:

         (i) The term emotional or behavioral disorder means a disability characterized by
         behavioral or emotional responses in school programs so different from appropriate age,

         cultural, or ethnic norm that the responses adversely affect educational performance,
         including academic, social, vocational, and personal skills. Such a disability

                  (A) is more than a temporary, expected response to stressful events in the
                  environment.
                  (B) is consistently exhibited in two different settings, at least one of which is
                  school-related; and
                  (C) is unresponsive to direct intervention in general education, or the child's
                  condition is such that general education interventions would be insufficient.

                           (ii) Emotional and behavioral disorders can co-exist with other disabilities.
                           (iii) This category may include children or youth with schizophrenic

This definition addressed some issues around concurrent disabilities and differences in culture
and ethnicities (Kavale et al., 2004). It clarified who would be eligible, provided a multi-step
evaluation process, and used multiple forms of data across settings (Mitchell et al., 2019). The
definition also expands upon the educational effects "to include academic, social, vocational, and
personal skills" (Mitchell et al., 2019, p. 79). It has yet to be included in IDEA.
         In Pennsylvania, the disability category does not automatically determine the types of
supports they can receive (22 PA Code Chapter 14 § 14.131). "Students may receive more than
one type of support as appropriate" (22 PA Code Chapter 14 § 14.131, para 1). Students with

Challenges and Adverse Outcomes

         "Students with EBD display a wide range of needs, but common characteristics include
poor social skills acquisition, disruptive behavior, and academic performance deficits" (Kumm et
al., 2021, p. 97). Students display behaviors such as inattentiveness, disruptive behaviors, and
rule-breaking. These behaviors can cause students with EBD to enter a failure cycle (Sanders et
al., 2018). This cycle begins with students displaying inappropriate behaviors, which prompts
them to be removed from the classroom. Their removal leads to lost instructional time,
negatively impacting their academic achievement. Lost instructional time and decreased
academic skills continue to impact their behavior negatively, and the cycle continues. Learning
loss negatively impacts present and future achievement (Frantz, 2023). These complex
challenges negatively impact their academic and behavioral outcomes (Ennis et al., 2018).

         Students with EBD often have deficits in all academic areas (Kumm et al., 2021; Ennis et
al., 2018) and perform one or two grade levels below their peers (Freeman et al., 2019). They
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  32

struggle with reading (Mathews et al., 2021) and written expression (Evmenova & Regan, 2019).
Students demonstrate decreased achievement in literacy and numeracy and are at risk of
academic failure (Vanderbilt Kennedy Center, 2014). They have poor social skills and engage in
disruptive behaviors that prompt them to be removed from the classroom environment (Kumm et
al., 2021; Hutchins et al., 2020). These challenges lead to a higher dropout risk (Freeman et al.,
2019; Vanderbilt Kennedy Center, 2014).

         Students with EBD display social skills deficits in relationships, self-management,
academic skills, compliance, and assertion (Hutchins et al., 2020). Social skill deficits lead to
adverse outcomes in relationships. Students with EBD report higher rates of bullying and are less
happy at school compared with other students with different disabilities (Lipscomb et al., 2017).

         Students with EBD are one of "the most socioeconomically disadvantaged groups and the
most likely to attend lower-performing schools" (Lipscomb et al., 2017, p. i). A longitudinal
study found that 62% of students with EBD lived in low-income households, and 33% attended a
low-performing school (Lipscomb et al., 2017). Students with EBD are less likely to have
parental assistance with homework (54%) compared to the overall population of students with
IEPs (72%). Additionally, they show lower participation in school events, with only 46%
engaged versus 58% of all students with IEPs.

         These challenges can lead to negative consequences beyond the educational system
(Chen et al., 2021; National Center for Education Statistics, 2024; Offerman et al., 2022; Office
of Special Education Programs, 2020; U.S. Department of Education, 2023; Vanderbilt Kennedy
Center, 2014). Students with EBD face higher rates of adverse outcomes (Chen et al., 2021) and
begin their education with traumatic experiences that negatively impact their future (Offerman et
al., 2022). Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) are traumatic events that a child experiences
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD                                                         33

between the ages of 0-17 (CDC, 2024). While approximately 64% of adults report having

experienced at least one ACE as a child, a study of students with EBD in a special education

school found that 96.4% had experienced at least one adverse childhood experience (ACE), and

more than half experienced four or more (Offerman et al., 2022). Children who experience ACEs

are more likely to develop emotional and behavioral problems, along with other negative

outcomes. These include lower academic achievement, higher rates of dropout and arrest, and

negative physical effects. Students who experience ACEs are also at higher risk of heart disease,

depression, and suicide (CDC, 2024).

Among all students with disabilities, those identified with EBD had the highest dropout

rate in 2021, with 28% leaving school before graduation (National Center for Education

Statistics, 2024; Office of Special Education Programs, 2020; U.S. Department of Education,

affect not only the students but also the impact on educators working with this population.
Impact on Educators

         Special education teachers (SETs) who work with students with EBD are at a high risk of
burnout (Brunsting et al., 2022; Brunsting et al., 2023). These teachers report higher levels of
emotional exhaustion than other teachers (Brunsting et al., 2022). Their risk for burnout and
exhaustion only amplifies the risk of lower use of evidence-based practices with fidelity.
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  35

Students of teachers experiencing burnout experience negative impacts on their motivation (Shen
et al., 2015), ability to meet goals (Wong et al., 2017), and decreased academic achievement
(Madigan & Curran, 2021). Student motivation is influenced by the level of burnout teachers
experience in general education settings (Shen et al., 2015). There are statistically significant
relationships between levels of teacher stress and teaching quality (p <.001) and student
engagement (p=.006) (Wong et al., 2017). Teachers' sense of accomplishment significantly
predicted IEP outcomes (p=.005) (Wong et al., 2017). Teachers with less support (including lack
of a partner teacher, lack of paraprofessionals, and inadequate time) provide weaker instruction
to their students (Mathews et al., 2021). Teacher burnout negatively impacts student exam
scores, grades, and GPA (Madigan & Curran, 2021).

         Despite these negative impacts, SETs of students with EBD report higher levels of
personal achievement and lower levels of depersonalization than other primary or secondary
teachers (Brunsting et al., 2022). Special education teachers of students with EBD are more
likely to have reduced burnout if they feel supported by their administrators, access to curricular
resources, and perceive their workload to be manageable with enough planning time (Bettini et
al., 2020; Brunsting et al., 2023; Cumming et al., 2021). In these conditions, teachers report
higher use of evidence-based practices with their students (Cumming et al., 2021). It is important
for SETs to have a manageable workload and support not only to prevent burnout and lower
stress but also to improve their effectiveness in teaching students with EBD (Bettini et al., 2020;
Brunsting et al., 2023; Cumming et al., 2021).
Approaches to Support Students with EBD

         In 1991, the Peacock Hill Working Group (1991) published their findings on effective
practices for teaching students with EBD. During that time, less than half of the students with
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  36

EBD were in inclusive settings, with a dropout rate of 40%. Teacher shortages for this
population were prevalent, with high attrition rates. They recommended successful strategies and
programs, including systematic data-based interventions, continuous assessment and monitoring
of progress, provision for the practice of new skills, treatment matched to the problem, multi-
component treatment, programming for transfer and maintenance, and commitment to sustained
intervention. Programs they identified that were successful included preventative programs in
preschool that included parent education and support, elementary programs that included aspects
of peer modeling, explicit social skills curriculum, classroom contingency programs, and token
economy systems. Secondary programs included token economy systems and social skills
instruction. Following their research, they recommended preventative policies and practices and
increasing funding for programs and policies focused on cross-agency collaboration. While these
recommendations gained some traction, such as Part D of IDEA funding research for effective
funding for programs (Freeman et al., 2019), the present risks for students with EBD remain high
(Chen et al., 2021; National Center for Education Statistics, 2024; Office of Special Education
Programs, 2020).

         Effective strategies that have been empirically tested include:
         (a) teacher praise (reinforcement); (b) high rates of opportunities to respond; (c) precision
         requests and behavioral momentum; (d) clear instructional methods, including direct
         instruction, class-wide peer tutoring, and continuous monitoring of student performance
         (e.g., curriculum-based measurement); (e) positive behavior support, including functional
         assessment-based planning, self-management, and modifying antecedent and
         consequence events; and (f) group-oriented contingencies. (Mitchell et al., 2019, p. 78)
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  37

         Tiered systems of support allow educators to implement evidence-based problem-solving
and tools to positively impact all students (Mitchell et al., 2019). "A multi-tiered system of
supports (MTSS) is a proactive and preventative approach that integrates data and instruction to
maximize student achievement and support students' social, emotional, and behavioral needs
from a strengths-based perspective" (American Institutes for Research, 2024a, para. 1). There are
three tiers in MTSS (American Institutes for Research, 2024b). Tier 1 strategies are those that
can be helpful to all students. Tier 2 strategies focus on small group interventions for students
who are identified as being at-risk. Tier 3 strategies are for individuals who do not respond to
Tier 1 or Tier 2 strategies or require more intensive, individualized intervention. Teachers focus
on Tier 3 strategies for individuals with significant EBD.

         A meta-analysis of behavioral interventions in 2022 outlined eight evidence-based
practices for students with EBD: Check-in check-out (CICO), functional assessment-based
interventions, group contingencies, peer-mediated interventions, self-management, self-regulated
strategy development, token economies, and video modeling (Riden et al., 2022). In addition,
students with EBD require social skills interventions (Kumm et al., 2021), which allow these
students to develop positive relationships with others and learn more effectively. Other strategies
showed promise, such as "praise, OTR [opportunities to respond], behavior contracting,
cooperative learning, goal setting, good behavior game, high probability requests, instructional
choice, self-determination, social skills, and time out" (Riden et al., 2022, p.262).
Inclusive Settings

         The evidence of effective practice for students with EBD in inclusive settings remains
limited (Soares et al., 2024). Current research lacks empirically supported recommendations for
this population. A meta-analysis in 2024 found three strategies with the potential for positive
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  38

outcomes for students with EBD who spend their time in inclusive settings: a self-determined
learning model of instruction (Kelly & Shogren, 2014), behavior-specific praise (BSP) (Allday et
al., 2012), and the use of sound amplification (Maag & Anderson, 2006).

         A self-determined model of instruction provides students with skills in making choices,
self-advocating, self-determination, and self-regulation (Kelly & Shogren, 2014). This model
allows the teacher to implement it in any curricular area to apply the skills to a student-set goal.
When implemented, this intervention improved and maintained on-task behaviors for high school
students with EBD.

         Behavior-specific praise (BSP) directs teachers to write or verbally give students specific
positive feedback, and the impact of the behavior of students on controllable behaviors, such as
"Good job studying for this science test, your effort paid off" (Royer et al., 2019, p. 113).
Students with EBD in inclusive settings have experienced positive outcomes when exposed to
BSP (Allday et al., 2012), consistent with findings in other settings ( et al., 2019).

         Sound amplification addressed attention problems with students with EBD (Maag &
Anderson, 2006). The amplified sound increased the rate at which students responded to low-
interest directions. This intervention did not impact the rate at which students responded to high-
interest directions.
Self-Contained Settings

         A self-contained classroom is a special education placement where special education
teachers instruct a class that includes only students with disabilities (Spencer, 2013). A meta-
analysis in 2023 found six themes consistent with research practices for students with EBD in
elementary self-contained settings (Gersib & Mason, 2023). Themes included "(1) a highly
structured classroom environment, (2) use of positive strategies, (3) explicit social-emotional
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  39

(SEL) instruction, (4) applied behavior strategies, (5) classroom engagement, and (6)
maintaining a relational focus" (Gersib & Mason, 2023, p. 273). A highly structured environment
with clear rules and expectations reduced negative behaviors. Positive strategies rather than
punitive strategies had a greater impact on student behavior. Social and emotional skills must be
explicitly taught and reinforced. Skills include, but are not limited to, problem-solving,
emotional regulation, social skills, behavior rehearsal, and self-management. Applied behavior
strategies such as modeling, instructive feedback, and error correction improve social outcomes.
Classroom engagement strategies included opportunities to respond, active supervision,
academic engagement, instructional feedback, and scaffolding. The relational component showed
that the stronger the teacher-student relationship, the lower the number of aggressive behaviors.
Of these six themes, classroom structure and positive strategies had a positive, though
nonsignificant impact, and "SEL, applied behavior techniques, and engagement resulted in
negative estimates" (Gersib & Mason, 2023, p. 278), with recommendations for further research
in self-contained settings.
Other Strategies

         Schools must ensure their programs are aligned with academic standards with appropriate
"modifications, adaptations, and accommodations" (PaTTAN, 2023, p.1) and provide social and
emotional support and behavior management based on student needs. Due to the complex needs
of students with EBD, researchers recommend that teachers use a toolbox method, utilizing
common practice elements found across many evidence-based practices (Chen et al., 2021;
Sutherland et al., 2019). Common practice elements include group contingencies, manipulating
the instructional antecedent, problem-solving, social skills instruction, choices, and scaffolding
(Chen et al., 2021). Other common elements include teacher feedback, routines, choice,
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  40

modeling, self-management, emotional regulation instruction, problem-solving, and building
relationships (Sutherland et al., 2019). Educators should include frequent opportunities to
respond to maximize engagement. Incorporating peer-mediated interventions had a positive
impact on social skills and academic behavior (Wills-Jackson, 2019). This approach allows for
flexibility, where educators can rely on their adaptive expertise to use specific elements based on
the needs of the students in real time (Chen et al., 2021) and align with the need for
individualized interventions (Vanderbilt Kennedy Center, 2014).
Challenges Implementing Strategies

         While evidence-based practices (EBPs) for students with EBD exist, implementing them
with fidelity has typically not been as successful in the classroom (Criss et al., 2024; Scheibel,
2023; Sutherland et al., 2019). Implementing EBPs requires knowledge, time, and resources to
learn and effectively implement (Scheibel, 2023). While teachers may know an effective EBP,
they may not have the time, resources, or support to implement it with fidelity (Scheibel, 2023).
Teachers self-reported having at least some knowledge of 68% of effective behavioral support
practices, at least somewhat knowledgeable of differentiation strategies and classroom
management, and aware of approximately 66% of instructional practices effective for students
with EBD in inclusive settings (McKenna et al., 2022). Statistically significant differences
existed between special educators and general educators. Special educators had more knowledge
of behavior practices, differentiation, and classroom management; however, there were no
statistically significant differences in knowledge of instructional practices.

         Knowledge of practices is important, but educators must implement these practices
(McKenna et al., 2022; Scheibel, 2023). Educators reported using 45.45% of behavioral
practices, 60% of differentiation strategies, 100% of classroom management practices, and
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  41

33.33% of institutional practices at least once a week (McKenna et al., 2022). Statistically
significant differences were found between special and general educators, with special educators
reporting a higher percentage of using behavioral practices, differentiation, and instructional
practices. Overall, all participants reported using less than half of the practices at least once a
week, which remains a barrier to inclusive education for students with EBDs.

         While EBPs exist, there may not be an EBP that addresses the needs of every individual
student (Scheibel, 2023). The existing EBPs are also based on research in specific contexts,
which may not apply to every student (Rowe, 2020). While EBPs exist for populations with
learning disabilities or autism, the amount for students with complex needs is more limited.

         Often, educators will identify non-EBP practices when they cannot identify or feasibly
implement an existing EBP (Scheibel, 2023). To mitigate the risk of using methods unsupported
by sufficient evidence, educators can use high-leverage practices (HLPs) to adapt non-EBPs to
increase effectiveness, with recurrent progress monitoring to identify the potential impact on
students.
High Leverage Practices for Students with Disabilities

         Teachers of students with disabilities have access to different research-based instructional
practices; however, it can be challenging to find the time to identify, study, and practice the
different research-based strategies (Brownell et al., 2021). In response to this challenge, the
Council for Exceptional Children (CEC) and the Collaboration for Effective Educator
Development, Accountability, and Reform (CEEDAR) Center identified high-leverage practices
(HLPs) for special educators to use with students with disabilities to help improve instruction.
Their original publication focused on HLPs in special education (Aceves & Kennedy, 2024).
Although HLPs were initially developed for special educators, they were not intended to be
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD                                                             42

exclusive to them (Council for Exceptional Children, 2024). These practices can and should be
used by any educator who works with students with disabilities, including those teaching in
general education classrooms. While the practices help students with disabilities, the HLPs are
also effective with non-disabled students (Nelson et al., 2022). The newly revised practices are
labeled as High Leverage Practices for Students with Disabilities to reflect that HLPs are
accessible to all educators who work with students with disabilities (Aceves & Kennedy,
2024). Figure 1 outlines the four domains and twenty-two practices of the HLPs.

The Twenty-Two HLPs
         I used the HLPs and the theoretical foundations as the theoretical framework for this

study. Twenty-two HLPs help improve instruction and student behavior and are "foundational to
effective instruction" (Brownell et al., 2021, p. 13). When successfully applied in classrooms, the
practices have improved student outcomes. These revised practices fall under four domains:
collaboration, data-driven planning, instruction in behavior and academics, and intensify and
intervene as needed (Aceves & Kennedy, 2024). Within each domain, six pillars identify the
most valuable practices within these domains, along with embedded practices within the pillars.

         Collaboration. The first domain highlights the importance of stakeholder collaboration
to identify student strengths and needs and effectively create and monitor individualized
education programs (IEPs) to maximize student success (Billingsley et al., 2024). The domain of
collaboration includes two pillars and one embedded practice.

         Domain One Pillars. "HLP 1: Collaborate with professionals to increase student success"
(Billingsley et al., 2024, p. 27). Special educators must collaborate through sharing, problem-
solving, and planning with all professionals who work with students with disabilities. These can
include general educators, support staff, and paraprofessionals. Collaboration allows the team to
meet the student's needs, and supports and services help students progress towards their goals
(Billingsley et al., 2024).

Culturally Inclusive Pedagogies and Practices in HLPs

         The updated HLPs are rooted in culturally inclusive pedagogies and practices (CIPP)
(Taylor & Monaco, 2024). Educators can more effectively address all students' academic,
behavioral, social, and emotional needs by being more culturally aware and inclusive. CIPP
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  48

guides educators to see students as "complex individuals" (Taylor & Monaco, 2024, p. 19) with
"multilayered identities, experiences, and cultures" (Taylor & Monaco, 2024, p. 19). These
guiding ideas help educators to understand and address the complex, individualized needs of
students with EBD (Smith et al., 2018; Sutherland et al., 2019) by understanding that the needs
of these students are also intertwined with their identities, cultures, and environments (Taylor &
Monaco, 2024). These practices are built upon culturally relevant pedagogy, culturally
responsive teaching (CRT), and intersectionality.

         Culturally Responsive Pedagogy. Gloria Ladson-Billings originated culturally
responsive pedagogy to address the need to embed students' social and cultural backgrounds into
pedagogy and how it impacts their success (Ladson-Billings, 1995). CRP emphasizes the
importance of remaining nonjudgmental of students' cultural backgrounds and intentionally
being inclusive of their cultures (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011). It was further developed by
Brown-Jeffy and Cooper (2011) into culturally relevant pedagogy.

         Culturally Relevant Pedagogy. The five themes identified in culturally relevant
pedagogy are: "identity and achievement, equity and excellence, developmental appropriateness,
teaching the whole child, and student-teacher relationships" (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011, p.
71). Figure 2 outlines the five themes in culturally relevant pedagogy.

         Identity and Achievement. Identity and achievement focus on "identity development,

         Equity and Excellence. The theme of equity and excellence encompasses the concepts of
"dispositions, incorporation of multicultural curriculum content, equal access, and high

give the students what they need, highlighting the importance of differences and differentiation

while holding high expectations for all (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011). It also includes the
importance of ensuring students see themselves in the curriculum in a positive light.

         Developmental Appropriateness. This theme focuses on "learning styles, teaching styles,
and cultural variation in psychological needs" (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011, p. 75). Teachers
must be aware of their students' developmental needs and stages, provide instruction, and adapt
their teaching styles to meet these developmental needs through a cultural lens (Brown-Jeffy &
Cooper, 2011). This theme includes understanding that people and cultures have varying
psychological needs and motivations.

         Teaching the Whole Child. The theme of teaching the whole child outlines the following
concepts: "skill development in a cultural context, home-school-community collaboration,
learning outcomes, supportive learning community, and empowerment" (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper,
2011, p. 76). Teachers need to look beyond the surface-level needs of students and "remember
the needs of the total child" (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011, p. 76). Teachers need to consider the
cultural backgrounds, beliefs, and priorities of families and how these intersect to empower
students to be successful (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011).

         Student-Teacher Relationships. The final theme of culturally relevant pedagogy focuses
on "caring, relationships, interaction, and classroom atmosphere" (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011,
p. 77). Teachers are a significant part of their students' lives and must be aware of how they
cultivate caring relationships with all their students (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011). They must
find ways to connect with their students and create opportunities for them to connect with one
another by creating a safe classroom environment that fosters community development.

         Culturally Sustaining Pedagogy. Culturally sustaining pedagogy was developed to
further the importance of valuing multiethnic and multilingual environments (Paris, 2012).
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  51

Pedagogy needs to be responsive, relevant, and supportive of students' cultural and linguistic
differences.

         Intersectionality. Intersectionality focuses on the intersection of multi-level identities
and how these identities shape individuals' experiences, including their privileges and
experiences with oppression (Garcia, 2024). Intersectionality challenges educators "to consider
the layers of oppression that affect students across these diversities" (Taylor & Monaco, 2024, p.
21) as they develop their instructional practices and integrate culturally inclusive pedagogies and
practices (Taylor & Monaco, 2024).

         Culturally Inclusive Pedagogies and Practices and High Leverage Practices. CIPP
within the HLPs highlights culturally responsive pedagogy and teaching, culturally relevant
pedagogy and teaching, and culturally sustaining pedagogy and teaching. Culturally responsive
teaching directs teachers to take a cultural lens with curriculum and teaching strategies to make
them more meaningful to their students (Gay, 2018). Culturally relevant teaching guides teachers
to be aware of the cultural identities of their students, supporting those identities alongside
academic support (Ladson-Billings, 1995). Culturally sustaining teaching emphasizes the
importance of supporting cultures in the classroom through recognizing and sharing cultural
aspects throughout the educational process (Taylor & Monaco, 2024). CIPP and intersectionality
guide perspectives to be considered throughout all the HLPs to ensure educators understand and
address students with disabilities and their families in an inclusive, culturally appropriate
manner. Teachers can use these guiding frameworks alongside HLPs to reflect on their cultures
and identities, recognizing how they may differ from their students. This reflection can help
educators connect with their students and communities. It is essential for teachers to understand
their students' diverse identities and backgrounds and ensure they are teaching from an asset-
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  52

based perspective that includes all students' identities and cultures (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper,
2011; Paris, 2012; Taylor & Monaco, 2024). HLPs with a culturally inclusive lens provide a
solid foundation for effective teaching when applied to instructional approaches. (Taylor &
Monaco, 2024). The HLPs and foundational frameworks in CIPP were the theoretical
frameworks that guided this study.
Project-Based Learning

         Project-based learning is part of a larger category of teaching methodology called
inquiry-based learning (Savery, 2006). Inquiry-based learning includes project-based learning,
problem-based learning, and design-based learning. Inquiry-based learning focuses on answering
questions through student research and problem-solving. Problem-based learning focuses on
having students set goals for solving a problem and is more autonomous. In contrast, project-
based learning allows teachers to scaffold, model, and guide students. The terminology can be
confusing because project-based learning and problem-based learning share the same acronym:
PBL. To avoid confusion, I will use PjBL for project-based learning, following the convention
established by other researchers (Culclasure et al., 2019; Mulcahy & Wertz, 2021).

         Project-based learning (PjBL) is a cooperative teaching method that involves learning by
doing (Fischer, 2023). More than a culminating project, the students' process is a key aspect of
PjBL. PjBL traces its roots back to John Dewey, who believed children should be active learners
in collaborative, experiential learning environments where the teacher was a facilitator rather
than a lecturer (Virani-Murji, 2020). Project-based learning (PjBL) is currently defined as "a
teaching method in which students gain knowledge and skills by working for an extended period
to investigate and respond to an authentic, engaging, and complex question, problem, or
challenge" (PBLWorks, 2024b, para. 1).

High Quality Project-Based Learning
         PBLWorks is an organization under the Buck Institute for Education that focuses on

research, development, and support for teachers and schools using project-based learning
(PBLWorks, 2024a). They developed two gold-standard guides for PjBL: the "Seven Essential
Project Design Elements" (PBLWorks, 2024e) and "Seven Project Based Teaching Practices"
(PBLWorks, 2024e). The design elements focus on developing quality PjBL units, and the
practices focus on assessing and improving existing PjBL. Both the design elements and teaching
practices focus on the goals of "key knowledge, understanding, and success skills" (PBLWorks,
2024e). In addition to these guides, the Buck Institute for Education developed, in partnership
with teachers and researchers, the High Quality Project-Based Learning Framework (HQPBL),
which outlines six criteria that must be present for a PjBL experience to be considered high
quality (The Buck Institute for Education, 2018).

         Seven Essential Project Design Elements. The essential project design elements model
assists professionals in developing quality PjBL units for their classes (PBLWorks, 2024e). The
seven elements are a challenging problem or question, sustained inquiry, authenticity, student
choice and voice, reflection, critique and revision, and public product (PBLWorks, 2024b). A
PjBL unit must begin with a problem or question that provides an "appropriate level of
challenge" (PBLWorks, 2024b, para. 3). In efforts to solve this problem or question, students
must be engaged in sustained inquiry where they are "posing questions, finding resources, and
applying information" (PBLWorks, 2024b, para. 4). It is important that the unit focuses on a real-
world situation or context that is relevant to the students and that they can make decisions
throughout the project. Both teachers and students engage in reflective practices while critiquing
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  54

each other's work and revising along the way. The culmination of a unit results in a public
product that the students share with people outside their class.

         Seven Project-Based Teaching Practices. Project-based teaching practices provide a
framework for educators to use when assessing and improving their practice (PBLWorks,
2024e). The seven practices are design and plan, align to standards, build the culture, manage
activities, scaffold student learning, assess student learning, and engage and coach (PBLWorks,
2024c). Teachers begin planning their unit by identifying their context and how it relates to their
students while ensuring the academic standards are addressed. Building culture is a vital element
of PjBL. Teachers need to create a culture that promotes "student independence and growth,
open-ended inquiry, team spirit, and attention to quality" (PBLWorks, 2024c, para. 5).
Throughout the project, teachers must ensure they manage activities as a guide and coach while
scaffolding for learners with diverse needs. Formative and summative assessment opportunities
are used for individuals, peer assessments, and the group.

         High Quality Project-Based Learning Framework. Educators and researchers
developed the HQPBL framework to determine what students would experience when engaging
in high-quality PjBL (The Buck Institute for Education, 2018). This framework can be used by
educators when designing and teaching PjBL experiences. The framework outlines six criteria to
use as a starting point for HQPBL: intellectual challenge and accomplishment, authenticity,
public product, collaboration, project management, and reflection (The Buck Institute for
Education, 2018). Students should be engaging in projects requiring them to think critically
about complex issues and take time to complete them. These projects must be relevant and
meaningful to their lives, with student voice and choice embedded in the work. Projects are
meant to be publicly shared so students receive ongoing feedback from their peers, teachers, and
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  55

others outside the classroom. While some aspects of the learning experience can be completed
individually, collaboration on project work must also be included in HQPBL to give students
real-world experiences working on a team. Students must engage in project management within
their team, whether managing their time, tasks, resources, or design thinking. HQPBL
experiences prompt students to reflect on their work through self-assessment and consider ways
to improve. These six criteria are "an essential baseline, but they are not all-encompassing" (The
Buck Institute for Education, 2018, p. 2). Classroom culture, student voice, and "the fundamental
belief that all students can learn" (The Buck Institute for Education, 2018, p. 2) are valuable in
implementing PjBL (The Buck Institute for Education, 2018).
PjBL and Students Without Disabilities

         The effects of PjBL on students without disabilities are well documented, with positive
outcomes related to academic outcomes (Aydin & Mutlu, 2023; Culclasure et al., 2019; Duke et
al., 2021; Eldiva & Azizah, 2019; Febriana, 2017; Gomez-Pablos et al., 2017; Krajcik et al.,
2023; Revelle et al., 2020; Saavedra et al., 2021; Schneider et al., 2022; Sormunen et al., 2019;
Suryanti et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023), behavior, and social-emotional
learning (Culclasure et al., 2019; Febriana, 2017; Krajcik et al., 2023; Juniar et al., 2023; Lee et
al., 2015; Samsudin et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022). Students reported positive feelings towards
their PjBL experiences, including helping them learn, excitement about their learning, and being
able to work with others (Culclasure et al., 2019; Mariadas & Yusop, 2024). Teachers also
reported positive opinions, with improvement in student achievement, enthusiasm, and
motivation compared to other teaching approaches (Culclasure et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023).

         Several studies show statistically significant differences in favor of PjBL (Aydin &Mutlu,
2023; Duke et al., 2021; Suryanti et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023). Elementary students had
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  56

statistically significant growth (p=.05) in scientific literacy skills (Suryanti et al., 2024), social
studies knowledge (p<.001) (Duke et al., 2021), academic scores (p=.005) (Aydin & Mutlo,
2023), and reading skills (p<.05) (Zhang et al., 2023). There were mixed results on motivation,
with one study finding no impact (Duke et al., 2021) and another stating that it "intrinsically
motivates students who learn history" (Marias & Yusop, 2024, p. 72). One study earned a Tier
Two moderate rating on What Works Clearinghouse (What Works Clearinghouse, 2024). This
rating was developed as evidence tiers through the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) for
schools to identify what products, policies, programs, or practices work (What Works
Clearinghouse, n.d.). A Tier Two rating on What Works Clearinghouse means the targeted item
has moderate evidence of effectiveness. This rating is attained by meeting the standards with or
without reservations, having statistically significant positive results, at least 350 students, and at
least two educational sites in the study. Researchers conducted a randomized control trial with
4,000 students in high school physics and chemistry. They found that students in the
experimental group performed an average of 0.2 standard deviations above the control group on
a summative science assessment, with p < 0.05 (Schneider et al., 2022).
PjBL and Students with Disabilities

         While the benefits of PjBL have been documented for students without disabilities,
research on PjBL with students with disabilities is limited (Boardman & Hovland, 2024; Wertz
& Mulcahy, 2024). Studies on students with disabilities focused on those with speech, reading,
and writing disabilities (Berkeley et al., 2019), learning disabilities (Lu et al., 2022; Sormunen et
al., 2019), students who were blind, deaf, or intellectually disabled (Eldiva & Azizah, 2019), or
in mixed settings (Purnomo et al., 2022; Hovey & Ferguson, 2014). Mixed results included
constraints regarding self-efficacy (Berkeley et al., 2019), positive impact on creativity and
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  57

learning outcomes (Lu et al., 2022), improved critical thinking skills (Eldiva & Azizah, 2019),
and the need for more intensive support (Sormunen et al., 2019).

         Students with disabilities report that PjBL positively impacted their collaboration skills,
ability to have choices in their learning, and the authenticity of the experience (Boardman &
Hovland, 2024). They also reported needing more time throughout the process, navigating the
stresses of group work, such as when a team member does not contribute, and needing more
support from their teachers.

         Research that included teacher perspectives established that educators view PjBL as an
effective approach for students with disabilities (Hovey & Ferguson, 2014; Purnomo et al.,
2022). Teachers believed that PjBL improved student enthusiasm, increased communication and
social skills, and gave them more control over their behaviors (Purnomo et al., 2022). The
current findings demonstrate the possibility that PjBL may be an effective method when adapted
for students with disabilities (Boardman & Hovland, 2024; Eldiva & Azizah, 2019; Hovey &
Ferguson, 2014; Lu et al., 2022; Purnomo et al., 2022; Sormunen et al., 2019).
PjBL and EBD Research

         Initial findings on PjBL with students with EBD demonstrate the possibility of a positive
impact on this student population (Foster & Shriner, 2023; Taylor et al., 2018; Taylor et al.,
2024). One study suggests using PjBL in an art context (Taylor et al., 2018), while another found
positive impacts on the students and teachers (Taylor et al., 2024). Art-based PjBL can allow for
more opportunities for student expression outside typical academic contexts, allowing for a more
strengths-based approach that also "helps develop positive relationships that students with
challenging behaviors need" (Taylor et al., 2018, p. 64). PjBL improved student behavior related
to prosocial behavior and task completion (Taylor et al., 2024). It also improved job satisfaction
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  58

scores for teachers and paraprofessionals using PjBL with students with EBD. Teachers in one
residential special education school for students with significant emotional and behavioral
challenges noted that negative behaviors decreased when students were engaged in PjBL lessons,
with an increase in engagement and academics (Foster & Shriner, 2023). While limited, the
initial research is promising.
HLP and PjBL

         While PjBL can improve engagement and provide an authentic learning experience for
students, educators must address the barriers to students with disabilities (IRIS Center, 2024).
Barriers can include executive functioning needs and the ability to generalize information and
skills. Though explicit instruction is a HLP student with disabilities benefit from, this does not
preclude them from learning successfully in a PjBL setting (Boardman & Hovland, 2024).
Students with disabilities need support to be successful in an inquiry-based environment (Rizzo
& Taylor, 2016).

         PjBL naturally incorporates many HLPs that are effective for students with disabilities

         continuous feedback in PjBL.

    · Creating an environment with expected norms and routines with student collaboration
         aligns with HLP 7 by constructing a "consistent, organized, and respectful learning
         environment" (Wertz & Mulcahy, 2024, p. 4).

    · PjBL incorporates collaborative learning opportunities, which align with HLP 9.

         Though PBLWorks' vision is to have project-based learning for all (PBLWorks, 2024d),
the research on PjBL with students with disabilities is limited (Boardman & Hovland, 2024;
Wertz & Mulcahy, 2024). This scarcity of research is even more pronounced for students with
EBD (Foster & Shriner, 2023; Taylor et al., 2024). Given the positive impact it has had on

students with disabilities (Boardman & Hovland, 2024; Eldiva & Azizah, 2019; Foster &
Shriner, 2023; Hovey & Ferguson, 2014; Lu et al., 2022; Purnomo et al., 2022; Taylor et al.,

who are frequently absent or not in the classroom (Mulcahy & Wertz, 2021). Examining
teachers' implementation of PjBL with students with EBD and their perspectives on its impact
can offer valuable insights. This information can guide educators to effectively use PjBL to
address the complex needs of students with EBD (Wertz & Mulcahy, 2024).

using project-based learning (PjBL) with students with emotional and behavioral disorders
(EBD). Students with EBD experience challenges that result in adverse outcomes both in school
(Chen et al., 2021; Didion et al., 2018; Kumm et al., 2021; Enis et al., 2018; Hutchins et al.,
2020; Mathews et al., 2021; National Center for Education Statistics, 2024; Smith et al., 2018)
and out of school (Chen et al., 2021; Lipscomb et al., 2017; Offerman et al., 2022). While
evidence-based practices exist, implementing them with fidelity has not been as successful in the
classroom (Criss et al., 2024; Scheibel, 2023; Sutherland et al., 2019). Educators can use high-
leverage practices in conjunction with instructional methods that may not be evidence-based to
meet the needs of these students. Project-based learning (PjBL) has been successful in
environments with non-disabled students (Culclasure et al., 2019; Duke et al., 2021; Eldiva &
Azizah, 2019; Febriana, 2017; Gomez-Pablos et al., 2017; Revelle et al., 2020; Samsudin et al.,
2020; Sormunen et al., 2019) and shares similarities with HLPs (Wertz & Mulcahy, 2024). The
limited research conducted with students with EBD demonstrates the need for more research on
how PjBL is used with students with EBD and its impact (Taylor et al., 2024). This qualitative
case study will collect data to understand the experience and perspectives of teachers who use
PjBL with students with EBD.
Rationale for Approach
         Case study research is a qualitative approach that investigates "a real-life, bounded
system (case) or multiple bounded systems (cases) over time, through detailed, in-depth data
collection involving multiple sources of information" (Creswell & Poth, 2018, pp. 96-97).
Qualitative research is used when a problem has variables that are difficult to measure (Creswell
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  63

& Poth, 2018). A qualitative case study allowed me to explore the complex challenges associated
with teaching students with EBD through the story of teachers' experiences, focusing on research
conducted in a real-life context (Creswell & Poth, 2018). Data collection included interviews and
artifacts, allowing me to understand what the participants experienced, how they implemented
PjBL, how teachers felt PjBL impacted their students, and their perceptions of how successful
they felt using PjBL as an instructional methodology.

         Qualitative researchers must examine participants' perspectives in their natural
environment to understand a phenomenon (Suter, 2012), allowing researchers to discover what
meaning people attribute to a problem (Creswell & Poth, 2018). Researchers for case studies use
"real life cases that are in progress so that they can gather accurate information not lost by time"
(Creswell & Poth, 2018, p. 97). This design allowed me to gather relevant and timely data
(Creswell & Poth, 2018) on educators currently using PjBL with students with EBD.

         One issue is chosen in a collective case study, and the researcher identifies multiple cases
to research (Creswell & Poth, 2018). Multiple cases highlight different perspectives, and while
generalizing qualitative research is difficult, researchers can use multiple cases to mitigate this
issue. A collective case study allowed me to gain perspectives from three different teachers. This
design offered the opportunity to understand teachers' insights regarding the perceived
effectiveness and feasibility of PjBL with students with EBD. This approach contributed
valuable insights into the effectiveness of PjBL for students with EBD.

Site and Sample Selection

         Criterion sampling is used in qualitative research to assist in identifying participants with
similar characteristics and experiences (Creswell & Poth, 2018). This sampling method is helpful
in qualitative research because it can "reflect the purpose of the study and guide in the
identification of information-rich cases" (Merriam & Tisdell, 2016, p. 97). Using criterion
sampling for this study, I identified three participants with similar characteristics who all
experienced using PjBL with students with EBD in grades 6-12.
The criteria for the participants for this study were the following:

         1. Participants must have at least one year of teaching experience with students with
         EBD.
         2. Participants must have taught a complete project-based learning unit in a class
         with students with EBD within the prior school year.
Students with EBD may be identified under different primary disability categories in their IEP,
such as ED, other health impairment (OHI), intellectual disabilities (ID), or autism. For this
study, I focused specifically on teachers with students whose primary disability category was ED
or a combination of ED and OHI to minimize potential data inconsistencies and maintain a clear
focus on a defined group. Teachers were asked to reflect only on their experiences with students
identified under these categories, rather than those with other classifications, such as autism. The
term EBD is used throughout this report to refer to students who were identified with ED or ED
and OHI in school settings, received emotional support services, had a positive behavior support
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  65

plan included in their IEP, or whose IEP includes services and supports related to social or
emotional skill development or functional behavior (22 PA Code Chapter 14 § 14.131, para. iv).
EBD is also a more inclusive and less stigmatizing way to describe these students and is used by
other researchers in the field (Culclasure et al., 2019; Kumm et al., 2021).

         Requiring participants to have at least one year of teaching experience with students with
EBD ensured they had a minimum amount of time to draw upon when relating their experiences.
Requiring participants to have completed at least one unit of study where PjBL was the
instructional approach in a classroom with students with EBD was also essential to maintaining
relevant and timely data (Creswell & Poth, 2018). Requiring participants to have completed an
entire unit ensured their experiences were similar. This assisted in data analysis when identifying
"common experiences, themes, and the overall essence of the experience" (Creswell & Poth,
2018, p. 152). The addition of requiring the unit to be completed in the past school year could
have also increased the likelihood of participants more fully recalling their experiences.

Summary
         The themes identified through an iterative analysis described the experiences and

perspectives of educators using PjBL with students with emotional and behavioral disabilities.
Participants valued building relationships and understanding their students' needs, influencing
their adaptation strategies. Notably, two participants mentioned that PjBL included adaptations
within the learning experiences. Participants observed positive growth in their students'
academic, cognitive, behavioral, social, and emotional skills. Their students were more engaged
and motivated, with increased flexibility and ownership of their learning. The participants

challenges of group work and workload. Finally, they reflected that their experiences broadened
their understanding of learning, highlighted ways to improve, and inspired hope for other
educators to recognize the value of PjBL.

                            Chapter V. Conclusions and Recommendations
         This collective case study revealed teachers' experiences and perceptions using project-
based learning (PjBL) with students with emotional and behavioral disorders (EBD). Students
with EBD are at risk for many adverse outcomes (Chen et al., 2021; Didion et al., 2018; Kumm
et al., 2021; Enis et al., 2018; Hutchins et al., 2020; Lipscomb et al., 2017; Mathews et al., 2021;
National Center for Education Statistics, 2024; Offerman et al., 2022; Smith et al., 2018).
Applying evidence-based practices with fidelity in this group has been difficult because of their
complex needs (Criss et al., 2024; Scheibel, 2023; Sutherland et al., 2019). PjBL is an
instructional approach that shares features with high-leverage practices (HLP) (Wertz &
Mulcahy, 2024) and has seen success with students without disabilities (Culclasure et al., 2019;
Duke et al., 2021; Eldiva & Azizah, 2019; Febriana, 2017; Gomez-Pablos et al., 2017; Revelle et
al., 2020; Samsudin et al., 2020; Sormunen et al., 2019). The limited research on PjBL with
students with EBD shows potential (Foster & Shriner, 2023; Taylor et al., 2024).
         The study was focused on the following research question: What are the experiences and
perspectives of teachers using PjBL with students with EBD? High leverage practices (HLPs)
and the theoretical foundations of culturally responsive pedagogy and practices (CIPP) served as
frameworks for this study. Three participants were interviewed, and artifacts related to their
PjBL experiences were shared. Three themes emerged from the data: supporting student
learning, impact on students, and impact on teachers. This chapter discusses findings, limitations,
and implications for future research.
Discussion of the Findings
         Participants included a special education teacher at a military academy, an eighth-grade
STEM teacher from a central Pennsylvania school district, and a former special education
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  94

teacher from Pennsylvania teaching in self-contained settings. Data from participant interviews
and artifacts were analyzed multiple times through various sessions of coding transcripts and
materials to identify words and phrases that helped answer the research question in line with the
theoretical frameworks. The findings revealed the participants' value in building relationships to
understand how to meet their students' needs, their perceptions of the positive impact on the
whole student, and the effect using PjBL had on them as educators.
Supporting Student Learning

         The findings on supporting student learning described how teachers establish
foundational relationships to support students with EBD in PjBL. This involved establishing
trust, understanding their backgrounds, and adapting instruction. This theme aligned with several
principles within CIPP and HLPs. Data analysis revealed that participants emphasized building
rapport and trust with students by showing empathy, understanding their backgrounds and needs,
and being flexible in addressing student challenges.

         Participants emphasized the importance of building "strong relationships with students."
M.G. described this as essential for success: "It's more about the rapport with the students." S.F.
reflected on the lasting value of building relationships when discussing comments from former
students, saying, "We had a lot of kids that would call in, you know, and like check in and be
like, Miss, you know, I really miss you guys." These findings align with the CIPP principle of
student-teacher relationships, emphasizing "caring, relationships, interaction, and classroom
atmosphere" (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011, p.77). The findings also support the principle of
identity and achievement within CIPP, which encourages awareness of students' identities and
perspectives, including honoring student assets (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011). The participants
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  95

described building trusting relationships as rooted in understanding students' backgrounds and
needs, which led to a deeper understanding of what students required to succeed.

         Participants discussed how they implemented adaptation strategies, including
modifications to content, assessments, instructional methods, and flexible grouping
arrangements. The CIPP principle of equity and excellence aligned with this subcategory. This
principle encompasses the concepts of "dispositions, incorporation of multicultural curriculum
content, equal access, and high expectations" (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011, p. 74). Adaptations
included making content more relevant, creating more hands-on experiences, and using grouping
strategies. M.G. described how she adapted assignments from the content teacher: "I scaled the
complexity and structure to match their developmental level." Similarly, B.Q. mentioned during
planning that he would "start where they're at." S.F. discussed how her team used behavioral
data and learning style data to adapt instructional approaches and implement PjBL to meet the
needs of students requiring more experiential learning. Additionally, the data from her lesson
plan artifacts showed extensive differentiation strategies for struggling and advanced learners.
These strategies aligned with the CIPP principle of developmental appropriateness, guiding
teachers to tailor instruction based on learning styles and psychological needs (Brown-Jeffy &
Cooper, 2011). They also align with HLP 13, 15, and 17, (see Appendix M) emphasizing the
importance of adapting curriculum tasks and materials (HLP 13), using scaffolding to support
learning (HLP 15), and employing flexible grouping to address diverse needs (HLP 17).
(Lancaster et al., 2024).

         Teaching the whole child is an additional CIPP principle aligned with the findings. This
principle guides teachers to look beyond the surface-level needs of students and "remember the
needs of the total child" (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011, p. 76). Teachers need to consider
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  96

students' cultural backgrounds, beliefs, and priorities of families and how these intersect to
empower students to succeed (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011). Although family background was
not part of the data, all participants mentioned aspects of meeting student needs beyond
academics. These included addressing social needs by adjusting group roles and makeup and
leveraging their strengths in decision-making. Social skills deficits and behavioral challenges
were common. As B.Q. explained, "setting them up for success is super important." M.G. added,
"So we try our best, all of us, to accommodate whatever they need." These examples
demonstrate how teachers adapted project-based learning to meet students' needs, preferences,
and developmental levels.

         Notably, two participants felt that adaptations were naturally part of the PjBL process.
S.F. explained, "I do think project-based learning kind of has its own adaptations built-in," while
M.G. described it as "actually scaffolding." S.F. added,

         Well, with the integration of vocabulary and terminology, it allowed for them to actually
         gain the understanding of the actual physical manipulation tied in with the vocabulary. In
         contrast, when it was just vocabulary and just rote memorization, it didn't allow for them
         to accommodate to those needs.
         The theme of supporting student learning emphasizes how participants combined
relationship building with instructional practices to address the complex needs of students with
EBD. Establishing trusting relationships provided the foundation for effective instructional
strategies. The combination of relationship building and adaptations closely aligns with CIPP
(Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011). It is reflected in HLPs 13, 15, and 17 (see Appendix M),
highlighting adapting materials, scaffolding, and flexible grouping (Lancaster et al., 2024). These
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  97

strategies fostered classroom environments where students could engage, participate, and grow
academically and socially.

         The second theme that emerged from the data was its impact on students. The findings
reflected teachers' perceptions of the outcomes of PjBL for students with EBD, including
academic, behavioral, social, emotional, and motivational effects. This theme includes four

improve their replacement behaviors and "actually recognize their own capabilities."

and learning styles and adjust their instructional approaches, as described in the principle of
developmental appropriateness (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011), students are more likely to be
engaged and motivated to learn. PjBL is one method to foster this engagement, as the findings
showed that providing relevant, hands-on experiences and flexible structures helped students
participate more actively and consistently, aligning with HLP 18 (see Appendix M), which

These experiences support research emphasizing the role of PjBL in fostering engagement and
reducing behaviors among diverse learners.

         Like the engagement and motivation subtheme, fostering student ownership aligns with
HLP 18 (see Appendix M), which emphasizes using strategies to engage students in active
participation (Lancaster et al., 2024). Providing opportunities for choice and autonomy also
supports high expectations, as teachers encourage students to make decisions about their work
instead of relying on predetermined instructions. This approach aligns with the CIPP practice of
equity and excellence, emphasizing high expectations for students and empowering learners to
succeed (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011). Prior research also found that teachers perceived
allowing students to develop ideas and make choices as increasing engagement (Foster &
Shriner, 2023). These findings regarding the impact on students show how PjBL can be an
empowering experience for students with EBD, leading to positive outcomes across both
academic and non-academic areas.
Impact on Teachers

         The final theme identified from the data was the impact on teachers. The findings
revealed teachers' perceptions of how implementing project-based learning affected their
professional experiences, including positive aspects, challenges, and opportunities for personal
growth. Participants described project-based learning as a rewarding and effective instructional
approach that increased professional satisfaction, encouraged creativity, and offered more
instructional flexibility. B.Q. explained that despite the increased workload, "it's more rewarding
and more fun." The participants appreciated the flexible nature of PjBL for teachers, whether that
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  101

involved the instructional choices they could make or the addition of other teaching skills. S.F.
added that it allowed her to include more opportunities to teach students replacement behaviors.

         These findings are similar to other studies in which teachers reported positive perceptions
of using PjBL with students with EBD (Taylor et al., 2024). Participant reports of flexibility in
their instruction align with the principles of CIPP regarding the variation of needs and teaching
styles based on developmental appropriateness (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011).

         While most data indicated positive perceptions and experiences, participants shared
difficulties managing group dynamics and increased preparation demands. The social challenges
emphasized the need for intentional groupings. Participants reflected on these challenges, and
their experiences highlighted the importance of building relationships and classroom
communities, as seen in the CIPPs of equity and excellence and student-teacher relationships.
B.Q. mentioned the difficulties of managing the group, but added that it required him to
deliberately set up successful groups or support those who needed more help. He also described
fostering empathy and relationships within the classroom community when other students
expressed frustration over classmates who were not participating. Considering these social
challenges, he added, "It's the learning that's more important to them, and getting them to realize
also that treating everyone the same isn't the best option."

         The findings showed that participants believed PjBL required more planning and
preparation. Despite this, they continued to feel that it was worth the effort. Their enjoyment of
the work aligns with prior research, where teachers reported increased job satisfaction using
PjBL with students with EBD (Taylor et al., 2024).

         The final impact revealed by the findings was the professional growth of the participants.
Data showed how PjBL expanded their instructional perspectives, changed their mindsets about
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  102

teaching and learning, and supported their ongoing professional development. Their reflections
and growth align with part of HLP 6 (see Appendix M), where teachers are asked to analyze their
instructional practices and adjust as needed (Lancaster et al., 2024). The findings indicated
professional growth in understanding learning, a desire to continue learning more, and ideas for
improving instructional practices. S.F. noted that she would change how she introduces
vocabulary in the future and felt that using PjBL helped her improve her lesson planning. Despite
the workload and management challenges, participants' reflections suggest that project-based
learning was professionally rewarding and transformative. "I hope that everybody sees the
positivity that can really come out of it."
Limitations

         Several limitations should be considered when interpreting the findings of this study. The
participants implemented PjBL differently, reflecting the differences in their instructional
contexts, student populations, and professional roles. This variability limits the consistency of
comparisons across cases. While M.G. had experience planning full PjBL experiences in small,
self-contained classes, her experiences at the time of the study were more aligned with adapting
content and assessments from the content teacher at the military academy. B.Q. implemented
PjBL in an inclusive setting, and S.F. in a self-contained emotional support setting. Additionally,
when considering the elements of high-quality project-based learning, the data did not clearly
represent all elements (The Buck Institute for Education, 2018). For example, the element of a
public product appeared limited to peers in their classrooms or other teachers, except for B.Q.,
who noted that students collaborated across classes during one unit. It was also unclear how
much emphasis was placed on student reflection outside of S.F.'s statements.
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  103

         The participants held different professional roles. During this study, M.G. was a special
educator supporting content teachers at a military academy, B.Q. was an eighth-grade STEM
teacher in an inclusive classroom, and S.F. was a special educator working with students in a
self-contained classroom. Although their diverse experiences provided valuable and diverse
perspectives, the small sample size means the findings may not be transferable to other types of
instructional settings.

         My professional connection with two of the participants was a limitation. Although I had
never met or interacted with M.G. before this study, she worked for the organization I was
employed by at that time. We did not work in the same school or department within the
organization, but this should be noted as a potential limitation. Additionally, I briefly interacted
with S.F. during a statewide networking session before this study. These prior relationships could
have influenced the study's results. There may have been potential bias in data collection,
whether participants may have responded more positively or found it more difficult to feel
anonymous or completely honest. It could also have influenced how I interpreted their
experiences.

         The classrooms described by the participants included students with various disabilities.
All three participants mentioned they taught students with a range of different disabilities.
Although the informed consent process and interviews emphasized the need to focus specifically
on students with emotional disturbance, their recollections and examples may have included
experiences with students with other disabilities.

         The study's data were limited to interviews and artifacts. Artifacts included student work
from two participants and lesson plans from two participants. No artifacts related to participant
reflection or lesson materials were included. This data might have been influenced by recall bias
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  104

or participants' desire to present their experiences positively. A lack of observations may have
been a limitation. Observations would have provided an additional opportunity to triangulate the
data from interviews and artifacts by offering a different perspective on implementing PjBL with
students with EBD.

         This study focused on teacher experiences and perspectives but lacked the perspectives of
students, families, and administrators. Including these perspectives would have provided a more
complete understanding of how PjBL affected students with EBD. It could also have revealed
additional insights about implementation challenges, student experiences, and systematic factors
that influenced the use of PjBL with students with EBD.

         Finally, my interpretations of the data may have been shaped by my professional
experiences and beliefs about PjBL. I have professional experience using PjBL with students
with EBD in self-contained settings. My recollection of these experiences is that PjBL is an
effective way to teach students with EBD. Although I reflected on my biases and experiences
before interviews, data analysis, and writing, they may still have influenced my report.
Implications

         As mentioned in Chapter I, research on using PjBL with students with EBD is limited.
This study's results add to the existing research in this area. Teachers' perceptions and
experiences with PjBL were based on their understanding and student-teacher relationships.
They perceived a positive impact on students' academic and non-academic areas. Although
challenges related to group dynamics and planning were present, the findings indicated that
participants believed PjBL was an effective strategy for students with EBD.

         Participants perceived a positive impact on students. The findings showed that the
teachers in this study believed PjBL positively affected their students. Educators and school
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  105

leaders can use these findings to advocate for more PjBL with students with EBD in inclusive
and self-contained settings. PjBL could benefit other students beyond this study, as well as
teachers. Educational leaders and administrators should provide additional professional
development on PjBL, identify resources to help teachers reduce workload, and allocate more
planning time for educators using PjBL to help manage workload demands. Additionally,
professional development is recommended to include specific training on integrating social skills
instruction and flexible grouping strategies to help teachers address common challenges when
implementing PjBL with students with EBD. Regional organizations, such as intermediate units,
should also consider coordinating a professional learning community for educators across their
region to coordinate, collaborate, and learn from each other.
Recommendations for Future Research

         As noted in previous chapters, research on PjBL with students with EBD is limited.
Future studies should explore the impact and application of PjBL with students with EBD. The
participants perceived a positive effect in this study, and S.F. cited behavior data for one unit;
however, this can be expanded into a quantitative study that directly measures PjBL's perceived
impact on student academic achievement, social skills, and behaviors. A quantitative study is
recommended to measure student achievement through a curriculum-based assessment.
Additionally, conducting a quantitative study to examine pre- and post-intervention behavior
data for students participating in PjBL would provide valuable insights into its impact on
behavioral outcomes. This approach could also be applied using a social skills assessment to
examine the impact of PjBL on students' social development.

         As mentioned in the limitations, this study did not include classroom observations of
teachers. A recommendation is to conduct a case study that involves observing classes where
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  106

PjBL is used with students with EBD. In such research, it would be beneficial to have
participants engage in high-quality PjBL experiences, using a rubric to ensure consistency if
conducting a collective case study. A qualitative case study would build on the research
presented here. Additionally, qualitative research could explore students' perceptions of PjBL to
better understand its impact.

         Finally, the long-term effects of PjBL on these students should also be explored. A
mixed-methods longitudinal study tracking students over several years could determine if
improvements in academic achievement and social skills last beyond the PjBL experience.
Tracking students with EBD over multiple years could also show whether PjBL reduces adverse
outcomes, including dropout rates (National Center for Education Statistics, 2024) and isolation
from typical, same-aged peers (Office of Special Education Programs, 2020).
Conclusion

         Students with emotional and behavioral disorders face various negative outcomes,
including lower academic achievement and behavioral challenges (Chen et al., 2021; Smith et
al., 2018), higher dropout rates (National Center for Education Statistics, 2024), and social
isolation from their typical, same-aged peers (Office of Special Education Programs, 2020).
While evidence-based practices are available to reduce these risks, consistent implementation
remains difficult (Farley et al., 2023; Smith et al., 2018; Sutherland et al., 2019). Project-based
learning (PjBL) is a promising instructional approach because it aligns with many high-leverage
practices that support students with disabilities (Wertz & Mulcahy, 2024). Research with
students without disabilities has shown positive results in academics (Aydin & Mutlu, 2023;
Culclasure et al., 2019; Duke et al., 2021; Eldiva & Azizah, 2019; Febriana, 2017; Gomez-
Pablos et al., 2017; Krajcik et al., 2023; Revelle et al., 2020; Saavedra et al., 2021; Schneider et
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  107

al., 2022; Sormunen et al., 2019; Suryanti et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023), as
well as improvements in behavior and social-emotional learning (Culclasure et al., 2019;
Febriana, 2017; Krajcik et al., 2023; Juniar et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2015; Samsudin et al., 2020;
Wang et al., 2022). Studies involving students with other disabilities, such as speech, reading,
and writing disabilities (Berkeley et al., 2019), learning disabilities (Lu et al., 2022; Sormunen et
al., 2019), those who are blind, deaf, or intellectually disabled (Eldiva & Azizah, 2019), as well
as students in inclusive settings with disabilities, including learning disabilities, giftedness
(Hovey & Ferguson, 2014), autism, speech impairments, visual impairments, intellectual
disabilities, physical disabilities, deafness, ADHD, and learners with "social and behavioral
barriers" (Purnomo et al., 2022, p. 294), have reported similarly positive outcomes. Although
specific research on PjBL with students with EBD is limited, existing studies suggest that this
approach shows promise as an effective instructional method (Foster & Shriner, 2023; Taylor et
al., 2024).

         This collective case study revealed teachers' experiences and perceptions of using
project-based learning (PjBL) with students with emotional and behavioral disorders (EBD).
Criterion sampling was employed to select three participants who had used PjBL with students
with EBD during the 2024-2025 school year. Data analysis, guided by the theoretical
frameworks of high-leverage practices and culturally inclusive pedagogy and practices, revealed
themes of supporting student learning, impact on students, and impact on teachers.

         The findings of this study aligned with high-leverage practices (Lancaster et al., 2024)
and culturally inclusive pedagogy and practices (Brown-Jeffy & Cooper, 2011), as well as with
prior research on PjBL and students with EBD (Foster & Shriner, 2023; Taylor et al., 2024).
Data analysis revealed that participants emphasized building rapport and trust with students by
TEACHER EXPERIENCES WITH PJBL AND EBD  108

demonstrating empathy, understanding their backgrounds and needs, and maintaining flexibility
in addressing challenges. Their perceptions of student impact illustrated how PjBL can serve as
an empowering experience for students with EBD, contributing to positive outcomes across both
academic and non-academic domains. Teachers also reported that implementing PjBL enhanced
their professional experiences and supported their growth. Despite the challenges of workload
demands and group dynamics, PjBL was worthwhile for the participants and their students.
