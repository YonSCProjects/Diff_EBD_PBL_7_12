ABSTRACT
Since adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) seem highly prevalent for students with EBD, it is 
important to understand how ACEs are related to their severe internalizing problems and externa­
lizing problems. In this study, we assessed the mediating role of post traumatic stress (PTSD) 
symptoms and executive functioning (EF) in the potential association between ACEs and inter­
nalizing problems and externalizing problems. Participants were 174 students with EBD (mean age 
11.58 years, 85.1% boys) in five urban separate special education (SE) schools. We conducted 
multiple mediation modeling, examining pathways from ACEs (original and expanded framework) 
via PTSD symptoms and EF to behavioral problems (internalizing and externalizing problems, 
respectively). We used data from multiple sources, including self-reports, parent reports, and 
school files. Overall, PTSD symptoms and self-reported EF fully mediated the association between 
ACEs and internalizing problems and externalizing problems. Findings were consistent for both the 
ten original ACEs as for the expanded ACE framework. Teachers’ perspectives of EF did not mediate 
the association between ACEs and internalizing problems or externalizing problems. PTSD symp­
toms and self-reported EF seem to play an important role in the pathway from ACEs to internalizing 
problems and externalizing problems of students with EBD in separate SE schools.

Introduction
The term emotional behavioral disorders (EBD) is used 
to describe students with elevated levels of emotional, 
behavioral or social difficulties compared to their peers 
and are therefore often in need of special education (SE) 
support (National Research Council and Institute of 
Medicine, 2009; Popham et al., 2018). Despite SE ser­
vices and additional mental health care support 
(Offerman et al., 2022; Stoutjesdijk et al., 2012), schools 
often struggle to fulfill the needs of these students 
(Bradley et al., 2008) and long-term perspectives there­
fore remain poor (e.g., Kauffman & Landrum, 2018; 
Mitchell et al., 2019).
Since adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) appear 
to be highly prevalent in the lives of students with EBD 
(Mueser & Taub, 2008; Offerman et al., 2022, 2025), 
a trauma-informed perspective could advance our 
understanding of their severe and persistent internaliz­
ing problems and externalizing problems. ACEs are 
broadly defined as single or chronic exposures in the 
environment during childhood (0–18 years) that are 
distressing, 
potentially 
harmful 
and 
traumatic 
(Asmundson & Afifi, 2019; Felitti et al., 1998). An accu­
mulation of ACEs could severely impact functions of the 
brain that are crucial for self-regulation of emotions and 
behavior (Nelson et al., 2020; Van der Kolk, 2003), 
which are important aspects for educational outcomes, 
and mental health (Robson et al., 2020). Dysfunctions of 
the brain appear to be associated with symptoms of 
a post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD symptoms) on 
the one hand and problems in executive functioning 
(EF) on the other hand (De Bellis et al., 1999). 
Therefore, PTSD symptoms and problems in EF could 
play a key role in understanding the mechanisms of how 
ACEs are potentially linked to internalizing problems 
and externalizing problems of students with EBD, and 
address new ways to support these students.

As stated by the United Nations, efforts are needed to 
ensure that students with disabilities – such as EBD – 
have equal access to quality education, access to inclu­
sive and equitable education systems with lifelong 
opportunities to learn (United Nations Department of 
Economic and Social Affairs, 2018). To support students 
with EBD, special educational services are often offered, 
though globally definitions and criteria for SE services 
vary widely, reflecting different educational systems, 
cultural norms, and resources (Gagnon, 2022; Lopes,  
2014). Even so, most Western countries provide 
a continuum of SE services for students with EBD, 
ranging from least to most restrictive, aiming to inte­
grate students with disabilities into regular classrooms 
whenever possible, and providing separate SE schools 
for those with the most severe and persistent problems 
(Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, 2004; 
Kauffman & Landrum, 2018; McLeskey et al., 2012; 
UNESCO, 1994).
Currently, the identification rate of students with 
EBD in the U.S. who receive special education services 
under the category of ED is 0.7% of the school popula­
tion age 3–21 years (National Center for Education 
Statistics, 2024). In European countries (e.g., the UK, 
Ireland), these numbers vary from 0.9% to 1.8% 
(Popham et al., 2018). In other parts of the world data 
on EBD students receiving SE services is less well docu­
mented (Lopes, 2014). In the Netherlands, recent data 
on the total number of students with EBD receiving 
services along the SE continuum are not available. Yet, 
1.35% of all Dutch students, aged 4–18 years, receive 
education in separate SE schools for EBD (Ministerie 
van Onderwijs, Cultuur en Wetenschap, 2023). After 
placement, the great majority of these students remain 
in SE schools (Breeman, 2015), in relatively small classes 
of 10–15 students (Smeets, 2007), and with additional 
support from paraprofessionals, school psychologists, 
and youth mental healthcare (Offerman et al., 2022; 
Zweers, 2020). Students from these separate SE schools 
for EBD participated in this study.
While progress has been made in EBD research over 
the past 40 years, developing effective interventions for 
students with the most complex needs continues to be 
challenging on a global scale (Lloyd et al., 2019; 
McKenna et al., 2021; Mitchell et al., 2019; Pollack 
et al., 2024). Despite intensive support both in and out­
side of school, students with EBD face increased risks of 
early school dropout in the long term, just as reduced 
school performance, juvenile offending, disconnection 
from the community, and unemployment (Bradley 
et al., 2008; Mendoza et al., 2020; Mitchell et al., 2019). 
Furthermore, from a teacher’s perspective, although it is 
well-established that high quality relationships are cru­
cial for positive behavioral outcomes (Sabol & Pianta,  
2012), teachers often struggle to fulfill the complex 
relational needs of students with EBD, especially in 
segregated SE settings (Little & Kobak, 2003). An 
important challenge for teachers in segregated SE is 
preventing a negative cycle of interaction with students, 
where conflicts escalate externalizing behaviors, which 
then leads to a further increase of conflicts (De Swart 
et al., 2023). Research suggests that more adequate 
training is needed for teachers to feel prepared for the 
job (Billingsley et al., 2006; Bradley et al., 2008; De Swart 
et al., 2023). Currently, teachers of students with EBD 
report high levels of stress (Park & Shin, 2020), and 
show higher attrition rates than other SE teachers 
(Gilmour & Wehby, 2020). Both students’ and teachers’ 
perspectives highlight the necessity for new approaches 
to better understand and address the challenges of stu­
dents with EBD, and inform educational policies and 
daily practice. The critical role of trauma-informed 
approaches are emphasized, as many students with 
EBD are profoundly impacted by adverse childhood 
experiences, and in need for nurturing and supportive 
relationships (Lloyd et al., 2019; Pollack et al., 2024).
ACEs and EBD
Over the past years, a growing number of studies demon­
strated that accumulating ACEs are associated with a wide 
range of developmental health problems (Liming & Grube,  
2018; Oh et al., 2018). In many studies, ACEs have been 
operationalized in line with the early work of Felitti et al. 
(1998), who described 10 “original” ACEs: physical abuse, 
sexual abuse, emotional abuse, physical neglect, emotional 
neglect, alcohol or substance abuse, mental health pro­
blems, domestic violence, parent incarcerated, and paren­
tal divorce. It has increasingly been recognized that there 
are other ACEs, both within and outside the family, that 
are important to take into account, such as bullying, pov­
erty and community violence (Kalmakis & Chandler, 2014; 
Portwood et al., 2021). Studies using such an expanded 
framework proved to have similar outcomes as the original 
framework (Cronholm et al., 2015; Portwood et al., 2021), 
or, adding peer and community stressors, showed even

better predictions of childrens’ mental health outcomes 
(Finkelhor et al., 2013).
Students with EBD – with internalizing and externa­
lizing behavior as core problems – are, more than chil­
dren without EBD, exposed to ACEs (Bethell et al.,  
2016). In Dutch samples, ACEs appeared to be highly 
prevalent among these students (Wassink - de Stigter 
et al., 2024), compared to students in regular education 
schools (Vink et al., 2019). Among students with EBD in 
Dutch separate school facilities, all students (96.4% self- 
report, 89.5% parent report, 95.3% school files) 
appeared to be exposed to at least one ACE and more 
than half of students (74.4% self-reports, 52.7% parent 
reports, 59.9% school files) to four or more ACEs 
(Offerman et al., 2022).
In general populations, the accumulating effects of 
ACEs on both internalizing problems and externalizing 
problems has been well documented (Khanijahani & 
Sualp, 2022; Scully et al., 2020). Subsequently, the need 
for a deeper understanding of why identical environmental 
experiences (e.g., ACEs) can result in different forms of 
psychopathology in individuals has led to the emergence of 
transdiagnostic approaches. For example, the transdiag­
nostic model of risk and resilience of McLaughlin et al. 
(2020) points out the importance of identifying core psy­
chological and neurobiological mechanisms that link ACEs 
to multiple forms of internalizing and externalizing psy­
chopathology. Increasing insight into these core mechan­
isms could help identify key targets for intervention, in 
order to prevent or reduce negative effects of ACEs on 
emotional and behavioral health (McLaughlin et al., 2020).
Various scholars have analyzed factors and mechanisms 
that may account for how and why ACEs lead to inter­
nalizing problems and externalizing problems. It appears 
that genes, gender, psychological resilience, social and 
emotional information processing, parenting behavior, 
parental emotional distress, and neighborhood support 
are examples of factors that play a role in understanding 
why some children who experience ACEs develop more 
internalizing and externalizing problems than others 
(Bethell et al., 2014; Khanijahani & Sualp, 2022; Schwartz 
et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2022). Furthermore, a combination 
of genes, gender and environmental factors increased the 
chance of developing internalizing and externalizing pro­
blems (Wright & Schwartz, 2021). However, for the educa­
tional practice, more knowledge is needed on mechanisms 
that are important specifically for school functioning and in 
the school context. As students with EBD appear to experi­
ence high rates of ACEs, trauma-informed theory could be 
of additional value to understand the mechanisms that link 
ACEs to their internalizing problems and externalizing 
problems (see also Lloyd et al., 2019; Pollack et al., 2024).
Trauma-informed theoretical framework
Trauma-informed theories state that the impact of 
ACEs on academic, emotional, and behavioral problems 
could be understood from the negative effects of pro­
longed stress on the functions of the body and the brain 
(Nelson et al., 2020). In response to adverse events, 
activation of the stress-response system helps children 
to cope with their circumstances (Hornor, 2017). As the 
body and brain is focused on survival, there is lower 
activity in higher-region brain parts, associated with 
abstract thinking and planning, whilst adrenaline and 
cortisol are released to raise heartbeat, temperature and 
muscle tension in order to fight, flight or freeze. Yet, 
when faced with an accumulation of ACEs – especially 
at a young age, for a longer period of time and without 
sufficient social support – children’s stress responses 
become chronically dysregulated (Hornor, 2017; 
Nelson et al., 2020). Whereas a prolonged stress 
response could be viewed as an adaptive and adequate 
response to the child’s inadequate circumstances 
(Marsman, 2021), chronic dysregulation of the stress 
system is associated with dysfunctions of several brain 
areas (Vaidya et al., 2024).
PTSD symptoms
Theoretically, it has been argued that chronic stress 
affects the limbic system, location of both the amygdala 
and the hippocampus, areas that are responsible for 
processing emotional memories (Lupien et al., 2018). 
Abnormalities in these areas could lead to higher sensi­
tivity and reactivity to new stressors or could trigger 
stimuli even if real danger is absent (Iacona & Johnson,  
2018), and are particularly observed in people with 
PTSD (De Bellis et al., 1999; Tupler & De Bellis, 2006). 
Symptoms of PTSD include intrusions of frightening 
events, persistent avoidance of stimuli associated with 
traumatic event(s), negative alterations in cognitions 
and mood and marked alterations in arousal and reac­
tivity (American Psychiatric Association, 2013).
In education, core symptoms of PTSD such as reex­
periencing, hyperarousal, dissociation, sleep problems, 
attention and memory problems, could substantially 
interfere with school functioning, and are associated 
with impaired educational performance over time 
(Vilaplana-Pérez et al., 2020). PTSD symptoms can 
cause classroom disruption and misunderstanding of 
the students behavior by the teacher as – for students 
with EBD – symptoms could easily be perceived as parts 
of behavioral disorders (Mueser & Taub, 2008). More 
knowledge on the role of ACEs and PTSD symptoms in

internalizing problems and externalizing problems of 
students with EBD could help teachers reconceptualize 
behavioral problems from a trauma-informed perspec­
tive, enhance their compassion for the students’ pro­
blem behavior (Anderson et al., 2022; Loomis et al.,  
2023), and increase their capacity to respond in 
a sensitive manner (Berger et al., 2021).
Various empirical studies, both in general and clin­
ical populations, have reported that an accumulation of 
ACEs leads to an increased risk for PTSD symptoms 
(Copeland et al., 2007; Darnell et al., 2019; McLaughlin 
et al., 2013; Tabb et al., 2022). For example, Grasso et al. 
(2009) found that in children with one type of maltreat­
ment (sexual abuse, physical abuse, emotional maltreat­
ment, domestic violence or physical neglect), 30% met 
criteria of PTSD, whereas for children who were 
exposed to four different types of maltreatment, 80% 
met PTSD criteria. Furthermore, a systematic review on 
studies in school-aged youth showed that PTSD symp­
toms are associated with both internalizing problems 
and externalizing problems (Perfect et al., 2016). In 
addition, associations and high rates of comorbidity 
were found between PTSD and various psychiatric dis­
orders. These include more internalizing psychiatric 
disorders anxiety disorders and mood disorders (Chiu 
et al., 2024; Famularo et al., 1996), as well more exter­
nalizing disorders as ADHD and conduct disorder 
(Bernhard et al., 2018; Boodoo et al., 2022; Daud & 
Rydelius, 2009; Famularo et al., 1996).
Despite this, to our knowledge, the mediating role of 
PTSD in the accumulating effects of ACEs on internaliz­
ing and externalizing problems is less well understood. So 
far, most studies have focused on associations between 
a specific type of ACE and the mediating role that PTSD 
symptoms could have on the associations between these 
specific ACEs and internalizing problems and externaliz­
ing problems. These studies showed that, in general youth 
populations of preschool children, PTSD symptoms both 
partially and fully mediated the association between 
a specific type of ACE (maltreatment; intimate partner 
violence) and internalizing problems and externalizing 
problems (Miller et al., 2012; Milot et al., 2010). 
Furthermore, in a school-aged sample Yoon et al. 
(2016) found that PTSD symptoms mediated the effects 
of witnessing violence at home on internalizing problems, 
but not for externalizing problems.
Executive functioning
Chronic dysregulation of the stress system is also related to 
dysfunctional development of the prefrontal cortex (Van  
der Kolk, 2003), often seen in children with problems in EF 
(Lund et al., 2020; Shonkoff et al., 2012). EF encompasses 
a set of higher cognitive processes that facilitate planning 
and goal-directed regulation of thoughts, emotions and 
behavioral actions (Diamond, 2013). EF includes three 
component processes: inhibitory control, working mem­
ory, and cognitive flexibility (Diamond, 2013) and is con­
sidered essential for navigating nearly all daily activities 
(Blair & Diamond, 2008; Snyder et al., 2015).
EF has been argued to have a strong influence on 
academic learning (math, reading) and school success – 
presumably even more than IQ – as they support goal- 
directed problem solving, flexible adaptation to chan­
ging circumstances, and intentional self-regulated learn­
ing (Zelazo & Carlson, 2020). Moreover, impaired EF 
could lead to developing problem behaviors in school, 
such as inattentive and overactive behavior, as EF skills 
are needed to sustain attentional focus, refrain from 
responding impulsively, tolerate frustration and resist 
distraction (Zelazo et al., 2016). More knowledge on the 
specific role of ACEs and EF for internalizing and 
externalizing problems in students with EBD, could 
help teachers understand how behaviors of students 
could be explained and how they can help improve EF 
(Gagne & Nwadinobi, 2018; Muir et al., 2023; Samson 
et al., 2024).
A systematic review of Lund et al. (2020) revealed 
that the dose–response relationship between the accu­
mulation ACEs and EF deficits had not been explored. 
Yet, for a number of specific ACEs, namely maternal 
depression as and for maltreatment, associations with 
EF deficits were found (Lund et al., 2020). More 
recently, Op den Kelder et al. (2022) were among the 
first studies to examine the associations between ACEs 
(expanded framework) and EF. They found a long-term 
effect of accumulating ACEs on later EF of children. 
This association was found even though the children in 
that study predominantly experienced “less severe” 
trauma exposure, such as severe accidents rather than 
maltreatment or neglect. Furthermore, consistent with 
an earlier review of cross-sectional studies among pre- 
school children (Schoemaker et al., 2013), a recent 
meta-analysis revealed that EF in children may serve 
as a predictive factor for a range of externalizing and 
internalizing problems (Stucke & Doebel, 2024; Yang 
et al., 2022). For ADHD and conduct problems, associa­
tions were stronger in mixed and at-risk populations 
than in general populations (Yang et al., 2022).
For the mediating role of EF, a number of studies in 
general and clinical samples of school-aged children, 
showed the mediating effect of EF on the association 
between maltreatment and both internalizing problems

The present study
To date, research has revealed associations between the 
accumulation of ACEs and internalizing problems and 
externalizing problems. For a more in depth under­
standing on the underlying mechanisms between these 
associations, both trauma-informed theory and empiri­
cal research indicate that for educational practice, PTSD 
symptoms and EF could be important mediators to 
explore. This might be particularly of value for students 
with EBD in separate SE schools, and their teachers, as 
these students experience high numbers of ACEs and 
struggle with the most severe internalizing and externa­
lizing behavioral problems. Therefore, in the present 
study, we examined the following questions regarding 
this vulnerable population:
(1) What are the associations between ACEs on the one 
hand and PTSD symptoms, EF and internalizing 
problems and externalizing problems on the other 
hand?
(2) Do PTSD symptoms mediate the associations 
between ACEs and internalizing problems and 
externalizing problems?
(3) Does EF mediate the associations between ACEs 
and internalizing problems and externalizing 
problems?
We expected that ACEs are associated with interna­
lizing problems, externalizing problems (Nelson 
et al., 2020; Scully et al., 2020), PTSD symptoms 
(Alisic et al., 2014) and EF (Lund et al., 2020). 
Furthermore, we expected that both PTSD symptoms 
and EF are associated with internalizing problems, as 
well as with externalizing problems (Perfect et al.,  
2016; Yang et al., 2022). Finally, we expected that 
PTSD symptoms and EF would mediate the associa­
tions between ACEs and internalizing problems, as 
well as for the associations between ACEs and exter­
nalizing problems (Milot et al., 2010; Wei & Lü,  
2023). We proposed the following theoretical media­

Design and procedures
The current study is part of an ongoing research project, 
aimed at identifying factors that possibly influence the 
onset and maintenance of emotional and behavioral pro­
blems of students in Dutch urban separate SE schools for 
EBD. We used a descriptive retrospective cross-sectional 
design and a multi-informant perspective. Data were 
collected between the years 2017 and 2020. The study 
was approved by the Ethics Review Board of the 
University of Amsterdam (2017-CDE-7603). We briefed 
primary caregivers, teachers and students about the pro­
ject and asked for active and documented informed con­
sent. The consent rate of parents for child participation 
was 35% (N = 174). Students filled in questionnaires dur­
ing an individual assessment on a computer in the pre­
sence of a trained professional. Parents reported about 
their children at home, online or on paper if online was 
not possible. The primary SE schools had almost no full- 
time teachers. Therefore, per class we invited the teacher 
who spent the greatest amount of time in the classroom 
per week to take part in this research. In secondary SE 
schools, we asked each student’s mentor to fill in the 
questionnaires. These mentors are teachers who have 
the additional task to build a warm and supportive 
relationship with a student through one-on-one guidance 
and encouragement besides their daily classroom inter­
action. Furthermore, the mentor intensively collaborates 
with parents and the healthcare professionals involved. 
Additionally, for each participating student, their school 
file was analyzed. For full information on participation 
flowchart, adapted methods of assessment, and the school 
file study, we refer to Offerman et al. (2022).

Therefore, four parent reports and no self-reports were

In addition, school files were analyzed and the num­
ber and type of ACEs were coded. One of the first 
authors developed a codebook that was used to score 
the participating students’ school files. The codebook 
comprised six dimensions, e.g., socio demographic 
information, developmental history of the student 
(e.g., ACEs, complex trauma symptoms), school and 
healthcare trajectories. All available reports from pre­
vious and current schools settings and care settings were 
used, such as school progress reports, daily school jour­
nals, diagnostic reports, psychiatric reports and youth 
health care service reports. Four research assistants were 
trained in how to use the codebook. Subsequently, 
a total of 18 school files (10.5%) were separately scored 
by the assistants and then discussed until consensus was 
reached. With an agreement of 86.4%, the inter-rater 
reliability for the complete codebook was considered as 
good. Additionally, each coded ACE was checked for 
accuracy based on the reported substantiation by 
a different research assistant than the coder. Moreover, 
we rated the quality of the school files using a low, 
moderate and high score. Most school files appeared 
to be of low (69.8%) or moderate (27.9%) quality.
For the current study we only used the coded 
number and type of ACEs from the school files. 
The codebook included 10 ACEs from the original 
ACEs framework (Felitti et al., 1998), 12 ACEs from 
an expanded ACEs framework, and 3 ACEs that 
could be scored as “other” e.g (Anda et al., 2006; 
Asmundson & Afifi, 2019; Balistreri & Alvira- 
Hammond, 2016). Each ACE item in the codebook 
was scored and substantiated with text from the 
school file. ACEs that were substantiated by official 
reports, and ACEs that were reported in the school 
files according to the operationalization in the code­
book (unsubstantiated ACEs) were scored as pre­
sent. If no information was found or the available 
information did not match the operationalization, 
the ACE was scored as absent (“00”).
Based on the answers of all three informants, we 
compiled a composite ACE variable for 10 ACEs 
(ACE10) from the original framework and for an 
expanded framework of 31 ACEs (ACE31). If at least 
one of the informants indicated that an ACE either had 
happened or was witnessed, a score of 1 was given for 
that particular ACE. We directed all ACEs into cate­
gories based on the work of Asmundson and Afifi 
(2019).
Internalizing problems and externalizing problems
To measure the severity of emotional and behavioral 
problems of students, we used the internalizing pro­
blems and externalizing problems subscales of the 
Brief Problem Monitor Youth (BPM-Y) (Achenbach 
et al., 2011). The BPM-Y is an abbreviated version of 
the Achenbach System of Empirically Based Assessment 
instruments (ASEBA) (Achenbach & Rescorla, 2001; 
Piper et al., 2014), for children aged 6–18 years, to rate 
internalizing problems (six items) and externalizing 
problems (seven items) with 0 = not true, 1 = somewhat 
true or 2 = very true. Seven reports were missing and 
therefore excluded. In a previous study, the BPM-Y 
showed to have very good internal reliability (Hair 
et al., 2015) for internalizing problems (Cronbach’s α  
= .89) and externalizing problems (Cronbach’s α = .84) 
(Rognstad et al., 2022). In our present study, the internal 
reliability for internalizing problems was good 
(Cronbach’s α = .78) and moderate for externalizing 
problems (Cronbach’s α = .69) (Hair et al., 2015).
PTSD symptoms
We used the child version of the Children’s Revised 
Impact of Event Scale (CRIES-13) to measure PTSD 
symptoms of students. The CRIES-13 is designed for 
children aged 8–18 and contains 13 items of PTSD symp­
toms of which frequency during the past 7 days was 
scored on a four-point scale 0 (not at all), 1 (rarely), 3 
(sometimes) and 5 (often) (Perrin et al., 2005). We 
excluded 28 reports due to more than two missing 
answers, and one report because of an unreliable answer. 
Previous studies showed good psychometric properties of 
the CRIES-13 (Olff, 2005; Perrin et al., 2005; Verlinden 
et al., 2014). In a Dutch clinical sample, Cronbach’s alpha 
was .89 and test-retest reliability coefficient was .85 for the 
total score on the CRIES-13 (Verlinden et al., 2014). In 
our present study the Cronbach’s alpha can be considered 
as very good (.86) (Hair et al., 2015).

Executive functioning (EF)
To measure the level of EF, we used the self-report and 
teacher report version of the Behavior Rating Inventory 
of Executive Function (BRIEF) screener (Gioia et al.,  
2000). The BRIEF screener is an abbreviated version of 
the complete BRIEF questionnaire, and has been devel­
oped to provide a global picture of the level of EF in 
health care and education. The BRIEF screener consists 
of 14 items that can be scored on a three point scale 
(never, sometimes, often). Examples of the self-report 
items are “I have problems sitting still,” and “I have 
trouble switching from one activity to another.” 
Examples of the teacher report items are “Has trouble 
remembering things even for a few minutes,” and “Does 
not take initiative.” Higher scores indicate more EF 
problems. Previously, the BRIEF screener showed inter­
nal consistency of .81 for the child version, and .91 for 
the teacher version (Huizinga & Smidts, 2016). In our 
present study, Cronbach’s alphas were very good for 
both self-report (.80) and teacher report (.88) (Hair 
et al., 2015). Six self-reports and three teacher reports 
were missing and therefore excluded in the analysis.
Data analysis
We used SPSS version 28 for descriptives and prelimin­
ary analyses. First, we examined the correlations among 
study variables, and the correlations among the covari­
ates and study variables. Consistent with the findings of 
Offerman et al. (2022), gender, age, family composition 
and economic hardship correlated with ACEs. 
Therefore, these were included as exogenous covariates. 
We regarded the univariate skewness of 2.0 and higher 
and kurtosis of 7.0 and higher as non-normality indica­
tors (Curran et al., 1996). In addition, multivariate out­
liers were detected using Mahalanobis distance, with 
a cut off of p < .001. The values of all variables in this 
study indicated normality and no outliers were detected.
Second, we conducted multiple mediation modeling, 
using Mplus version 7.31 (Muthén & Muthén, 
1998–2013), in order to test our hypotheses using the 
maximum likelihood estimation with a bootstrap techni­
que of 1000 samples to obtain more accurate estimates of 
direct, indirect and total effects. We tested mediation 
hypotheses using the conditions of Baron and Kenny 
(1986): a significant association between (a) predictors 
and the mediators (EF, PTSD symptoms), (b) mediators 
and outcome variables (internalizing problems and exter­
nalizing problems), and (c) predictors (ACE10 and 
ACE31) and the outcome variables. Also, (c’) the direct 
effect of predictors on outcomes should change when 
including the effect of the mediator. We stopped building 
the model when one of the conditions was not met.

Mediation models
Multiple mediation models
We tested a total of eight mediation models to investi­
gate whether EF and PTSD symptoms mediated the 
associations between ACEs and students’ internalizing 
problems and externalizing problems. Separate models 
for ACEs using the original framework (ACE10) and 
ACEs using the extended framework (ACE31). We also 
tested separate models for teacher and student-reported 
EF. PTSD symptoms were only measured using the 
students’ perspective. Gender was not included as 
a covariate as it did not correlate with either of the 
study variables. Family composition and economic 
hardship only correlated with the predictor and not 
with other variables. Therefore, they were not included 
in the models as covariates. Students’ age correlated 
with all variables and was included in all mediation 
models as an exogenous covariate.

student-reported EF

Discussion
In the current study we aimed to gain a better under­
standing of the impact of ACEs on the internalizing pro­
blems and externalizing problems of students with EBD in 
separate SE schools. Therefore, we examined mediating 
effects of PTSD symptoms and EF on the potential asso­
ciations between ACEs and internalizing problems and 
externalizing problems. Our results revealed high numbers 
of ACEs in the lives of students with EBD in separate SE 
schoolsSSES, with over 60% of the students being exposed 
to four or more ACEs. When students experienced 
a cumulative exposure to ACEs, we found more PTSD 
symptoms, EF problems, internalizing problems, and 
externalizing problems. Overall, we found that PTSD 
symptoms and self-reported EF fully mediated the associa­
tions between ACEs and both internalizing problems, and 
externalizing problems. However, teacher-reported EF was 
not significantly associated with internalizing problems 
nor with externalizing problems. In conclusion, our study 
suggests that PTSD symptoms and EF play a key role in the 
pathway from ACEs to the severe internalizing problems 
and externalizing problems of students with EBD in sepa­
rate SE schools.
Our study confirmed that students with EBD experi­
ence higher numbers of ACEs compared to general popu­
lations (Carlson et al., 2020; Hughes et al., 2017; Vink et al.,  
2019). Consistent with studies in general populations, we 
also found that cumulative exposure to ACEs is associated 
with an increase in PTSD symptoms, EF problems, inter­
nalizing problems, and externalizing problems (Copeland 
et al., 2007; Khanijahani & Sualp, 2022; Op den Kelder 
et al., 2022; Scully et al., 2020). As stated in the transdiag­
nostic model of risk and resilience (McLaughlin et al.,  
2020), this underlines the need to understand the mechan­
isms that contribute to this association, in order to find 
important pathways for intervention. So far, PTSD (Miller 
et al., 2012; Milot et al., 2010) and EF (Heleniak et al., 2016; 
McNeilly et al., 2021; Wade et al., 2019; Wei & Lü, 2023) 
have been studied as separate mediators for single ACEs 
such as maltreatment, neglect or poverty. Our study 
demonstrated that for students with EBD in separate SE 
schools, PTSD symptoms and self-reported EF fully 
mediated the associations between accumulating ACEs 
and both internalizing and externalizing problems.
Therefore, in line with trauma-informed theory, our 
study suggests that PTSD symptoms and EF play a key 
role in the pathway from ACEs to the severe internaliz­
ing problems and externalizing problems of students 
with EBD in separate SE schools. This provides impor­
tant insights for educational professionals. If educators’ 
perspective on the severe emotional and behavioral pro­
blems of their students widens toward the potential 
impact of ACEs in terms of PTSD symptoms and EF, 
it could increase their capacity to respond to students’ 
trauma by adopting trauma-informed practices focusing 
on stress-regulation and recovery (Berger et al., 2021).
From our results, we can conclude that especially 
PTSD symptoms were important for predicting students 
internalizing problems, considering the larger explained 
variance in all models. Therefore, findings suggest that 
trauma symptoms play a more pronounced role in the 
development of internalizing problems than EF and are 
therefore a prioritized target in education and youth 
health care for students with EBD.
While our findings are overall in line with previous 
studies, compared to different samples, we found some 
differences for both mediators and externalizing problems. 
For example, our findings contrasted those of Yoon et al. 
(2016) who did not find a mediating effect of PTSD on the 
association between violence at home and externalizing 
problems in a school-aged sample. Possibly, this difference 
could be explained by the lack of control for gender in this 
study. Externalizing behavior might be a result of exposure 
to violence particularly among boys (Ruchkin et al., 2007), 
who are dominantly represented in our sample. Bernardes 
et al. (2020) and Kent et al. (2022) did not find a mediating 
effect of EF on the association between maltreatment on 
the one hand and disruptive behavior disorders and anti­
social personality disorder on the other hand. These dif­
ferent findings could potentially be explained by the fact 
that, in contrast to our sample, Bernardes et al. (2020) 
found only a low rate of maltreatment, whereas effects on 
EF would predominantly occur among individuals with 
more prolonged stress responses, within samples with 
higher rates of maltreatment (Bernardes et al., 2020). 
Moreover, participants in the study of Kent et al. (2022) 
were substantially older than in our study, which may have 
hindered capturing changes in EF after maltreatment.
Informants
As mentioned, findings on the mediating role of EF did 
not hold for teacher-reported EF, as in our mediating 
models we did not find significant associations between 
teacher-reported EF and either self-reported internaliz­
ing problems or externalizing problems. Overall, there 
appears to be low agreement between teachers’ and self- 
reports of EF (Andersen et al., 2024). In our study, we 
found even lower correlations between teacher- and

99

student EF reports than in Andersen et al. (2024). It 
means that self-reports and teacher reports are highly 
different and potentially provide unique perspectives in 
understanding the role of EF as a mediator between 
ACEs and internalizing problems and externalizing pro­
blems. Future research could include such multiple per­
spectives and additionally test competing models of 
same-informant and cross-informant mediation.
Limitations, strengths and future research
Several limitations in our study should be noted. First, 
our sample was relatively small, which restricts the gen­
eralizability of our findings. Second, we expect to have 
a potential underreport of ACEs and trauma-related 
problems, as parents’ main reasons for refusal were 
that their child already had experienced many diagnos­
tic assessments and participation was expected to be too 
stressful because of the child’s trauma (Offerman et al.,  
2022). In addition, while using aggregated ACE scores 
gave us the best possible estimation of the number of 
ACEs, we cannot rule out underestimation for several 
reasons. These include the fact that parents may not be 
aware of all ACEs, or may have a feeling of shame that 
have led to underreporting (Zelechoski et al., 2021), and 
the fact that the quality of school files varied, not all 
information on ACEs had been documented. 
Furthermore, our study was limited due to a cross- 
sectional and retrospective design. Therefore, state­
ments about causal effects between variables could not 
be made. Nevertheless, we view our study as a first step 
toward a better understanding of the role of ACEs, 
PTSD and EF in understanding internalizing problems 
and externalizing problems in students with EBD. 
Research among such a highly vulnerable population, 
using a multi-informant perspective, is relatively scarce.
Future research could explore the role of ACEs, 
PTSD symptoms and EF in students with (less severe) 
EBD in regular education settings as essential factors for 
early intervention and prevention of high service needs 
(Pollack et al., 2024). Currently, almost all students 
within our sample experienced multiple school switches 
and health care trajectories before referral to separate SE 
schools. Although we did not find significant differences 
for gender or for students in single parent households, 
these factors remain important to be considered in 
future research, as males and students from single par­
ents households are greatly overrepresented among stu­
dents with EBD, compared to a general population. 
Furthermore, future research may also be directed at 
exploring other potential mediators in the further 
understanding of the onset and maintenance of inter­
nalizing problems and externalizing problems after 
ACEs, such as resilience (Bethell et al., 2014; Lackova 
Rebicova et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022), attachment or 
close relationships with significant others (e.g., Baglivio 
et al., 2020; Burstein et al., 2021; Hays-Grudo & Morris,  
2020; Kobak et al., 2001), social processes such as coer­
cive family interactions in the absence of positive social 
interactions (Asmussen et al., 2020; Patterson et al.,  
1998), and school connectedness (Yang et al., 2022).
Lastly, while our study focused on underlying mechan­
isms for the association between ACEs and internalizing 
problems and externalizing problems in students with 
EBD in separate SE schools, it is important that future 
research will pay attention to predictors of their ACEs, for 
example on the intergenerational transmission of paren­
tal ACEs (Ma et al., 2022; Narayan et al., 2021; Racine 
et al., 2023) and for what is called “the circle of adversity” 
in which behavioral problems may be a precursor of 
ACEs as well (Lugo-Candelas et al., 2021).
Implications for practice
Raising awareness among educational professionals on 
the prevalence and impact of ACEs, and the crucial role 
of PTSD symptoms and EF in understanding emotional 
and behavioral problems, is a first and important step 
toward finding new, trauma-informed, ways to support 
students with EBD. Schoolwide trauma-informed 
approaches encompass training educational profes­
sionals on the impact of ACEs on development, and 
finding ways to support their dysregulated students in 
class. Trauma-informed approaches also address profes­
sionals’ own emotional health and well-being by pro­
moting self-care and organizational support, as the 
professionals play a crucial role in fostering high- 
quality relationships, and provide regulatory support 
for their students who are impacted by ACEs and EBD 
(Blodgett & Dorado, 2016).
Furthermore, in daily practice, teachers could adopt 
positive mental health practices to counterbalance the 
overactive stress response system of their students and 
reduce PTSD symptoms, and prompt a more resilient 
baseline. Predictable daily structures, and behavioral 
practices such as deep breathing, muscle relaxation and 
mindfulness training are such examples (Samson et al.,  
2024). Moreover, the importance of EF in understanding 
internalizing problems and externalizing problems of 
students with EBD, appeals to a need to include (stress) 
regulation activities in education and youth mental health 
support. Physical exercise, mindfulness, yoga and

mediated play have proved to be effective in improving 
EF and self-regulation and could have an important role 
to reduce internalizing and externalizing problems, and 
promote educational achievement for students with EBD 
(Gagne & Nwadinobi, 2018; Muir et al., 2023).
Based on our findings, it may be crucial to conduct early 
assessment for both ACEs and PTSD symptoms in chil­
dren with (emerging) EBD. It should be noted that in our 
sample, prevalence rates of diagnosed PTSD (6.3%) were 
much lower than other diagnoses such as ADHD (35.1%) 
and autism spectrum disorders (35.1%). This could indi­
cate that underdiagnosis and undertreatment of PTSD 
symptoms could be an important issue for students with 
EBD in separate SE schools. Based on our overall high 
prevalence rates, we advise the assessment of a broad range 
of ACEs, as they provide a wide understanding of the 
circumstances students – and their parents – have lived 
and survived in and potentially influence the way they 
think, feel and behave. As students in our sample are 
consequently exposed to ACEs within the family context, 
we recommend that every assessment should include par­
ental (rearing) stressors as well as family social circum­
stances (debts, housing and work, social support).
Early assessment could help to timely referral to 
trauma treatment (EMDR, T-CBT, KID-NET), which 
could play an important role in reducing internalizing 
problems and externalizing problems and therefore 
improving wellbeing for these students and their readi­
ness to learn (Burback et al., 2024; Gutermann et al.,  
2017; Karatzias et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2022). In recent 
years, family-based trauma treatment has come forward 
as a promising and potentially more effective way to 
support both parents and children that suffer from the 
effects of (intergenerational) trauma (Martin et al., 2019; 
Mevissen et al., 2020; Mooren et al., 2023), that could 
stop the cascade of problems and provide parents with 
the support they need.
Conclusion
Our current study suggests that – through pathways of 
PTSD symptoms and EF – ACEs play a key role in 
understanding the severe internalizing problems and 
externalizing problems of students with EBD in separate 
SE schools. The strongest evidence was found for the 
role of PTSD symptoms in the understanding of inter­
nalizing problems. Our findings underscore the need of 
a shift in perspective toward viewing emotional and 
behavioral disorders as a coping mechanism to deal 
with the adversity these students are confronted with 
early in life (Marsman, 2021). From such a perspective, 
family-based trauma-informed approaches in education 
and care, with interventions specifically targeting PTSD 
symptoms and EF deficit, could help to improve 
students’ wellbeing and, ultimately, their outcomes.
