# Differentiated PBL for the Robotics Workshop
## A Practical Framework for Students with EBD — Groups of 4–8, Individual Projects

---

## A NOTE ON THE EVIDENCE

This framework is an honest synthesis. I want to be transparent about what's grounded in research and what's a responsible inference:

- **Directly supported:** DI improves outcomes for secondary students (Smale-Jacobse, 2019; d ≈ 0.5), especially low-achieving students. Self-management and antecedent strategies work for ADHD (Harrison et al., 2019). EBD students can self-identify effective learning conditions (Billingsley, 2018). PBL shows preliminary positive effects on EBD student behavior (Taylor, 2024; small sample).
- **Reasonably inferred but not directly tested:** Combining DI with PBL for EBD students. Applying classroom DI findings to a workshop setting. Robotics as the specific PBL vehicle.
- **Professional wisdom, not yet empirical:** Specific scaffolding sequences below. The exact tiering structure. The session routine timing.

Where research is strong, I cite it. Where I'm bridging gaps, I say so. Use your professional judgment to adapt — you know your students.

---

## YOUR CORE CHALLENGE

You have 4–8 students, each on a different project, and you are **one person**. You cannot be next to every student at every moment. The entire design must solve for one thing:

> **How do I set each student up so they can work productively for 10–20 minutes without me, so I can rotate between students?**

Everything below serves that goal.

---

## PART 1: THE SESSION ROUTINE (Same Every Time)

The single most supported finding across trauma, ADHD, anxiety, and EBD research is that **predictable routines reduce behavioral incidents and anxiety** (Watson et al., 2025; Koslouski et al., 2023; Harrison et al., 2019). Every workshop session should follow the same sequence. Students should be able to predict what happens next without asking you.

### Suggested Session Structure (for a ~60–90 min session)

| Phase | Time | What Happens | Your Role |
|-------|------|-------------|-----------|
| **1. Arrival & Board Check** | 5 min | Students arrive, check their personal task board, review where they left off | Greet each student by name. Brief check-in: "How are you today?" (1–2 words is fine) |
| **2. Mini-Huddle** | 5 min | Whole group, standing at a central table. You announce the plan: "Today everyone works on their next step. I'll start with [name], then rotate." | Set expectations. Acknowledge anyone who reached a milestone. |
| **3. Independent Work Block 1** | 15–20 min | Students work on their project using their task card. Timer visible. | Rotate: spend ~4–5 min with each student who needs support. |
| **4. Movement Break** | 3–5 min | Stand up, stretch, get water, walk around. Structured, not free-for-all. | Required for all. Non-negotiable. Reduces ADHD restlessness and anxiety buildup. |
| **5. Independent Work Block 2** | 15–20 min | Continue project work. | Rotate to students you didn't reach in Block 1. |
| **6. Clean-Up** | 5 min | Tools away, workspace tidy. Same routine every time. | Consistent. Every student responsible for their own station. |
| **7. Close-Out Circle** | 5 min | Each student says ONE thing: "Today I worked on ___" or "I got stuck on ___." | Listen. Validate. Don't problem-solve here — just acknowledge. |

**Why this structure matters:**
- Predictability reduces anxiety and need for teacher direction (Koslouski et al., 2023)
- Chunked time blocks with a break support ADHD attention spans (Harrison et al., 2019)
- The close-out builds self-awareness and language for self-monitoring (Harrison et al., 2019 — self-management is evidence-based for ADHD)
- Arrival board check is an **antecedent strategy** — it sets the student up to know what to do BEFORE they need to ask you

## PART 2: THE TASK CARD SYSTEM (Your Key to Independence)

This is the most important practical tool. The task card is what allows a student to work without you.

### What Is a Task Card?
A **single physical sheet** (laminated or in a clear sleeve) at each student's workstation that tells them:
1. What they're building (with a picture)
2. The step they're on right now
3. Exactly what to do for that step
4. How to know when the step is done
5. What to do if they're stuck (BEFORE asking the teacher)

### Task Card Template

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  PROJECT: [Name]              STUDENT: [Name]    │
│  ─────────────────────────────────────────────── │
│                                                   │
│  ★ YOUR CURRENT STEP: Step [#] of [total]        │
│                                                   │
│  WHAT TO DO:                                      │
│  □ [Clear, concrete action 1]                     │
│  □ [Clear, concrete action 2]                     │
│  □ [Clear, concrete action 3]                     │
│                                                   │
│  YOU'RE DONE WITH THIS STEP WHEN:                │
│  → [Observable result: "the LED lights up when    │
│    you press the button"]                         │
│                                                   │
│  STUCK? TRY THIS FIRST:                          │
│  1. Re-read the step above                        │
│  2. Check the reference card in your folder       │
│  3. Look at the example on the demo shelf         │
│  4. Ask a neighbor: "Can you look at this?"       │
│  5. Put the RED card up → teacher will come       │
│                                                   │
│  ☐ STEP COMPLETE → Move clip to next step         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Why This Works (Research Basis)

- **Chunking into small steps** reduces EF demand — critical for students with ADHD, anxiety, and trauma-related EF deficits (Cumming et al., 2024)
- **"Done when"** criteria give a clear finish line — reduces perfectionism spirals (relevant for OCD, anxiety) and provides a sense of completion (motivation for depression)
- **"Stuck? Try this first"** is a **self-management protocol** — the most evidence-based classroom intervention for ADHD (Harrison et al., 2019). It teaches students to problem-solve before seeking help.
- **The red card signal** — a non-verbal way to request help. Students don't have to interrupt, call out, or wait with hand raised. This reduces anxiety about asking for help and reduces impulsive blurting (ADHD/ODD).
- **Physical checkbox** — tactile progress tracking. Each checkmark is a small dopamine hit (relevant for ADHD motivation).

### How Many Steps Per Task Card?

**3–5 steps maximum per card.** When a student finishes all steps on one card, you swap it for the next card. This prevents overwhelm when a student sees "Step 4 of 27."

Think of it as chapters: they only see the current chapter.

## PART 3: TIERED PROJECT DESIGN

Research supports **tiering by readiness** as one of the most effective DI strategies in secondary settings (Smale-Jacobse, 2019). In your robotics workshop, this means offering projects at different complexity levels — but all connected to robotics, so no student feels they got the "easy" version.

### Three Tiers for Robotics Projects

**The goal is not to label students.** It's to match the project to what a student can do with moderate challenge and scaffolding. Students can move between tiers across projects.

#### 🟢 TIER 1 — Guided Build (High Structure, Low Ambiguity)
**For students who need:** maximum predictability, clear steps, frequent success experiences
**Typical profile:** High anxiety, new to workshop, severe EF deficits, post-trauma (needs safety first)

- Project follows a **step-by-step build guide** (like a LEGO instruction booklet)
- Each step has a picture of what it should look like
- Limited decision points — the student builds as instructed
- Success is visible and concrete: "it moves," "it lights up," "it follows the line"
- **Independence level:** HIGH — student can follow the card without you for 15+ min

**Examples:**
- Build a line-following robot from a kit with numbered instructions
- Assemble a pre-designed LED circuit on a breadboard
- Follow a block-coding tutorial step-by-step to make a robot drive in a square

#### 🟡 TIER 2 — Guided Design (Moderate Structure, Some Choice)
**For students who need:** some challenge but with guardrails, can handle 2–3 decision points
**Typical profile:** ADHD (needs engagement + novelty), mild ODD (needs some autonomy), moderate anxiety (can handle limited ambiguity if supported)

- Project has a **defined goal** but the student makes **some design choices**
- Task card has decision points: "Choose ONE: Option A or Option B"
- Reference materials available (photos, short videos, example builds)
- Student tests, adjusts, and iterates within the constrained design space

**Examples:**
- Build a robot that can push an object off a table — choose your own chassis design from 3 options
- Code a robot to navigate a simple maze — choose your sensors
- Design a custom LED pattern for a school display — choose colors and timing

#### 🔴 TIER 3 — Open Design (Low Structure, High Autonomy)
**For students who need:** challenge, creative freedom, self-direction, respect for competence
**Typical profile:** ODD (responds to autonomy and respect), ADHD with high ability (needs novelty and complexity to stay engaged), student who has mastered Tier 1–2 projects

- Student defines the problem and designs the solution
- Task card is a **project planner** rather than step-by-step instructions
- Student sets their own milestones (with your approval)
- You serve as consultant, not instructor
- **Independence level:** HIGH — but student needs periodic check-ins to avoid scope creep or frustration

**Examples:**
- "Build something that solves a real problem in our school" (student-defined)
- Design and build a robot for a specific challenge/competition
- Create a functioning prototype of the student's own idea

### How to Assign Tiers

**Do not assign tiers based on diagnosis.** A student with severe ADHD might be a Tier 3 builder because they're highly skilled and need the challenge. A student with ODD might start at Tier 1 because they're new and need to experience success before they trust you enough for autonomy.

Based on Billingsley (2018), students with EBD can often **self-identify** what works for them. Consider:
- Start new students at Tier 1 (safety and success first — trauma-informed)
- After 1–2 successful projects, ask: "Do you want to try one where you make more choices?"
- Let students move down without shame: "Let's do a guided one this time — sometimes it's good to just build"

## PART 4: DESIGNING FOR SPECIFIC CHALLENGES

This section maps specific EBD-related challenges to design features. **Note:** These are NOT diagnosis-based prescriptions. They're challenge-based adaptations. One student may show challenges from multiple categories.

### Challenge: Can't Sustain Attention (common in ADHD)

**What research says:** Antecedent modification + self-management are evidence-based (Harrison et al., 2019)

**Workshop design:**
- Task card steps take **no more than 5–10 minutes each** — the student gets a checkbox after each one
- **Visual timer** at their station (Time Timer or similar — students can see time passing)
- Alternate between high-focus tasks (wiring, coding) and high-movement tasks (testing, adjusting, retrieving parts)
- Allow **fidget tools** at the station that don't interfere with work (stress ball, textured strip on desk edge)
- If a student finishes early or stalls: have a **"bonus challenge"** card ready (a quick, fun side task)

### Challenge: Refuses Tasks / Power Struggles (common in ODD)

**What research says:** Choice and autonomy reduce oppositional behavior. Avoid power struggles — offer genuine choices (general EBD best practice; supported by DI research on choice — Billingsley, 2018)

**Workshop design:**
- ALWAYS offer at least 2 choices: "Do you want to start with the wiring or the coding today?"
- Give leadership opportunities: "Can you be in charge of the parts bin today?"
- Frame tasks as requests, not commands: "When you're ready, the next step is on your card"
- If refusal happens: **don't escalate.** Say "That's okay. Take a minute. The card will be here when you're ready." Then move to another student.
- Tier 3 projects are often the best fit — the student owns it, there's nothing to refuse
- **Never take the project away as punishment.** The project is the relationship.

### Challenge: Anxiety / Perfectionism / Fear of Failure (common in anxiety, OCD)

**What research says:** Predictability reduces anxiety. Multiple ways to demonstrate learning reduce pressure (Koslouski et al., 2023; Boardman, 2025)

**Workshop design:**
- Same routine every session — no surprises
- Task cards with explicit "done when" criteria — removes the question "is this good enough?"
- **Normalize iteration:** "In robotics, nothing works the first time. That's the process."
- Allow students to **watch you fail** — model debugging: "Hmm, that didn't work. Let me check the wiring. Okay, found it." This destigmatizes mistakes.
- For students who freeze at decision points (Tier 2): reduce options to 2, not 3
- Don't display unfinished work without permission
- Allow student to keep project at their station until they're satisfied (no forced sharing)

### Challenge: Withdrawal / Low Energy / "I Don't Care" (common in depression)

**Workshop design:**
- Start with the smallest possible first step: "Just plug in these two wires. That's it for now."
- Build momentum through **visible, tangible results** — robotics is powerful here because things physically MOVE
- Pair with a high-interest theme if possible: connect project to gaming, music, art, sports
- Don't require verbal participation in the close-out circle: "You can pass, or just give a thumbs up/down"
- Celebrate micro-progress privately: "Hey — yesterday you didn't start. Today you did two steps. That's real."
- **Don't interpret slowness as defiance.** Keep expectations low, praise genuine.

### Challenge: Emotional Dysregulation / Outbursts (common in trauma, ADHD, ODD)

**What research says:** Trauma-informed environments provide safety and co-regulation (Watson et al., 2025; Koslouski et al., 2023)

**Workshop design:**
- Designate a **cool-down area** in the workshop (a chair near the back, away from tools, with something calming — a fidget, a book, a sketch pad). Make it available to everyone, not just "that kid."
- If a student escalates: safety first (other students, tools), then **calm, quiet, short sentences.** "I see you're upset. You can take a break. Your project will be here."
- After de-escalation: **don't process the incident right away.** Let them re-engage with their project. Processing happens later, or with the counselor.
- Store projects safely — if a student damages their project during an outburst, help them repair it next session without judgment. "Things break in robotics. We fix them."
- **Never have consequences that destroy student work.** The project is their proof they can build something. Destroying or confiscating it re-traumatizes.

## PART 5: THE INDEPENDENCE SYSTEM — PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER

Here's how all the pieces combine to give you the **rotation time** you need:

### Setup (Before Students Arrive)

1. Each workstation has:
   - The student's current **task card** (face up)
   - Materials/tools needed for today's steps (pre-staged)
   - A **RED card** and a **GREEN card** (signal system)
   - Visual timer (if that student uses one)

2. Demo shelf has:
   - Example builds for reference
   - Finished versions of Tier 1 projects (so students can compare)

3. Your rotation plan:
   - Know which 2–3 students are most likely to need you today
   - Start your rotation with them

### During the Session

| Student Status | Signal | What It Means | Your Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| Working fine | GREEN card up (or no card) | "I'm on track" | Brief positive check-in when you rotate past: "Looking good" |
| Stuck but trying | No signal yet | Working through "Stuck? Try this first" protocol | Don't intervene. Let the protocol work. |
| Needs help | RED card up | "I've tried the steps and I'm stuck" | Come when you can. Acknowledge: "I see your red card, I'll be there in 2 min" |
| Disengaged | No card, no work | Various (tired, upset, confused, bored) | Brief check-in: "Hey — what's going on?" Offer a choice: "Want to keep going or take a break first?" |
| Escalating | Warning signs you know | Building toward outburst | Prioritize. Go to that student. Use calm de-escalation. |

### The Key Insight

> **You are not trying to eliminate the need for your attention. You are trying to make your attention *strategic* rather than *reactive*.**

When 6 students each have a clear task card, a signal system, and a predictable routine, at any given moment **3–4 of them will be working independently.** That gives you focused time with the 2–3 who need you.

Smale-Jacobse (2019) reviewed DI in secondary settings and found small-to-moderate positive effects on student achievement (d ≈ 0.5–0.7) across studies. The mechanism this framework relies on — student self-direction freeing teacher attention for targeted support — is a responsible inference from the DI literature, not a claim the review itself makes. Boardman (2025) similarly supports the value of scaffolding in PBL for diverse high school learners.

## PART 6: SAMPLE TIERED PROJECT SET — "LIGHT-UP ROBOT"

Here's one concrete example of a project that can be offered at all three tiers simultaneously. Different students, different versions, same workshop session.

### 🟢 Tier 1: "Build the Light-Bot"
**Goal:** Assemble a small robot with LED eyes that light up when turned on.

| Step | Task | Done When |
|------|------|-----------|
| 1 | Attach the battery holder to the base using the guide picture | Battery holder is secure and matches the picture |
| 2 | Connect the red wire from battery to the switch | Wire is snug in the terminal |
| 3 | Connect the switch to the LED using the green wire | All three parts are connected in a line |
| 4 | Place LED "eyes" into the head piece | LEDs are visible from the front |
| 5 | Flip the switch. Do the eyes light up? | ✅ Yes → DONE! ❌ No → Check wiring with reference card |

**Independence level:** Student can complete all 5 steps without teacher if materials are pre-staged.

### 🟡 Tier 2: "Design Your Light-Bot's Behavior"
**Goal:** Build a light-up robot AND program it to respond to something.

| Step | Task | Done When |
|------|------|-----------|
| 1 | Assemble base robot (same as Tier 1, steps 1–4) | LEDs light up |
| 2 | **CHOOSE:** Add a light sensor OR a button as input | Sensor/button is connected |
| 3 | Write code: IF [input] THEN [LED behavior] | Code compiles without errors |
| 4 | **CHOOSE:** LED blinks, fades, or changes color | Robot does what you programmed |
| 5 | Test 3 times. Adjust if needed. | Works reliably 3/3 times |

### 🔴 Tier 3: "Invent a Light Machine"
**Goal:** Design and build an original device that uses light in a creative way.

| Phase | Task | Check-In With Teacher |
|-------|------|----------------------|
| PLAN | Sketch your idea. What will it do? What parts do you need? | Show sketch → get materials approved |
| BUILD | Construct your device. | Mid-build check: "Is it coming together?" |
| CODE | Program the behavior. | When stuck for 10+ min |
| TEST | Does it work? What needs fixing? | Share test results |
| SHOW | Demonstrate to teacher (or group, if comfortable) | End of project |

## PART 7: WHAT TO TRACK (Keeping It Manageable)

You're one person. You don't need complex data collection. Track these three things — they'll tell you if the system is working:

### Simple Tracking Sheet (per session)

| Student | Started work within 5 min? (Y/N) | Completed at least 1 step? (Y/N) | Red card or behavioral incident? (Y/N + brief note) |
|---------|---|---|---|
| | | | |

**Over 4–6 sessions, you'll see patterns:**
- Student who never starts within 5 min → needs a different entry point or Tier adjustment
- Student who never completes a step → task card steps are too big, break them down further
- Student who never signals for help but doesn't progress → check-in strategy needed; scaffold the "asking for help" step itself

## PART 8: HONEST LIMITATIONS

What this framework **cannot promise** (based on current evidence):

1. **No controlled study has tested DI + PBL with EBD students in any setting**, let alone a robotics workshop. This framework is the best available synthesis, not a proven program.
2. **Some students will still have bad days.** No instructional design eliminates dysregulation. The goal is to reduce frequency and make recovery faster.
3. **This doesn't replace therapeutic support.** Students with severe trauma, OCD, or depression need counseling services alongside good instruction.
4. **The first 3–4 sessions will be rocky.** Students need time to learn the routine. Research on routines suggests 2–4 weeks for habituation (this is general behavioral research, not EBD-specific).
5. **You will need to adjust.** The task cards, the tiers, the routine — they're starting points. Your clinical and professional judgment about each student is irreplaceable.

The strongest evidence-based element here is the **routine + self-management + antecedent strategy** combination (Harrison et al., 2019, met evidence-based standards for ADHD). The weakest evidence base is the specific project tiering structure (reasonably inferred from DI research with general students, but untested with EBD). The overall approach — PBL with behavioral supports for EBD — has preliminary support from Taylor (2024) and Sciacca (2025) but needs more research.
