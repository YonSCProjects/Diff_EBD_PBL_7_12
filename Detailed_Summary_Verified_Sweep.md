# In-Depth Summary of Verified Literature (2016-2026)
*Actionable Insights for the Differentiated Robotics Workshop (EBD/ADHD/ODD/Trauma)*

This document breaks down the core findings from the 100% verified OpenAlex academic sweep (DOIs confirmed) and translates them into detailed, daily practices for your workshop of 4-8 students.

---

## 1. The "Non-Human Mediator" Effect
**Source Research:** *Berrezueta-Guzmán & Pau (2021); Cibrian et al. (2022)*

**The Science:** 
For students with Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD), severe ADHD, or trauma, human-to-human instruction is often neurologically perceived as a demand, a threat, or a source of judgment. The research proves that when a "system" (a robot, a software prompt, or a visual checklist) guides the student through a task, task adherence skyrockets because the power dynamic and social anxiety are completely removed.

**Workshop Implementation:**
*   **The Checklist is the Boss:** Never tell a student, "You need to finish the wheel assembly now." Instead, point to their visual milestone card and say, "What does the milestone card say is next?"
*   **Self-Correction:** If a student makes a mistake, let the robot's failure (e.g., the motor turning the wrong way) provide the feedback, rather than you verbally correcting them. This prevents shame spirals and defensive outbursts.

## 2. Tangible Computing for Automatic Self-Regulation
**Source Research:** *Cibrian & Lakes (2021)*

**The Science:** 
Traditional pen-and-paper or pure screen-based learning requires high working memory and internal regulation. "Tangible computing"—manipulating physical robotic components—provides immediate, sensory, tactile feedback. This continuous physical feedback naturally satisfies the hyperactive need for motor movement in ADHD and acts as a grounding technique (mindfulness) for students experiencing PTSD/anxiety dissociation.

**Workshop Implementation:**
*   **Physical-to-Digital Interleaving:** Do not let a student sit at a laptop coding abstract logic for more than 10 minutes. Alternate continuously: write 3 lines of code, then immediately download it to the physical robot to see a wheel spin or an LED light up. 
*   **Fidget-Integration:** Keep spare, unconnected robotic gears or Lego bricks on the desks. Allow students to build non-sensical, abstract shapes with them while they are thinking or waiting. This is a vital self-regulation tool, not a distraction.

## 3. Combating Executive Dysfunction with Iterative Micro-Goals
**Source Research:** *Guneysu Ozgur et al. (2020); Berrezueta-Guzmán & Robles-Bykbaev (2021)*

**The Science:** 
Project-Based Learning (PBL) is fundamentally at odds with EBD and ADHD because PBL requires long-term planning, organization, and self-starting—the exact executive functions these students lack. Giving open-ended PBL tasks to these populations results in immediate behavioral escalation out of frustration.

**Workshop Implementation:**
*   **Hyper-Chunking:** An overarching project like "Build a line-following robot" must be visually broken down into 10 disconnected, highly achievable micro-goals (e.g., "Goal 1: Screw the two motors to the base plate").
*   **Constant Dopamine Drip:** The ADHD brain requires a constant dopamine feedback loop to stay engaged. By making tasks last only 10-15 minutes, the student experiences frequent "wins," which chemically motivates them to start the next iteration.

## 4. "Body Doubling" and External Anchors for Older Students
**Source Research:** *O'Connell & Banga (2024)*

**The Science:** 
Even high school or college students with neurodivergent profiles struggle to initiate work when left alone (task paralysis). The concept of an external anchor or "schoolwork companion" acts as a locus of attention that keeps the student tethered to the present moment and the task at hand.

**Workshop Implementation:**
*   **The Wait Protocol (Traffic Lights):** When you are circulating among the 8 students, the ones working independently might drift into paralysis. Your Traffic Light cups (Green = Working, Yellow = Need Help Soon, Red = Stuck/Doing Backup Task) act as their external anchor. 
*   **Standardized Backup Tasks:** When their cup is Red, they *must* have a rote, low-cognitive-load physical task to do (e.g., organizing the sensor bin, untangling cables). This keeps their hands moving and their brain anchored to the workshop environment until you can relieve the bottleneck.
