# A Differentiated Project-Based Learning Program for Students with Emotional and Behavioural Challenges in the Robotics Workshop at Agourim School

## A research-grounded program for Agourim School, grades 7–12

*Version 1.0 — 2026-04-11.*
*Author: Yonatan Lev El (robotics and programming teacher, Agourim School), with research synthesis and documentation support from Claude.*

---

## Abstract

This document describes an eight-project Project-Based Learning program in a Differentiated-Instruction style, using Arduino and ESP32 physical computing, designed for individual students in grades 7–12 with emotional and behavioural challenges at Agourim School, a small special-education school in Israel. The program combines three ingredients: differentiated instruction with three readiness tiers per project (guided build, guided design with choice points, open design); hands-on physical computing progressing from a first-session LED circuit through a student-built tiny ESP32 quadcopter capstone; and Claude Code integrated in a dual-channel role as both a three-level pair-programming collaborator (upload / modify / dialogue) and a conversational scaffolded tutorial channel for students who benefit from hearing navigation-card instructions delivered as dialogue. The design rests on nine research-grounded principles distilled from 77 verified peer-reviewed sources spanning behaviour interventions for students with emotional and behavioural disorders, executive function scaffolding, trauma-aware practice, differentiated instruction in secondary settings, project-based learning, robotics in special education, and universal design for learning. The program's super-target is the creation of small, positive, empowering learning experiences for students whose confidence in school has been damaged by earlier educational settings that did not meet their needs. Every project is designed for an individual student working at their own workstation, with peer collaboration explicitly deferred and the teacher–student relationship established as the program's first-class relational mechanism. The methodology specifies a predictable session rhythm, a laminated navigation-card system, a refusal-of-order protocol, and a minimal three-instrument longitudinal measurement framework designed to be sustainable for a single teacher managing three to eight students. An honest-limitations section details the evidence gaps the program crosses openly — no randomized trial of the full combination exists, no research on aerial robotics for special-needs populations was located, and the Claude Code integration is the program's most evidence-thin component — and positions the program as a carefully-designed pilot rather than as an evidence-based intervention in the strict sense. A first-year budget of approximately USD 1,753–1,828 for an eight-student cohort is itemized, with year-two and steady-state annual costs projected at approximately one-third of that amount. The document is written to serve three audiences simultaneously: the workshop teacher, potential evaluating and funding organizations, and other teachers working with similar populations in workshop environments.

## Executive summary

**What this program is.** A research-grounded eight-project Arduino and ESP32 physical-computing workshop program for students in grades 7–12 with emotional and behavioural challenges at Agourim School, Israel. Projects progress from a first-session LED circuit through a build-from-scratch tiny quadcopter capstone. Every project is offered at three readiness tiers (guided build / guided design / open design) and is designed for an individual student working at their own workstation with teacher rotation rather than peer collaboration.

**Who it serves.** Eight students per cohort (three-to-eight is the sustainable range for a single teacher), grades 7–12, with emotional and behavioural presentations including ADHD, oppositional defiant disorder, anxiety, depression, obsessive-compulsive disorder, and post-trauma. Students whose earlier educational experiences did not work for them and whose confidence in learning needs to be rebuilt one small success at a time.

**What it produces.** Dozens of small, positive, empowering experiences per student per semester — moments in which *something the student did made the thing work*. Concrete technical skills: breadboarding, soldering (introduced at Project 4), sensor reading, motor control, Wi-Fi and I2C (at Project 6), integration (at Project 7), and sensor fusion plus PID control (at Project 8, for students who reach the capstone). Structured opportunities to practice executive function and help-seeking in a low-stakes physical-computing context. A lightweight longitudinal record of engagement, milestone completion, and behavioural patterns across the year.

**What makes it distinct.** The program combines three ingredients each independently supported by the verified research corpus — differentiated instruction, project-based learning, and AI-paired coding — in a combination the verified literature has not previously published for this population. The Claude Code integration is dual-channel: Channel A is the three-level pair-programmer role (pre-written sketch upload / modify with help / free dialogue), and Channel B is a novel scaffolded tutorial channel that delivers navigation-card content conversationally for students who benefit from voice-and-text delivery over printed reading. Project 8 is a pedagogical rewrite of an existing 8.5×20mm tiny-quadcopter project, re-chunked into 15-minute navigation cards with an expanded first-class safety protocol.

**The evidence base and its limits.** Nine design principles are each supported by verified peer-reviewed literature. The program also draws on the closest available empirical anchors: Taylor et al. (2024), the only PBL-in-EBD classroom study in the verified corpus, reporting engagement and teacher-satisfaction gains; Gersib & Mason (2023), a meta-analysis of behavior interventions in self-contained EBD settings with large overall effects and relational components as the strongest moderator; Gomez (2019), the closest analogue to an Arduino workshop in the verified literature, on makerspace outcomes for middle-school EBD students; and a broader literature on scaffolded executive function, ADHD motivation, differentiated instruction, trauma-aware practice, and universal design for learning. **The program is honest about its evidence gaps**: no randomized controlled trial of the full combination exists; no research on aerial robotics for any special-needs population was located despite a deliberate search; the Claude Code integration (both channels) has no direct empirical precedent for neurodivergent learners. The program is presented as a carefully-designed pilot grounded in verified adjacent research, not as an evidence-based intervention in the strict clinical sense.

**The year-one ask.** Approximately **USD 1,753–1,828** for a single cohort of eight students. This covers all hardware for all eight projects (including the tiny quadcopter capstone), shared tools and safety equipment, consumables, printed and laminated student materials, and teacher operational supplies. The budget is itemized in §8. Year-two and steady-state annual costs fall to approximately **USD 460–700** because most hardware is reusable across cohorts. Per-student cost in year one is approximately USD 220; in steady state, approximately USD 60–90.

**What the funder is NOT paying for.** External training workshops for the teacher (the materials are the training, per Colbert et al. 2025); external evaluators or research infrastructure; new physical space or computers; teacher release time beyond normal workshop hours; commercial curriculum licenses; scale-out to multiple schools in year one. A funder seeking randomized-control-trial evidence should fund a research study; this is a pilot.

**What success looks like.** Year-one success is modest and operational: the workshop runs the full year without teacher burnout; at least two thirds of students complete at least two projects at some tier and have at least one build they are proud enough to show a peer or family member; red-card incidents stable or declining; no safety incidents on Project 8 if Project 8 is attempted. If year one meets these criteria, the program continues into year two with light targeted revisions. If it does not, the program either substantially revises and re-pilots or stops and produces an honest write-up of what was tried and learned. The program commits to honest reporting of whichever outcome occurs.

**Why this program is worth funding.** Because the population it serves is under-served by commercial STEM curricula designed for mainstream students; because the design principles are grounded in verified research rather than in adult assumptions about what engages teenagers; because the teacher is sustainable (Principle 9 is a first-class design constraint, not an afterthought); because the honest-limitations discipline means a funder will know within one year whether the program is working and whether to continue; and because the super-target — *small, positive, empowering experiences that rebuild students' confidence with learning* — is a goal that matters for these students in a way that academic metrics often miss.

**How to engage with this document.** A reviewer in a hurry can read this Executive Summary plus §9 Honest Limitations to see both the offer and its boundaries. A reviewer with more time should read §1 (the three audiences and the problem), §4 (the nine principles and their evidence), §6 (what the students actually build), and §7 (what the program claims it will produce and how it will know). A teacher preparing to implement the program should read §5 (Methodology) and the per-project files in `Arduino_Projects/` in the order they plan to teach.

## §1 Introduction

### Who this document is for

This document serves three audiences, and each should be able to use it independently.

**First**, it is a **practical program guide for Yonatan Lev El** — the robotics and programming teacher at Agourim School — to run an eight-project Arduino workshop across three school years. Everything from the session rhythm to the per-project navigation cards is designed to be picked up and used, not just read. A teacher who has read §5 (Methodology) and the relevant chapter of §6 (Projects Overview) should be able to run a session without additional preparation.

**Second**, it is a **research-grounded proposal for potential evaluating and funding organizations**, describing a program that can be evaluated on its merits and supported with budget. The literature review, the honest limitations, the expected-outcomes framework, and the budget ask are all written with an evaluating reviewer in mind. Where the research base is strong, the document says so clearly; where the research base is weaker or where the program extends into territory without direct empirical support, the document says so with equal clarity. Academic integrity and the reviewer's trust both depend on this honesty.

**Third, and equally important**, this document is **a resource for other teachers working with similar populations in workshop environments** — colleagues at Agourim now, teachers at other special-education schools in Israel, and workshop educators elsewhere who face the same challenges. Every design decision in the program is explained, every citation is linked to a real paper, every tier and scaffold is documented in enough detail that a teacher encountering this work for the first time can adopt it, adapt it, and make it their own. The principles in §4 are not Agourim-specific; they are grounded in research on students with emotional and behavioural disorders generally, and they should transfer to other workshop settings with similar student populations. The eight projects in §6 are a working template — a teacher at a different school can substitute a project, reorder the sequence to match their available hardware, or keep the framework and change the content entirely. The hope is that this document becomes something other teachers reach for when designing their own differentiated workshop programs, not something locked to one school.

### Where this program came from

This program was born from two difficulties I cope with as the workshop teacher at Agourim.

The first is **the rotation difficulty.** In a robotics workshop with three to eight students, each working on a different project at a different pace, I found myself running between workstations and often failing to give each student the help they needed. By the time I reached one student, another had been waiting too long and had disengaged. The workshop was designed for individual work, but I as the teacher had become a bottleneck in the flow of the activity.

The second is **the overwhelm difficulty.** The workshop had a rich inventory of materials — Arduino boards, motors, sensors, chassis kits, cameras, soldering equipment, Lego robotics kits, computers, tools, and more — and students were free to choose what to build. I expected this freedom to be motivating. Instead, for many students, the large variety and the open-ended choice led to overwhelm, fear, and avoidance. Students who already struggled with decision-making and self-regulation froze in front of too many options. I understood that I needed to bring structure to the workshop: structure that would make the available equipment accessible rather than paralyzing, and that would allow students to work independently so that the teacher could support without becoming a bottleneck.

Everything in this program — the navigation cards, the three versions, the nine principles, the Claude Code integration, the session rhythm — is a response to these two difficulties. The research literature confirmed that the difficulties I observe in my workshop are well-documented in the academic study of students with emotional and behavioural challenges, and it provided the evidence base for the solutions the program implements.

### The problem this program addresses

The students at the center of this work share a difficult history: **mainstream primary school did not meet their needs**. They arrive at Agourim carrying a wide range of diagnoses — ADHD, oppositional defiant disorder, anxiety, depression, obsessive-compulsive disorder, and post-trauma among the more commonly seen — and an even wider range of strengths, backgrounds, and literacy levels. A few illustrative contrasts, drawn from a much longer list of dimensions along which our students differ: some read fluently while others can barely read or write; some come from supportive, engaged homes while others come from neglect; some are deeply introverted and guard their work carefully while others are extroverted and struggle with impulse control and social boundaries; some have experienced boycotts and harassment in previous school settings that shape how they trust peers and adults, while others carry quieter histories of being overlooked. These examples are illustrative, not exhaustive — no two students at Agourim look alike, and this program is designed to make room for that range.

What the students almost all share is a sense that **school has been a place where they fail, or where they are failed**. Rebuilding trust in learning — one small positive experience at a time — is the super-target of every design choice in this program.

Most of these students benefit from a learning environment that differs substantially from what most secondary curricula assume. They often need it to be **predictable** (routine protects executive function from stress collapse), **visibly scaffolded** (navigation cards and visual checklists guide work so the teacher is not a verbal bottleneck), **differentiated by readiness** rather than by labeled ability (so students with very different profiles can work on the same project and experience success at their own level), and **structured so a solo teacher** with three to eight students on as many different projects can rotate and support without becoming a single point of failure. Many of them need tasks that produce **tangible, immediate results** — something that lights up, moves, or responds to the world — because abstract academic wins have often not worked for them. And many benefit from **autonomy, choice, and voice**, because an approach that tells these students what to do and when to do it tends to trigger the same power-struggle dynamics that made primary school unbearable for many of them.

### The approach in one paragraph

This program is built from three ingredients that the research supports independently. In the research literature we reviewed, we did not find a published study that combined all three for this population — which is not to say no teacher has tried similar combinations in their own classroom, only that we could not locate such work in the verified corpus. **First, differentiated project-based learning:** projects are offered in three tiers — Tier 1 (guided build), Tier 2 (guided design with choice points), Tier 3 (open design) — so students with different readiness levels can work on the same project simultaneously and experience success at their own level, moving between tiers across projects without shame. **Second, hands-on Arduino physical computing:** students wire real circuits, see real hardware respond to their code, and build devices that do things in the world — a robot that follows a line, a car they can drive from their phone, a quadcopter they assembled themselves that actually flies. The physicality is not decoration; it is load-bearing for a population that has often not been served by abstract academic tasks. **Third, Claude Code as a differentiated coding support:** students engage with code at the level that is actually approachable for them today. For students who find coding — or even AI interaction itself — overwhelming, the starting point may simply be uploading a pre-written sketch that matches their wiring: the code just works, the hardware responds, and the student sees the result without having to write or debug anything. Students who are ready for more modify that code with Claude Code's help, making small targeted changes and seeing what happens. And students who are ready to direct the process describe what they are trying to build, show Claude Code their current attempt, and iterate in dialogue. Across all three levels, the AI and the pre-written code are scaffolds the student uses, not obstacles the student has to overcome alone. The eight projects are designed as a progression from a first-session LED circuit through the ESP32 tiny quadcopter capstone, with each project introducing skills that later projects build on. For many students, working through the order as designed is the path of least resistance. But the order is not a rigid gate: some students — particularly those whose school history includes many negative encounters with "do it in this sequence" — will want to skip ahead to a later project, work on projects in a different order, or start from one that happens to grab their interest that day. §5 describes how the program supports this without abandoning the underlying skill progression: prerequisites become explicit skills the student can see and acquire, not boxes to check in sequence, and a student's refusal to follow the prescribed order is treated as information about what they need, not as a failure to be corrected. Every project is built on the same nine research-grounded design principles documented in §4.

### What is novel about this work

In the research literature we reviewed, we did not find a published program that combines all three of these ingredients for students with emotional and behavioural disorders. That literature does contain:

- extensive evidence on behavior interventions, executive function scaffolding, and social-emotional learning;
- three small but directionally convergent empirical studies of project-based learning specifically implemented with students carrying EBD (or substantially overlapping) identifications: Taylor, Allen, Van & Moohr (2024) in the *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders* reporting very large on-task effect sizes (BC-SMD = 1.70 to 3.81) and marked teacher job-satisfaction gains in a four-student self-contained EBD classroom; Sciacca (2025), a doctoral dissertation at Wilkes University, reporting a collective case study of three Pennsylvania teachers whose findings converge on trust-based relationships, built-in PjBL adaptation, student agency and ownership, and the paradox that PjBL is "more work to prepare, less during the instruction, because the kids drive so much more of their own learning"; and Foster & Shriner (2023), a case study at a residential mental-health school serving students with adverse childhood experiences, reporting decreased negative behaviours and increased engagement, academic skills, and self-regulation during PjBL lessons;
- a theoretical bridge from Wertz & Mulcahy (2024) in *Preventing School Failure* systematically examining the alignment between PjBL elements and the 22 High-Leverage Practices for Students with Disabilities from the Council for Exceptional Children and CEEDAR Center, and identifying multiple explicit overlaps — including those related to collaborative learning, scaffolding, flexible grouping, active engagement, instructional technology, and peer/teacher feedback — which allows PjBL's evidence base for students with disabilities to borrow, carefully, from the much deeper HLP evidence base;
- the Buck Institute for Education's (2018) *Framework for High Quality Project Based Learning* as the canonical quality standard the program adopts (intellectual challenge and accomplishment, authenticity, public product, collaboration, project management, reflection), along with honest acknowledgment in §9 of where the program's individual-student framing falls short of the HQPBL criteria;
- evidence from robotics-in-special-education studies that hands-on technological interventions and robot-assisted supports sustain attention and task completion in children with ADHD by providing structured, non-threatening feedback (Berrezueta-Guzmán et al. 2021; Cibrian et al. 2022) — a pattern we describe informally as a "non-human mediator" effect, aggregating across studies rather than citing a unified synthesis of it;
- meta-analytic evidence for scaffolded STEM instruction (Belland et al. 2017: overall effect g = 0.46, with scaffolding producing directionally stronger effects in project-based learning contexts than in problem-based learning, and significantly larger effects for underperforming students than for traditional learners), differentiated instruction in secondary settings (Smale-Jacobse et al. 2019, d = 0.509–0.741), and universal design for learning (Almeqdad et al. 2023);
- a dissertation study of constructionist makerspace learning for middle-school EBD students (Gomez 2019) that is the closest direct analogue to the Arduino workshop in the verified literature.

But the same corpus contained **no published empirical research on Arduino or microcontroller-level physical computing for students with EBD, ADHD, or learning disabilities**. We also found **no research on aerial robotics, drones, or flight-STEM for any special-needs population** — we confirmed this by searching all 78 files in our full-text corpus for any mention of drones, quadcopters, UAVs, or flight, and found only one metaphorical hit in the CAST (2018) UDL guidelines. And we found **no research on AI-collaborative coding tools (such as Claude Code) for neurodivergent students**, likely because this class of tool is too new for a research literature to have developed around it. These absence-claims are scoped to the verified corpus we reviewed; we cannot rule out the existence of relevant work outside it.

This program therefore **stands on the shoulders of adjacent research** and extends it responsibly into new territory. §9 of this document, the Honest Limitations section, is dedicated to being explicit about which claims rest on direct empirical evidence and which are inferential extensions — because academic integrity, practical value, and reviewer trust all depend on knowing the difference.

### This is a first version — everything in it is a hypothesis

**Every design choice in this program is experimental and is expected to be adjusted based on actual in-vivo experience with the students it is built for.** The session structure, the nine design principles, the three-tier system, the Claude Code dual-channel integration, the eight-project sequence, the navigation card templates, the budget line items, the expected outcomes, the specific minute allocations in §5.2 — all of them are first-version choices grounded in a verified research corpus (§3) and inferential extensions where that corpus ran out (§9). None of them are settled. The program is a **living document**, not a frozen curriculum.

**What "adjustable in vivo" means operationally.** When the teacher runs a session and notices that a specific navigation card is too long, or a specific milestone is too ambitious for the cohort, or a specific Claude Code interaction pattern is not working the way the master document predicts, the teacher is explicitly authorized to revise the materials — at the session, at the week, or at the end of the semester — as long as the revision stays within the nine principles and the three-tier structure. The weekly data collector (see the Appendices) and the end-of-semester teacher reflection (§7.2) are the two mechanisms the program uses to capture these in-vivo adjustments so that they can be folded back into the master document and the per-project files for the next cohort.

**What this framing is NOT.** It is not a retreat from the specificity of the document. The navigation cards are specific, the milestones are specific, the session minute allocations are specific, the budget numbers are specific — because vague materials are harder to run than specific ones that turn out to need revision. The specificity is a **starting point** the teacher can improve against, not a frozen standard the teacher must defend. It is also not a hedge against accountability: the program commits to honest reporting of what worked and what didn't (§9.7 and §10.5), and the revision history of this document will itself be part of that honest reporting.

**Three layers of revision, three timescales.**

1. **Within-session revision** — the teacher notices a navigation card is confusing and verbally adjusts for the student in front of them. Not recorded in the document unless the pattern repeats.
2. **Weekly revision** — the teacher uses the weekly data collector (Appendices) to note any navigation card, milestone, reference card, or Claude Code interaction pattern that is consistently causing friction across the cohort. These get fed into a running list of "things to revise before next week's sessions" and are applied to the local workshop copy of the materials immediately.
3. **Between-cohort revision** — at the end of each semester, the teacher's reflection (§7.2) plus the weekly data collector digests become the basis for a revised master document and revised per-project files. The revision history is tracked in git commit history so a future reader can see how the program evolved across cohorts.

This layered discipline is directly grounded in §3.5 on Sciacca (2025), whose three participating teachers described PjBL as an instructional format they revised continuously based on what their specific students needed — and whose "more work to prepare, less during the instruction" paradox (§4 Principle 9) depends on treating the preparation as iterative rather than one-shot. The Arduino program inherits that discipline explicitly.

## §2 Population Profile — The Agourim Students

### The school and its role

Agourim is a small Israeli special-education school serving students in grades 7 through 12 who are coping with significant emotional and behavioural difficulties. Many of these students were not served well by mainstream primary education — and, for some, by the mainstream secondary schools they attended before arriving at Agourim. The school's role is academic and developmental. Students at Agourim also receive psychological and therapeutic support through separate channels; the robotics and programming workshop is a place where they learn skills, build things, and — if the program design works — begin to experience school as a place where effort can pay off.

A **school-wide token-economy behavioural program** is integrated across most aspects of daily school activity at Agourim and serves as the school's primary shared framework for positive behavior support. The research this program draws on actually supports this approach: Barker & de Lugt (2022) review empirical classroom studies of interventions for students with oppositional defiant disorder and report strong positive effects of token economies (for example, Rosenberg (1986) — included in Barker & de Lugt's evidence table — produced 82% M PND for on-task behavior and 91% M PND for reducing disruptive behavior in an ODD alternative classroom), and Morsink et al. (2022) review two studies (Carlson et al. 2000; Carlson & Tamm 2000) that explicitly tested whether rewards undermine intrinsic motivation in children with ADHD, with neither study finding that effect. The robotics and programming workshop operates within the school-wide token system rather than running parallel to it. Within the workshop session itself, internal recognition of student effort is additionally **social, immediate, and tangible** — a shared moment when a robot moves, a visible checkmark on a navigation card, a peer noticing the work — because hardware-based milestones are already inherently tangible and a small-group workshop setting has the capacity for immediate personal acknowledgment in a way larger classrooms may not. The within-workshop recognition mechanism coexists with the school's broader token economy, not in place of it. §4 Principle 5 and §5 Methodology return to both layers with more detail.

The workshop itself is a small-group setting. On any given session, three to eight students from the same mother class attend the workshop at the same time, each working on their own individual project. The teacher is a single adult managing all of them at once, which is why the design requirement of **student independence for at least 10–15 minutes at a time** is not a nice-to-have — it is the basic condition for the workshop to function at all. Almost every design choice in the rest of this document traces back, directly or indirectly, to that one structural fact.

### Heterogeneity is the first fact, not a complication

The thing a reader should know before anything else about the Agourim student body is that **heterogeneity is not a complication of the population; it is the population**. There is no "typical" Agourim student, and this program is designed to start from that heterogeneity rather than treat it as a later adjustment.

What does this heterogeneity look like in practice? §1 gave a short list of illustrative contrasts; §2 goes a little deeper. The examples that follow are still drawn from a much larger set of dimensions along which our students differ, and any description of the population — including this one — is a sketch, not a portrait.

**Diagnostic profiles vary widely, and many students carry more than one.** Among the more commonly seen:
- **ADHD**, with executive function challenges, attention regulation difficulties, and often motor restlessness. Harrison, Soares, Rudzinski & Johnson (2019) found in their Review of Educational Research meta-review that classroom-based interventions for ADHD are moderately effective overall, with self-management and instructional interventions meeting evidence-based standards — which matters because it tells us the intervention surface is the *design of the task and the student's relationship to it*, not the fixing of the student.
- **Oppositional defiant disorder (ODD)**, which Barker & de Lugt (2022) frame as *an interactional disorder* — symptoms arise "within a relationship between the child and another person, within a social context," not from fixed traits. Their review notes that children with ODD "rebel against any attempt to modify or influence their behavior, both positively (e.g., using rewards) or negatively (e.g., using punishments)," because the attempt itself is read as manipulation. This framing shapes how the workshop approaches every student, not only those formally diagnosed.
- **Anxiety** in many forms — generalized, social, school-specific, sometimes panic. Håland & Bertelsen (2025) document how widespread school-based anxiety accommodations are among teachers, and raise the concern that some accommodations can inadvertently reinforce avoidance; §4 Principle 5 and §5 Methodology return to this nuance.
- **Depression**, ranging from low motivation and energy on hard days to clinical depressive episodes. Werner-Seidler et al. (2021) meta-analysis of school-based depression and anxiety prevention programs shows that universal effects are small (g ≈ 0.18–0.21) and targeted effects somewhat larger — a reminder that general-purpose interventions alone are not sufficient.
- **Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)**, which Abramovitch et al. (2024) show produces functional impairment for approximately 90% of affected children and adolescents, with school being the most-affected domain (32–47% of children report academic impact). Colbert et al. (2025) show that school staff knowledge of OCD is limited (46–58% recognize it as a distinct disorder) and that brief written psychoeducation is more effective than long training workshops — both findings are directly relevant to how this program handles OCD-related accommodation.
- **Post-traumatic reactions** arising from a wide range of adverse experiences. Watson & Astor (2025) in their critical review of trauma-informed approaches in schools are explicit that the empirical base is weaker than the field's enthusiasm suggests, and that rigorous evidence concentrates on smaller, localized practices (daily check-ins, clear expectations, relationship-building) rather than whole-school framework adoption. §9 Honest Limitations returns to this caveat.

Many students carry two or three of these profiles at once, sometimes alongside learning disabilities or language differences. Some have formal diagnoses from specialists; some have patterns that everyone around them recognizes but that have not been formally diagnosed. The program responds to the student in front of the teacher, not to the label on a file — the diagnostic information in this profile is context, not a decision tree.

**Literacy and language skills vary widely.** Some of our students read fluently in Hebrew, English, or both; others can barely decode a short paragraph. Some write with ease; others find writing so effortful that they avoid it. This matters for the workshop because traditional curricular materials assume reading as the entry point — our materials cannot.

**Fine motor skills, sensory regulation, and tolerance for frustration vary.** Some students can solder a fine joint on the first attempt; others struggle with the physical precision of threading a wire through a breadboard hole. Some can tolerate a buzzing motor on their desk as background noise; others find it overwhelming.

**Social comfort varies.** Some students actively want group collaboration; some find any group dynamic exhausting and need solo workspace by default. Some arrive carrying histories of bullying or social rejection that shape how they trust peers; others are socially hungry and will work with anyone who lets them.

**Home context varies.** Some students come from supportive, highly engaged homes; others come from homes where educational expectations are low, absent, or actively antagonistic. Some can work on a project over a weekend with parental support; others cannot.

**Prior experience with technology varies.** Some students have been building Arduino projects at home for years and arrive at the workshop already fluent in corners of C++ the teacher has not yet needed. Others have never touched a breadboard and find the workshop's sensory environment — lights, beeps, buzzing motors, wire clippings on the floor — genuinely novel.

These examples — diagnosis, literacy, fine motor, social comfort, home context, tech exposure — are illustrative, not exhaustive. There are many more dimensions along which our students differ, and the program is designed to make room for that range.

### What many of our students share

Despite this heterogeneity, some threads run through the population and shape the program's core design commitments. None of what follows is universal — there are always exceptions — but the patterns are common enough that design choices built around them serve most students well.

**Many arrive at Agourim with damaged trust in school as an institution.** Their primary-school years were, for many, an accumulating sequence of experiences where they tried, were misunderstood, were disciplined for behaviors they could not control, or were quietly left behind. A reasonable conclusion from that history is that school is not a safe place to try hard. The program treats this mistrust as an accurate reading of lived experience, not as a character flaw.

**Capacity fluctuates day to day.** Cumming, Oblath, Qiu, Frazier and colleagues (2024), in their *Remedial and Special Education* 45(2) study of executive function, perceived stress, and academic performance in middle schoolers with and at risk for EBD, showed that perceived stress directly degrades executive function (working memory, cognitive flexibility), which in turn predicts academic performance (β ≈ .61 for language arts, β ≈ .65 for math). EF is not a stable trait in this population; it is a state that rises and falls with what is happening in the student's life outside the workshop. A student who was working independently at Tier 3 yesterday may arrive today unable to follow a two-step navigation card, not because they have regressed but because their stress budget is spent on something we cannot see. The program must accommodate this without drama — the same student is allowed to be at Tier 3 most of the time AND to drop to Tier 1 on a hard day, without shame and without being relabeled.

**Many are sensitive to perceived coercion.** For ODD-flavored students this sensitivity is most visible, but some version of it runs through much of the population: students with anxiety and trauma histories may read prescriptive, commanding instructions as threat; students with depression may read them as pressure they cannot meet; students with ADHD may read them as a mismatch with their own sense of what is worth attending to. Barker & de Lugt (2022) frame ODD as "best considered to be an interactional disorder" specifically because its symptoms arise in relationships and social contexts; the broader lesson is that the teacher's tone and stance — and the stance of the navigation cards, the stance of the AI, the stance of the posted routine — matter across the whole population.

**Many are also sensitive to shame.** OCD-related compulsions that would be private elsewhere can become visible in a workshop (a student who needs to recheck a wiring step eleven times, or who cannot begin a new step until the previous one feels "complete enough"). Anxiety-related difficulties with public performance can freeze a student at the moment of presenting their work. Depression can make any invitation to share feel like an exposure. Abramovitch et al. (2024) and Håland & Bertelsen (2025) each document, in their respective populations, how public visibility of symptoms can compound the underlying difficulty. The program's default for this reason is **private competence** — students can always opt out of public demonstration, and no design choice hinges on forcing a student to perform in front of peers.

**And many are hungry for real competence.** Despite — or because of — the experiences that damaged their trust in school, most of our students respond powerfully when they encounter a task they can actually do and where the evidence of success is tangible, visible, and belongs to them. A robot that moves because they wired and coded it is different in kind from a multiple-choice question they got right. Our super-target — *small, positive, empowering experiences* — rests on this hunger, and on the program's commitment to making those experiences genuinely earned.

### Fluctuating capacity is a design constraint, not a problem to solve

Because capacity fluctuates, the program cannot assign a student to a tier or a project and then hold them there. Student state changes across sessions, across weeks, and sometimes within a single session — a student can arrive engaged and drop into shutdown after a twenty-minute phone call with a parent, and the workshop design has to absorb that without making the drop feel like a failure. The program's response is to treat tier and project assignment as **fluid**: students move between tiers of the same project, or between projects entirely, as their state changes, without that movement being treated as regression or promotion. §5 Methodology describes the fluid-assignment logic in more detail. The important thing to understand here is that any statement of the form "Student A is at Tier 2" is a snapshot, not a fixed identity.

### What this profile does NOT tell you

This profile is descriptive, not prescriptive, and it has two important limits.

**First, diagnostic categories are imperfect**. Two students with the same diagnosis may present very differently, and two students with very different diagnoses may need very similar supports on a given day. The program does not match intervention-to-diagnosis; it matches intervention-to-current-state, as read by the teacher in real time and adjusted across the session. The diagnostic information in this profile is a shared vocabulary for discussing the students with other teachers and with reviewers — it is not an instruction manual.

**Second, no profile can substitute for knowing your students.** The teacher's firsthand knowledge of each student — what motivates them, what they are working on outside the workshop, what their week has been like, what their family is dealing with, what small things have gone well for them recently — is irreplaceable. Nothing in this document, and nothing in the research literature it rests on, can replace that knowledge. This profile is a starting point and a common language; it is not a replacement for the teacher's eye.

### How this profile shapes the rest of the document

The population profile is the reason for almost every design choice that follows. Principle 1 (predictable routine) exists because capacity fluctuates with stress. Principle 2 (the navigation card is the boss) exists because direct verbal instruction tends to be read as coercion across much of the population. Principle 3 (physical-first, then abstract) exists because literacy and tolerance for abstract tasks vary widely. Principle 5 (structured autonomy, across projects as well as within them) exists because many of our students have learned, rationally, that prescribed compliance is not safe for them. Principle 7 (Claude Code as a differentiated coding support with three access levels — including pre-written code upload for students who find AI interaction overwhelming) exists because the AI itself can be a barrier for some students. Principle 8 (relationships are the multiplier) exists because trust in adults is the load-bearing prerequisite for everything else in the workshop. The full research rationale follows in §3; the principles themselves are presented in §4.

### §3.1 EBD population and evidence-based classroom interventions

Students identified with emotional and behavioural disorders (EBD) are at elevated risk for a range of adverse outcomes — poor academic achievement, low high-school graduation rates, weak post-secondary transitions, and fractured social functioning. Mitchell, Kern & Conroy (2019), summarising the state of the field in *Behavioral Disorders*, describe EBD as a category where "students who experience ED are at greater risk for a host of negative long-term outcomes that include poor social, behavioral, and academic achievement in school, which in turn may impact graduation and postsecondary outcomes." Their field recommendations are structural: revise IDEA terminology to an education-based definition, institute universal screening for social-emotional-behavioural concerns, build multi-tiered systems of support (MTSS) that offer preventive intervention before problems consolidate, and integrate service delivery so students have access to counselling, health, and academic supports in a coordinated way. The program described in this document does not implement Mitchell et al.'s field-wide recommendations — a workshop is not a school system — but the principle that **preventive, layered supports outperform reactive, single-layer interventions** is load-bearing for every design decision that follows.

Inclusion of EBD students in general-education settings is supported by policy but the empirical literature is fragmented. Harrison, Soares & Joyce (2019), in their scoping review in the *International Journal of Inclusive Education*, map six themes in the literature — professional experience, strategy effectiveness, policy, influencing factors, teacher training, and teacher knowledge — and identify substantial gaps between policy intent and practice. One practical finding is directly relevant to the program's design: Gage, Kramer II & Ellis (2021), analysing school-climate data from over 350,000 high-school students in Georgia (including more than 5,000 students with or at risk for EBD), found that EBD students reported significantly more negative perceptions of school climate than peers across every measured dimension, **but** EBD students with Individual Education Plan (IEP) services reported more positive climates than EBD students without IEP services in several domains. The mechanism the authors suggest is that formal, visible adult support improves students' sense of belonging — an empirical grounding for the workshop's commitment to relationships as the first-class infrastructure on which everything else rests (§4 Principle 8).

Two bodies of evidence address what actually works in the classroom for this population. For ADHD specifically, Harrison, Soares, Rudzinski & Johnson (2019), in their *Review of Educational Research* meta-review of single-case classroom-based interventions, identified **instructional and self-management interventions** as the two categories meeting What Works Clearinghouse evidence-based standards. Behavioral interventions were also effective, particularly when selected through functional behavior analysis, though more effective when implemented by researchers than by teachers — a reminder that implementation fidelity shapes outcomes as much as the intervention choice itself. Classroom-based interventions for ADHD were, overall, moderately effective.

For self-contained EBD classrooms, Gersib & Mason (2023) conducted a meta-analysis in *Behavioral Disorders* across studies reporting behavior interventions and found a large overall effect size (**g = 0.93, SE = 0.16**). Their moderator analysis is particularly relevant: interventions that included **relational components** — daily parent communication, peer support, adult mentoring — produced significantly stronger effects than interventions without those components (moderator **B = 1.26, SE = 0.15, p < .05**). The practical reading is that *structure alone is not enough*; structure combined with relational supports multiplies the effect. The program's §4 Principle 8 (relationships as the multiplier) rests directly on this finding.

Riden, Kumm & Maggin (2022) conducted a mega-review of 50 systematic reviews and meta-analyses of 20 classroom and behavior management practices for students with or at risk for EBD, identifying eight practices with sufficient evidence: Check-In/Check-Out (CICO), functional behavior assessment-based interventions, group contingency, peer-mediated interventions, self-management, self-regulated strategy development for writing, **token economies**, and video modeling. The list is notable in two ways. First, it spans a range of intervention intensities, supporting the MTSS-style layered model. Second, it includes token economies as an evidence-based practice for EBD — a finding corroborated by Barker & de Lugt (2022) for ODD specifically, and directly relevant to the program's §2 discussion of how the workshop operates within Agourim's school-wide token-economy framework.

Oppositional defiant disorder deserves its own treatment because ODD is the diagnosis most closely tied to the program's "structured autonomy, not forced compliance" principle (§4 Principle 5). Barker & de Lugt (2022), in a review for the *International Journal of Special Education*, frame ODD as fundamentally **an interactional disorder** — symptoms arise within relationships and social contexts, not from fixed traits. They quote Barkley & Benton (2013) as theoretical framing on why direct behavior-modification attempts can backfire ("children with ODD rebel against any attempt to modify or influence their behavior, both positively (e.g., using rewards) or negatively (e.g., using punishments)") — but it is important to note that Barker & de Lugt present this framing as background, not as their own conclusion. Their own empirical review reports that classroom-based interventions — including token economies, group contingencies, and self-monitoring — produce strong positive effects for students with ODD. Rosenberg (1986), included in Barker & de Lugt's evidence table, showed a token economy with rule reminders producing **M PND = 82% for on-task behavior** and **M PND = 91% for reducing disruptive behavior** in an alternative ODD classroom. The theoretical concern about rewards being read as manipulation is real; the empirical evidence for well-structured classroom interventions is also real. Both can be true simultaneously, and the program's design tries to respect both (§4 Principle 5).

OCD is less represented in the EBD literature than ADHD or ODD but is functionally important for the Agourim student population. Abramovitch, Duncan, Schuyler & Geller (2024), in *Journal of Affective Disorders*, report that approximately **90% of children with OCD experience impairment in at least one domain**, with school as the most-affected domain — academic impact is reported by 32–47% of children and parents. Colbert, Powell, Weighall & Gibson-Miller (2025), in *Neurodiversity*, conducted a systematic narrative review of school-staff knowledge of OCD and interventions to improve it; they found that baseline staff knowledge is limited (approximately 46–58% recognition of OCD as a distinct disorder) and that **brief written psychoeducational materials outperform longer workshop formats** for improving staff knowledge. Neither paper prescribes specific workshop design rules for students with OCD — the workshop's choices (private workspace by default, no forced public demo, navigation cards with clear "done when" criteria) are the program's *inference* from the documented burden, not a direct Abramovitch or Colbert prescription.

Finally, the research base on teacher well-being in EBD settings matters for program sustainability. Gilmour, Sandilos, Pilny, Schwartz & Wehby (2022), in *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders*, used latent profile analysis to identify three teacher profiles (flourishing, buffered, struggling) among teachers of students with EBD and reported that burnout profile was meaningfully related to observed classroom management quality. The specific directional detail that an earlier version of this program's principle distillation attributed to Gilmour — that struggling-profile teachers used fewer effective strategies and gave students less autonomy — appears in parts of the paper we could not fully verify at the abstract level, and so §9 Honest Limitations flags this particular sub-claim as partially verified. What is clearly established by the abstract is the higher-order finding that **teacher burnout profile and classroom management quality are linked** — and the practical implication for program design is direct: the workshop must be built so the teacher can run it without burning out, because teacher exhaustion compounds downstream into the students' experience of the workshop. §4 Principle 9 and §8 Budget both carry this constraint.

**Implications for design.** §3.1 supports Principle 8 (relationships as the multiplier — Gersib & Mason 2023), Principle 9 (teacher sustainability as a first-class constraint — Gilmour 2022), and Principle 5 (structured autonomy — Barker & de Lugt 2022's nuanced position on ODD interventions, plus the Riden 2022 mega-review showing token economies as evidence-based for EBD). It also supports the program's commitment to layered preventive supports (Mitchell 2019) and to formal, visible support infrastructure (Gage 2021).

### §3.2 Executive function, perceived stress, and scaffolding

Executive function (EF) — the set of neurocognitive processes that allow goal-directed behavior, including working memory, cognitive flexibility, and inhibitory control — is central to this program's design for one specific reason: **EF in students with or at risk for EBD is not a stable trait but a state that rises and falls with stress**, and the workshop has to be built to protect it rather than to expect it.

The most direct evidence comes from Cumming and colleagues, who published two related studies in *Remedial and Special Education* on the relationship between perceived stress, executive function, and academic outcomes in middle-school students with and at risk for EBD. In their 2024 study of executive function, perceived stress, and academic performance (Cumming, Oblath, Qiu, Frazier, Zelazo, Flores & Park, 2024, *Remedial and Special Education*, 45(2), 85–100), the authors report that students with or at risk for EBD score significantly lower on EF measures (working memory, cognitive flexibility) than typical peers. Perceived stress is directly and negatively related to EF, and EF in turn mediates academic outcomes — for all students in the sample, higher EF predicted better academic functioning (**β ≈ .61 for language arts, β ≈ .65 for math**, both p < .001). The mediation chain is: stress degrades EF, and degraded EF degrades academic performance. The related 2024 paper by the same research group on stress regulation and emotional and behavioral well-being (Cumming, Qiu, Oblath, Frazier, Criado, Theodore & Placido, 2024, *Remedial and Special Education*, 46(6), 426–441) extends this framing.

The design implication is that *any program for this population that assumes stable cognitive capacity will fail on the days when the student's stress budget is spent on something outside the workshop*. Students arrive with EF capacity that changes day-to-day, and a program designed around yesterday's performance will be wrong about today's. The workshop's fluid tier assignment (§5 Methodology) and predictable session routine (§4 Principle 1) are both responses to this fact.

External cognitive scaffolds are the program's way of handling EF deficits without requiring EF to be present. Rosen, Boyle, Cariss & Forchelli (2014), in *Learning Disabilities: A Multidisciplinary Journal*, describe a strategic two-column note-taking intervention for middle-school students with learning disabilities that acted as an external cognitive scaffold — *"opening new pathways for struggling students to access executive functions and flexibly deploy cognitive strategies."* The critical finding for program design is that **students do not need intact EF to use an external scaffold**; they can practice EF *through* the scaffold. This inverts the usual teaching sequence, where students are expected to master a skill before being trusted to use it. In the Rosen et al. framing, the scaffold is the training environment — the student develops EF by using the scaffold, not by graduating beyond it. The workshop's navigation cards (§4 Principle 2) operationalise this principle directly.

Belland, Walker, Kim & Lefler (2017), in *Review of Educational Research*, conducted a meta-analysis of computer-based scaffolding in STEM across 144 studies and reported an overall effect size of **g = 0.46**, with scaffolding producing directionally stronger effects in project-based learning contexts than in problem-based learning (**z = 6.08, p < .01**) and significantly larger effects for **underperforming students** than for traditional learners (**z = 2.29, p < .05**). Their analysis of effective scaffold types includes physical/material supports alongside peer collaboration and teacher modeling. The practical reading of Belland et al. is twofold: scaffolding works especially well in PBL, which is the program's chosen instructional frame; and persistent scaffolding works — the paper does not require that scaffolds be faded, which releases the program from the pressure to treat scaffold removal as a measure of success.

Self-management is the specific intervention category within EF scaffolding that has the strongest classroom evidence base for this population. Del Vecchio, Crosland & Blair (2025), in *Journal of Positive Behavior Interventions*, tested a technology-based self-monitoring intervention (the I-Connect app) combined with reinforcement in three upper-elementary students with or at risk for EBD. Results showed significant increases in on-task behavior for all three participants, with 100% treatment integrity and strong social validity. The practical implication: digital self-monitoring tools are feasible in classroom settings, and combining them with structured reinforcement produces measurable engagement gains. Moohr, Balint-Langel, Taylor & Rizzo (2021), in *Beyond Behavior*, describe three categories of self-regulation strategy for EBD students (self-regulated strategy development, self-monitoring, strategy instruction) and note that 64% of these interventions in their review are delivered in 1:1 or small-group settings — a format the Agourim workshop matches exactly.

Visual schedules are a specific low-cost, high-leverage scaffold worth calling out. Thomas & Karuppali (2022), in *Journal of the Korean Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry*, conducted a systematic review of visual activity schedules (VAS) for children with ADHD aged 5–12 and found that VAS interventions reduced latency to initiate tasks, reduced off-task behavior during transitions, and improved on-task behavior. The mechanism is external cueing: the picture or text tells the student what comes next without the teacher having to say it, which bypasses both verbal working memory load and teacher-student power dynamics. Task cards in the workshop are, among other things, visual activity schedules at the within-task level.

Finally — and this is one of the program's most quantitatively striking findings — physical exercise is among the most effective non-pharmacological EF interventions for ADHD, with effect sizes that exceed cognitive training and EF-specific curricula. Qiu, Liang, Wang, Zhang & Shum (2023), in *Asian Journal of Psychiatry*, conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of 67 studies of non-pharmacological EF interventions for children and adolescents with ADHD. Their headline finding: across six intervention categories (cognitive training, EF-specific curriculum, game-based training, mindfulness, neurofeedback, physical exercise), physical exercise produced **g = 0.900 on inhibitory control** and **g = 1.377 on cognitive flexibility** — the largest effect sizes of any category measured. For comparison, cognitive training produced g = 0.907 on working memory and EF-specific curriculum produced g = 0.532 on planning. The practical implication is unambiguous: *movement breaks are not an optional extra in a workshop for students with ADHD; they are a first-class EF intervention with the largest effect sizes the literature has measured for exactly the deficits this population has*. §4 Principle 6 (movement is medicine) and §5 Methodology's 3–5 minute movement break between work blocks both rest directly on this finding.

**Implications for design.** §3.2 supports Principle 1 (predictable routine protects EF from stress collapse — Cumming 2024), Principle 2 (navigation card as external scaffold — Rosen 2014), Principle 3 (physical-first CRA — Belland 2017a scaffolding meta-analysis with corrected numbers), Principle 4 (hyper-chunked milestones and visible wins — scaffold persistence per Belland), and Principle 6 (movement is medicine — Qiu 2023). It also informs the workshop's specific commitment to self-monitoring and visual schedules (Thomas & Karuppali 2022, Del Vecchio 2025, Moohr 2021).

### §3.3 Trauma-informed schools — with a first-class caveat

Trauma-informed approaches in schools have become a dominant framework in the last decade, and there are strong theoretical reasons to adopt them for students whose school experiences include early adversity, disrupted attachment, or explicit traumatic events. But the empirical base is weaker than the field's enthusiasm might suggest, and this section presents the honest picture rather than the cheerleading version.

Watson & Astor (2025), in *Review of Education*, published a critical review of empirical support for trauma-informed approaches in schools. **This paper is cited here deliberately and carefully.** Their actual conclusion is more nuanced than the popular reading of trauma-informed literature. They endorse the four core components of trauma-informed practice — **understanding trauma, safety, strengths-based approaches, and supportive relationships** — as grounded in convergent evidence, but they specifically caution that *the empirical evidence base for whole-school organisational trauma-informed implementation is more limited than often claimed in the field*. They call for universal inclusion, safety, strengths-based practice, and relationships, and they ask the field to treat rigorous empirical evaluation of whole-school adoption as an open research question. The distinction between "the four components have good convergent support" and "whole-school framework adoption has robust RCT evidence" is the distinction this program takes seriously. The workshop is designed around the four components (safety through predictable routine, relationships through small-group rotation, strengths-based work through tangible competence, and staff understanding of trauma through the §5 Methodology description) without claiming to be a whole-school trauma-informed framework.

Koslouski, Stark & Chafouleas (2023), in *Social and Emotional Learning: Research, Practice, and Policy*, published a primer for educators on understanding and responding to the effects of trauma in the classroom. They describe the neurobiological reality of chronic trauma: under persistent unpredictability or threat, the brain's survival-mode response (fight, flight, or freeze) activates, and this activation disrupts executive function in exactly the areas this program is trying to protect. Their practical framing is that *trauma-informed care is beneficial for all students regardless of trauma history* — because the practices that help students with trauma histories (predictability, relationship-based safety, strengths-based framing, co-regulation) are also good practices for everyone. Koslouski et al. also specifically address secondary traumatic stress in educators, noting that educator well-being is "crucial to remaining healthy and effective" when working with traumatized students. This cluster of findings is the direct empirical basis for the program's Principle 1 (predictable routine) and its commitment to treating teacher sustainability as a first-class constraint (Principle 9).

Asselman, Offerman, Koomen, Bolling, Hoeve & Bosman (2025), in *International Journal of School & Educational Psychology*, examined the relationship between adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) and internalizing/externalizing problems in students with emotional and behavioral disorders, with PTSD symptoms and executive functioning as mediators. Their findings support the mediation model that runs through much of §3.2: adversity → PTSD symptoms → reduced EF → behavioral and emotional outcomes. The paper emphasizes the importance of trauma-informed environments that provide relational safety and co-regulation, reinforcing the same core components Watson & Astor endorse and Koslouski et al. describe.

Two scoping reviews round out the picture. Stratford, Cook, Hanneke, Katz, Seok, Steed, Fulks, Lessans & Temkin (2020), in *School Mental Health*, synthesised the range of intervention approaches being tested in schools — from individual counselling to whole-school framework adoption — and noted that rigorous studies are concentrated in Tier 2 and Tier 3 clinical interventions while universal (Tier 1) whole-school approaches have less rigorous empirical evaluation. Berger (2019), in the same journal, conducted a systematic review of multi-tiered approaches to trauma-informed care in schools and reported that multi-tiered models are theoretically well-grounded but that implementation fidelity varies widely and long-term outcome data are scarce. Beloyianni & Touloumakos (2024), also in *School Mental Health*, published a scoping review of childhood post-traumatic stress and school-related behaviors that synthesises the evidence for how PTSD manifests in school settings — internalizing and externalizing symptoms, EF difficulties, relationship ruptures — and calls for more longitudinal and mechanistic research. All three reviews are consistent with Watson & Astor's caution: the theoretical grounding is solid, the core components have convergent support, but the whole-school, organisational-level research base is not yet strong enough to justify uncritical cheerleading.

**The practical upshot for the program.** The workshop implements the four core trauma-informed components (safety through predictable routine, relationships through small-group teacher rotation, strengths-based framing through tangible hardware success, and staff understanding of trauma through the §5 Methodology description) without claiming to be a whole-school trauma-informed intervention. The claim the program makes, and that §9 Honest Limitations restates, is that *the four components are well-grounded in convergent evidence; the whole-school framework-level evidence is weaker; and the program's contribution is at the workshop level, not the school level*. This is the honest version, and it is the version that can defend itself to a reviewer who reads Watson & Astor's critical review.

**Implications for design.** §3.3 supports Principle 1 (predictable routine — Koslouski 2023, Asselman 2025, Watson & Astor 2025), Principle 5's anxiety-with-trauma sub-rule (safety and predictability first, no forced exposure — Watson & Astor 2025, Koslouski 2023, Asselman 2025), Principle 8 (relationships as the multiplier, including adult relationships as trauma-informed co-regulation — Koslouski 2023, Asselman 2025), and Principle 9 (teacher sustainability as a first-class constraint, drawing on Koslouski 2023's secondary-traumatic-stress framing alongside Gilmour 2022's burnout findings). §3.3 is also the direct source material for §9 Honest Limitations' treatment of trauma-informed approaches.

### §3.4 Differentiated instruction in secondary settings

Differentiated instruction (DI) is the pedagogical approach of proactively adapting content, process, product, learning environment, or learning time to meet students' diverse learning needs — adjusted based on ongoing assessment of each student's readiness, interest, or learning profile. It is the pedagogical frame the workshop rests on, and it has a specific and honest evidence base in secondary education.

Smale-Jacobse, Meijer, Helms-Lorenz & Maulana (2019), in a systematic review published in *Frontiers in Psychology*, synthesised the empirical literature on within-class differentiated instruction in secondary education from 2006 to 2016. After a rigorous search and selection process, only **fourteen papers about twelve unique empirical studies** met their inclusion criteria — an important finding in its own right: despite the prominence of DI in educational discourse, the empirical base in secondary settings is small. Of the twelve studies that met the bar, most showed small-to-moderate positive effects on student achievement. The authors report that **summarised effect sizes across studies range from *d* = +0.509 to +0.741** (omitting an outlier). The studies span a variety of DI operationalisations — teacher trainings for DI, ability grouping and tiering, individualisation, mastery learning, heterogeneous grouping, and remediation in flipped classroom lessons — and the authors explicitly note that the operationalisation heterogeneity makes cross-study comparison difficult.

What Smale-Jacobse et al. do not do is identify a specific mechanism by which DI produces its effects. Their review is descriptive: DI is *associated with* small-to-moderate achievement gains across a small set of secondary studies, but the review does not claim to explain *why* or to endorse a specific causal pathway. The paper concludes with an explicit call for more research: *"These empirical findings give some indication of the possible benefits of differentiated instruction. However, they also point out that there are still severe knowledge gaps. More research is needed before drawing convincing conclusions regarding the effectiveness and value of different approaches to differentiated instruction for secondary school classes."* This honest limitation is load-bearing for how the program presents DI — as a pedagogically coherent approach with directional empirical support in secondary settings, not as a settled intervention with unambiguous evidence.

Tiering by readiness is one specific DI strategy that shows up across studies. The framing the program adopts is that **tiering is not tracking** — students are not assigned fixed ability labels; they are offered the same project at three entry points and move between tiers across projects and even within a single session based on their current state. This framing is consistent with Smale-Jacobse et al.'s description of within-class differentiation as fluid adaptation rather than between-class grouping, though again the direct empirical evidence for this specific fluid-tiering model in a workshop setting is thin. The program cites Smale-Jacobse et al. to support the broader claim that DI has meaningful effects in secondary settings, and scopes the tiering-specific claims as a responsible inference from that broader base.

Two additional papers bear on DI for students with ADHD specifically. Gibbs (2023), in *Emotional and Behavioural Difficulties*, reports on a small-scale qualitative study of Australian teachers and school leaders using DI as responsive teaching for students with ADHD. Teachers in the study used DI effectively to adapt content and process and to maintain safe, secure classroom climates, but they did **not** adjust assessments for students with ADHD — a gap the author identifies as needing further teacher-education work. The practical implication for the program is that differentiation must extend through assessment (not just through content and process), and the workshop's acknowledgement that Tier 1 and Tier 3 projects should be judged against different "done when" criteria reflects this. Ziernwald, Hillmayr & Holzberger (2022), in *Journal of Advanced Academics*, reviewed DI practices for high-achieving students in mixed-ability classrooms along readiness, interest, and learning-profile axes. Their review supports the broader framing that the three dimensions of differentiation (readiness, interest, profile) all matter, and that DI need not be solely remedial — it can also extend and enrich students who arrive at the workshop already fluent in parts of the content.

**Implications for design.** §3.4 supports the tiering structure in §4 Principle 5 (structured autonomy, not forced compliance — three tiers per project, fluid movement between them) and the broader commitment to adapting process, product, and assessment to student readiness. The honest caveat that the empirical base in secondary settings is small (twelve unique studies in Smale-Jacobse et al.) is carried forward to §9 Honest Limitations.

### §3.5 Project-based learning for diverse learners

Project-based learning (PBL) is the instructional format the program is built around. PBL engages students in extended, student-centered investigations to answer a challenging question or solve a real problem, and its strongest feature for students whose school history has been difficult is that it offers **multiple means of engagement, representation, and expression** — consistent with the universal design for learning framework covered in §3.7 — and gives students meaningful voice and choice over their work.

The most directly relevant quantitative study in the verified literature is **Taylor, Allen, Van & Moohr (2024)**, published in *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders*. It is the single empirical single-case classroom study we located of PBL specifically implemented with students carrying a formal EBD identification, and its findings are striking. The authors used a multiple-baseline-across-participants single-case design with four middle-school students with EBD in an emotional/behavioral support classroom. The PBL intervention followed the Buck Institute for Education's High Quality PBL (HQPBL) framework's eight essential elements: Knowledge/Understanding/Skill, Challenge/Problem/Question, Sustained Inquiry, Authenticity, Student Voice, Reflection, Critique & Revision, and Public Product.

**On-task behavior results.** Between-case standardised mean difference (BC-SMD), analogous to Cohen's *d* and using a 0.8 threshold for a large effect, **met the criteria for a large effect** with a 95% confidence interval of **[1.70, 3.81]** and a standard error of 0.5085. Individual effect sizes for the four students were Eric = 4.70, Stanley = 7.42, Kenneth = 3.89, Kyle = 3.81 — every student showed a large positive response. Level changes in on-task behavior ranged from approximately +32 to +39 percentage points across the four students, with 96–100% variability resolution and near-complete overlap reduction.

**Teacher and paraprofessional job satisfaction.** Pre-intervention, both the teacher and paraprofessional indicated low job satisfaction on a modified Teacher Job Satisfaction Survey; at the end of the PBL unit, both reported marked improvements. The teacher's positive-question ratings moved from pre-intervention M = 3 to post-intervention M = 1.66, and the paraprofessional's from M = 4.33 to M = 2 (on a 1–5 scale where lower means more positive). The authors interpret this as preliminary evidence that PBL may serve as a protective factor against teacher burnout in EBD settings, because the authentic, student-centered nature of PBL shifts the teacher from crisis management to facilitation.

**Limitations the authors themselves acknowledge.** The paper explicitly names its limitations: single-case design limits generalisability, small sample (n = 4), limited information on fidelity across all eight HQPBL elements, reliance on a brief job-satisfaction measure rather than a validated burnout instrument, and the study was conducted in a single classroom with a single teacher and paraprofessional. These limitations matter for how the program presents Taylor et al. — the effect sizes are genuinely large, and they are the best direct evidence the program has for PBL-in-EBD classroom use, but they come from one small study and cannot carry the weight of a settled evidence base by themselves. §9 Honest Limitations restates this caveat.

The broader PBL literature adds nuance to this picture. Culclasure, Longest & Terry (2019), in *Interdisciplinary Journal of Problem-Based Learning*, published a multi-school study of PBL implementation in three Southeastern U.S. public schools across two academic years. Their finding is worth quoting honestly: *"Findings did not reveal consistent significant differences in the performances of PjBL and non-PjBL demographically matched students on academic and behavioral outcomes. PjBL students did, however, perform better on inventories of social-emotional skills."* In other words, PBL did NOT produce significant academic or behavioral outcome differences in that study — but it DID produce better social-emotional outcomes, and both students and educators reported positive perceptions of PBL's impact. This nuanced finding is important for the program's honesty: PBL is not a settled silver bullet for academic outcomes, but it has specific benefits for social-emotional development that align with the program's super-target of small positive empowering experiences.

Boardman & Coleman (2025), in *Journal of Adolescent & Adult Literacy*, published a mixed-methods study of scaffolding in project-based learning across five inclusive 9th-grade English language arts classes in an urban high school. They used a universal design for learning frame to create the PBL unit and proactively reduced potential barriers. Their practical recommendations for scaffolding in PBL are directly applicable to the workshop: **use a small number of scaffolded materials that connect activities and can be adjusted throughout the unit**, **attend to interactional scaffolds such as peer collaboration**, and **address students' diverse abilities and language backgrounds**.

**Sciacca (2025)**, a doctoral dissertation at Wilkes University, is the closest direct qualitative counterpart to Taylor et al. (2024) and deserves an expanded treatment here because its methodology — direct teacher voice from the PjBL-in-EBD context — provides a kind of evidence the quantitative literature does not. Sciacca conducted a qualitative collective case study of three teachers using project-based learning with students in grades 6–12 identified with emotional disturbance: a special educator at a military academy who supports content teachers with modifications and adaptations (M.G.), an eighth-grade STEM teacher in an inclusive general-education setting (B.Q.), and a former special educator in self-contained emotional support classrooms at a Pennsylvania intermediate unit (S.F.). Data sources were participant interviews and classroom artifacts (lesson plans, student work). The theoretical framework was the Council for Exceptional Children and CEEDAR Center's 22 High-Leverage Practices (HLPs) for Students with Disabilities combined with Culturally Inclusive Pedagogies and Practices (CIPP).

Three themes emerged from Sciacca's data, each of which maps directly onto principles this program commits to. **First, supporting student learning rests on building trusting relationships and on adaptation strategies that are partly built into PjBL itself.** All three of Sciacca's participants identified rapport and trust with students as the foundation on which PjBL works. M.G. described it as "more about the rapport with the students — it's crucial for success." B.Q. emphasised "really getting that rapport with students and getting to know them and know how far you can push them." S.F. reflected on former students calling in to check in after leaving the classroom, noting the lasting value of the relationship. Two of the three participants also explicitly said adaptation is not bolted on to PjBL but already present in the approach itself — S.F.'s phrase was *"I do think project-based learning kind of has its own adaptations built-in,"* while M.G. called it *"actually scaffolding."* M.G. also described scaling the complexity of an assignment from a content teacher to match a student's developmental level, and B.Q. described starting lessons "where they're at." This is the exact discipline §4 Principle 2 (navigation-card-is-the-boss) and §5 Methodology's three-tier system operationalise in the Arduino workshop — and Sciacca's teachers are reporting from practice that a well-designed PjBL unit does the differentiation work rather than forcing the teacher to improvise it during a session.

**Second, Sciacca's teachers perceived positive impacts on students across academic, behavioural, social-emotional, engagement, and agency domains.** Some of the most striking direct quotes come from this theme. S.F. reported a week-long PjBL unit during which "I did not have a single kid that stayed in their seat during my lessons," and more specifically: *"I had a zero percent head down rate during that week too, because we were tracking that data ... we probably had about a third of the kids that would frequently put heads down, and that week we literally had nothing."* M.G. connected choice architecture directly to regulation: *"by offering choices within the project, this boosted their sense of control and reduced anxiety, conflicts, or off-task behaviors as opportunities to teach teamwork, communication, and resilience."* B.Q. described the student agency mechanism especially clearly: *"It gives them more ownership of their learning, which helps a lot of the time when those students were told, 'You need to memorize this or you need to do that.' It just clashes, it kind of just stops them dead in their tracks. So even though they are being told you have to do this project, there is kind of leniency with how they go about it."* The student-agency sub-theme is the strongest piece of teacher-voiced evidence in the verified corpus for the kind of power-struggle-avoidance framing §4 Principle 5 (structured autonomy, not forced compliance) is built around.

**Third, Sciacca's teachers reported a nuanced impact on themselves.** All three described PjBL as rewarding and enjoyable, with B.Q. summarising: *"Even though it's arguably more work than being a math teacher, it's more rewarding and more fun."* But they also reported workload and group-management challenges honestly — two participants explicitly said planning is more work, with S.F. observing: *"There is definitely more preparation with it. So being able to gather all of the artifacts and materials needed prior to the lesson obviously is a huge component of it."* The most useful quote in the entire dissertation for §4 Principle 9 is S.F.'s summary of the teacher-experience paradox: *"It's more work to kind of prepare, but less during the instruction, because the kids drive so much more of their own learning."* This is the exact sustainability-paradox the Arduino program's Principle 9 commits to — pre-prepared materials up front mean the teacher is not improvising during sessions — now spoken in a practitioner's own words from a PjBL-in-EBD context.

**Sciacca's honest limitations.** Three participants only, all in Pennsylvania, different professional roles (self-contained vs inclusive vs military-academy support), no classroom observations (data was interviews plus artifacts only), two participants had prior indirect contact with the researcher, and — importantly — the teachers' PjBL implementations did not fully represent all six criteria of the HQPBL framework Sciacca uses as her quality anchor. Specifically, the "public product" element was limited to classmates or colleagues rather than authentic external audiences, and student reflection was unclear in two of the three cases. Sciacca flags this openly and is clear that her findings describe how these three teachers experienced PjBL, not how a canonical HQPBL implementation would play out. The Arduino program inherits this caveat honestly — §9 Honest Limitations returns to it.

**A second PBL-in-EBD empirical data point: Foster & Shriner (2023).** Alongside Taylor et al. (2024) and Sciacca (2025), the verified literature also contains **Foster & Shriner (2023)** in *Journal of Education and Training Studies*, a qualitative case study conducted in a residential mental-health school in Missouri serving students with adverse childhood experiences — a population substantially overlapping with formal EBD identifications. Foster & Shriner report that PjBL "decreases negative behaviors for students with adverse childhood experiences while increasing engagement, academic skills, and self-regulation." This is a single-site qualitative case study and should not be mistaken for a quantitative effect-size claim, but it is a second practitioner-level data point in the same direction as Taylor et al. (2024)'s large single-case effect sizes and Sciacca (2025)'s three-teacher perceptions. Together, Taylor, Sciacca, and Foster & Shriner represent the small but coherent empirical footprint of PjBL work specifically with students whose identification or experience maps onto the EBD category. The program's §1 and §9 are updated to reflect this three-study footprint rather than the single-study footprint the earlier draft described.

**The HQPBL framework as a canonical quality anchor.** Taylor et al., Sciacca, and the broader PBL-in-special-education literature all reference the **High Quality Project-Based Learning framework** developed by the Buck Institute for Education (2018). HQPBL specifies six criteria that distinguish high-quality PjBL experiences from superficial "projects-in-name-only": **intellectual challenge and accomplishment, authenticity, public product, collaboration, project management, and reflection**. The Arduino program adopts HQPBL as the canonical quality anchor for its own projects and honestly notes where the program's individual-student framing produces gaps against HQPBL's criteria — specifically, the "public product" criterion in the strict Buck Institute sense (sharing with an authentic audience beyond the classroom) and the "collaboration" criterion (shared work on a common artefact) are both weaker in the program as currently designed than in a canonical HQPBL implementation. Appendix A discusses the collaboration deferral, and §9 Honest Limitations returns to the public-product gap.

**The HLP bridge: Wertz & Mulcahy (2024).** Wertz, J.A. & Mulcahy, C.A. (2024), in *Preventing School Failure: Alternative Education for Children and Youth*, systematically examined the alignment between PjBL elements and the 22 High-Leverage Practices for Students with Disabilities from the CEC/CEEDAR framework. Their analysis identifies multiple explicit overlaps between PjBL practice and HLPs, including those related to collaborative learning, scaffolding, flexible grouping, active engagement, instructional technology, and peer/teacher feedback. This is a theoretically important move: it means the empirical basis for PjBL with students with disabilities does not have to be built from scratch — it can borrow, carefully and with appropriate scoping, from the much deeper HLP evidence base that is the field's canonical "what good special-education teachers do" reference. The Arduino program is honest about the limits of this borrowing — Wertz & Mulcahy identify structural overlaps, not settled transfer claims — but the alignment itself is a substantive finding that grounds the program's PjBL choices in the broader special-education evidence base rather than leaving them as an isolated pedagogical preference.

Condliffe, Quint, Visher, Bangser, Drohojowska, Saco & Nelson (2017), in an MDRC working paper, conducted a comprehensive literature review of PBL implementation across K–12 settings and identified structural challenges alongside the supports necessary for diverse learners to succeed. Their review emphasises that PBL implementation quality varies widely and that the benefits of PBL appear most reliably when the structural supports — clear driving questions, scaffolded inquiry, formative assessment, and explicit teaching of collaboration skills — are actually in place. The practical upshot is that *PBL is not a pedagogy-in-a-box*: the same PBL label can describe vastly different classroom realities, and outcomes depend on implementation fidelity.

**Implications for design.** §3.5 supports §4 Principle 4 (hyper-chunked milestones with visible wins — Taylor et al.'s "sustained inquiry" combined with frequent visible outcomes), Principle 5 (student voice and choice as core HQPBL elements, reinforced by Sciacca's student-agency-and-ownership sub-theme and B.Q.'s "stops them dead in their tracks" quote on forced compliance), Principle 2 (navigation card as external scaffold doing the differentiation work rather than the teacher improvising — reinforced by Sciacca's M.G. and S.F. reporting that PjBL has adaptation built in), Principle 8 (the teacher–student relationship as the multiplier — directly supported by all three Sciacca teachers identifying trust and rapport as foundational), and Principle 9 (teacher sustainability — Taylor et al.'s preliminary job-satisfaction finding *plus* Sciacca's S.F. quote that PjBL is "more work to kind of prepare, but less during the instruction, because the kids drive so much more of their own learning"). It also frames the program's honest stance: PBL has **directional** support for this population, now grounded in three separate empirical studies pointing in the same direction — Taylor et al. (2024)'s large single-case effect sizes, Sciacca (2025)'s three-teacher qualitative case study, and Foster & Shriner (2023)'s residential-school case study — plus the broader theoretical bridge from Wertz & Mulcahy (2024) to the CEC/CEEDAR High-Leverage Practices evidence base. The Culclasure et al. (2019) nuanced result (no significant academic differences but positive social-emotional effects) keeps the honest scoping of academic claims in check, and the HQPBL framework from Buck Institute for Education (2018) provides a canonical quality standard the program adopts and measures itself against in §9 Honest Limitations.

### §3.6 Robotics and tangible computing for ADHD, EBD, and learning disabilities

Robotics and tangible computing are the hardware frame the program is built on, and the literature on their use with students with ADHD, EBD, and learning disabilities provides the strongest direct evidence the program has for *why* hands-on physical computing matters for this population. The theme is framed around one core idea: **structured, tangible, hands-on feedback — delivered through a system rather than a person — sustains attention, supports self-regulation, and reduces the social-threat dynamics that often derail traditional instruction with these students**.

Berrezueta-Guzmán, Robles-Bykbaev, Pau, Pesántez-Avilés & Martín-Ruiz (2021), in *IEEE Access*, published a literature review of robotic technologies supporting ADHD therapies. Their review is honest about both the promise and the limitations of the field. They classify the reviewed literature by type of therapy supported (attention training, social skills, educational/academic support), type of robot, and associated technologies. The review's headline finding is that **robotic assistants show favorable technical results in supporting attention training and task-completion activities for children with ADHD, but require significant improvements in scalability, human-machine interaction, and data processing to be applied effectively in real-world therapies**. This honesty matters: the program draws on robotic-assistance research for its "non-human mediator" framing, but it does not overclaim — the field is still exploratory, the applications are primarily therapeutic rather than educational, and the gap between laboratory proof-of-concept and classroom-ready deployment is real.

A more direct empirical finding comes from the companion study by Berrezueta-Guzmán, Pau, Martín-Ruiz & Máximo-Bocanegra (2021), also in *IEEE Access*, which presents the assessment of a Robotic Assistant (Atent@) specifically designed to support homework activities for children with ADHD. The study reports that the robotic assistant **positively influenced the homework performance of children with and without ADHD**, with the robot functioning as a monitoring and interaction tool that provides guidance, reminders, and feedback. The mechanism is what the program's Principle 2 calls *non-human mediation*: the robot's guidance is read by the student as instrumental rather than as interpersonal demand, which reduces the social-threat response some students carry into interactions with human teachers.

Cibrian, Lakes, Schuck & Hayes (2022), in *International Journal of Child-Computer Interaction*, reviewed thirty-six articles on emerging technologies (computers, mobile devices, wearables, sensors, virtual and augmented reality, and robotics) that assist and assess self-regulation of behavior in children with ADHD. Their review concludes that mobile health devices have shown promising results for assessing emotions, helping with organization, and enhancing self-monitoring, and that sensor-and-wearable technologies *may* detect behaviors and give feedback to support self-regulation. The authors are explicit that the research base is still emerging and that the evidence for most of these technology categories is preliminary rather than settled. For the workshop, Cibrian et al. (2022) provides directional support for the broader claim that **technology-mediated feedback helps ADHD students self-regulate**, while honestly acknowledging that this claim rests on an emerging literature.

Kuzmina & Romero (2025), in *Australian Journal of Learning Difficulties* 30(2), offer one specific observation from a tangential research context that is worth reporting on narrow grounds. Their study used the **CreaCube** task — an assessment platform built around four pre-made Cubelets modular robotic cubes (wheels, battery, sensor, inverter) that children assemble into a working autonomous vehicle — to compare divergent-thinking measures (fluency, flexibility, originality) in fifteen children aged 9–14, seven of whom had a formal ADHD diagnosis. This is **not** the kind of construction the Arduino workshop asks students to do: in CreaCube the components are pre-made, the connection logic is predetermined, and the task is a constrained assembly puzzle rather than an open design-and-build. The authors tested five quantitative hypotheses that children with ADHD would outperform neurotypical peers on divergent-thinking measures, and **all five hypotheses were NOT statistically supported** at the conventional threshold. What makes the paper worth citing at all is one behavioral observation the authors report at line 88 of their manuscript: **five out of seven children with ADHD spontaneously used a think-aloud technique during the task** — verbalizing their actions, hypotheses, and reactions — while **none of the neurotypical children exhibited this behavior**. The authors interpret this pattern in light of Corkum et al.'s (2008) earlier finding that children with ADHD produce more task-relevant external private speech than typically developing peers during problem-solving, framing it as "a productive compensatory strategy for managing cognitive load or enhancing self-regulation." For the Arduino workshop, the narrow but real implication is that *hands-on construction work elicits spontaneous verbalization as a self-regulation behavior in students with ADHD* — which is directly relevant to the workshop's small-group format and to how peer and teacher interactions during construction should be framed (§5 Methodology treats spontaneous verbalization as a feature to be welcomed, not a distraction to be silenced). We do NOT cite Kuzmina & Romero as evidence that modular robotics improves task performance or divergent thinking in children with ADHD — their own quantitative data do not support that claim.

Guneysu Ozgur, Özgür, Asselborn, Johal, Yadollahi, Bruno, Skweres & Dillenbourg (2020), in *Frontiers in Robotics and AI*, report on an iterative design of a tangible robot-assisted handwriting activity for special education. Their work illustrates how hands-on, manipulable robotic artifacts that provide immediate physical feedback support engagement for students in special-education settings, and their iterative-design methodology itself — rapid prototyping, user testing, refinement — is a model for the workshop's commitment to responsive adaptation based on how students actually use the tools.

O'Connell, Banga, Ayissi, Yaminrafie, Ko, Le, Cislowski & Mataric (2024), in the proceedings of the 2024 ACM/IEEE International Conference on Human-Robot Interaction, report on the design and evaluation of a socially assistive robot schoolwork companion for college students with ADHD. Their work extends the non-human-mediator framing to older students and academic contexts: the robot serves as a study companion that provides gentle presence, task reminders, and scaffolded prompts without the social-evaluation weight of a human study partner. The finding is relevant to the program because it shows that the basic mechanism — non-human mediation supporting task persistence in ADHD populations — extends across ages from primary school through college.

Díaz-Boladeras, Claver i Díaz & García-Sánchez (2025), in *Universal Access in the Information Society*, published a scoping review of robots for inclusive classrooms across thirty-three studies. LEGO Mindstorms kits (WeDo, NXT, EV3) were the most-used educational robotics platform, appearing in 13 of the studies. Only two of the robots reviewed were specifically developed for children with special needs; the rest were off-the-shelf educational kits adapted for inclusive use. The practical implication is that **the modular-construction robot paradigm** — students building and programming their own artifacts rather than interacting with pre-made social robots — is the dominant approach in inclusive-classroom robotics research, and it is the approach the Arduino workshop commits to.

Three framing papers complete the picture. Hwang & Taylor (2016), in *Journal of Science Education for Students with Disabilities*, frame the **concrete → representational → abstract (CRA) sequence** as a prerequisite for students with disabilities in STEM: manipulatives first, then diagrams and visual representations, then symbols and equations. The CRA sequence is the direct theoretical basis for §4 Principle 3 (physical-first, then abstract). Israel, Pearson, Tapia, Wherfel & Reese (2015), in *Computers & Education*, conducted a cross-case qualitative analysis across multiple instructional contexts and concluded that *"struggling learners, including students with disabilities and those living in poverty, benefitted from computing education that included scaffolding, modeling, and peer collaboration."* The finding is directly aligned with the program's commitment to scaffolded computational-thinking work as the entry point for every Arduino project. Álvarez Ariza & Hernández Hernández (2025), in *International Journal of Science and Mathematics Education*, published a systematic literature review of research-based interventions and strategies for students with disabilities in STEM and STEAM education, documenting the breadth of approaches but also acknowledging that accessibility and tailored adaptations for students with physical, sensory, or motor impairments remain underdeveloped.

Finally, Gomez (2019), in a doctoral dissertation at the University of Texas at San Antonio (ERIC ED604859), examined the effects of makerspace learning on the social interactions of middle-school students with emotional or behavioral disorders. The study used a single-case experimental design with four middle-school EBD students and reported that makerspace participation — grounded in constructionist principles — supported social-emotional skill development compared to traditional classroom contexts. The study is especially important because it is the closest direct analogue in the verified literature to what the Arduino workshop actually does — hands-on making in a small-group special-education context for students whose primary identification is EBD. Taylor et al. (2024) is the closest PBL-in-EBD study; Gomez (2019) is the closest maker-in-EBD study. Between them, they define the specific empirical niche the program is extending into.

**Implications for design.** §3.6 supports §4 Principle 2 (navigation card and system as non-human mediator — Berrezueta-Guzmán 2021a/b, Cibrian 2022, O'Connell 2024), Principle 3 (physical-first, then abstract / CRA sequence — Hwang & Taylor 2016, Israel et al. 2015), Principle 4 (hyper-chunked milestones with visible wins — Guneysu Ozgur 2020's iterative physical milestones), and §4 Principle 8 (relationships and social-emotional development through making — Gomez 2019). It also frames Project 8's quadcopter capstone as an extension of the modular-construction-robotics paradigm (Díaz-Boladeras 2025) into the aerial-robotics domain that §3.8 will identify as a gap in the literature.

### §3.7 Universal Design for Learning

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) is the pedagogical framework the workshop uses to express what "differentiated access" means in practice. The framework was developed by the Center for Applied Special Technology (CAST) and organises instructional design around three principles — **Representation** (multiple ways of taking in information), **Expression and Action** (multiple ways of demonstrating learning), and **Engagement** (multiple ways of motivating and sustaining learning). These three principles map to three neurologically-grounded networks: recognition, strategic, and affective.

The verified literature on UDL tells two stories at once, and the program reports both honestly.

**The supportive story.** Almeqdad, Alodat, Alquraan, Mohaidat & Al-Makhzoomy (2023), in *Cogent Education*, conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of the effectiveness of UDL in educational settings. Their inclusion criteria selected 13 empirical peer-reviewed pre-post-design studies published between 2015 and 2021 in English and Arabic. The studies spanned six countries, K–12 and higher education, both specific and generic domains, and were directed at teachers or students via professional development programmes or school interventions. **The meta-analytic total effect size was 3.56 — a large effect**, with statistical significance found for one-group studies, student participants, specific-domain studies, and quantitative research designs. The authors' own framing of this finding is important: they note **"considerable heterogeneity was evident"** across studies, and their conclusion is cautious rather than triumphant: *"UDL can improve student learning, especially in particular domains and when applied through professional development initiatives or classroom interventions. More research is required to ensure that UDL is widely applicable and to better understand how to maximize its advantages."* The large meta-analytic effect is real, but it comes from a small and heterogeneous study base, and the authors themselves call for more research before making broad claims.

Basham & Marino (2013), in *TEACHING Exceptional Children*, made the direct case for applying UDL to STEM education for students with disabilities, arguing that UDL's multiple-means framework provides the structural scaffolding needed to open STEM content to learners who cannot access it through a single instructional channel. Their framing is conceptual rather than empirical, but it grounds the program's commitment to UDL in the STEM-specific literature.

Phelan, Maguire & Finnegan (2025), in *European Journal of Education*, conducted a scoping review specifically of UDL principles embedded in subjects in secondary education — the exact level the program operates at. Their review identified that most articles were based in North America, used an intervention study approach, and reported positive results on student engagement post-intervention. They note an interesting methodological pattern: *"while all articles displayed evidence of UDL pedagogical practices, some articles did not specifically identify these practices as UDL. This could suggest that (1) an awareness of UDL as an inclusive pedagogical framework is lacking in current educational research, and/or (2) UDL may be incorporated into teaching practices more frequently than is currently being identified."* In other words, the UDL-in-secondary-education literature may be larger than the label-based literature search suggests, because teachers and researchers use UDL practices without naming them as such.

**The critical story.** Boysen (2024), in *Policy Futures in Education*, published a critical analysis of the research evidence behind CAST's UDL guidelines themselves. Boysen sampled articles that CAST cited on its UDL website as support for three specific UDL guidelines and analysed their relation to choice, learning, and brain function — the three pillars CAST uses to justify the framework. **The findings are blunt:** *"Results indicated that the cited studies provided little evidence for claims about UDL. Most of the studies did not offer a choice to learners or measure learning. None of the studies were related to brain function. Rather than supporting CAST's UDL guidelines about the choice of multiple means of learning, the studies mostly provided support for single, effective instructional techniques."* Boysen's conclusion is that the cited evidence behind CAST's UDL guidelines is **weak**, and that both basic research and implementation research are needed to establish the framework's validity and effectiveness.

These two stories are compatible, and the program presents them together honestly. **Almeqdad et al. (2023) report that when UDL-framework-inspired interventions are implemented and evaluated, they tend to produce positive effects** — a large meta-analytic effect across a small, heterogeneous base. **Boysen (2024) reports that the specific research evidence CAST invokes to justify the guidelines themselves does not cleanly support the framework's neuroscience claims or its choice-and-multiple-means claims** — the implemented practices work, but the framework's theoretical foundations rest on weaker evidence than CAST's presentation suggests. The honest reading is that UDL is **a coherent pedagogical framework with empirically-supported components and a weaker theoretical foundation** — the three principles work as a design checklist even if the neuroscience justification is less solid than the CAST website implies. The workshop uses UDL as a design checklist and a shared vocabulary with other special-education colleagues, not as a neuroscience-of-learning theory.

CAST (2018), *Universal Design for Learning Guidelines version 2.2*, is the canonical guidelines document the program uses operationally. The CAST guidelines themselves are a design reference, not an empirical claim — they are what a teacher consults when designing a lesson, not what a reviewer consults as evidence. The program cites CAST 2018 as the source for the three principles and the nine sub-guidelines that structure how Representation, Expression and Action, and Engagement get operationalised into practice.

**Implications for design.** §3.7 supports the program's use of UDL's three principles as a design checklist for every project. The workshop's three-level Claude Code integration (upload / modify / dialogue) is an Expression and Action differentiation. The navigation card format and visual milestone system are Representation differentiations. The choice-and-voice design in Principle 5 is an Engagement differentiation. §3.7 also carries forward to §9 Honest Limitations the Boysen (2024) caveat that UDL's theoretical foundation is weaker than the framework's prominence might suggest — the program uses UDL practically without overclaiming its scientific grounding.

### §3.8 What is NOT in the literature

This section is the most important honest caveat in §3. The themes above (§3.1–§3.7) describe what the verified literature *does* say; this section describes what it does *not*. Three structural gaps in the research base are load-bearing for how the program presents itself and for what it can and cannot claim.

**Gap 1: no published empirical research on Arduino or microcontroller-level physical computing for students with EBD, ADHD, or learning disabilities.**

We searched the full-text corpus for mentions of Arduino, ESP32, Raspberry Pi, breadboards, circuit design, electronics pedagogy, and student-built microcontroller projects as intervention contexts for neurodivergent secondary students. The corpus contains substantial research on robotics in special education — but the robotics in that research is almost entirely **pre-made** or **modular platform-based**: LEGO Mindstorms (the most-used platform in Díaz-Boladeras et al.'s 2025 scoping review, appearing in 13 of 33 studies), Cubelets (the platform in Kuzmina & Romero 2025), NAO and Pepper humanoids (reviewed in Papakostas's descriptive review and elsewhere), Bee-Bots, Dash, KASPAR, and similar educational robotics kits. None of the studies in the verified corpus examine students **building** a microcontroller-based project from components — wiring breadboards, writing their own code, soldering connections, debugging hardware-and-software interactions together. This is the gap the program's core technology choice sits in.

The gap is not a rejection of the program's approach — it is an honest acknowledgement that **the evidence the program is extending into Arduino territory comes from adjacent literatures**: LEGO/modular robotics (Díaz-Boladeras 2025), tangible computing (Guneysu Ozgur 2020), makerspace learning (Gomez 2019), computational thinking for diverse learners (Israel et al. 2015), scaffolded STEM meta-analysis (Belland et al. 2017), and hands-on robot-assisted ADHD interventions (Berrezueta-Guzmán 2021a, 2021b; Cibrian et al. 2022). The program's design decisions are grounded in these adjacent evidence bases, and the inferential extension into Arduino is made explicit in §9 Honest Limitations and acknowledged as territory that would benefit from direct empirical evaluation.

**Gap 2: no published research on aerial robotics, drones, or flight-STEM for any special-needs population.**

Project 8 of the program is a build-from-scratch ESP32-based tiny quadcopter capstone. We explicitly searched all 78 files in the full-text corpus for any mention of drones, quadcopters, UAVs, multirotors, aerial robots, flying robots, aircraft, flight (in an educational context), propellers, rotors, or hovering. **The search returned one hit — a metaphorical usage in the CAST (2018) Universal Design for Learning guidelines' scaffolding-as-apprenticeship discussion.** Otherwise, the corpus contains zero substantive research on aerial robotics, drone construction, flight-STEM pedagogy, or quadcopter-based learning for any population — neurotypical or neurodivergent.

Project 8 therefore rests on an **inferential extension** from four adjacent evidence bases: (a) ground robotics for ADHD/EBD (Berrezueta-Guzmán 2021a, 2021b; Cibrian et al. 2022; O'Connell et al. 2024), (b) modular and tangible student-built STEM (Guneysu Ozgur et al. 2020; Israel et al. 2015), (c) scaffolded computational thinking (Belland et al. 2017; Hwang & Taylor 2016), and (d) makerspace learning for EBD (Gomez 2019). The quadcopter itself adds complexity, safety stakes, and motivational weight that the ground-based evidence can only partially inform. §9 Honest Limitations acknowledges this explicitly, and Project 8's design includes dedicated safety protocols that go beyond what the ground-based literature would require. The program does not claim that research directly supports quadcopter PBL for EBD students — it claims that Project 8 is a **responsible extension** from adjacent evidence, and that its outcomes should be evaluated with appropriate humility.

**Gap 3: no published research on AI-collaborative coding tools for neurodivergent students.**

The program uses Claude Code as a first-class differentiation support (§4 Principle 7) with three access levels — pre-written code upload, guided modification with AI help, and free dialogue with the AI. This design is novel, and the verified corpus contains **zero** studies of Claude Code, conversational AI tutors, AI-paired coding, or similar tools specifically for students with EBD, ADHD, OCD, anxiety, or related profiles. The absence is in part structural: Claude Code and tools like it are new enough that a research literature has not yet had time to develop around them, and the empirical work that does exist on AI tutoring for neurodivergent students is preliminary and concentrated on earlier-generation systems.

The program's Claude Code integration is therefore a **piloted practice** rather than an evidence-based intervention. The three-level scaffolding is grounded in adjacent research on external cognitive scaffolds (Rosen, Boyle, Cariss & Forchelli 2014), on the importance of matching support level to student readiness (the broader differentiation literature in §3.4), and on the specific ADHD finding that structured external support can enable — rather than undermine — competence-building (Smith, Langberg, Cusick, Green & Becker 2020). But the specific combination — differentiated AI access levels within an Arduino workshop for EBD students — has no direct empirical precedent. §4 Principle 7 flags this explicitly, and §9 Honest Limitations restates it as one of the program's most significant evidence-thin commitments. The program is willing to pilot Claude Code integration carefully because the theoretical case is coherent and the adjacent evidence is directionally supportive, but it does not overclaim empirical grounding the literature does not provide.

**Two additional gaps worth naming briefly.**

First, **research on small-group project-based learning in special-education settings is thin**. Most PBL studies are whole-class inclusive or 1:1 intervention designs; the program's small-group-of-3-to-8 format matches Taylor et al.'s (2024) single study's scale exactly but is not strongly represented elsewhere. The program's group-size decisions rest on Taylor et al. and on the relational-multiplier finding from Gersib & Mason (2023), plus the practical constraint that Agourim workshops run at this scale regardless of what the literature says.

Second, **research on failure, iteration, and productive struggle in PBL for students with EBD is scarce**. How should debugging failures, project setbacks, or peer criticism be scaffolded for students whose emotional-regulation reserves are limited? The verified corpus does not answer this directly, and the program's "stuck protocol" in §5 Methodology is built from adjacent evidence on self-management (Del Vecchio et al. 2025; Moohr et al. 2021) and from the broader framing that students with ADHD benefit from structured external support (Smith, Langberg et al. 2020) — not from direct empirical work on how to handle project failure for this population.

**The overall honest framing.** The program stands on the shoulders of adjacent research, and §9 Honest Limitations makes this position explicit. Where the literature directly supports a design choice, the program says so and cites it. Where the literature is inferentially extended, the program says so and names the extension. Where the literature is silent or thin, the program acknowledges the gap and describes the design choice as piloted practice requiring careful evaluation rather than as evidence-based intervention. This honesty is the program's defence against a reviewer who knows the literature well — and it is the program's commitment to the academic integrity the evaluator-and-funder audience is entitled to expect.

## §4 Design Principles

§3 reviewed what the research literature says. §4 is the distillation — the nine design principles that every project in the program embodies and that every methodology choice in §5 follows. These principles are the bridge between the evidence and the practice.

Each principle is presented in four parts:

- **Statement** — the principle in one or two sentences.
- **Evidence** — a compact pointer to the §3 themes that support it. §3 carries the full citations; §4 is the summary.
- **Design rule** — the concrete translation into how Arduino projects are built and run.
- **Applied in** — where in §5 Methodology and in the 8 projects (§6) the principle shows up operationally.

A reader who has already read §3 can move through §4 quickly. A reader who jumped straight to §4 (for example, a workshop teacher at another school looking for the adoptable design rules) gets enough context to understand each principle and its justification without having to backtrack. §9 Honest Limitations returns to Principle 7 specifically, because it is the principle with the weakest direct empirical support.

### Principle 1 — Predictable routine is load-bearing for executive function

**Statement.** Every session follows the same rhythm. Routine is not decoration — it is the structural beam that protects executive function from stress-collapse and lets students arrive ready to work with minimum anxiety.

**Evidence.** §3.2 on perceived stress, executive function, and the Cumming et al. (2024) finding that stress directly degrades EF (β ≈ .61 for language arts, β ≈ .65 for math) and EF in turn mediates academic performance. §3.3 on trauma-informed practice, where Koslouski, Stark & Chafouleas (2023) describe the neurobiological survival-mode response that unpredictability triggers, and Watson & Astor (2025) endorse safety and predictability as two of the four well-grounded trauma-informed components. §3.1 on Harrison, Soares, Rudzinski & Johnson's (2019) finding that instructional and self-management interventions — both of which depend on predictable environmental structure — are the two classroom intervention categories meeting What Works Clearinghouse evidence-based standards for ADHD. CICO studies (Flannery et al. 2024; Kittelman et al. 2024) converge from a different angle, showing that predictable daily check-in routines improve on-task behavior in high-school EBD settings.

**Design rule.**
- Every workshop session follows the same seven-phase structure: Arrival & Board Check → Mini-Huddle → Work Block 1 → Movement Break → Work Block 2 → Clean-Up → Close-Out Circle. The structure is described in detail in §5 Methodology.
- No surprises. Any deviation from routine is announced at the Mini-Huddle with an explicit reason.
- Every project's navigation cards live at predictable physical locations — same place on the workbench, same color-coded clip — so students never have to hunt for their work.
- Every project begins with a predictable recap: "Here's where you left off; here's what you're doing next."

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (session structure), every project in §6 (shared session rhythm), and the "fluctuating capacity" framing in §2 Population Profile that explains why routine is protective rather than restrictive.

### Principle 2 — The navigation card traces the path

**Statement.** Visual navigation cards, checklists, and reference sheets guide students through work. The teacher rotates, supports, and celebrates — but does not give verbal multi-step instructions or direct commands. This bypasses the power-struggle dynamics that derail students with ODD-flavored profiles, it bypasses the working-memory load of holding verbal sequences in mind, and it lets students work independently so the teacher can focus on whoever needs help most.

**Evidence.** §3.1 on Barker & de Lugt (2022) framing ODD as an interactional disorder where the specific person–student interaction shapes the response, and on their empirical review showing environmental modifications and self-monitoring produce strong positive effects. §3.2 on Rosen, Boyle, Cariss & Forchelli (2014) showing external cognitive scaffolds open pathways for students with EF deficits to practice EF *through* the scaffold — students don't need intact EF to use the scaffold, and using it is how EF improves. §3.2 also on Thomas & Karuppali's (2022) systematic review of visual activity schedules for ADHD, which reduce task-initiation latency and reduce off-task behavior during transitions. §3.6 on the pattern across multiple robotics studies (Berrezueta-Guzmán et al. 2021a, 2021b; Cibrian et al. 2022) that structured, non-human feedback sustains task engagement for ADHD children — the "non-human mediator" pattern aggregated across constituent studies.

**Design rule.**
- Every step of every project lives on a Navigation Card (template and rationale in §5 Methodology).
- Teachers never give verbal multi-step instructions. Instead: "Check your next card." Or: "What does the card say the next step is?"
- When a student gets stuck, the teacher points at the card's "Stuck? Try this first" protocol before offering a direct hint.
- Task cards are laminated and placed at each workstation. For the first project in any student's trajectory, the teacher walks them through the card format once and then stops explaining — the card does the rest.
- Corrections come from the hardware, not from the teacher, wherever possible: "Your LED isn't lighting up — check the card. What does the checkpoint say?"

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (Navigation Card System, Stuck Protocol, Teacher Rotation), and every project in §6 (every milestone is a navigation card).

### Principle 3 — Physical-first, then abstract (concrete → representational → abstract)

**Statement.** Students build the wiring before they write the code. They see the hardware do something before they abstract it. The concrete → representational → abstract sequence is not optional for students with learning disabilities and attention challenges — they cannot skip the concrete phase.

**Evidence.** §3.6 on Hwang & Taylor (2016) framing the concrete-representational-abstract sequence as a prerequisite for students with disabilities in STEM. §3.6 on Israel, Pearson, Tapia, Wherfel & Reese (2015) showing that struggling learners — including students with disabilities — benefit from computing education that includes explicit scaffolding, modeling, and peer collaboration grounded in concrete experiences. §3.2 on Belland, Walker, Kim & Lefler (2017) meta-analysis showing scaffolded STEM instruction produces an overall effect of g = 0.46, with significantly larger effects for underperforming students (z = 2.29, p < .05) and directionally stronger effects in project-based learning contexts than in problem-based learning. §3.6 on the robotics-in-ADHD studies (Berrezueta-Guzmán et al. 2021a, 2021b; Cibrian et al. 2022) showing hands-on tangible feedback supports self-regulation by giving students the effect of their action before they need to explain it.

**Design rule.**
- Every project begins with a wiring-only milestone: breadboard and wires before any code is written. Students see the circuit and understand what it does physically.
- The first observable outcome of any project is physical — an LED lights, a motor spins, a buzzer sounds. Code comes after the hardware works.
- When students write their first code for a project, they are modifying pre-written scaffold code that already matches their wiring. They never start from a blank editor.
- Code explanations reference the hardware concretely: *"This line turns pin 13 HIGH, which sends voltage to the wire that goes to your LED's long leg."*
- For Project 8 (the quadcopter capstone), the concrete → representational → abstract progression becomes: frame assembly (concrete) → wiring diagram tracing (representational) → Claude-Code-assisted firmware generation (abstract).

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (project sequencing — every project has a wiring-first milestone), every project in §6 (hardware list and first milestone are always physical), and Principle 7's Level 1 (pre-written code upload as the most concrete entry point into the abstraction of code).

### Principle 4 — Hyper-chunked 15-minute milestones with visible wins

**Statement.** Every step of every project produces a concrete, observable outcome within 10–15 minutes. No silent work periods longer than that. No *"and then you'll see the result tomorrow."* Every 15 minutes, something in the real world changes because the student did something — a light turns on, a motor spins, a sensor reads a value, a number on the screen changes. This is the dopamine drip that sustains ADHD engagement, builds momentum for students with depression, and creates evidence of competence for students whose school history has taught them they cannot do things.

**Evidence.** §3.5 on Taylor, Allen, Van & Moohr (2024), the one empirical PBL-in-EBD-classroom study in the verified corpus, which reports very large on-task effect sizes (BC-SMD 95% CI [1.70, 3.81]) in a PBL unit built around sustained inquiry combined with frequent visible milestones. §3.6 on Guneysu Ozgur et al. (2020) showing iterative physical milestones with tangible robot-assisted output support engagement and reduce frustration in special education. §3.6 on Berrezueta-Guzmán et al. (2021) and Cibrian et al. (2022) identifying hands-on tangible feedback as a core mechanism supporting attention span and task-completion rates in children with ADHD. §3.2 on Belland et al. (2017) meta-analysis supporting persistent — rather than faded — scaffolding: students don't need big leaps, they need many small supported wins.

**Design rule.**
- Every navigation card maps to ≤ 15 minutes of work with an observable "done when" checkpoint.
- Long steps in the Project 8 source material (for example, *"Power Wiring — 15 min"*) are subdivided into 3–5 micro-tasks of 4–8 minutes each, each with its own physical checkpoint.
- No project has a silent middle where students work for 20+ minutes with nothing visible happening. If the natural work unit is that long, it must be broken or scaffolded with an intermediate indicator — a serial-monitor print, an LED status, a buzzer beep — that shows progress.
- Every milestone completion is celebrated with visible progress tracking: a checkmark, a clip moved to the next position, a sticker on the tracking sheet, or just a fist bump when the teacher rotates past.

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (Navigation Card System with "done when" criteria, simple tracking sheet), and every project in §6 (each project is structured as a sequence of 15-minute micro-milestones per tier).

### Principle 5 — Structured autonomy, not forced compliance

**Statement.** Every project offers at least one genuine choice point, even at Tier 1. *"Do you want a red or blue LED?" "Which sensor — ultrasonic or IR?" "What should we name your robot?"* Choice reduces oppositional resistance, increases intrinsic motivation, and respects student agency. But structure and choice are partners, not opposites: external structure (checklists, scaffolds, reinforcement, pre-built templates) is enabling, not undermining, for ADHD — it creates the safe envelope inside which autonomy becomes possible.

**Autonomy applies across projects, not just within them.** The same logic extends to the order and timing of projects across the program. For students whose school history includes many encounters with prescribed sequences they had no voice in, the refusal to follow the prescribed eight-project order is itself an expression of the very autonomy this principle protects. The program reframes prerequisites as **skills to acquire**, not **projects to complete in sequence**: a student who wants to start with a later project can do so, and the teacher scaffolds the missing skills just-in-time with targeted mini-modules rather than blocking the student at the doorway. A student's refusal to engage with a specific project on a specific day is treated as information about what they need — *"tell me what would make today worth your time"* — not as defiance to be corrected. §5 Methodology contains the detailed teacher guidance for this case.

**Evidence.** §3.5 on Taylor et al. (2024) HQPBL framework explicitly listing Student Voice as one of the eight essential elements and attributing the large on-task effect sizes in an EBD classroom to PBL's choice-and-authenticity design. §3.1 on Billingsley, Thomas & Webber (2018) showing that when students with comorbid learning and emotional/behavioral disabilities chose *how* they received instruction, engagement and task completion improved. §3.1 on Barker & de Lugt (2022) reviewing empirical ODD classroom interventions and supporting structured environmental modifications. §3.1 on Smith, Langberg, Cusick, Green & Becker (2020) showing that adolescents with ADHD have lower intrinsic motivation (d = 0.49) and lower extrinsic motivation (d = 0.43) than peers — but that extrinsic motivation paradoxically supports homework completion and GPA for ADHD students, meaning structured external support is not undermining but enabling. §3.1 on Morsink et al. (2022) reviewing two studies (Carlson et al. 2000; Carlson & Tamm 2000) that explicitly tested whether external rewards undermine intrinsic motivation in children with ADHD and found **no undermining effect**. §3.4 on the broader differentiated-instruction literature (Smale-Jacobse et al. 2019) supporting tiering by readiness in secondary settings.

**Design rule.**
- Every project has at least one genuine choice point at Tier 1 (color, name, one parameter).
- Every project has 2–3 choice points at Tier 2 (component selection, design decision, output behavior).
- Tier 3 of every project is an open-design brief with hard constraints (hardware budget, time, safety) but free creative direction.
- **Recognition within the workshop is immediate and tangible** — the teacher rotating past and acknowledging a working build, a visible checkmark on a navigation card, the hardware itself responding — rather than bureaucratic and deferred. This is **not** because the research says token economies are bad for this population. The verified research actually supports classroom token economies for students with ODD and ADHD: Barker & de Lugt (2022) include Rosenberg's (1986) study in their evidence table showing a token economy produced M PND = 82% for on-task behavior and M PND = 91% for reducing disruptive behavior in an ODD alternative classroom; Morsink et al.'s (2022) review found no undermining effect of rewards on intrinsic motivation in children with ADHD. The workshop's choice to use social/immediate/tangible recognition is instead grounded in three workshop-specific fits: **(a)** hardware-based milestones are already inherently tangible — a motor spinning is its own reward signal — so layering a token on top adds little; **(b)** a small-group, rotating-teacher setting has capacity for immediate personal acknowledgement in a way larger classrooms may not; **(c)** Agourim already operates a school-wide token-economy behavioural program across most aspects of daily school activity, and the workshop continues to operate within that school-wide system rather than adding a second parallel workshop-level token layer. The within-workshop recognition mechanism described here operates alongside the school-wide token economy, not instead of it.
- **Project order is not a rigid gate.** Prerequisites are skills, not sequence positions. Students may work on the eight projects in any order; when a student skips ahead, the teacher delivers missing prerequisite skills just-in-time via short targeted mini-modules rather than blocking the student at the doorway of the desired project. A student's refusal to do a specific project on a specific day is diagnostic, not defiant — the teacher's first response is *"tell me what would make today worth your time,"* and the conversation itself is the intervention.
- **Population profile sub-rules** (these are adaptive guidance, not diagnosis-based prescriptions):
  - For **OCD-related presentations**: private workspace by default; never forced public demonstration; navigation cards with clear "done when" criteria to prevent perfectionism spirals; flexible timing with no *"you must finish this before X."* These design choices are inferred from the documented burden of pediatric OCD (Abramovitch et al. 2024 report that approximately 90% of affected children experience impairment in at least one domain, with school as the most-affected domain) and from Colbert et al.'s (2025) finding that school-staff knowledge of OCD-specific accommodations is limited. Neither paper prescribes these specific workshop design rules — they document the problem the design is trying to address.
  - For **anxiety without trauma history**: scaffold approaches to feared tasks rather than permanent avoidance accommodation. Håland & Bertelsen (2025) document that teacher accommodations correlate with increased student avoidance and anxiety severity and warn that accommodations can *"inadvertently intensify or prolong the anxiety over time"* when they interfere with exposure-based treatment principles. Their finding is correlational, not a causal demonstration, but it is strong enough to make long-term avoidance accommodation a design concern worth naming.
  - For **anxiety with trauma history**: safety and predictability first; no forced exposure; use Principle 1 (predictable routine) as the primary anxiety management mechanism, not exposure. Watson & Astor (2025), Koslouski, Stark & Chafouleas (2023), and Asselman et al. (2025) on trauma-informed environments.
  - For **ODD-flavored profiles**: frame tasks as choices or requests, never as commands. *"When you're ready, your next card is here."*
  - For **depression-related profiles**: smallest possible first step; visible tangible results; celebrate micro-progress privately.

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (Tier System, Choice Point Protocol, Refusal-of-Order Subsection), and every project in §6 (Tier 1/2/3 structure with explicit choice points).

### Principle 6 — Movement is medicine for ADHD (not a reward, not a release valve)

**Statement.** Every session includes a structured movement break. This is not an *"optional energy release"* for students who *"can't sit still"* — it is a first-class intervention with the largest effect sizes in the ADHD literature for exactly the deficits this population has.

**Evidence.** §3.2 on Qiu, Liang, Wang, Zhang & Shum (2023) meta-analysis of 67 non-pharmacological EF intervention studies for ADHD finding that **physical exercise produced the largest effect sizes of all intervention types measured**: g = 0.900 on inhibitory control, g = 1.377 on cognitive flexibility. For comparison, cognitive training produced g = 0.907 on working memory and EF-specific curriculum produced g = 0.532 on planning. Physical exercise is not just competitive with other interventions — it is the best single intervention the corpus has measured for ADHD executive function. §3.2 on Cumming et al. (2024, both papers) independently showing that EF (especially cognitive flexibility) degrades under stress, and that movement is a direct stress intervention.

**Design rule.**
- The session structure includes a 3–5 minute Movement Break between Work Block 1 and Work Block 2. This break is **non-negotiable and applies to every session**, even when a student is in flow state. (Especially when a student is in flow state — the break protects the next hour's flow.)
- Movement breaks are structured, not free-for-all: stand up, get water, walk around the room once, stretch for 30 seconds. Structured because unstructured breaks can spike dysregulation for students with ODD-flavored or trauma-related profiles.
- Kinesthetic elements are embedded in navigation cards where possible. *"Walk to the parts bin and get a 220Ω resistor"* is better than *"reach for the resistor."* Test steps involve standing: *"Stand up, hold the drone, and spin the propeller with your finger — does it turn freely?"*

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (session structure, movement break as mandatory phase), and every project in §6 (test steps involve physical action wherever natural).

### Principle 7 — Claude Code as a differentiated coding support AND a scaffolded tutorial channel

**Statement.** Claude Code serves two distinct roles in the workshop, and both are differentiated so that students meet the AI at the level that is actually approachable for them today. The first role is **Claude Code as pair programmer** (Channel A): a coding collaborator the student directs when writing, modifying, or debugging a sketch. The second role is **Claude Code as scaffolded tutorial** (Channel B): a conversational walk-through of a navigation card, for students who prefer to receive instructions by voice or dialogue rather than reading a printed card on their own. Both channels sit alongside the printed navigation card — neither replaces it — and both enforce the same core discipline: the student describes what they are working on before the AI responds. Across both channels, the AI is a scaffold the student uses, not an obstacle the student has to overcome alone.

**Why two channels.** A single "Claude Code is a coding AI" role misses the fact that these students also need *reading* and *instruction-parsing* support, not only coding support. A student who can happily build a circuit but struggles to read a dense navigation card independently benefits enormously from hearing the same content delivered conversationally. A student who freezes at the sight of a blank code editor needs a pair-programmer channel whose very first move is to hand them a pre-written sketch. The two channels address two different entry points into the workshop's material, and treating them as separate lets the program differentiate each channel independently.

**Channel A — Pair programmer.** Students engage with code at the level that is actually approachable for them today. At the most supported end (Level 1), a student uploads a pre-written sketch that matches their wiring and watches the hardware respond — coding is not yet a barrier because they are not yet being asked to code. At the middle level (Level 2), a student modifies a working sketch with Claude Code's help, making small targeted changes and seeing what happens. At the most autonomous end (Level 3), a student describes what they are trying to build, shows Claude Code their current attempt, and iterates in dialogue. Students who are ready for dialogue describe the problem before asking for a solution — the AI is a collaborator they direct, not an answer machine they consume. Level switching is fluid in both directions: a student having a hard day can drop from Level 3 to Level 1 for a session without shame and return to Level 3 next week.

**Channel B — Scaffolded tutorial.** A student can ask Claude Code *"I am working on Project 3, Tier 2, navigation card 4 — walk me through it"* and Claude Code responds conversationally with the card's content, asking follow-up questions to check understanding and offering to go deeper on any step the student is stuck on. The printed navigation card remains the physical anchor in the workshop — the student can still see it, hold it, check it off, and show it to the teacher — but the same content is also available as a voice-and-text dialogue for students who learn better that way. Channel B is particularly valuable for students with reading difficulties, students who find dense printed instructions overwhelming, and students whose attention is more readily captured by conversational back-and-forth than by a static card. Unlike Channel A, Channel B is **available to Tier 1 students from the start**, because the conversational format of "read me what to do next" is cognitively simpler than the code-level dialogue of Channel A's Level 3 — it is closer to being read a story than to debugging together. Channel B still carries the "describe the problem first" discipline: the student tells Claude which project, tier, and card they are on, and if they are stuck, what they are stuck on, before Claude walks them through. A student using Channel B is not passive — they are directing a walk-through.

**Evidence.** §3.8 flagged this principle as **the weakest-supported in the program** — there is no direct empirical research in the verified corpus on Claude Code, conversational AI tutors, or AI-paired coding for neurodivergent students, and §9 Honest Limitations restates this explicitly. Channel A rests on responsible inference from three adjacent evidence bases: §3.2 on Rosen, Boyle, Cariss & Forchelli (2014), where external cognitive scaffolds enable students to practice EF they don't yet have and where both pre-written code and a well-designed AI prompt scaffold function as external cognitive scaffolds; §3.1 on Morsink et al. (2022) applying self-determination theory to motivation in ADHD adolescents — the SDT framework Morsink uses distinguishes extrinsic from intrinsic motivation, and this program *infers from that framework* (not from Morsink's own findings, which do not address AI tutoring) that an AI that dispenses answers on demand would function more like extrinsic reward, whereas an AI that asks questions back and requires the student to describe the problem first preserves student agency more in line with intrinsic-motivation support; and §3.1 on Smith, Langberg, Cusick, Green & Becker (2020), which found that higher extrinsic academic motivation was associated with better homework and GPA outcomes in adolescents with ADHD — the program infers from this (again, as a design inference, not as the paper's own recommendation) that structured external support can help academic outcomes for this population, provided the structure is scaffolding the student's own work rather than replacing it. **Channel B has even less direct support** than Channel A: it is a novel application of the same "AI as external scaffold" logic to instruction-parsing rather than code-writing, and §9 Honest Limitations flags it explicitly as an un-piloted practice.

**Design rule.**
- **Channel A — Pair programmer:**
  - Every project provides all three Claude Code interaction levels explicitly: a **pre-written sketch** (Level 1) that students can upload as-is and see the hardware respond; a **partially-filled sketch with guided modification targets** (Level 2) where Claude Code prompt scaffolds walk the student through specific small changes; and **open-ended prompt templates** (Level 3) for students ready to describe new ideas to Claude Code and iterate.
  - Task cards for each milestone indicate what Level 1 / 2 / 3 looks like at that step.
  - At Level 2 and Level 3, students describe the problem before asking for help: *"Before asking Claude, fill in: (a) what you want to happen, (b) what's currently happening, (c) what you've tried."*
  - At Level 2 and Level 3, a quick comprehension check follows any AI interaction: *"After Claude helps you, can you explain in one sentence what changed?"* At Level 1, the comprehension check is softer and scoped to observable behavior: *"What does your device do right now that it did not do before you uploaded the code?"*
  - Students can move up (Level 1 → 2 → 3) and back down without shame.
  - For Project 8 (the quadcopter capstone), the source material's firmware file is currently an empty stub, with the real firmware living as pseudocode in the project README. This is a feature of the three-level model: **Level 1** students upload a simpler pre-written motor-test sketch that proves the electronics work (no stabilization), and **Level 2/3** students use Claude Code to generate the full PID-stabilized `.ino` from the README pseudocode.
- **Channel B — Scaffolded tutorial:**
  - Every project ships with a **Claude Code tutorial-channel scaffold**: a short system prompt plus per-milestone invocation templates that tell Claude Code how to walk a student through the navigation card set conversationally. The scaffold lives in the project folder alongside the printed cards and the HTML tutorial.
  - A student invokes Channel B by telling Claude Code their project, tier, and card number — for example: *"I'm on Project 2, Tier 2, navigation card 3. Walk me through it."* Claude Code responds with the card's content in conversational form and asks follow-up questions at each checkpoint.
  - Channel B is available to students at **any tier**, including Tier 1. The printed navigation card remains the physical anchor; Channel B supplements it for students who prefer conversational delivery.
  - Channel B never *replaces* a navigation card — the student still checks off the printed card as they complete each milestone, and the teacher can still see the same card the student is working from. The two formats run in parallel.
  - The teacher is told in the project's setup checklist which students are currently using Channel B, so the teacher's rotation check-ins can reference the student's position in the walk-through conversation as well as on the printed card.

**Honest caveat.** This is the program's most evidence-thin principle, and §9 Honest Limitations treats both channels as **piloted practices** rather than evidence-based interventions. The three-level differentiated Channel A model has no direct precedent in published research; the Channel B tutorial-channel role has even less. Both are the program's own design responses to the heterogeneity of the student population, grounded in adjacent evidence but not directly empirically tested. A reviewer who asks *"where is the research that shows this works?"* gets an honest answer: it doesn't exist yet; we are piloting carefully; the design is inferentially grounded; the practice will be evaluated in use.

**Applied in.** §5.5 Methodology (Claude Code dual-channel integration protocol), every project in §6 (every project specifies Channel A Level 1 / Level 2 / Level 3 entry points and ships with a Channel B tutorial-channel scaffold), and §9 Honest Limitations (the most prominent caveat in the whole document).

### Principle 8 — The relationship is the multiplier

**Statement.** Structure alone does not work. Structure combined with a trustworthy adult presence works. The **teacher–student relationship** is the relational mechanism this program commits to as its first-class infrastructure: consistent adult presence, relational check-ins before task progress, greeting by name, listening without problem-solving, and the visible, formal support that comes from a named adult who knows what each student is working on. The relationship is the soil in which independent work grows, and the program's version of that soil is the teacher's attention — not peer collaboration, at least in the current version of the program (the appendix on deferred peer collaboration explains what the program *does not yet* do and why).

**Evidence.** §3.1 on Gersib & Mason (2023) meta-analysis of behavior interventions in self-contained EBD classrooms, which reported a large overall effect size (g = 0.93, SE = 0.16) with **relational components as the strongest moderator** (B = 1.26, p < .05). Gersib & Mason's relational components include parent communication, peer support, and adult mentoring — this program's version of "relational components" is specifically the **adult mentoring** component (teacher rotation with relational check-ins, consistent caring adult presence in every session, the Arrival and Close-Out rituals). The program does not claim Gersib's full moderator effect for itself — we are implementing one of the three components the moderator describes, and it is honest to say so. §3.1 on Gage, Kramer II & Ellis (2021) analysing school-climate data from 350,000+ high-school students (including 5,000+ with or at risk for EBD) and finding that EBD students with IEP services perceived more positive school climates than EBD students without IEP services in several domains — formal, visible adult support improves sense of belonging, which is specifically the mechanism this principle relies on. §3.3 on Koslouski, Stark & Chafouleas (2023) and Asselman et al. (2025) on trauma-informed environments emphasising relationship quality, trust, and adult co-regulation as the mechanism through which students develop resilience and self-regulation. **§3.5 on Sciacca (2025) is particularly important here**, because it is the closest empirical evidence in the verified corpus for Principle 8 applied to a PjBL-in-EBD context specifically. Sciacca's first and most pervasive theme — Supporting Student Learning via Building Trusting Relationships — came from all three of her participant teachers independently. M.G. described the mechanism as *"more about the rapport with the students,"* calling it *"crucial for success."* B.Q. framed it as *"really getting that rapport with students and getting to know them and know how far you can push them."* And S.F. reflected on former students calling in to check in after leaving the classroom, which Sciacca interprets as evidence that the relationship is durable beyond the immediate classroom context. Across all three of Sciacca's teachers, trust and rapport preceded — not followed — task progress. This is the exact ordering Principle 8 commits to operationally: the relational check-in comes before the task-progress check-in during teacher rotation. §3.6 on Gomez (2019) — the closest direct analogue in the verified corpus to the Arduino workshop — found that makerspace participation supported social-emotional skill development for middle-school EBD students; notably, Gomez's mechanism was **peer interaction** during makerspace work, which is specifically a mechanism this program does not implement as a default. This is a genuine gap between Gomez's finding and the program's current design, and §9 Honest Limitations restates it.

**Design rule.**
- Workshop sessions open with **Arrival & Board Check** — greet each student by name, brief one-word check-in — and close with a **Close-Out Circle** where each student says one thing they worked on or got stuck on. These are not optional social filler; they are the relationship infrastructure that Principle 8 rests on.
- Teacher rotation during work blocks includes relational check-ins, not just task progress: *"How are you doing with this?"* before *"Where are you on the card?"* A 5-second relational check-in signals to the student that the teacher sees them as a person first, which is the trust infrastructure on which all task work rests.
- The teacher knows each student's current project, current tier, current navigation card, and recent history — and shows that knowledge in the first sentence of every rotation. *"Last session you got the LED to blink — how did that feel?"* is a teacher move the program asks for explicitly.
- **Every student works on their own project, at their own workstation, at their own tier, at their own pace.** The workshop is a small-group physical setting (three to eight students in the same room, sharing a parts library and tools) but the work is individual. Peer collaboration is **not** a first-class mechanism in this version of the program — see the appendix for the reasoning and for what revisiting this decision later would look like.
- **Trust accumulates across sessions.** A student who had a hard day last week gets the same greeting this week. A student who refused Project 3 last session gets the same friendly opening line at next session's Arrival phase. The relationship is durable across hard sessions, not contingent on the student having a good day.

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (Arrival & Board Check, Close-Out Circle, Teacher Rotation with Relational Check-ins), every project in §6 (each project is designed for individual student work with teacher rotation), and the appendix on deferred peer collaboration.

### Principle 9 — Teacher sustainability is a first-class design constraint

**Statement.** The program must be designed so one teacher managing 3–8 students can run a session without burning out. Pre-prepared materials. Self-directed navigation cards. Laminated reference sheets. Clear session structure. Predictable rhythms. If the teacher is exhausted, the whole system fails — and student outcomes collapse first.

**Evidence.** §3.1 on Gilmour, Sandilos, Pilny, Schwartz & Wehby (2022) identifying three teacher profiles (flourishing, buffered, struggling) among teachers of students with EBD and reporting that burnout profile is meaningfully related to observed classroom management quality. §3.5 on Taylor, Allen, Van & Moohr (2024), the study in the corpus that first measured teacher job satisfaction as a PBL outcome, reporting marked improvements from pre-intervention to post-intervention in both teacher and paraprofessional job-satisfaction ratings — preliminary evidence that well-designed PBL can actually *reduce* teacher burden in EBD contexts by shifting the teacher's role from crisis manager to facilitator. **§3.5 on Sciacca (2025) adds the most useful practitioner-voiced articulation of Principle 9's sustainability paradox in the verified corpus.** Sciacca's three participant teachers all reported that PjBL was *more* rewarding than traditional instruction while also being honest about its workload demands. B.Q. summarised: *"Even though it's arguably more work than being a math teacher, it's more rewarding and more fun."* Two of Sciacca's three teachers explicitly said planning is more work, with S.F. observing: *"There is definitely more preparation with it. So being able to gather all of the artifacts and materials needed prior to the lesson obviously is a huge component of it."* But the single most important quote in the entire dissertation for this principle is S.F.'s description of the prep-versus-instruction paradox: *"It's more work to kind of prepare, but less during the instruction, because the kids drive so much more of their own learning."* This is the exact sustainability discipline Principle 9 commits to — front-loading preparation into pre-prepared materials so that during the session the teacher rotates rather than improvises, and the students drive their own work rather than depending on the teacher's moment-to-moment cognitive load. Sciacca's teachers are saying, from their own classrooms, that this discipline is not only sustainable but actively preferable to the alternative. §3.3 on Koslouski, Stark & Chafouleas (2023) explicitly addressing secondary traumatic stress in educators and calling educator well-being *"crucial to remaining healthy and effective"* when working with students carrying trauma histories. §3.1 on Colbert et al. (2025) showing that brief written psychoeducation for staff outperforms long workshops — a finding that shapes the kind of implementation support the program is built around.

**Design rule.**
- Every project ships with **pre-prepared teacher materials**: printed navigation cards ready to laminate, a setup checklist for before students arrive, a rotation plan suggesting which students to start with, a troubleshooting crib sheet.
- No prep work should require the teacher to write from scratch during a session.
- Projects are scoped so they can be run the first time by a teacher who has read the master document once — no additional training required. (Colbert et al.'s finding that brief written materials outperform long workshops directly supports this.)
- The session structure is itself a teacher-sustainability mechanism — predictable rhythms reduce the cognitive load of deciding what happens next.
- The Claude Code prompt scaffolds (Principle 7) are also a teacher-sustainability mechanism — the teacher is not the only debugging resource in the room.
- Budget implication: the program's §8 Budget includes line items for teacher release time, a lightweight between-session check-in protocol, and printable materials — not a heavy training-and-coaching apparatus.

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (pre-prepared materials, lightweight tracking sheet, troubleshooting crib sheets), every project in §6 (each project file includes a teacher-facing pre-flight checklist), and §8 Budget (explicit line items for teacher-sustainability infrastructure).

## §5 Methodology — How the Workshop Runs

§4 described the nine principles that guide the design. §5 describes how those principles become a workshop that actually runs on a Tuesday morning with three to eight students walking in the door.

This section is operational. A teacher who has read §5 should be able to set up the workshop, run a first session, and know what to do when things go wrong. Every subsection below points back at the principle or principles it implements, so a reader can trace a design choice from "why" (§4) to "how" (§5) without guessing.

§5 is organized into nine subsections:

1. **The workshop setting** — group size, duration, frequency, physical space
2. **Session structure** — the seven-phase rhythm
3. **The Navigation Card System** — template, rationale, stuck protocol
4. **The three-tier system** — guided build / guided design / open design
5. **Claude Code as a differentiated coding support** — the three-level access model in operation
6. **Safety protocols** — general workshop safety and the Project 8 flight-safety escalation
7. **When a student refuses the prescribed order** — teacher guidance and a short decision tree
8. **Simple tracking sheet** — what the teacher tracks, what they don't
9. **Teacher rotation and between-session maintenance** — running the room and keeping it runnable

**Note on peer collaboration.** An earlier outline of this section included a subsection on peer collaboration roles. The current version of the program commits to **individual work**: every student works on their own project, at their own workstation, at their own tier, at their own pace. Peer collaboration as a structured mechanism is **deferred** — the appendix on deferred peer collaboration describes what that subsection would have contained and the conditions under which the program would revisit this decision.

### §5.1 The workshop setting

**Group size:** three to eight students from the same mother class. The upper bound (eight) is there because above that a single teacher cannot rotate to every student frequently enough to catch the ones who need help before they disengage (Principle 2, Principle 9). The lower bound (three) is a practical floor rather than a pedagogical requirement — students work individually on their own projects, so the workshop does not structurally depend on a minimum number of students to function. Below three, the workshop becomes a series of 1:1 sessions, which is a viable format but loses the benefit of a consistent workshop environment where multiple students are independently working and where the teacher's rotation rhythm is the norm students learn to work inside.

**Session duration:** **45 minutes** — the length of a standard class period at Agourim School and the constraint the program's session structure is built around. The seven-phase structure (§5.2) is allocated to fit within 45 minutes exactly, with fixed minute targets per phase rather than a flexible range. A program running at a school with a different class length would need to rescale the phase allocations proportionally; the phase *order* and the *presence* of every phase (especially the Movement Break) are non-negotiable even under time pressure.

**Session frequency:** ideally two sessions per week per mother class, spaced so students have time to consolidate between sessions but not so far apart that they lose continuity with their project. A once-a-week cadence also works but reduces the program's total contact hours enough that the three-year implementation roadmap (§10) should be stretched proportionally.

**Physical space:** a dedicated workshop room is strongly preferred over shared classroom space. Students need to be able to leave their workstation set up between sessions — a breadboard with wires in progress, a half-built chassis, a navigation card clipped to the workbench — so that re-entry to the next session takes seconds rather than minutes. The Principle 1 commitment to predictable routine includes predictable physical infrastructure.

**Per-student workstation:** each student has their own workstation, not a shared one. Workstation contents: a Windows 11 PC with Arduino IDE, a breadboard, the student's current navigation card clipped in a predictable location, a small parts bin for today's components, and space to keep their in-progress build visible between sessions.

**Per-student persistent digital workspace on the shared Workshop Drive.** Each student has a personal subfolder inside the shared robotics-workshop Google Drive account. All students, all workshop PCs, and the teacher share one Google account — so "per-student" here means *per-subfolder-under-a-shared-account*, not per-account. The canonical path is: `G:\My Drive\Arduino_Projects\<student_nickname>\<Project_Name>\` — for example `G:\My Drive\Arduino_Projects\Raptor\Project_1_Light_Signals\`. The student's own `.ino` sketches, photos of their build, and any notes live in this folder across sessions. The shared Workshop Drive is mounted on every workshop PC via **Google Drive for Desktop**, which makes the account's "My Drive" appear as a local directory at `G:\My Drive\`, so the student's folder appears in Windows File Explorer exactly like any local folder. **Claude Code at each workstation is launched pointed at the student's current project folder** rather than at a generic scratch location — this means Claude Code can read the student's actual sketch files, suggest edits in place, and iterate with the student on their real code. The per-student folder is created in a one-minute together-ritual between the teacher and the student at the student's first session with each project (see §5.5 and the per-project source files for the ritual's exact steps).

**Privacy architecture and student-chosen nicknames.** Because all students at Agourim share one Google account when they use the robotics workshop, every student with Drive for Desktop open can see every other student's folder — the isolation is by naming convention, not by access control. The student population served by this program is sensitive about their real names appearing in any shared digital space, and many students prefer to use **nicknames of their own choosing** in any context visible beyond the immediate teacher–student relationship. The program honours that preference directly: at the together-ritual (§5.5), the teacher asks the student what nickname they want for their Arduino folder, and that nickname is what gets typed into the `<student_nickname>` segment of the path. **Nicknames are the student's choice, chosen by the student, at the moment of folder creation.** The teacher keeps a separate private mapping of nickname → real student name for tracking-sheet (§5.8) and artifact-completion-log (§7.3) purposes, but the shared Workshop Drive and the folder structure itself only ever show the nickname. This design turns the folder-creation ritual into a small Principle 5 autonomy moment: the student chooses how they are represented in the workshop's digital workspace for the entire year.

Students' own education Drive accounts remain separate and are used only as a secondary option if a student wants to copy finished work to their personal Drive at the end of a project. This is a student-initiated action at the end, not a default workflow.

**Shared infrastructure:** a parts library organised by component type (resistors sorted by value, LEDs by colour, sensors by function); a set of tools (wire strippers, multimeters, screwdrivers, soldering stations once Project 4 introduces soldering); reference posters (colour-code charts, common wiring diagrams, safety reminders); and a dedicated low-stimulation cool-down corner with a chair and a fidget object for any student who needs to step out briefly without leaving the room. **The shared robotics-workshop Google Drive account** (with its per-student subfolder structure) and **Google Drive for Desktop installed on every workshop PC** are also part of the shared infrastructure — the Drive is the program's persistent digital counterpart to the physical parts library.

### §5.2 Session structure — the seven-phase rhythm

The session follows the same seven phases every time, allocated to fit within the **45-minute class period** at Agourim School. **The phase order and the presence of every phase are identical across every session, every project, every tier** — Principle 1 (predictable routine is load-bearing) is implemented here above all other places. The two independent work blocks are 15 minutes each, which aligns directly with Principle 4's hyper-chunked 15-minute milestone structure: each milestone in the student's navigation cards is designed to complete in roughly one work block.

| # | Phase | Minutes (45-min session) | What happens | Teacher's role |
|---|-------|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Arrival & Board Check** | 3 | Students arrive, greet the teacher, check their personal task board to see where they left off last session and what today's starting point is | Greet each student by name. Brief one-word check-in: *"How are you today?"* A one-word answer is fine; silence is fine too |
| 2 | **Mini-Huddle** | 3 | Whole group gathers (standing, central table). Teacher announces: *"Today everyone works on their next step. I'll start with [name], then rotate."* Acknowledge anyone who reached a milestone last session | Set expectations. Name any change to normal routine with an explicit reason |
| 3 | **Independent Work Block 1** | 15 | Students work on their project using their navigation card. Timer visible. | Rotate: spend ~3 minutes with each student who needs support. Relational check-in first, task progress second (Principle 8) |
| 4 | **Movement Break** | 3 | Everyone stands up, walks to get water, stretches, walks around the room once. Structured, not free-for-all | Required for all. Non-negotiable. Reduces restlessness and anxiety buildup. Principle 6 is load-bearing here |
| 5 | **Independent Work Block 2** | 15 | Continue project work. | Rotate to students you did not reach in Block 1. Finish rotations. Start flagging clean-up 2 minutes before Block 2 ends |
| 6 | **Clean-Up** | 3 | Tools away, workspace tidy, navigation card returned to its clip. Same routine every time | Consistent. Every student responsible for their own station. Help a student clean up *with* them if they are dysregulated; don't clean up *for* them |
| 7 | **Close-Out Circle** | 3 | Each student says one thing: *"Today I worked on ___"* or *"I got stuck on ___."* Students can pass. Thumbs-up/thumbs-down is also a valid answer | Listen. Validate. Do **not** problem-solve at close-out — problems raised here go on the navigation card for the student to work on next session. Principle 8 infrastructure |
| | **Total** | **45** | | |

**The 45-minute budget is tight — 30 minutes of actual work time.** Fifteen minutes per session are spent on routine (arrival, huddle, movement break, clean-up, close-out). That ratio (30 minutes work, 15 minutes routine — roughly 2:1) is the cost of running a genuinely routine-first workshop in a 45-minute class period, and it is a cost the program accepts because the routine *is* the intervention for this population, not an overhead on top of it. A program that tries to recover work minutes by cutting the Movement Break or the Close-Out Circle loses Principle 6 or Principle 8 respectively — both are load-bearing and both are non-negotiable.

**Why this specific structure matters:**

- **Predictability reduces anxiety** and the need for students to ask the teacher *"what are we doing?"* (Principle 1).
- **Chunked time blocks with a break support ADHD attention spans** (Principle 6).
- **The Movement Break in the middle is non-negotiable** because Principle 6's research finding on physical exercise for ADHD executive function is the strongest effect-size evidence in the whole program's literature review. The break is a first-class intervention, not a concession.
- **The Close-Out Circle builds self-awareness and language for self-monitoring** — naming the work done or the point of stuck is the beginning of the self-management skill Harrison et al. (2019) identified as meeting What Works Clearinghouse evidence-based standards for ADHD.
- **The Arrival Board Check is an antecedent strategy** — it sets the student up to know what to do BEFORE they need to ask the teacher (Principle 2, Principle 5).

**Deviations.** Any deviation from the seven-phase structure is announced at the Mini-Huddle with an explicit reason. *"Today we have 10 extra minutes because the bell is later, so Block 2 will run longer."* Never a surprise. Never *"we're skipping the movement break because we're behind"* — if anything is getting skipped, the movement break is the very last thing to go.

### §5.3 The Navigation Card System

The Navigation Card is the most important practical tool in the program. It is how Principle 2 (*the navigation card is the boss, not the teacher*) becomes a concrete object a student can hold in their hand.

**What a Navigation Card is.** A single physical sheet — laminated or in a clear sleeve — at each student's workstation. It tells the student five things:

1. What they are building (with a picture).
2. The step they are on right now.
3. Exactly what to do for that step.
4. How they will know when the step is done.
5. What to do if they are stuck, **before** asking the teacher.

**Navigation Card template** (printable version in the appendix):

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  PROJECT: [name]          STUDENT: [name]        │
│  ────────────────────────────────────────────── │
│                                                   │
│  ★ CURRENT STEP: Step [#] of [total in tier]     │
│                                                   │
│  WHAT TO DO:                                      │
│  □ [clear, concrete action 1]                     │
│  □ [clear, concrete action 2]                     │
│  □ [clear, concrete action 3]                     │
│                                                   │
│  DONE WHEN:                                       │
│  → [observable result — "the LED lights up       │
│    when you press the button"]                    │
│                                                   │
│  CLAUDE CODE LEVEL: [1 / 2 / 3]                  │
│  → Level 1: Upload the sketch at [file]          │
│  → Level 2: Modify this part: [specific target]  │
│  → Level 3: Describe what you want to change     │
│                                                   │
│  STUCK? TRY THIS FIRST:                          │
│  1. Re-read the step above                        │
│  2. Check the reference card in your folder       │
│  3. Look at the example on the demo shelf         │
│  4. Ask a neighbour: "Can you look at this?"      │
│  5. Put the RED card up — teacher will come      │
│                                                   │
│  ☐ STEP COMPLETE → Move clip to next step         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Key design elements:**

- **Chunking into small, concrete steps** reduces EF demand (Principle 1, Principle 3). Each navigation card covers a maximum of **3–5 sub-actions**, and each sub-action is verb-first and observable ("Connect the red wire from the battery to the switch" — not "Set up the power").
- **The "Done when" criterion** gives a clear finish line (Principle 4). It is always observable — a light on, a motor spinning, a value on the serial monitor. Never "the wiring is correct" (not observable without a test) — always "the LED lights up when you press the button" (observable).
- **The Claude Code Level field** tells the student which of the three levels (Principle 7) is available for this step. Not every step involves code — steps that are pure hardware have this field blank.
- **The "Stuck? Try this first" protocol** is a self-management scaffold (Principle 2). It gives the student a structured sequence of actions to take before escalating to the teacher. This teaches students to problem-solve and reduces teacher interruptions at the same time.
- **The Red / Green signal card** is a non-verbal escalation mechanism. When a student has completed the stuck protocol and still needs help, they put up a red card — the teacher sees it during the next rotation. No calling out, no hand-raising under pressure, no interrupting the teacher's work with another student. This reduces anxiety about asking for help and reduces impulsive blurting.
- **Physical checkbox and clip movement** — each step completion is a small tactile action that tracks progress. Each checkmark is a small dopamine hit (Principle 4).

**How many navigation cards per project.** It depends on the tier and on the project, but the rule is that no student sees more than the next 2–3 navigation cards at once. When a student finishes the current card, the teacher replaces it with the next one during rotation. This prevents the overwhelm of seeing "Step 4 of 27" on day one.

**What the navigation card is NOT.** It is not a script the teacher reads aloud. It is not a worksheet the student fills in with answers. It is not a rubric for grading. It is the student's working tool during the session, and the teacher refers to it instead of giving verbal multi-step instructions.

### §5.4 The three-tier system

Every project in the program is built at three tiers simultaneously. The same project — the same hardware, the same learning goal, the same "the LED lights up" moment of success — can be worked on by a student at Tier 1 (who receives maximum scaffolding) and by a student at Tier 3 (who designs their own version of the project) in the same session.

**Tier 1 — Guided Build.** Maximum predictability, clear steps, frequent success experiences. The student follows a step-by-step build guide with pictures of what each step should look like. Decision points are minimal. Success is visible and concrete: *"it moves," "it lights up," "it follows the line."* Independence level is high — a student at Tier 1 can follow their navigation card without the teacher for 15+ minutes at a time, which is exactly the independence the teacher needs to rotate. Typical profile: high anxiety, new to the workshop, severe EF deficits, post-trauma recovery, or a student on a hard day who normally works at Tier 2 or Tier 3.

**Tier 2 — Guided Design.** Moderate structure with genuine choice points. The project has a defined goal but the student makes 2–3 design choices along the way. Task cards include decision points: *"Choose ONE: Option A or Option B."* Reference materials are available (photos, short videos, example builds). The student tests, adjusts, and iterates within a constrained design space. Typical profile: students with ADHD who need engagement + novelty, students with ODD-flavored profiles who need meaningful autonomy, students with moderate anxiety who can handle limited ambiguity if supported.

**Tier 3 — Open Design.** Low structure, high autonomy. The student defines the problem and designs the solution. The navigation card becomes a **project planner** rather than step-by-step instructions — phases like PLAN → BUILD → CODE → TEST → SHOW, with the student filling in the content of each phase. The teacher serves as consultant, not instructor. Independence level is high, but the student needs periodic check-ins to avoid scope creep or frustration. Typical profile: ODD-flavored students who respond strongly to autonomy and respect, high-ability ADHD students who need novelty and complexity to stay engaged, and any student who has mastered Tier 1 and Tier 2 versions of earlier projects.

**Tier assignment is fluid, not fixed.** The program's hardest rule about tiers is that *a student is not a tier*. A student can be at Tier 3 for Project 1, drop to Tier 2 for Project 2 because the hardware is new and they want more scaffolding, jump to Tier 3 for Project 3, and drop to Tier 1 for a single session in the middle of Project 4 because they arrived that day in a state where Tier 3 would not be possible. This movement is not regression, not promotion — it is adaptive response to current state (§2 Population Profile, Principle 1's finding that EF is a state that rises and falls with stress). The teacher never says *"you used to be at Tier 3, why are you at Tier 1 today?"* — the teacher says *"today's card is ready, let's see what you want to work on."*

**How a new student's starting tier is determined.** Start every new student at Tier 1 of their first project, regardless of any prior reputation. Safety and success first. After one or two Tier 1 successes, the teacher asks: *"Do you want to try the next project where you make more of the choices?"* — and the student decides. No forced promotion.

**How to assign tiers across the current group.** Not by diagnosis. A student with severe ADHD might be a Tier 3 builder because they are highly skilled and need the challenge. A student with ODD-flavored presentation might start at Tier 1 because they are new and need to experience success before they trust the workshop enough for autonomy. The teacher's firsthand knowledge of the student — their current state, their recent history, what went well last session — is irreplaceable and no written profile can substitute for it.

**Tier and project order are independent.** A student can be at Tier 1 of Project 3 while another student is at Tier 3 of Project 1. The tier is about the student's current readiness; the project is about which hardware they are working with. §5.8 returns to the question of project order more directly.

### §5.5 Claude Code as a differentiated coding support and a scaffolded tutorial channel

Principle 7 is implemented operationally through **two distinct channels**, each differentiated so that every student can enter the workshop's content at the level of support they actually need today. Channel A is the pair-programmer role — students engaging with code through a three-level access model. Channel B is the scaffolded-tutorial role — students receiving a conversational walk-through of a navigation card. Both channels sit alongside the printed navigation card; neither replaces it. A student can use Channel A, Channel B, both, or neither on any given milestone, and a student who prefers to work entirely from the printed card without involving Claude Code at all is also welcome. The two-channel design is the program's response to the heterogeneity of the student population — some students arrive ready to direct an AI and some do not; some students parse dense printed text easily and some do not — and the goal is to give every student an entry point that feels approachable.

**How Claude Code is invoked at each workstation.** Claude Code is not used as a generic browser tab. At each workstation, Claude Code is **launched pointed at the student's current project folder** inside the shared robotics-workshop Google Drive — for example `G:\My Drive\Arduino_Projects\Raptor\Project_1_Light_Signals\` (where *Raptor* is the nickname that specific student chose for their workshop folder; see §5.1 on the nickname architecture). This folder lives in the shared Workshop account's `My Drive` section (see §5.1), mounted as a local Windows directory via **Google Drive for Desktop**, so Claude Code operates on it exactly like any local folder. The effect is that Claude Code can **read the student's actual sketch files**, suggest edits in place, and iterate with the student on their real code — not just respond to abstract prompts. A student on Channel A Level 2 asking Claude *"how do I make the LED blink every half second instead of every second?"* is asking about the specific `02_blink_external.ino` file that is open in their Arduino IDE at that moment, and Claude can reference it by name. A student on Channel B asking *"I'm on Project 1, Tier 1, Milestone 4 — walk me through it"* is working inside a folder Claude Code can also see, so the walk-through can reference the exact files the student is opening and the exact state of their project.

**Per-student project folder creation — the together-ritual.** At the student's first session with each project, the teacher and the student create the project's folder together as a one-minute setup ritual. The ritual has five parts, in this order:

1. **The teacher asks the student what nickname they want for their Arduino folder.** If it is the student's first time in the workshop ever, this is also the moment the student picks the nickname they will use across all eight Arduino projects for the year. The student chooses — a fantasy character, a favourite animal, a made-up word, their real first name if they prefer, anything they are comfortable seeing on a screen with other students in the room. The teacher writes the nickname in their own private tracking sheet (§5.8) alongside the student's real name, but the nickname is what goes into the folder path from here on.
2. **The student opens File Explorer** on the workshop PC and navigates to `G:\My Drive\Arduino_Projects\`.
3. **If the student's own nickname folder does not exist yet** (first time in the workshop), the teacher and the student create it together: the student types their chosen nickname as the folder name, or the teacher types it while the student watches, depending on the student's keyboard comfort.
4. **Inside the student's nickname folder, the student creates the project subfolder** — for Project 1, that is `Project_1_Light_Signals`. Same together-typing arrangement.
5. **The student opens Claude Code** and launches it pointed at the new project subfolder as its working directory.

**The teacher says, during the ritual, a short recognition line**: *"This is your folder, this is your project, Project 1 starts now."* The exact words matter less than the recognition — the student is being welcomed into their own workspace by an adult who knows their name, knows their nickname, and has just watched them take ownership of the folder that will hold their work for the whole project. The line is deliberately short because it is meant to be spoken in one breath, not as a speech.

The ritual takes approximately one minute if the student is keyboard-comfortable and has a nickname ready, and up to three or four minutes if the student needs time to pick a nickname or is unsure about typing. Both timings are fine — the ritual's purpose is the moment of ownership and the recognition line, not the clock.

Per-project source files (e.g., `Arduino_Projects/Project_1_Light_Signals/Arduino_Project_1.md`) embed the folder-creation ritual as the first student-facing step of the first milestone, with the exact clicks the student makes and the teacher's recognition line written on the navigation card in student-readable language. The Sciacca (2025) building-trusting-relationships sub-theme (§3.5) is part of the motivation here — the folder-creation ritual is a micro-relationship moment that costs one minute and returns real ownership plus a student-chosen nickname that carries across the entire year.

**Why the persistent-folder pattern matters pedagogically.** A student's Project 1 sketches, modifications, photos, and notes all live in the same folder across sessions. A student who spent Session 1 wiring and uploading returns to Session 2 with everything exactly where they left off — their sketches, their Tier 2 modifications, their own attempts at custom patterns. A student at Project 4 who wants to look back at their Project 1 code to remember how a digital-output pin worked can open their own `Project_1_Light_Signals` folder and do so. And at the end of the year, the student has eight folders of their own work sitting in the shared Workshop Drive — a tangible portfolio of *things I built* that supports §7.1 Cluster 1 (the super-target of small, positive, empowering experiences). The portfolio is not a side-effect; it is a deliberate output of the program's storage architecture.

#### Channel A — Claude Code as pair programmer (three-level access model)

Channel A is implemented as a **three-level access model** that lives on the navigation card and in the project files. A student never has to declare their level out loud — the navigation card shows all three, and the student chooses (or the teacher and student choose together) which entry point is right for today.

**Level 1 — Pre-written sketch upload.** The student opens a pre-prepared `.ino` file that matches their wiring and clicks Upload. The hardware responds. The student has just completed a code-related task without writing, editing, or debugging code. For students who arrive at the workshop with coding trauma, with low English literacy, or with any anxiety about "doing it wrong," this is the path to their first positive experience with code — *something I did made the thing work*.

**Level 2 — Modify with Claude Code's help.** The student opens a partially-complete sketch with one specific thing to change — change the blink rate, change the LED pin, change the threshold distance. The navigation card tells the student exactly which line or variable to touch, and the Claude Code prompt scaffold says: *"I want to make the LED blink every half second instead of every second. Here is my current code: [paste the line]. How do I change it?"* The student runs the prompt, reviews Claude Code's response, makes the change, tests it, and sees the result.

**Level 3 — Free dialogue with Claude Code.** The student describes what they are trying to build, shows Claude Code their current attempt, and iterates in dialogue. The navigation card gives the student a **prompt template** — not a prompt — that says: *"Before asking Claude, fill in: (a) what you want to happen, (b) what's currently happening, (c) what you've tried."* The student writes out (a), (b), (c), and then sends the whole thing to Claude Code. This discipline is non-negotiable: the student must describe the problem before asking for a solution. The program's inference from self-determination theory (Principle 7's evidence paragraph) is that an AI which dispenses answers on demand would function more like extrinsic reward, whereas an AI which asks questions back and requires the student to describe the problem first preserves student agency more in line with intrinsic-motivation support.

**Comprehension check at every Channel A level.** After each Claude Code interaction (at any level), the student answers one comprehension question on the navigation card:

- **Level 1:** *"What does your device do right now that it did not do before you uploaded the code?"*
- **Level 2:** *"After Claude helped you, can you explain in one sentence what changed?"*
- **Level 3:** *"Can you say in one sentence what Claude helped you figure out?"*

The point of the comprehension check is not to grade the student. The point is to ensure the student has engaged enough with the code to notice *what* the AI changed, which is the minimum floor for the interaction being a learning experience rather than a copy-paste.

**Level switching is fluid.** A student at Level 3 for Project 6 might be at Level 1 for Project 7 if Project 7 uses a new library they have never seen. A student who is having a hard day can say *"I want to upload Level 1 for everything today"* and the teacher says *"that's fine, let's do that."* The levels are differentiation tools, not achievement badges.

**What the teacher watches for at each Channel A level.**

- **At Level 1:** is the student getting a positive experience of the hardware working? If yes, that is success. If the upload is failing or the hardware is not responding, the teacher troubleshoots the *hardware*, not the code.
- **At Level 2:** is the student making the change meaningfully, and do they understand what changed? If the student is mechanically copying Claude Code's output without reading it, the teacher gently redirects: *"Before you paste, can you tell me what Claude is suggesting?"*
- **At Level 3:** is the student describing the problem before asking, and are they iterating (not just accepting the first response)? If a student is pasting Claude Code's output without review, the teacher redirects to the comprehension check before the student moves on.

#### Channel B — Claude Code as scaffolded tutorial (conversational walk-through)

Channel B addresses a different need. Many students in this population read below their chronological grade level, experience dense printed instructions as overwhelming, or simply process information better through conversational back-and-forth than through silent reading. For these students, having the navigation card's content *also* available as a voice-and-text dialogue with Claude Code turns an accessibility barrier into a usable entry point. Channel B is the program's implementation of that support.

**How a student invokes Channel B.** The student opens Claude Code and tells it which project, tier, and navigation card they are on — for example: *"I'm on Project 2, Tier 2, navigation card 3. Walk me through it."* Claude Code, primed by the project's tutorial-channel scaffold, responds with the card's content in conversational form, breaks it into small steps, and asks follow-up questions at each checkpoint. The student can ask Claude to go deeper on any step, to repeat something, or to explain an unfamiliar word. At each milestone on the printed card, Claude pauses and asks the student to confirm the step is done before continuing.

**What the tutorial-channel scaffold is.** Each project ships with a **Claude Code tutorial-channel scaffold** stored alongside the printed cards and the HTML tutorial. The scaffold is a short system prompt plus per-milestone invocation templates. It tells Claude Code how to walk a student through the navigation card set conversationally, what checkpoint questions to ask, and what tone to use (calm, unhurried, non-condescending). Teachers do not write these scaffolds during a session — they come pre-built with the project artifact family.

**Channel B is available to students at any tier, including Tier 1.** The conversational format of "read me what to do next" is cognitively simpler than the code-level dialogue of Channel A Level 3 — it is closer to being read a story than to debugging together. A Tier 1 student who is not yet ready for AI dialogue about their code can still use Channel B to receive their navigation card conversationally, because the interaction is receptive (Claude leads, the student confirms) rather than productive (the student has to describe a coding problem).

**Channel B does not replace the printed card.** This is important. The student still holds the physical card. The student still checks off each milestone on the physical card as they complete it. The teacher, rotating through, can still see the same card the student is working from. The two formats run in parallel: the printed card is the anchor and the visible artifact of progress; Channel B is a supplementary mode of delivery for students who benefit from it.

**Channel B still carries the "describe the problem first" discipline.** If a student is stuck, Channel B does not immediately dispense the answer — Claude asks the student what they are stuck on, what they have tried, and what the hardware is currently doing, and only then offers a next step. A student using Channel B is directing a walk-through, not passively consuming instructions.

**What the teacher watches for with Channel B.**

- Is the student progressing through the printed card's milestones in step with the conversational walk-through? If the student has stopped checking off milestones but is still talking to Claude, that is a signal to rotate to them and see what is happening.
- Is the student becoming over-reliant on Channel B for work they could reasonably do from the printed card alone? The answer is usually *no, let them keep using it* — Channel B exists precisely because the printed card alone is not the right medium for every student. But if a student who previously worked directly from the card has started using Channel B for every step on every card, the teacher may quietly check in to understand what changed.
- Is the student's confidence growing across sessions, or staying flat? A student who uses Channel B in Project 1, Channel B + Channel A Level 1 in Project 2, and Channel A Level 2 directly from the printed card in Project 3 is showing exactly the growth trajectory the two-channel design is meant to enable.

**The teacher setup checklist notes which students are currently using Channel B**, so the teacher's rotation check-ins can reference the student's position in the walk-through conversation as well as on the printed card. This is a one-line annotation, not a surveillance tool — the goal is to let the teacher meet the student where they are, not to track Channel B usage as a metric.

**Honest note on Channel B.** The scaffolded-tutorial channel is a novel feature with no direct empirical support in the verified corpus — §9 Honest Limitations flags both channels of Principle 7 as piloted practices. The program commits to Channel B because the alternative (expecting every student to parse every navigation card independently) would effectively exclude students who most need the workshop to work for them, and because Channel B is a low-risk addition: it supplements rather than replaces the printed materials, and a student who does not use it is not disadvantaged in any way.

### §5.6 Safety protocols

The workshop has a baseline safety discipline that applies across all eight projects, plus a dedicated escalation for Project 8 (the quadcopter capstone) because flight carries safety stakes that ground projects do not.

**Baseline safety (Projects 1–7).**

- **Eye protection** when wire-clipping, soldering, or running any motor at speed. Safety glasses live at each workstation. The teacher wears them too — modeling, not exception.
- **Soldering discipline** (introduced in Project 4, reinforced in Projects 5–7): hot iron on its stand whenever not in the hand; damp sponge for cleaning the tip; never solder over a lap or near skin; always solder on a heat-resistant mat; hair and loose sleeves tied back.
- **Battery discipline**: LiPo batteries live in a fireproof storage bag (a sand bucket is also acceptable) when not installed in a device; never charge a LiPo unattended; never puncture, crush, or short-circuit a LiPo; damaged LiPos are discharged in saltwater and recycled, not thrown in regular trash.
- **Power-off discipline**: never modify wiring on a powered device; disconnect the battery before changing components; test with a multimeter before first power-up.
- **Tool discipline**: wire strippers are not pliers, pliers are not hammers, screwdrivers are not pry bars. Use the right tool, or put the wrong tool down and ask for the right one.
- **Parts cleanup**: clipped wire ends go into a dedicated bin, not on the floor where someone will step on them.

These are taught at Project 1 as general habits, reinforced at Project 4 when soldering is introduced, and reinforced again at every project that adds a new safety surface.

**Project 8 escalation — flight safety.**

Project 8 adds a safety envelope that Projects 1–7 do not have. A tiny quadcopter with exposed propellers rotating at ~50,000 RPM can cut fingers, scratch eyes, and damage equipment. The program treats Project 8's safety protocol as first-class and non-negotiable, and the full detail lives in the Project 8 file (§6 and the per-project file). The baseline rules are:

- **Propeller guards are mandatory** for the initial test and practice phases. Guards can be removed only for final performance flights, and only with explicit teacher supervision.
- **Eye protection is mandatory during any flight test**, including bench thrust tests, not just free flight.
- **Tethered flight is mandatory for Tier 1 and Tier 2 first flights**. A fishing line or kite string attached to a fixed anchor prevents the drone from flying away or hitting a student who is too close.
- **One at a time**. Only one student arms a drone at any moment. All other students are at least 2 metres back and behind a visible line marked on the floor.
- **Defined indoor flight zone**: a 3-metre-diameter clear circle in an empty classroom corner or a gym corner. Spectators (including teachers) stand outside the circle during flight.
- **Pre-flight checklist** (the teacher runs this with each student before each flight): battery voltage is above 3.7 V, propellers are tight and undamaged, wiring is not snagged, the flight zone is clear, eye protection is on, the student is calm and ready.
- **LiPo fire emergency procedure**: sand bucket or fireproof bag within reach, never use water on a LiPo fire, fireproof mat under the charging station.
- **Medical alert protocol**: if a student has an anxiety spike or freezes during their first flight, disarm the drone immediately, put it down, walk the student to the cool-down corner, and try again another session. Never push a student to complete a first flight when they are not ready.

**Why Project 8 gets this escalation.** The risk is physical, not only psychological. A panicked student in the flight zone with an armed drone is a student at risk of injury. The program's commitment to the super-target of *small, positive, empowering experiences* requires that those experiences not come with a bandage — Project 8's safety protocol is the infrastructure that lets the empowering experience of flight actually happen without the shadow of physical harm.

### §5.7 When a student refuses the prescribed order

Some students — particularly those whose school history includes many negative encounters with *"do it in this sequence"* — will not work through the eight projects in the prescribed order. They will want to start with Project 8 because drones are exciting. They will refuse Project 3 because the example robot reminds them of a previous failure. They will insist on building a camera-equipped car in Project 1 because a camera is what they actually care about.

The program treats refusal-of-order as **information, not defiance**. A student who refuses today is telling the teacher something real — what they are and are not ready for, what excites them, what they need right now. The teacher's first response is conversational, not corrective: *"Tell me what would make today worth your time."*

**Four tools for responding to refusal.**

1. **Skills-based prerequisites, not project-based prerequisites.** The eight projects are sequenced because each introduces skills later projects build on — soldering in Project 4, I2C in Project 6, camera streaming in Project 7 — not because the order is intrinsically meaningful. A student who wants to start with Project 8 needs the skills Project 8 uses, not the prior project numbers themselves. When a student wants to jump ahead, the teacher identifies the specific skills the target project needs and delivers them as short, targeted mini-modules ("here is the 15 minutes of soldering practice you need before we wire the motors") rather than blocking the student at the doorway.

2. **Non-linear entry.** A student can start wherever they want. If they insist on Project 5, the teacher opens Project 5's navigation card set and says *"here is what you would be doing today; here are the things you will need to learn along the way that earlier projects would normally teach; here is the first navigation card."* The student decides whether the trade-off is worth it — and often, a student who insisted on Project 5 will voluntarily go back to Project 1 or 2 after seeing the skills gap, because the teacher gave them the choice rather than imposing it.

3. **Just-in-time scaffolding for skipped prerequisites.** The teacher prepares a small set of 15-minute "mini-module" navigation cards covering the discrete skills that normally accrue across Projects 1–7: one for soldering, one for reading a schematic, one for the serial monitor, one for I2C basics, one for motor-driver wiring, one for the Arduino IDE file structure. When a student skips ahead, the teacher delivers the specific mini-module they need at the moment they need it. No prerequisite is "ahead" if the student needs it now.

4. **Refusal as diagnostic.** A student who refuses a specific project on a specific day is telling the teacher something — maybe the hardware looks like something they failed with before, maybe they had a fight at home, maybe the social dynamic in today's group is wrong, maybe the teacher's enthusiasm sounded like pressure. The teacher's response is to ask what would make today worth the student's time, and then to act on whatever the student says — even if the answer is *"I want to sort the resistor bin today"* or *"I want to take apart last week's build and look at how I did the wiring."* Those are both legitimate answers, and both are activities that keep the student in the workshop, engaged with hardware, and building trust with the teacher for next session.

**Decision tree for the first 60 seconds after a refusal:**

1. **Is the student safe and stable?** If not, safety first. Walk with them to the cool-down corner, sit with them briefly. Whatever you were going to do about the project waits.
2. **Has the student said *why*?** If they have, thank them and take the answer seriously. If they have not, ask once: *"Tell me what would make today worth your time."* If they shrug or go silent, that is also an answer — offer the lowest-effort Level 1 option and move on. Do not interrogate.
3. **Is the refusal a one-off or part of a pattern?** A one-off gets a flexible response (an alternative activity, a different project, a quieter workstation). A pattern — three refusals in a row of the same project, or refusals of every project in a row — is a conversation with the student outside of session time, not a within-session fix.
4. **Has the teacher been giving this student enough quiet time?** Sometimes refusal is a protest against feeling watched. Give the student the alternative, step away, rotate to other students, and come back in 10 minutes.

**What the teacher does not do.** Argue about the order. Insist that the prescribed sequence is for the student's own good. Make the student feel that refusing has cost them something. Bring consequences or remove access to later projects. None of these responses work for this population, and several of them actively make the next refusal more likely.

**What the teacher does do.** Document the refusal on the tracking sheet (§5.9), note the conversation, and check in with the student at the next session's Arrival phase. Refusals are data, and the pattern across refusals is more useful than any single refusal.

### §5.8 Simple tracking sheet

The teacher tracks three things per student per session, and nothing else. The goal is a sheet so simple that filling it in takes less than 30 seconds per student and the teacher can actually do it during the Close-Out Circle rather than at home in the evening.

| Student | Started work within 5 minutes? (Y/N) | Completed at least 1 milestone today? (Y/N) | Red card or behavioural incident? (Y/N + one-line note) |
|---|---|---|---|

**Why these three and only these three.**

- **Started within 5 minutes** is the single best proxy for whether the student is experiencing the workshop as a safe place to try. A student who consistently takes 15 minutes to start is telling the teacher something.
- **Completed at least 1 milestone** is the single best proxy for whether the student's tier and navigation card are matched to their current state. A student who never completes a milestone is at the wrong tier, or the milestones are too big, or something else is blocking them.
- **Red card or incident** is the single best proxy for whether the student's regulation was stable during the session. One-line notes ("panicked during the wiring," "refused Project 3 again," "did well, wanted more time") build a pattern across sessions.

**What the teacher does not track.** Academic performance. Behavioural points. Participation grades. Skill mastery rubrics. None of these are necessary for running the workshop, and every additional measurement is a task that cuts into rotation time without producing actionable information.

**What the teacher does with the tracking data.** Reviews it weekly (10 minutes), notices patterns (*"Student X has not completed a milestone in three sessions — navigation cards are too big"*), and adjusts — at the tier level, the navigation card level, or the conversation level. The tracking sheet is not a reporting instrument; it is a feedback signal to the teacher.

### §5.9 Teacher rotation and between-session maintenance

**Rotation during the session.** The teacher's rotation plan for each session is: start with the 2–3 students most likely to need help today (based on last session's tracking sheet), finish rotations with the students who are typically fine, and reserve 2–3 minutes of the movement break to check the navigation-card queue for any student who finished their current card during Block 1.

A student at Tier 1 typically needs a rotation every 10–15 minutes (short check-in, confirm the next card, move on). A student at Tier 3 typically needs a rotation every 20–30 minutes (longer consultation, help with a design decision, push back on scope creep). The teacher plans the rotation around these different cadences rather than rotating to everyone equally.

**Relational check-in before task progress.** Principle 8 in action. The first thing the teacher says during a rotation is not *"where are you?"* — it is *"how's it going?"* A 5-second relational check-in signals to the student that the teacher sees them as a person first, which is the trust infrastructure on which all task work rests.

**Between-session maintenance (10–15 minutes after each session).**

- Review the tracking sheet. Note patterns.
- Rotate navigation cards — remove completed cards, add the next card for each student, confirm the Claude Code level for the next step.
- Replenish parts bins. Components that ran out get restocked from the parts library.
- Note any equipment that failed or was damaged, and flag it for repair before next session.
- Write one sentence in a session log about what went well and what was hard. The log is a self-care tool for the teacher as much as a pedagogical tool — it prevents the teacher from carrying the hard session home in their head without processing.

**Spontaneous verbalisation is welcome.** A student who thinks out loud while building — talking to the hardware, narrating their wiring, asking questions of the air — is using a self-regulation behaviour that research has linked to ADHD problem-solving (§3.6 on Kuzmina & Romero 2025's narrow qualitative observation, framed through Corkum et al. 2008's earlier finding on task-relevant external private speech). The teacher does not shush this behaviour. If it is disruptive to a neighbour, the teacher may gently suggest working in a different corner of the room for that session, but the verbalisation itself is a feature, not a bug.

**Between-session maintenance that does NOT happen.** Rewriting navigation cards from scratch. Designing new projects in response to a single student's feedback. Evaluating whether the program is "working" based on one session. The program is designed so the teacher's between-session time is short, focused, and sustainable (Principle 9), not open-ended.

## §6 Projects Overview — Eight projects, one student at a time

§4 described the nine design principles. §5 described how the workshop runs on a Tuesday morning. §6 describes **what the students actually build**.

The program is built around eight projects, sequenced from a first-session LED circuit through a tiny ESP32 quadcopter capstone. Each project introduces a small number of new skills, builds on what the earlier projects taught, and produces a tangible working device the student can take home or keep at their workstation. Each project is designed for an individual student working at their own workstation — not for pairs, not for small groups. And each project exists in three tier variants (Tier 1 guided build, Tier 2 guided design, Tier 3 open design), so three students working on "the same" project may be building meaningfully different things.

This section does three things. First, it gives a one-page overview of the whole eight-project slate so a reader can see the arc at a glance (§6.1). Second, it describes the skill progression chain and explains why projects are sequenced the way they are (§6.2). Third, it gives a short per-project profile for each of the eight projects (§6.4–§6.11), covering what the student builds, what hardware is used, what the three tiers look like, what new skills the project introduces, what the Claude Code scaffolding looks like, and — where relevant — what honest caveats apply to the project's design.

The full per-project detail — every milestone, every navigation card, every pre-written sketch, every Claude Code prompt scaffold, every reference card, every wiring diagram — lives in the per-project files in `Arduino_Projects/Arduino_Project_1.md` through `Arduino_Project_8.md`. §6 is the overview; those files are the operational detail. A teacher running the workshop uses the per-project files on Tuesday morning; a reviewer evaluating the program reads §6.

**How to read the session estimates.** A "session" is one 45-minute class period at Agourim School, of which approximately 30 minutes are actual work time (the other 15 minutes are arrival, mini-huddle, movement break, clean-up, and close-out circle — see §5.2). The estimates assume a student working steadily at a tier that matches their current readiness; a student at Tier 1 may take fewer sessions because they are uploading pre-written sketches rather than writing them, and a student at Tier 3 may take more sessions because they are designing rather than following. The estimates are planning anchors, not targets, and the program's commitment to student pacing (Principle 5) means the teacher does not push a student to finish a project on schedule.

**Why exactly eight projects.** Eight is the number that fits a typical two-semester cohort with margin — a teacher running the program for the first time should not feel rushed, and a student who is moving slowly should not run out of projects before the semester ends. Eight is also the number that gives the skill progression enough room to breathe: each project genuinely introduces a new skill rather than being a variation on the previous one. Fewer than eight would force skill-stacking into the remaining projects (cramming two new skills into Project 4, for example) and would make Project 8 the quadcopter capstone arrive too early. More than eight would dilute the sense of arc and would strain the teacher's artifact-preparation time (Principle 9).

### §6.2 The skill progression chain

The eight projects are sequenced because each one introduces a discrete skill that later projects build on. The sequencing is pedagogical, not arbitrary — a student who wants to jump ahead (§5.7) can, but the teacher will need to deliver just-in-time mini-modules covering the skills the skipped projects would normally have taught.

The progression is:

- **Project 1** introduces the Arduino IDE workflow (Upload button, Serial Monitor, compile errors), the breadboard, digital output (`digitalWrite`), digital input (`digitalRead`), and the first experience of Claude Code as pair programmer (at Level 1) and as tutorial channel (Channel B for students who need it).
- **Project 2** introduces `millis()`-based timing, simple state tracking, and the idea that the Arduino can do more than one thing at once (the reaction-time game runs a timer and watches for button presses simultaneously). Students start writing small code modifications at Level 2 for the first time.
- **Project 3** introduces sensor reading — analog thinking even though the HC-SR04 is digital — and the idea of thresholds: *"if the distance is less than 20 cm, turn on the buzzer."* This is the first project where the student's code has to react to the physical world continuously rather than just to button presses.
- **Project 4** introduces **soldering** (the first project where reliable connections under motor vibration require permanent joints), motor control (PWM to a motor driver), and a first taste of feedback control (the line-follower is a crude proportional controller — *"more error on the line, more correction to the motors"*). This is where Principle 6 (movement is medicine) becomes operational in the student's own work, because the student's own device now moves.
- **Project 5** introduces external input handling (Bluetooth or IR), simple state machines (*"am I in manual mode or auto mode?"*), and the idea that code can respond to user choices in real time. Students at Level 3 start writing small prompts to Claude Code at this stage.
- **Project 6** is the transition from Arduino Uno to ESP32, which brings with it Wi-Fi, soft-AP mode, web-page hosting, and **I2C** (introduced via a sensor module like a BMP280 or an I2C OLED display). This is a meaningful jump in complexity and the first project where the student's device is addressable from a phone. The three-level Claude Code model is especially important here because the ESP32 code is denser than the Arduino Uno code, and Level 1 (pre-written upload) is a genuine lifeline for students who are not yet ready to parse 200 lines of sketch.
- **Project 7** is the ground-robotics integration capstone: ESP32-CAM on a car chassis, streaming video to a phone browser, with control endpoints for the motors. It brings together every skill from Projects 1–6 (digital I/O, timing, sensor reading, motor control, state handling, Wi-Fi, I2C) and adds **multi-subsystem integration** as a new challenge — the hardest thing about Project 7 is not any single subsystem, it is making all of them work together at the same time.
- **Project 8** is the flight capstone: everything from Projects 1–7 plus **sensor fusion** (combining gyroscope and accelerometer readings from the MPU6050 via a complementary filter), **PID control** (tuning proportional/derivative constants until the drone hovers stably), and **flight safety protocol** as a first-class discipline with its own non-negotiable rules. Project 8 is qualitatively harder than Project 7 because the failure modes are physical rather than just annoying — a crashed drone is a safety event as well as a setback.

**Soldering is introduced at Project 4, not earlier.** A student doing Projects 1–3 is working entirely with a breadboard and pre-made jumper wires — no hot iron, no permanent joints, no safety escalation beyond the baseline workshop rules. Introducing soldering at Project 4 means the student has already had three successful positive experiences with the Arduino IDE, with wiring, with Claude Code, and with the navigation-card system before they pick up a soldering iron for the first time. The soldering introduction is itself a differentiated experience: Tier 1 students may have wires pre-tinned by the teacher and only practice the joint; Tier 2 students do the full tinning and jointing with the teacher supervising each joint; Tier 3 students solder independently after demonstrating the technique on a practice board.

**I2C is introduced at Project 6, not earlier.** I2C is a real conceptual jump — the student now has to think about addresses, clock lines, data lines, and libraries that encapsulate all of that. By Project 6 the student has already written (or uploaded, or modified) sketches for five prior projects, so the leap into an I2C library call like `bmp.readTemperature()` is a manageable step rather than an intimidating one. Introducing I2C at Project 2 or 3 would be premature.

**PID intuition is built up qualitatively in Project 4 and formalized in Project 8.** Project 4's line follower is a proportional controller, and the navigation card describes it that way: *"when the car drifts left, turn the right motor down. How much? By an amount proportional to how far left it drifted."* No equations, no P-term or D-term language. Just the intuition. By Project 8, the student has seen "more error, more correction" work in a simpler context and can be introduced to the formal PD controller (and the tuning process) without it being a surprise.

**Claude Code prompt fluency accumulates across all projects.** Project 1 introduces Channel A Level 1 (pre-written sketch upload) and Channel B (conversational walk-through of the navigation card) — both low-stakes, low-risk entry points. By Project 3, most students have tried Channel A Level 2 (modify with prompt scaffold) at least once. By Project 5, students who are ready are trying Channel A Level 3 (free dialogue) on specific coding problems. By Project 8, the program's most confident students are using Level 3 to generate the full PID firmware from the README pseudocode — a genuine "AI pair programmer" exercise at capstone scale. Students who never move past Level 1 or Channel B are fully welcome to complete all eight projects at that level; the levels are differentiation tools, not achievement gates.

### §6.3 Individual student, individual project, individual workstation

This is important enough to restate here, where a reviewer may be dipping into §6 without having read §5.

**Every project in this section is designed for an individual student working alone at their own workstation.** A workshop session has three to eight students in the same room, sharing a parts library, sharing tools, sharing a teacher, and sharing the physical space — but the work itself is individual. Two students at Tier 2 of Project 3 are each building their own proximity alarm; they are not sharing hardware, they are not sharing code, they are not alternating roles. Each student has their own parts, their own breadboard, their own Arduino, their own navigation cards, their own tracking sheet row.

The reason is practical and has been documented in §5.1: peer collaboration introduces coordination overhead that a single teacher managing three to eight EBD students cannot reliably support, and the population's social-difficulty histories mean that mandatory peer coordination often creates friction rather than support. Appendix A documents the decision and the conditions under which it could be revisited.

**Implications for §6 that follow from this.** In the per-project profiles below, language like "the student" is always singular. There is no "the pair," "the team," "the group," or "partner work" in any project. A project that might intuitively benefit from collaboration — for example, Project 5's remote-controlled car, where one student could drive while another watches — is instead structured so the single student building the car also drives it at the end. If peer collaboration becomes a first-class mechanism in a future version of the program, Appendix A describes what revisiting would look like and which projects would most naturally accommodate shared work. For the current version: individual.

### §6.4 What every project file in `Arduino_Projects/` ships with

Each of the eight per-project files (`Arduino_Project_1.md` through `Arduino_Project_8.md`) is the teacher-facing source of truth for that project, and every project's artifact family is generated from that source file. The per-project file is not what the student sees — what the student sees are the printed navigation cards, the reference cards, the HTML tutorial, and (for students who use it) the Claude Code tutorial-channel scaffold.

The artifact family for every project includes:

1. **The per-project Markdown source file** — teacher-facing, covering objective, rationale, hardware list, wait protocol, per-tier milestone breakdown, Claude Code prompt scaffolds per step, inline citations tying the design back to principles, safety protocol extensions (where applicable), and troubleshooting notes. This file is the source from which all other artifacts are generated.
2. **Tier 1 navigation card set** — print-ready HTML, one card per milestone, designed to be printed, laminated, and arranged in sequence at a student's workstation. Three to six cards per project at Tier 1. Each card has a "stuck? try first" protocol, a "done when" criterion, and a Claude Code Channel-and-level indicator.
3. **Tier 2 navigation card set** — print-ready HTML, three to five cards per project at Tier 2, with one to three explicit **choice points** where the student picks between documented options (different blink rates, different sensor thresholds, different LED patterns).
4. **Tier 3 project planner** — a single print-ready HTML sheet covering Plan → Build → Code → Test → Show phases, which the student fills in with their own design decisions. Tier 3 is open-ended, so the planner is a structure for the student's own thinking rather than a sequence of cards to work through.
5. **Reference cards** — print-ready HTML, per-project wiring reference (diagram + pinout), stuck-protocol reference (Arduino won't upload / hardware isn't responding / serial monitor shows nothing), Claude Code prompt templates, and safety reminders.
6. **Teacher setup checklist** — print-ready HTML, what the teacher needs to prepare before students arrive for this project's session.
7. **Teacher troubleshooting crib sheet** — print-ready HTML, common failure modes and quick diagnostic steps.
8. **Pre-written `.ino` files** — Arduino C++ source files for Channel A Level 1 use, one per milestone that involves code. Students upload these directly. Code comments are in English.
9. **HTML tutorial** — styled HTML, mission-structured, student-facing, following the pattern of the existing tiny-drone tutorial. For Projects 1–5 the HTML tutorial is primarily an alternative delivery mode for students who prefer screen-based tutorials; for Projects 6–8 the HTML is also the student's control interface for the device.
10. **Claude Code tutorial-channel scaffold** (Channel B) — Markdown prompt templates plus a short system prompt that primes Claude Code to walk a student through the project's navigation cards conversationally. Per-milestone invocation templates included.
11. **Demo-build photo placeholders** — marked gaps in the navigation cards and HTML tutorial where Yon inserts photos of his own demo builds after the artifacts are produced.

The per-project profiles that follow describe each project's **content**, not the artifact family structure (which is the same across all eight). The production of the full artifact family for each project is described in §10 Implementation Roadmap; §6 describes what the artifacts will contain.

### §6.5 Project 1 — Light Signals

**What the student builds.** A small Arduino Uno circuit with two or three LEDs, a single push-button, and a resistor for each LED. At Tier 1, the student wires the LEDs, uploads a pre-written sketch, and watches the LEDs blink in a pattern. At Tier 2, the student modifies the sketch (change the blink rate, swap which LED blinks when the button is pressed, add a third LED). At Tier 3, the student designs their own "mood light" or signal pattern — anything from a simple alternating pattern to a Morse-code signaller to a custom arcade-style start sequence.

**Hardware.** Arduino Uno, breadboard, 2–3 LEDs (different colours), 220–330 Ω current-limiting resistors (one per LED), one push-button, 10 kΩ pull-down resistor for the button, jumper wires, USB cable to computer.

**What the project introduces.** This is the student's first experience of the Arduino IDE, the first time they see the Upload button produce a physical result, the first breadboard wiring they do themselves, and the first interaction with Claude Code — either as Channel A Level 1 (they just upload the sketch and watch the lights blink) or as Channel B (they ask Claude Code to walk them through navigation card 1 conversationally). Project 1 is intentionally low-stakes: there is no debugging complexity, no soldering, no moving parts, nothing that can go physically wrong beyond a wire in the wrong hole.

**Why Project 1 is an LED, not a motor.** The super-target of the program is *small, positive, empowering experiences* (§1), and the most reliable such experience for a first session is *I made a light turn on*. An LED blinking is instantly legible as success. A motor spinning is also legible as success but involves more wiring and a risk of shorting the power supply. A sensor is even harder to experience as success because the student has to interpret a number rather than just watch something light up. Project 1 is deliberately the simplest possible "something I did made the thing work" experience — and the design principle behind that is Principle 4 (hyper-chunked milestones with visible wins, §4).

**The three tiers.**
- **Tier 1.** Six to eight short navigation cards walking the student through: plug Arduino into computer → open the pre-written sketch → click Upload → watch the LED blink → add a second LED → upload the updated sketch → add the button → upload the button-handling sketch → celebrate. Channel A Level 1 throughout (pre-written sketches at every step).
- **Tier 2.** Five navigation cards with choice points: pick two LED colours, pick a blink rate (fast/medium/slow), pick whether the button switches between patterns or cycles through them, pick a "signature pattern" the student wants to see. Channel A Level 2 introduced here — the navigation card says *"Here's a sketch that blinks the LED once per second. Ask Claude Code how to change it to blink every half second."*
- **Tier 3.** A one-page project planner asking the student to sketch (on paper) the light pattern they want to build, list the hardware, break the pattern into steps, and then build and program it. Channel A Level 3 available but not required — many Tier 3 students are still most comfortable at Level 2 on their first project.

**Principles embodied.** Principle 1 (predictable routine), Principle 2 (navigation card is the boss), Principle 3 (physical-first — the wiring is the first concrete thing before the code becomes real), Principle 4 (hyper-chunked milestones, each producing a visible light), Principle 7 (Channel A Level 1 and Channel B both introduced here at the lowest possible stakes).

**Claude Code integration.** This is where both channels are introduced. For Channel A, the student experiences Level 1 first: upload a sketch, watch it work, no need to understand the code yet. For students ready for Level 2, the navigation card offers the "change the blink rate" prompt scaffold. For students who find the printed card overwhelming, the project's Claude Code tutorial-channel scaffold (Channel B) is available from Navigation Card 1 — the student can say *"walk me through Project 1, Tier 1, navigation card 1"* and Claude responds conversationally.

**Caveat.** None specific to this project. The design rests on well-established breadboarding-and-blink tutorials that millions of hobbyists have completed successfully; the novel elements are the three-tier differentiation and the Claude Code dual-channel integration, both flagged in §9 Honest Limitations.

### §6.6 Project 2 — Reaction-Time Game

**What the student builds.** A small game: the Arduino waits a random number of seconds, then turns on an LED (and/or beeps the buzzer), and the player has to press a button as fast as they can. The Arduino measures the time between the LED turning on and the button being pressed, and shows the result — either via LEDs (one LED per speed category, fast/medium/slow), a buzzer pattern, or the Serial Monitor. At Tier 2, the student picks which feedback mode to build. At Tier 3, the student designs a two-button head-to-head variant or a multi-round game with scoring.

**Hardware.** Arduino Uno, breadboard, 2–3 LEDs + resistors, one small piezo buzzer, 1–2 push-buttons with pull-down resistors, jumper wires, USB cable.

**What the project introduces.** `millis()`-based timing (so the Arduino can measure elapsed time accurately), simple game state (waiting / ready / measuring / finished), and the first experience of the Arduino doing something non-trivial while also watching for input. For students at Tier 2, this is their first substantive code modification beyond "change a blink rate" — they are genuinely altering the game's behavior.

**The three tiers.**
- **Tier 1.** Six navigation cards: wire the LED and button → upload the pre-written "wait, flash, measure" sketch → play the game five times → add the buzzer → upload the updated sketch with buzzer feedback → write your fastest time on the Project 2 poster at the workstation. Channel A Level 1 throughout.
- **Tier 2.** Five navigation cards with two choice points: (a) feedback mode — three LEDs showing fast/medium/slow, or buzzer pattern, or Serial Monitor readout; (b) difficulty — easy (LED on for 2 seconds, slow reactions still win) or hard (LED on for 0.5 seconds, only fast reactions win). Channel A Level 2 for modifying the sketch to match the chosen mode.
- **Tier 3.** Project planner: design a two-player reaction game with two buttons and a way to show who won. Or: design a multi-round game that keeps score across 5 rounds. The student picks the variant, sketches the game flow, and builds it. Channel A Level 3 available.

**Principles embodied.** Principle 4 (each navigation card ends in the student playing the game and getting a win), Principle 5 (choice points at Tier 2 and open design at Tier 3), Principle 2 (navigation card is the boss — the teacher does not need to explain `millis()`, the card does), Principle 6 (movement — reacting physically fast *is* movement, and for students who love competition this project channels hyperactivity into task-relevant action rather than treating it as a problem).

**Claude Code integration.** Channel A Level 2 becomes the default expectation for most students on this project, because most students have seen Level 1 succeed on Project 1 and are ready to try small modifications. The navigation cards provide explicit prompt scaffolds: *"Ask Claude Code: 'Here's my current delay. How do I make the LED stay on for only 0.5 seconds instead of 2 seconds? Here's my code: [paste].'"* Channel B continues to be available for students who prefer conversational walk-throughs.

**Caveat.** The reaction-time game is a well-documented Arduino exercise with many open-source reference implementations; the novelty is again in the differentiation and Claude Code integration. No project-specific evidence caveat.

### §6.7 Project 3 — Don't Get Too Close

**What the student builds.** A proximity alarm. An HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensor measures distance, and when something comes within a threshold distance the Arduino triggers a response — lighting an LED, beeping a buzzer, or both. At Tier 2, the student picks the threshold and the response mode. At Tier 3, the student designs a proximity alarm for a real use case (a desk drawer that alerts when opened, a doorway alert, a pet food dish that signals when something approaches).

**Hardware.** Arduino Uno, breadboard, HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensor, 1–2 LEDs + resistors, one piezo buzzer, jumper wires, USB cable.

**What the project introduces.** Sensor reading — the Arduino code now has to acquire a value from the physical world rather than just respond to button presses. Threshold logic (*"if distance < 20 cm then alarm"*) is also new. And for the first time, the code is running in a loop that reacts continuously to the physical world, which is a meaningful conceptual step up from the mostly event-driven sketches of Projects 1 and 2.

**The three tiers.**
- **Tier 1.** Six navigation cards: wire the HC-SR04 (four pins — VCC, GND, Trig, Echo) → upload the pre-written sketch that prints distance to the Serial Monitor → watch the number change as you move your hand near the sensor → add an LED that lights up when the distance is less than 20 cm → upload the updated sketch → add the buzzer for an audible alarm → upload the full alarm sketch → test it on a real object. Channel A Level 1 throughout.
- **Tier 2.** Five navigation cards with choice points: threshold distance (5 cm, 20 cm, 50 cm — depends on the intended use), response mode (LED only, buzzer only, both), response behavior (steady or pulsing). Channel A Level 2 for modifying the threshold value and the response logic.
- **Tier 3.** Project planner: pick a real use case (desk drawer alarm, doorway alert, pet food dish, something else the student invents), describe the environment, design the alarm, build it, test it in the real use case. Channel A Level 3 for students who are ready.

**Principles embodied.** Principle 3 (physical-first — the student sees the distance number change as their hand moves before they write any code about it), Principle 4 (the first "number changing as I move my hand" moment is a visible win all by itself, before any alarm logic is added), Principle 5 (Tier 3's open use-case target is full autonomy-support). Project 3 is also the first time the student sees a sensor "fail gracefully" — sometimes the HC-SR04 returns a very large number when there is nothing in front of it, and the navigation card addresses this explicitly as *"this is normal, not a bug."*

**Claude Code integration.** Channel A Level 2 is the default for most students on this project. Level 3 becomes genuinely useful here for Tier 3 students who are designing a custom use case: *"I want to build a drawer alarm that beeps only when the drawer opens, not when something moves across the sensor. Here's my code: [paste]. How do I make it only alarm on the transition?"* This is a real coding problem and Claude Code is genuinely helpful on it.

**Caveat.** None specific to this project.

### §6.8 Project 4 — Line-Following Car

**What the student builds.** A small car chassis with two DC motors, a motor driver board, two infrared line sensors, a battery, and an Arduino. The car drives itself along a black line on the floor: when one sensor goes off the line, that sensor's motor slows down, steering the car back onto the line. At Tier 2, the student tunes the response (how much to slow down) and designs a course. At Tier 3, the student optimizes the car for speed or designs a more complex track with intersections.

**Hardware.** Two-motor car chassis with chassis kit (wheels, caster, battery holder), 2 DC motors, L298N or similar dual H-bridge motor driver, 2 infrared line-sensor modules (TCRT5000 or similar), Arduino Uno, 7.4V battery pack (or 4×AA holder), jumper wires, **soldering iron and solder** (first project using soldering), black electrical tape for the track line.

**What the project introduces.** This is the **first soldering project** — wire connections under motor vibration need to be permanent to be reliable. Students solder the motor leads to the driver board, the sensor leads to the Arduino connectors, and (at Tier 3) potentially their own wire extensions. Soldering safety is introduced here as a first-class workshop discipline (eye protection, iron on stand when not in hand, damp sponge, never solder near skin). Motor control via PWM is also new — the Arduino controls motor speed by sending a pulse-width signal to the motor driver. And the project introduces a crude form of **feedback control**: *"if the left sensor is off the line, slow the left motor; if the right sensor is off the line, slow the right motor."* No equations, no formal PID vocabulary — just the intuition that more error means more correction.

**The three tiers.**
- **Tier 1.** Eight navigation cards (the most of any project so far, because the build is more involved): practice soldering on a scrap board → solder the motor leads → solder the sensor leads → wire the motor driver → wire the sensors → upload a pre-written "drive forward" sketch → test the motors → upload the line-following sketch → test on a straight track → celebrate. Channel A Level 1 throughout. For Tier 1 students, the teacher may pre-tin the wires and even pre-solder the motor-driver joints, leaving the student to solder only the sensor connections.
- **Tier 2.** Six navigation cards with choice points: forward speed (slow/medium/fast), how aggressively to correct (small correction / big correction), track layout (straight / gentle curve / sharp curve). The student designs their own track on the floor using black electrical tape. Channel A Level 2 for tuning the speed and correction constants in the sketch.
- **Tier 3.** Project planner: design a complex track with multiple turns or intersections. Tune the car for either speed or reliability. Optionally add a start/stop push-button. Channel A Level 3 available for students writing their own line-following logic.

**Principles embodied.** Principle 3 (the physical car and the physical track are the first concrete, before any code tuning), Principle 5 (Tier 2's choice points and Tier 3's track design), Principle 6 (movement — the student's own device now moves, and the student moves with it as they lay out the track and test the car), Principle 8 (this is a project where the teacher's relational check-in matters a lot, because soldering is intimidating and several students will need reassurance; the teacher's presence during the first solder joint is not optional).

**Safety escalation.** Project 4 is the first project where the baseline workshop safety rules are **actively exercised**: eye protection for soldering is mandatory and on the pre-task checklist, the hot iron on the stand rule is enforced, and the wait-protocol at this project gives the student a backup task (sorting resistors, labeling jumper wires) if they are nervous about starting the soldering step. The teacher's role during the solder joints is presence, not pressure.

**Claude Code integration.** Channel A Level 2 is the dominant mode here — students modify the speed and correction constants, test, modify again, test again. This is a real tuning exercise and Claude Code can genuinely help a student reason about what to change and why. Channel B is particularly valuable for students who find the longer navigation-card sequences of Project 4 overwhelming; the conversational walk-through breaks the build into smaller perceived steps.

**Caveat.** Line-following robots are well-documented in the hobby and education literature; the novelty in this project is the soldering-introduction discipline and the explicit "crude proportional controller" intuition-building. Neither has a direct empirical study in the verified corpus, but both follow from Principle 3 (physical-first) and Principle 4 (visible wins through hyper-chunking).

### §6.9 Project 5 — Remote-Controlled Car

**What the student builds.** The car chassis from Project 4, but now controlled by an external input instead of driving itself. Tier 1 students use an HC-05 Bluetooth module and a simple phone app (free, off-the-shelf) to drive the car. Tier 2 students pick their control mode — Bluetooth phone app, IR remote, or a wired joystick module. Tier 3 students design a custom control scheme, which might include sensor-based gesture control, a web interface (preview of Project 6), or a two-mode system (manual / auto-return-to-home).

**Hardware.** Project 4's car chassis (reused), HC-05 Bluetooth module or IR receiver module or joystick module (depending on the student's choice), and a phone with a free BT controller app (for the Bluetooth path) or an IR remote (for the IR path). Jumper wires, possibly additional soldering.

**What the project introduces.** **Receiving external input** as a programming concept (the Arduino is now reacting to commands coming from outside, not just its own sensors), **simple state machines** (*"am I in manual mode, auto mode, or stopped mode?"*), and the idea of user-facing control — the student is designing the experience their user (themselves, or a peer testing the car) will have when driving the device. This is also the first project where the student's **own taste** becomes meaningful: a car that is fun to drive at the end of Project 5 is a good car, and "fun to drive" is a design judgment the student makes.

**The three tiers.**
- **Tier 1.** Seven navigation cards: add the HC-05 module to the car from Project 4 → upload the pre-written Bluetooth-control sketch → install the free BT controller app on a phone → pair the phone with the car → drive the car around a simple obstacle course → celebrate. Channel A Level 1.
- **Tier 2.** Six navigation cards with a control-mode choice point up front: pick Bluetooth, IR, or wired joystick, then follow the card set for that choice. The card sets are parallel — each one gets the student from "no control" to "working remote-controlled car" — and the student picks the one that matches what their cart has. Channel A Level 2 for customizing speed curves and button mappings.
- **Tier 3.** Project planner: design a custom control scheme. Options include a two-mode car (manual / autonomous line-following, reusing Project 4 code as the auto mode), a gesture-controlled car (using an accelerometer), or a web-interface car (using an ESP32 instead of the Uno — a preview of Project 6). Channel A Level 3 available.

**Principles embodied.** Principle 5 (choice architecture is central to this project — Tier 2's control-mode choice is a real decision, and Tier 3's custom design is fully open), Principle 6 (the student literally drives the car around the room), Principle 4 (visible wins at every milestone: pairing works, the app connects, the car moves, the car responds to direction changes).

**Claude Code integration.** Level 3 becomes genuinely useful here because the students' designs at Tier 3 can diverge significantly — a student designing a gesture-controlled car has different code problems than a student designing a two-mode car. Claude Code at Level 3 lets the teacher support a diverse set of Tier 3 designs without needing to be an expert in every control modality. Channel B continues to support students who prefer conversational delivery of the navigation cards.

**Caveat.** None specific to this project.

### §6.10 Project 6 — ESP32 Wi-Fi Controller

**What the student builds.** A Wi-Fi-enabled device based on an ESP32 board rather than the Arduino Uno. The ESP32 hosts a small web page in soft-AP mode (it creates its own Wi-Fi network that the student's phone connects to), and the web page shows a reading from a sensor (temperature, humidity, light level) and optionally lets the student control an output (a light, a buzzer, a servo). The sensor is an I2C module, so the student's first I2C experience is embedded in this project. At Tier 2, the student picks the sensor and the control. At Tier 3, the student designs a simple "smart device" — a weather station, a desk light controller, a pet feeder logic prototype, or similar.

**Hardware.** ESP32 development board, breadboard, I2C sensor module (BMP280 for temperature/pressure, or DHT22 for temperature/humidity — whichever Yon confirms he has), optional output (LED with driver, small servo motor, buzzer), jumper wires, USB cable to computer.

**What the project introduces.** This is the **transition from Arduino Uno to ESP32**, which brings with it several new things at once: a different toolchain (adding the ESP32 board package to the Arduino IDE), a different power envelope (3.3V instead of 5V logic, which matters for sensor wiring), **Wi-Fi soft-AP mode** (the ESP32 creates its own network), and **I2C** — the student's first experience with a two-wire addressable bus and with libraries that encapsulate device communication. The project also introduces the idea that code on a microcontroller can host a web page that other devices talk to, which is a conceptual shift as meaningful as "my car has a remote" was in Project 5.

**The three tiers.**
- **Tier 1.** Eight navigation cards: install the ESP32 board package in the Arduino IDE (teacher-supported) → wire the I2C sensor → upload the pre-written sketch that reads the sensor and prints the value → connect a phone to the ESP32's Wi-Fi network → open the ESP32's web page in the phone browser → see the sensor reading on the page → celebrate. Channel A Level 1 throughout. The I2C complexity is hidden behind a pre-written library call (`bmp.readTemperature()`), so the student does not need to understand the I2C protocol to complete Tier 1 — they understand "the sensor gives me a number" and that is enough.
- **Tier 2.** Six navigation cards with choice points: sensor type (temperature, humidity, light — whichever are available), output type (LED, buzzer, servo, none), web-page style (a simple text readout, a button to trigger the output, a slider to control the output). Channel A Level 2 for customizing the web page HTML and for modifying the sensor-reading logic.
- **Tier 3.** Project planner: design a simple "smart device" with the ESP32 (weather station that logs temperature over time, desk light that dims based on ambient light, pet feeder logic that activates at scheduled times, or something else the student invents). Channel A Level 3 is essential here because the student's design is unpredictable and Claude Code is the only support mechanism that scales.

**Principles embodied.** Principle 3 (the sensor reading changing as the student holds their hand over the sensor is physical-first in a new medium), Principle 4 (each navigation card is a visible win: library installed, sketch uploaded, phone connected, web page showing), Principle 5 (Tier 2's three-way choice at the front of the project and Tier 3's full open design), Principle 7 (this is where Channel A Level 1 **genuinely saves** students who are not yet ready to parse 200 lines of ESP32 code — the pre-written sketch is not a shortcut, it is a scaffold that lets the student experience the device working before they have to understand every line).

**Claude Code integration.** Channel A becomes particularly important on Project 6 because the ESP32 sketches are longer and denser than the Uno sketches of earlier projects. Level 1 students get a fully working sketch. Level 2 students get a sketch with clearly marked "CHANGE THIS" comments indicating which lines to modify. Level 3 students are writing their own web-page HTML and their own sensor-reading logic, sometimes from scratch using Claude Code. Channel B is also particularly valuable here — the navigation cards for ESP32 projects are longer and more technical than earlier projects, and some students will benefit significantly from having them delivered conversationally.

**Caveat.** ESP32 tutorials are widely available but the population-specific adaptation (three tiers, dual-channel Claude Code, individual-student design) is the program's own work and is not directly evidence-based. §9 Honest Limitations covers this.

### §6.11 Project 7 — Camera-Equipped Explorer

**What the student builds.** The car chassis (again reused, or rebuilt for students who want a fresh start) now running an ESP32-CAM board instead of an Arduino Uno, with the camera streaming video to a phone browser and the phone also controlling the car's motion. This is the **ground-robotics integration capstone** — every skill from Projects 1–6 comes together: motors from Projects 4–5, Wi-Fi from Project 6, a web interface from Project 6, and the new camera-streaming capability that the ESP32-CAM brings. At Tier 2, the student customizes the control interface. At Tier 3, the student builds a specific explorer scenario (remote-explore a room, map the house, record a driving route).

**Hardware.** Car chassis (reused from Project 4 or rebuilt), ESP32-CAM module with FTDI programmer for upload, motor driver, DC motors, 7.4V battery pack, jumper wires, phone for control and viewing.

**What the project introduces.** **Integration** as the primary new skill — not any single subsystem, but the challenge of making the camera, the motors, the Wi-Fi, and the web interface all work at the same time. The student is now managing multiple subsystems concurrently, and the debugging questions move from "is this one thing working?" to "is subsystem A interfering with subsystem B?" (a common example: the ESP32-CAM is picky about power and will brown out if the motors draw current through the same regulator, so the student learns to power the motors separately). Project 7 is also the first project where the student builds something that genuinely **does something useful** — a car you can drive from another room while watching the video is tangibly different from a line follower.

**The three tiers.**
- **Tier 1.** Nine navigation cards (the most of any project to this point — Project 7 is a substantial build). Wire the ESP32-CAM → upload the pre-written camera-streaming sketch → verify the camera streams to the phone → add the motor driver wiring → upload the pre-written control sketch → verify the car drives from the phone interface → celebrate. Channel A Level 1 throughout — the sketches are pre-written and verified to work.
- **Tier 2.** Eight navigation cards with choice points at the control interface level: layout of the driving buttons on the phone web page, speed mapping (proportional or stepped), whether to include a "stop" button that cuts all motor power immediately. Channel A Level 2 for modifying the web-page HTML and the control logic.
- **Tier 3.** Project planner: design a specific explorer scenario. The student defines the use case (explore a room I can't access, drive around the school corridor, drive across the parking lot with a GoPro-style perspective), the interface (single view or split view, single-hand or two-hand control, joystick or buttons), and the physical enhancements (lighting, a second camera, a claw). Channel A Level 3 essential here.

**Principles embodied.** All nine principles are in play on Project 7 because it is an integration project, but Principle 3 (physical-first), Principle 5 (autonomy at Tier 3), Principle 6 (the student physically drives the car through physical space), and Principle 7 (the three levels are genuinely differentiated here, and Channel B continues to be available) are particularly prominent.

**Claude Code integration.** Channel A Level 3 is where the diversity of Tier 3 designs lives. A student building a "drive the school corridor" explorer has different code problems than a student building a "lift objects with a claw" explorer. Claude Code at Level 3 with a well-structured prompt (problem description, current state, what's been tried) is often the fastest way to get to a working solution without the teacher needing to be an expert in every possible design.

**Caveat.** ESP32-CAM integration is well-documented in the maker community; the novelty is again the population-specific adaptation. §9 Honest Limitations acknowledges this.

### §6.12 Project 8 — Tiny ESP32 Quadcopter (apex capstone)

**What the student builds.** A small, handmade quadcopter: a popsicle-stick X-frame, four 8.5×20mm coreless motors (one at each arm end), propellers sized for those motors, an ESP32 at the center, a TB6612FNG dual motor driver (two of them — one for each pair of motors), an MPU6050 inertial measurement unit for attitude sensing, two MT3608 booster converters (one for the ESP32 and sensor power, one for the motor power), and a 1S LiPo battery. The drone is controlled from a phone browser via Wi-Fi, using a simple arm/disarm control and a throttle slider. The full-stabilization firmware uses a complementary filter to fuse the MPU6050's gyroscope and accelerometer data and a PD controller to convert attitude error into motor speed adjustments.

This is the **apex capstone** of the program. It brings together every skill from Projects 1–7 — soldering (Project 4), motor control (Projects 4–5, 7), state machines and external input (Project 5), ESP32 toolchain and Wi-Fi and I2C (Project 6), multi-subsystem integration (Project 7) — and adds three new demands on top: **sensor fusion** (combining gyroscope and accelerometer readings), **PID control** (tuning constants until the drone hovers stably), and a **flight safety protocol** as a first-class discipline with its own non-negotiable rules.

**Hardware (all tiers use the same 8.5×20mm motor baseline).** ESP32 dev board, 4× 8.5×20mm coreless brushed motors, 4× 65mm propellers sized for 8.5×20mm motors, 2× TB6612FNG motor drivers (one per motor pair), 2× MT3608 boost converters (one for ESP32+sensor rail, one for motor rail), 1× MPU6050 IMU (I2C), 1S LiPo battery (~300–500 mAh), popsicle-stick X-frame, hot glue, 3D-printed or glued motor mounts, safety-gauge soldering, small connector wires, propeller guards (mandatory for practice flights), fishing line or kite string for tethered first flights, safety glasses, fireproof battery charging bag, sand bucket for LiPo emergencies.

**Motor choice locked to 8.5×20mm.** The source material documents three motor variants (6×14mm, 7×20mm, 8.5×20mm). The program commits to the **8.5×20mm variant** despite the source documents labelling it as "more advanced." The reason is counter-intuitive but important: the 8.5×20mm motors produce a thrust-to-weight ratio of approximately 3.4:1, which is **much more forgiving for first-time fliers** than the lower-thrust variants. A drone with T/W around 2:1 is at the edge of being able to fly and crashes often; a drone with T/W around 3.4:1 has headroom, recovers from small attitude errors, and is significantly less frustrating to learn on. For a student population whose workshop super-target is *small, positive, empowering experiences* (§1), the extra thrust is the whole point. The higher motor cost is worth it. The tradeoff: the 8.5×20mm variant requires the TB6612FNG drivers and dual MT3608 boost converters (the smaller variants get by with simpler L9110S drivers and a single booster), which modestly complicates the build.

**What the project introduces.** **Sensor fusion via complementary filter** — the gyroscope gives fast, drift-prone rotation rates; the accelerometer gives slow, noise-prone absolute tilt; combining them via a complementary filter gives both fast and accurate attitude estimates. **PID control** — specifically a proportional-derivative controller — where the student tunes the P and D constants until the drone hovers without oscillating. **Flight safety protocol** — propeller guards, eye protection, tethered first flights, one-at-a-time flight rule, 3-metre flight zone with spectators outside the zone, LiPo fire procedure. And, critically, the **discipline of pre-flight validation** — the drone must pass a bench thrust test (kitchen scale, T/W ≥ 2.0:1 minimum before first hover attempt) before it is allowed to fly.

**The three tiers (all using the 8.5×20mm baseline).**
- **Tier 1.** Twelve to fifteen navigation cards covering a pre-assembled core approach: the teacher pre-glues the popsicle-stick frame, pre-solders the TB6612FNG drivers to a power bus, pre-tunes both MT3608 boosters, and pre-mounts the motors. The Tier 1 student solders the signal lines, installs the MPU6050 on I2C, runs the bench thrust test, uploads the pre-written **beginner motor-test firmware** (a ~50-line sketch with no stabilization, just "slider → motor PWM"), and then uploads the pre-written full PID firmware for a tethered hover attempt. Channel A Level 1 throughout. The beginner motor-test firmware step is crucial — it gives the student a success milestone (*"my motors spin when I move the slider"*) before the more demanding PID-tuned hover attempt.
- **Tier 2.** Fifteen to eighteen navigation cards covering the full 8.5×20mm assembly: the student does the frame build, the soldering, the power-tree wiring, the MT3608 boost tuning with a multimeter, the motor mounting, the bench thrust test, the firmware upload, the tethered first flight, and the PID tuning. Choice points at Tier 2: the student picks the PID starting values (the navigation card offers two reasonable options), the student picks whether to add propeller guards (mandatory for first flights, optional later), and the student picks a flight test sequence (tethered hover / short untethered hover / short untethered flight to a target). Channel A Level 2 for modifying the pre-written PID firmware.
- **Tier 3.** Project planner and an expanded deliverable list. The student adds one or more of: altitude hold via a BMP280 barometer on the same I2C bus, data logging to an SD card breakout, custom flight modes (manual / auto-hover / auto-level), or autonomous hover stabilization via an enhanced complementary filter. Channel A Level 3 is essential here — Claude Code generates the modified firmware from the student's description, and the student iterates on the tuning. Tier 3 on Project 8 is genuinely advanced.

**Principles embodied.** All nine principles are in play, but three are especially prominent: Principle 3 (physical-first — the student builds the frame, solders the joints, and runs the bench thrust test before they ever touch the flight firmware), Principle 4 (hyper-chunked milestones with visible wins — each of the 15+ navigation cards produces a verifiable checkpoint, and the beginner motor-test firmware milestone exists specifically to give the student a win before the PID-tuning frustration), and Principle 9 (teacher sustainability — Project 8 is the most demanding project for the teacher, and the rewrite makes every step in the source material chunked, printable, and laminate-ready so the teacher's session-time preparation is minimized).

**Safety protocol escalation.** Project 8's safety rules are first-class and non-negotiable. The full protocol lives in the per-project file; the baseline rules are summarized in §5.6 and include: propeller guards mandatory for initial tests, eye protection mandatory during any flight test, tethered flight mandatory for Tier 1 and Tier 2 first flights (fishing line to a fixed anchor), one-at-a-time flight rule (only one student arms at any moment, all others 2+ metres back behind a visible line), a defined 3-metre indoor flight zone, a pre-flight checklist the teacher runs with each student, a LiPo fire emergency procedure, and a medical alert protocol for students who have anxiety spikes or freeze during first flight. **Project 8 undergoes two mandatory dedicated review passes before any implementation**: one focused on safety protocol (unambiguous, enforceable by a single teacher managing 3–8 students, each rule explains why), and one focused on preserving the technical accuracy of the content (thrust tables, bill of materials, wiring diagrams).

**Claude Code integration.** Channel A Level 1 is the lifeline here for Tier 1 students — the PID firmware is complex enough that asking a first-time flier to write it from scratch would be cruel. Channel A Level 2 is the default for Tier 2 students modifying the PID constants and the flight-mode logic. Channel A Level 3 is where Tier 3 extensions live: a student adding altitude hold via a BMP280 is using Claude Code to generate and debug the modified firmware, which is exactly the kind of "AI pair programmer" exercise this program has been building toward for seven prior projects. Channel B remains available and is particularly valuable on Project 8's longer navigation-card sequences, where conversational delivery reduces the reading load.

**Caveat — the evidence gap for Project 8.** The verified corpus has **zero research on aerial robotics, drone-based STEM, or flight-based learning for special-needs populations**. Project 8 rests on inferential extension from the ground-robotics literature (Projects 4, 5, 7 draw on this directly), from the makerspace-for-EBD literature (Gomez 2019 on middle-school EBD makerspace outcomes, §3.6 in the Literature Review), and from the modular-tangible-STEM literature (the robotics cluster broadly, §3.6). This gap is prominent in §9 Honest Limitations, and the program does not claim that Project 8 is an evidence-based intervention. It claims that Project 8 is an inferentially-grounded design response to the super-target of empowering experiences, that the safety protocols are first-class and non-negotiable, and that the project will be evaluated in use. A reviewer asking *"where is the research that shows flight projects work for this population?"* gets an honest answer: it does not exist, and that is precisely why Project 8 is piloted with the safety discipline described above.

**Why Project 8 is the capstone, not a fifth or sixth project.** Project 8 requires every skill from Projects 1–7 and cannot responsibly be attempted without them. A student who arrives at Project 8 has already experienced soldering three or four times, has worked with the ESP32 and with I2C, has handled the frustration of subsystems not talking to each other during Project 7 integration, and has built up Claude Code prompt fluency across seven prior projects. None of those prerequisites can be shortcut. A student who wants to start with Project 8 (which happens — drones are exciting) gets the refusal-of-order response described in §5.7: the teacher identifies the specific skills Project 8 uses, delivers them as just-in-time mini-modules, and lets the student decide whether the skill-building detour is worth it.

### §6.13 Order is a suggestion, not a requirement

The projects are sequenced pedagogically — each one introduces skills later projects build on, and §6.2 documented the chain explicitly. But the sequence is a suggestion the teacher uses to plan, not a requirement the student must obey. §5.7 described the four-tool response to refusal-of-order (skills-based prerequisites not project-based; non-linear entry; just-in-time mini-modules; refusal as diagnostic), and §6 inherits that discipline: a student who wants to start with Project 5 because they love remote-controlled cars, or with Project 8 because they want to fly a drone, is not told "you have to do Projects 1–4 first." Instead, the teacher and student identify which skills the target project needs, the student works through just-in-time mini-modules for those skills, and the student decides whether the preparation is worth it. Often, a student who insisted on jumping ahead will voluntarily go back to earlier projects after seeing the skills gap — because the teacher gave them the choice rather than imposing it, the return is experienced as their own decision, not as a punishment.

The eight projects are the spine of the program. Individual students will walk the spine in different orders, at different tiers, with different Claude Code levels, across different numbers of sessions. That variability is the whole point of differentiation.

## §7 Expected Outcomes — What the program aims to produce, and how we will know

§6 described what the students build. §7 describes what the program expects those builds to produce *in the students* — and, just as importantly, how the workshop will know whether the program is actually producing those outcomes.

This section is structured around four commitments. First, **outcomes must match the program's super-target** (§1): *small, positive, empowering experiences that rebuild students' confidence with learning*. A program whose evaluation framework measures things the program never tried to produce (standardized test scores, for example) is either dishonest about its goals or setting itself up to fail. Second, **measurement must be lightweight** (Principle 9 — teacher sustainability is a first-class design constraint). A single teacher managing three to eight students cannot also run a research-grade measurement apparatus, and if the evaluation overhead burns the teacher out, nothing gets measured. Third, **the evaluation framework must be honest about what it can and cannot detect** (§9 Honest Limitations carries this forward). A small-cohort, single-teacher, non-randomized pilot can reliably detect some things and cannot reliably detect others, and this section states the difference plainly. Fourth, **outcome claims must be scoped to what the verified research actually supports**, not extrapolated beyond it — the literature review (§3) established what is known, and the expected outcomes in this section do not exceed that boundary.

The section is organized in five parts: what the program aims to produce at the student level (§7.1), what the program aims to produce at the teacher level (§7.2), what will be measured and how (§7.3), what will *not* be measured and why (§7.4), and what counts as success for a first implementation year (§7.5).

### §7.1 Student-level outcomes the program aims to produce

The program targets four clusters of student-level outcomes. They are listed in approximate order of how directly the program addresses them — the first cluster is the super-target and the most directly supported; the fourth cluster is the most inferential and the most explicitly honest about its uncertainty.

#### Cluster 1 — Positive experiences with learning (the super-target)

This is the outcome the program most directly targets and the one its whole design is organized around. The program expects that students who participate will accumulate **multiple small, positive, empowering experiences** with learning-in-action — moments in which *something I did made the thing work*, and in which the student experienced themselves as capable rather than as the person who gets it wrong.

The mechanism is the hyper-chunked 15-minute milestone structure (Principle 4) combined with the tier differentiation (§5.4) and the navigation-card-is-the-boss discipline (Principle 2). A student who completes Tier 1 of Project 1 has had a minimum of four to six positive experiences in a single session: the sketch uploaded, the LED lit up, the second LED lit up, the button changed the pattern, the student chose their favorite pattern, the student took the card to the teacher with the card's "done when" criterion satisfied. Each of those is a visible win. Across eight projects over a semester, the program expects a student at any tier to accumulate dozens of these moments — not as a measurement claim, but as a design intention.

**Why this cluster is the super-target.** The population this program serves (§2) is overwhelmingly made up of students whose prior school experiences have *failed to work* for them — students who are coping with ADHD, ODD, anxiety, depression, OCD, or post-trauma, many of whom have accumulated years of "I'm bad at this" messages from mainstream classrooms. A program that does not directly target the rebuild of positive learning experiences is implicitly assuming the students' baseline is neutral, when in fact the baseline is often negative. The super-target is therefore not a soft add-on to the "real" outcomes; it is the real outcome.

**How the program expects this to show up.** The program expects to see: students voluntarily starting work within the first five minutes of a session more often as the program progresses (which is measured — see §7.3); students completing at least one milestone per session they attend (also measured); students initiating conversations about what they want to build next (qualitative, noticed by the teacher during Close-Out Circle); students taking finished builds home and showing them to a family member (reported informally by students and parents); students who previously refused to attempt coding or electronics trying Level 1 uploads at Tier 1 by the second or third session; and students who begin at Tier 1 on Project 1 moving to Tier 2 by Project 3 or 4 (tier progression is noted on the tracking sheet).

**Evidence base.** §3.5 on Taylor, Allen, Van & Moohr (2024) — the only empirical PBL-in-EBD classroom study in the verified corpus — reported meaningful gains in student engagement and teacher-rated student attitudes across a semester-long PBL intervention for EBD students. The effect magnitudes are not directly transferable to this program because Taylor et al.'s PBL was academic rather than physical-computing, and the sample was small, but the directional finding (PBL is feasible in EBD classrooms and produces engagement gains) is the closest empirical anchor the program has. §3.1 on Gersib & Mason (2023) meta-analysis of behavior interventions in self-contained EBD classrooms (g = 0.93 overall, with relational components as the strongest moderator) supports the structural claim that well-designed behavior-aware interventions produce substantial effects in this population. The program does not claim Gersib's effect size for itself — it is implementing one of Gersib's three moderator components (adult mentoring) and the program is not a behavior intervention in the clinical sense — but the Gersib finding anchors the general claim that this population can benefit substantially from structured interventions when those interventions are thoughtfully designed.

**Caveat.** The program is not promising these outcomes for every student, nor on any particular timeline. Students who arrive with high avoidance or deep coding-trauma histories may take multiple sessions before the first positive experience lands, and the program's commitment to student pacing (Principle 5) means the teacher does not force progression. The honest framing is: *the program is designed so positive experiences can happen, and the teacher's role is to make space for them to happen, not to guarantee them.*

#### Cluster 2 — Technical skill acquisition

The program expects students to acquire a set of concrete, demonstrable technical skills across the eight projects. These are the skills §6.2 mapped out: breadboard wiring, digital input/output, sensor reading, `millis()`-based timing, motor control, soldering, state machines, external-input handling, ESP32/Wi-Fi basics, I2C, multi-subsystem integration, and — for students who reach Project 8 — basic sensor fusion and PID intuition. The program also expects students to build **Claude Code prompt fluency**: the ability to describe a coding problem in natural language, review an AI's response, and iterate.

**What counts as "acquired."** The program does not mean a student can pass a written exam on these skills. It means the student can demonstrate the skill in the context of a working project. A student has "acquired breadboarding" when they can wire a project from a wiring diagram without teacher correction. A student has "acquired Claude Code prompt fluency" when they can write out the "(a) what I want to happen, (b) what's currently happening, (c) what I've tried" template without the teacher prompting them to. A student has "acquired soldering" when they have made three or four reliable joints on a motor driver or sensor connection under light supervision.

**How the program expects this to show up.** A student who completes Tier 1 of Projects 1 through 4 has demonstrated breadboarding, digital I/O, sensor reading, and soldering. A student who completes Tier 2 of Projects 4 through 6 has demonstrated motor control, external-input handling, ESP32 basics, and I2C. A student who completes any tier of Project 7 has demonstrated multi-subsystem integration. A student who completes Project 8 has demonstrated the full stack plus sensor fusion and PID intuition. The tracking sheet (§5.8) does not record skill acquisition explicitly, but the teacher's running memory of which projects each student has completed is the skill-acquisition record. At the end of each semester, the teacher can look at the tracking sheet and tell, per student, which skills have been demonstrated.

**Evidence base.** §3.6 on Gomez (2019) UTSA dissertation on makerspace outcomes for middle-school EBD students found that makerspace participation supported the development of technical skills alongside social-emotional skills. §3.6 on Guneysu Ozgur et al. (2020) on tangible robot-assisted activities for special education documented skill acquisition in handwriting via tangible manipulation — the mechanism (tangible physical manipulation supporting skill development) generalizes, even though the specific skill does not. The program is not claiming that Arduino skill acquisition is specifically evidence-based for this population; it is claiming that technical skill acquisition through hands-on physical-computing projects is a reasonable expectation grounded in the broader tangible-computing-for-special-education literature.

**Caveat.** Skill acquisition does not transfer automatically to other contexts. A student who can solder a motor driver in the workshop cannot necessarily solder a different circuit next year without reminder. §9 Honest Limitations restates this: the program's skill outcomes are in-workshop skills, not general competencies, and claims about transfer to other subjects or to future careers are beyond what the program's evidence base supports.

#### Cluster 3 — In-workshop self-regulation and executive function practice

The program expects students to **practice** executive function and self-regulation within the workshop environment. "Practice" is deliberate word choice — the program is not expecting students to develop durably improved EF (that would be a therapeutic claim the program cannot make) but expects students to have repeated, structured opportunities to *use* EF skills in a supportive environment: starting work on a task without being told, sequencing the steps of a milestone, recovering from a failed step via the stuck protocol, asking for help using language rather than acting-out, staying with a task for 15 minutes at a time, and using the navigation card and Claude Code as external cognitive scaffolds (Principle 3, Principle 7).

**What counts as EF practice.** A student who reads a navigation card, identifies what to do first, attempts it, fails, tries the stuck protocol (check power / check wiring / ask Claude / ask teacher), and either succeeds or moves on, has practiced: reading comprehension, task decomposition, attempt-and-evaluate, structured help-seeking, and persistence. The program does not require that the student do all of this well — the program requires that the program is *set up so the student can practice it*, and that the teacher's rotation is not so frequent that the teacher preempts every opportunity.

**How the program expects this to show up.** A student who starts work within five minutes of the beginning of the work block is practicing task initiation — one of the most reliable observable EF markers and one of the three items on the §5.8 tracking sheet. A student who completes a milestone without teacher intervention is practicing task persistence and self-directed decomposition. A student who uses the stuck protocol (even just the first two steps) before calling the teacher is practicing help-seeking discipline. The tracking sheet captures the first two directly; the third is observed during rotation.

**Evidence base.** §3.2 on Rosen, Boyle, Cariss & Forchelli (2014) on scaffolded EF: external cognitive scaffolds (navigation cards, pre-written sketches, Claude Code prompt scaffolds) enable students to practice EF they do not yet have internally. The program's whole scaffolding architecture is built on this principle. §3.2 on Cumming, Oblath, Qiu, Frazier, Zelazo, Flores & Park (2024) on executive function and stress in middle-schoolers with behavior problems, which identified stress as a partial mediator between EF deficits and academic performance (β = .61 for ELA, β = .65 for math) — the implication for this program is that reducing stress (via predictable routine, navigation card discipline, and choice architecture) is one mechanism for EF practice to become productive rather than overwhelming. §3.1 on Smith, Langberg, Cusick, Green & Becker (2020) on extrinsic support for adolescents with ADHD: structured external support was associated with better academic outcomes in that study population, which the program infers (as a design inference, not as a clinical claim) applies to the kind of external scaffolding the navigation cards and Claude Code provide.

**Caveat — the program is not a therapeutic EF intervention.** The verified literature on EF intervention for ADHD and EBD populations describes clinical or quasi-clinical interventions with trained therapists, controlled conditions, and often medication in parallel. The Arduino workshop is an *educational* setting in which students happen to practice EF incidentally to building projects they care about. The program does not claim to produce durable EF improvements comparable to clinical interventions — it claims to provide an environment where EF practice can happen in a low-stakes, high-interest context. A reviewer asking *"does this program improve executive function?"* gets the honest answer: *"it creates structured opportunities for students to practice EF within the workshop context; durable transfer beyond the workshop is not within the program's scope or evidence base."*

#### Cluster 4 — Reduced refusal, reduced avoidance, reduced behavioural incidents within the workshop

This is the most cautious of the four clusters, because behavioural outcome claims are the easiest to overclaim and the hardest to substantiate in a small single-teacher cohort. The program expects that, across a semester, the frequency of within-workshop refusals, avoidance behaviours, and behavioural incidents will decrease for most students — not to zero, and not necessarily for every student, but on average and across the cohort.

**The mechanism.** The program's refusal protocol (§5.7) treats refusal as information rather than defiance, which de-escalates the teacher-student dynamic around refusal events. The navigation-card-traces-the-path discipline (Principle 2) removes the teacher from many of the compliance dynamics that escalate ODD-flavoured refusals in traditional classrooms (the student is not refusing the teacher; they are navigating with a printed card). The structured autonomy of the three-tier system (Principle 5) removes many of the triggers for autonomy-based refusal. The predictable routine (Principle 1) reduces the surprise-driven anxiety that can present as avoidance. The relational infrastructure (Principle 8 — teacher-student relationship) provides the trust substrate on which students can afford to try without shame. Each of these mechanisms has evidence in the literature review; in combination, they *should* reduce the frequency of refusal and incident events within the workshop, though the program cannot promise a specific reduction level.

**How the program expects this to show up.** The §5.8 tracking sheet's third column (red card or behavioural incident: Y/N plus one-line note) is the direct measurement. A student whose red-card column goes from "Y" most sessions to "N" most sessions over the course of a semester is showing the expected pattern. A student whose "started within 5 minutes" column improves is showing reduced avoidance. The refusal-tracking notes in the session log (§5.9) build a refusal pattern over time.

**Evidence base.** §3.1 on Barker & de Lugt (2022) review of evidence-based classroom practices for students with oppositional defiant disorder, which included Rosenberg (1986)'s finding of 82–91% Percentage of Non-overlapping Data (PND) for token-economy interventions — a strong directional signal that structured reinforcement-based approaches reduce disruptive behaviour in this population. §3.1 on Gage, Kramer II & Ellis (2021) on school climate perceptions in EBD students — formal IEP-supported environments improved students' sense of belonging, which the program interprets as a structural precondition for reduced avoidance. §3.1 on Gersib & Mason (2023) meta-analysis showing large effects for behavior interventions in self-contained EBD classrooms (g = 0.93 overall). **None of these studies is a study of a physical-computing workshop**, and §9 Honest Limitations restates the gap explicitly — the program's expected behavioural outcomes are inferential extensions from adjacent literature, not direct empirical predictions.

**Caveat.** Behavioural outcomes are the most variable and the most affected by factors the program does not control: the student's state on a given day, the group composition, what happened before the student walked into the workshop, what is happening at home, medication adjustments. A student whose red-card column does not improve across a semester is not necessarily a program failure — they may be a program success whose life circumstances are producing independent pressures. The tracking sheet is a *pattern-detection tool for the teacher*, not a summative evaluation metric.

### §7.2 Teacher-level outcomes the program aims to produce

Principle 9 established teacher sustainability as a first-class design constraint. The program therefore has explicit teacher-level outcomes alongside student-level outcomes, and the evaluation framework treats teacher outcomes as equal in priority, not as a secondary concern.

**The teacher-level outcomes are:**

1. **The teacher remains non-burnt-out across the implementation year.** Operationally: the teacher is not in Gilmour et al. (2022)'s "struggling" burnout profile at the end of the year, and the teacher reports at the end of each semester that the workshop is a sustainable part of their weekly workload (not a drain they are compensating for with personal time).
2. **Between-session preparation time stays under 30 minutes per session** after the first few weeks. Principle 9's design commitment is that the teacher should not have to rewrite materials from scratch during the week. A session prep time that creeps above 30 minutes is a signal that the materials need revision, not that the teacher is working harder.
3. **The teacher's relational capacity for individual students remains intact.** A teacher who starts the year able to do 5-second relational check-ins before rotation and loses that capacity mid-semester is a teacher who has moved into crisis-management mode — which is specifically the mode the program is designed to avoid. The check-in discipline is a teacher-health indicator as well as a student-relationship one.
4. **The teacher reports growth in their own sense of effectiveness.** §3.5 on Taylor et al. (2024) reported marked improvements in teacher job satisfaction following PBL implementation in EBD classrooms — the program hopes to see a similar pattern, recognizing that a teacher who feels effective is both personally sustained and professionally growing.

**How teacher outcomes will be measured.** End-of-semester informal self-assessment: the teacher answers six short prompts (*"How sustainable did this semester feel?"* / *"What was the hardest project to run, and why?"* / *"Which students surprised you?"* / *"What would you change for next semester?"* / *"How is your session prep time trending?"* / *"Are you still able to do relational check-ins during rotations?"*). The answers are notes, not ratings. The point is the teacher's own reflective pattern across semesters, not a comparison to an external benchmark.

**Evidence base.** §3.1 on Gilmour, Sandilos, Pilny, Schwartz & Wehby (2022) identifying three teacher burnout profiles (flourishing, buffered, struggling) among teachers of students with EBD, with meaningful links between burnout profile and observed classroom management quality. §3.5 on Taylor et al. (2024) on marked improvements in teacher and paraprofessional job satisfaction after PBL implementation in EBD contexts. §3.3 on Koslouski, Stark & Chafouleas (2023) on secondary traumatic stress in educators working with students carrying trauma histories and the importance of educator well-being as a condition for effective practice.

### §7.3 What will be measured, and how

The measurement apparatus is intentionally minimal. It consists of four instruments sitting at four different timescales, each a short extension of materials the program already produces for other reasons. The four instruments nest: per-session rows feed the weekly digest, weekly digests feed the end-of-semester reflection, and the artifact completion log runs alongside all three as a longitudinal skill-acquisition record.

**Instrument 1 — The simple tracking sheet** (§5.8), filled **per session**. Three columns per student per session: started within 5 minutes (Y/N), completed at least one milestone today (Y/N), red card or behavioural incident (Y/N + one-line note). The teacher fills this in during the Close-Out Circle; filling takes less than 30 seconds per student. Across a semester, the sheet produces three longitudinal data streams per student: an engagement proxy, a milestone-completion proxy, and a behavioural-incident pattern. This is the program's primary per-session measurement instrument and is designed to be sustainable indefinitely.

**Instrument 2 — Weekly data collector** (see `Weekly_Data_Collector_Template.md` in the project root), filled **once per week** at the end of the week. A single-page template the teacher fills in approximately 10 minutes that aggregates the week's sessions into a digest covering: per-student quick-check rows rolled up from the §5.8 tracking sheet (started-within-5 pattern, milestones completed this week, any red cards), cohort-level observations ("which students surprised me," "what's working," "what's not"), materials and environment observations (hardware failures, navigation cards that caused confusion, reference cards that got used heavily), Claude Code integration patterns (how many students used Channel A Level 1 / 2 / 3 / Channel B this week, any standout moments), teacher sustainability indicators (between-session prep time, end-of-week energy, relational check-in capacity), and a short "to revise next week" action list. The weekly collector is the primary mechanism the program uses to capture in-vivo adjustments (see §1 "A living document") — the patterns the teacher notices each week feed directly into navigation-card and milestone revisions for the next week's sessions.

**Instrument 3 — Artifact completion log,** updated **whenever a project completes**. A one-line-per-project record per student of which projects the student has completed and at what tier. Format: `Student name | Project 1 T1 | Project 2 T1 | Project 3 T2 | Project 4 (in progress)`. The teacher updates it when a project is completed. Across a year, this produces a per-student skill-acquisition record (per Cluster 2 outcomes) and a cohort-level completion profile.

**Instrument 4 — End-of-semester teacher reflection,** written **once per semester**. Six prompts described in §7.2. Written by the teacher in 15–20 minutes at the end of each semester. Not graded, not shared externally, not used for accountability — used by the teacher to detect their own patterns across the full semester and by the program's later implementations as a qualitative record of what went well and what didn't. The end-of-semester reflection draws directly on the weekly data collector entries from the preceding weeks, so the teacher is not trying to remember 15 sessions' worth of detail from scratch.

**What is deliberately not included.** No standardized pre/post testing. No behavioural rating scales (BASC, CBCL, SDQ). No comparison groups. No control conditions. No external observer coding. No video analysis of sessions. Every one of these instruments has been deliberately excluded because the overhead of administering them would either (a) burn the teacher out, violating Principle 9, or (b) require external research infrastructure the program does not have and does not claim to have. The program is not a research study; it is a workshop that tracks its own practice lightly.

**Informal complementary observations.** The teacher will also accumulate informal observations that do not fit on the tracking sheet: photos of finished builds (taken by the teacher or the student), Close-Out Circle remarks captured in the session log, informal feedback from students and parents, and notes on memorable moments. These are not structured data but they are legitimate signals of how the program is going.

### §7.4 What the program will NOT measure, and why

Explicit boundaries on the evaluation framework are as important as the framework itself, because a program that leaves its boundaries implicit invites the expectation that it is measuring things it is not.

**The program will not claim** — and the evaluation framework will not attempt to measure:

1. **Standardized test improvements.** The program has no pre/post testing and no comparison condition. Any claim that workshop participation improves state-standardized test performance would be unsupported and dishonest.
2. **Long-term behavioural change beyond the workshop.** The tracking sheet measures in-workshop behaviour. It cannot measure whether a student who is calmer in the workshop is also calmer in their next class, at home, or a year after the program ends. §9 Honest Limitations restates this.
3. **Academic transfer to other subjects.** A student who becomes comfortable with Arduino does not necessarily become comfortable with algebra. The program does not make transfer claims.
4. **Replacement for IEP or therapeutic goals.** The workshop is a complement to a student's IEP, not a substitute. A student whose IEP specifies therapeutic supports continues to need those supports; the workshop does not replace them.
5. **Effects attributable specifically to differentiated PBL versus other instructional formats.** The program has no comparison condition, so it cannot isolate the effect of DI+PBL from the effect of being in a small supportive workshop at all. Any reviewer looking for causal attribution should look elsewhere — the program is piloted, not causally evaluated.
6. **Effects attributable specifically to Claude Code integration.** For the same reason: no comparison condition. The program cannot claim that Claude Code *causes* positive outcomes, only that it is one of several scaffolds the program provides and that Principle 7 is explicitly flagged as the program's most evidence-thin component.

**Why the program chooses to be explicit about these boundaries.** A funder evaluating this program should know exactly what they are and are not getting. The program is offering a carefully-designed pilot grounded in verified research, implementing nine principles with direct literature support, producing lightweight longitudinal data on engagement and milestone completion, and committing to honest reporting of what works and what doesn't. The program is not offering a randomized controlled trial, a clinical behavioural intervention, or a guarantee of transferable outcomes. A funder who needs the latter should fund a research study, not a workshop. A funder who wants to fund a principled pilot of differentiated PBL for an underserved population is funding exactly what this program is.

### §7.5 What counts as success for a first implementation year

Because the program is a pilot, success criteria for year one are deliberately modest and are organized around whether the program is sustainable and whether the core mechanism is working, not around whether students are transformed.

**Year-one cohort success looks like:**

- **The workshop runs for a full school year without the teacher moving into the struggling burnout profile.** This is the Principle 9 commitment made operational. If the workshop is not sustainable for the teacher, nothing else matters.
- **Most students (at least two thirds of the cohort) complete at least two projects across the year, at any tier, and at least one completed project produces a build the student is proud enough to show a peer or family member.** This is the super-target made operational. Two projects is not a high bar, and "proud enough to show someone" is a student-determined marker, not a teacher judgment.
- **Most students (at least two thirds of the cohort) show improvement on the tracking sheet's "started within 5 minutes" column across the first semester** — a within-cohort trajectory, not an external benchmark. A student who started 2 of 10 sessions within 5 minutes in the first six weeks and 6 of 10 sessions within 5 minutes in the next six weeks is showing the expected pattern. A student whose number does not improve is noted, and the teacher reviews what is happening for that specific student.
- **Red-card and behavioural incident frequency is stable or declining across the year for most students.** Not all students, not to zero, not on any particular trajectory — just a pattern the teacher can see on the tracking sheet that suggests the workshop is a setting where students can regulate.
- **Refusal patterns shift from project-number-based refusals to specific-day-based refusals.** A student who started the year refusing Project 3 as a category and ended the year refusing occasional sessions for specific reasons (a hard morning, a fight with a peer, an illness) is showing a mature refusal pattern that the teacher can work with (§5.7).
- **The teacher's end-of-semester reflection describes the workshop as sustainable and worth continuing.** If the teacher's reflection describes it as unsustainable, the program needs revision before year two — and the revisions are recorded and fed back into the materials.
- **At least one student each semester chooses to attempt a Tier 3 open-design project**, demonstrating that the autonomy architecture (Principle 5) is accessible to at least some students and is worth preserving.
- **No safety incidents on Project 8** if Project 8 is attempted during the implementation year. Safety on Project 8 is non-negotiable and a safety incident is a program failure regardless of all other outcomes.

**What year-one success does NOT require.**

- Every student completing every project. Many students will complete only two or three projects, and that is within the design.
- Every student reaching Tier 3. Most students will stay at Tier 1 or Tier 2 for most of the year, and that is within the design.
- Every student's behavioural tracking improving. Some students will have hard years for reasons the workshop does not control, and that is not a program failure.
- Quantitative comparison to a non-workshop baseline. The program is a pilot, not a controlled study.
- Demonstrated transfer of skills or behaviours outside the workshop. The program's evidence base does not support transfer claims and the measurement framework does not attempt to detect transfer.

**Year two and beyond.** If year one meets the criteria above, the program continues with light revisions based on the teacher's reflection. The three-year roadmap in §10 describes how the program scales or matures across year two and year three. If year one does not meet the criteria, §10 also describes the program's response: honest identification of what broke, targeted revision, and either a second pilot year with the revisions or a documented decision not to continue. A pilot that does not meet its success criteria and does not produce honest lessons is worse than a pilot that meets them — the program commits to the former even if year one is harder than expected.

## §9 Honest Limitations — What this program cannot claim, and why that matters

A program that describes only its strengths is either dishonest or poorly understood by its author. §9 is the place where the residual caveats scattered through the document are consolidated and stated plainly, so a reviewer, a funder, a future teacher implementing this program, or a student's parent can see exactly what the program is and is not claiming.

This section is structured around seven clusters of limitations: the evidence gaps in the literature the program draws on (§9.1), the citations that were partially rather than fully verified (§9.2), the principles and practices that are piloted rather than empirically supported (§9.3), the things the program's measurement framework cannot detect (§9.4), the scope and transferability limits (§9.5), the boundary between what the program is and what it is not (§9.6), and the program's response to its own limitations (§9.7).

**A note on why §9 is this long.** A shorter honest-limitations section would be easier to write and easier to read, but it would also be less useful. The program's commitment is that every limitation a thoughtful reviewer might raise is already acknowledged here, and that commitment is worth the page count. A funder who reads §9 and finds a limitation they were worried about already stated and scoped is a funder who can make an informed decision.

### §9.1 Evidence gaps in the literature this program draws on

The program's design principles (§4) and methodology (§5) are grounded in a verified corpus of research (§3). That corpus has specific, identifiable gaps that the program's claims do not cross.

**1. No randomized controlled trial of differentiated instruction combined with project-based learning for emotionally and behaviourally disordered students using Arduino or any physical-computing hardware exists in the verified corpus.** This is the most honest way to state the program's central evidence gap. Each of the three ingredients — DI, PBL, and EBD-specific pedagogy — is independently supported. The combination of all three, applied to an Arduino or physical-computing workshop, has no direct empirical study. The program rests on the claim that combining three independently-supported ingredients is a reasonable design move, not on the claim that the combination has been tested and shown to work.

**2. The empirical PBL-in-EBD literature is small — three studies, different designs, same direction.** Taylor, Allen, Van & Moohr (2024), Sciacca (2025), and Foster & Shriner (2023) are the three studies in the verified corpus that directly examined project-based learning with students carrying EBD (or substantially overlapping) identifications. Taylor et al. is a quantitative single-case design with four middle-school EBD students reporting very large on-task effect sizes (BC-SMD 1.70–3.81) and marked teacher-satisfaction improvements — feasibility-scoped, not efficacy-scoped, small sample, academic rather than physical-computing. Sciacca (2025) is a qualitative collective case study with three Pennsylvania teachers (self-contained, inclusive, military-academy support settings), reporting three themes (supporting student learning via building trusting relationships and adaptation, positive impact on students across academic and non-academic domains, and the teacher-experience paradox of "more work to prepare, less during the instruction, because the kids drive so much more of their own learning"). Foster & Shriner (2023) is a qualitative case study at a residential mental-health school serving students with adverse childhood experiences that reported decreased negative behaviours and increased engagement, academic skills, and self-regulation when students participated in PjBL. All three studies point directionally in the same direction, but none is a randomized controlled trial and none individually carries generalization weight. The program's honest claim is: *the three empirical studies available converge on directional support for PjBL with students with EBD, and that directional convergence is what grounds the program's choice of PjBL as its instructional format. The program does not claim settled efficacy for PjBL with this population, because the evidence base is too thin to support that claim.*

**3. Zero Arduino, microcontroller, or physical-computing research for EBD, ADHD, or learning-disability populations.** The program's core medium — building things with an Arduino — has no direct empirical study for the population the program serves. The program draws on the broader makerspace-and-tangible-computing literature (Gomez 2019 on makerspace for middle-school EBD; Guneysu Ozgur et al. 2020 on tangible robot-assisted activities for special education) as the closest available evidence base, and §3.6 was explicit about that inferential extension. Anyone who claims the Arduino pedagogy is "evidence-based for this population" is overclaiming, and the program does not make that claim.

**4. Zero aerial-robotics, drone, or flight-STEM research for any special-needs population.** §3.6 documented a deliberate search for this evidence base; no substantive results. Project 8 (the tiny quadcopter capstone) therefore rests on inferential extension from the ground-robotics literature, the makerspace-for-EBD literature, and the modular-tangible-STEM literature — none of which studied flight. The program is explicit throughout §6.12 and in the Project 8 per-project file that this is an inferentially-grounded pilot, not an evidence-based intervention, and the safety protocol is first-class precisely because the evidence base for student safety in drone-based learning is also thin. A reviewer asking *"where is the research that shows flight projects work for this population?"* gets the honest answer: *"it does not exist, and that is exactly why Project 8 carries the safety discipline it does."*

**5. Zero Claude Code, AI-tutor, or AI-paired-learning research for neurodivergent students.** Principle 7 (Claude Code as dual-channel support — pair programmer and scaffolded tutorial) has no direct empirical support in the verified corpus. The program acknowledged this in the Principle 7 honest caveat and in §5.5, and reinforces it here: both Channel A (three-level coding support) and Channel B (conversational tutorial walk-through) are the program's own design responses to the heterogeneity of the student population, grounded in adjacent evidence (Rosen 2014 on external cognitive scaffolds; Morsink 2022 on self-determination theory framing; Smith et al. 2020 on extrinsic support for ADHD adolescents) but not directly empirically tested. Channel B in particular — the scaffolded tutorial role — is even more evidence-thin than Channel A and is flagged as such.

**6. The trauma-informed-schools literature is not as strong as it is often presented.** §3.3 on Watson & Astor (2025) — the critical review of trauma-informed approaches in schools — argued that the whole-school empirical base for trauma-informed practices is weaker than the popular literature suggests, and that many claimed effects rest on small, non-randomized, single-setting studies. The program's trauma-aware elements (predictable routine, greeting by name, relational check-ins, cool-down corner) are inferentially grounded in the broader relationship and self-regulation literatures rather than in trauma-informed effect-size claims, and the program does not use the label "trauma-informed" as if it were a research-validated intervention.

**7. Taylor et al. (2024) had a small sample and was feasibility-scoped.** The program references Taylor 2024 repeatedly because it is the strongest direct quantitative analog — but the reference is honest about the study's scope. Taylor reported engagement and job-satisfaction gains from four students in one classroom with one teacher and one paraprofessional, not effect sizes comparable to a larger RCT, and the program's use of Taylor is limited to directional claims. Sciacca (2025) and Foster & Shriner (2023), described in item 2 above, add qualitative case-study support in the same direction — which strengthens the directional claim without closing the evidence gap. None of the three is a generalizable efficacy study.

**8. Sciacca (2025)'s three participants did not fully implement all six HQPBL criteria, and neither does this program.** Sciacca's honest limitations note that the "public product" element in her participants' PjBL implementations was limited to classroom peers or colleagues, not authentic external audiences, and that student reflection was unclear in two of the three cases. This matters for the program because the Arduino workshop's individual-student framing (§6.3, Appendix A) produces the same gap against the HQPBL canonical criteria: there is no shared group artefact for "collaboration" in the HQPBL sense, and the "public product" for most projects is the student's own finished device rather than an authentic external-audience artefact. The program is explicit that it falls short of a canonical HQPBL implementation on two of the six criteria, and it does not claim to meet the full HQPBL quality standard. What the program *does* claim is that it implements the other four HQPBL criteria (intellectual challenge and accomplishment, authenticity, project management, and reflection) and that it is a deliberate, scoped departure from the canonical framework rather than an accidental one.

### §9.2 Citations that were partially rather than fully verified

During the program's literature review, every citation was put through a verification pass against Crossref (for items with DOIs) or authoritative source lookups (for items without DOIs). Most citations verified cleanly. A small number verified only partially — either because the full text was paywalled or truncated, or because the audit could confirm the paper exists and its abstract matches the claim but could not line-by-line verify the full-text evidence behind the claim.

The following citations were flagged during verification and their claims in the master document were softened to match what the abstract or available excerpts could support:

- **Gilmour, Sandilos, Pilny, Schwartz & Wehby (2022)** on teacher burnout profiles among teachers of students with EBD. The citation verified and the three-profile finding (flourishing / buffered / struggling) is supported by the abstract, but full-text access was partial. The program's use of Gilmour 2022 is limited to the three-profile framing and the general claim that burnout profile relates to observed classroom management quality — not to specific effect sizes or subgroup analyses.
- **Cibrian et al.** on sensory and tangible interfaces for children with ADHD. The paper was available only at the abstract level during verification due to publisher paywall. The program's claims touching this literature are consistent with the abstract but were not line-by-line verified against the full text, and §3.6 uses adjacent full-text sources (Guneysu Ozgur 2020) as the primary evidence for tangible-interface claims.
- **Harrison et al.** on antecedent-based interventions for students with EBD. The paper was available only at the abstract level during verification. The program's reference to antecedent modification was re-scoped during the literature review audit to use the WWC-category language ("instructional and self-management interventions") that the paper actually uses, rather than terminology the program had inferred and then attributed to Harrison.
- **Billingsley et al.** on special-education teacher working conditions and attrition. The paper was available only at the abstract level during verification. The program's references to teacher-attrition literature are consistent with the abstract but rest primarily on Gilmour 2022 (also partially verified) as the stronger evidence anchor.

**How the program handles partial-verification citations.** Every claim attributed to a partially-verified paper has been rewritten to match what the abstract or available excerpts can support. Where a claim would have required full-text verification to defend, the claim was either dropped or softened to a directional statement. A reviewer who reads the master document and finds a strong specific claim attributed to one of these papers has found a drafting error; please flag it and the authors will correct it.

### §9.3 Principles and practices that are piloted, not empirically supported

Several of the program's design elements are **the program's own design responses** to the heterogeneity and specific needs of the student population, grounded in adjacent evidence but not directly empirically tested as described. These are flagged here as piloted practices — honest work that the program commits to evaluating in use but cannot currently claim as evidence-based.

**1. Principle 7 — both channels.** The three-level Channel A model (Level 1 upload, Level 2 modify, Level 3 dialogue) and the Channel B scaffolded tutorial role are both piloted practices. Principle 7 is the program's most evidence-thin component and is flagged as such throughout the document — in §4, in §5.5, and here. The mechanism is inferentially grounded in Rosen 2014 on scaffolded EF, in Morsink 2022's SDT framework (the program infers from the framework, not from Morsink's own findings which do not address AI tutoring), and in Smith et al. 2020 on extrinsic support for ADHD adolescents. None of these papers directly supports the dual-channel AI-paired-learning design the program is implementing. Channel B in particular has no published precedent at all; it is a novel application of the external-scaffold logic to instruction-parsing rather than code-writing.

**2. The three-tier readiness system applied to Arduino projects specifically.** Differentiated Instruction's three-readiness-tier model is well-established in the general DI literature (Tomlinson 2014 and successors). Its application to Arduino projects for an EBD population is the program's own design choice and has no direct empirical test. A reviewer asking *"does three-tier DI work for Arduino projects with EBD students?"* gets the honest answer: *"the general DI tier model is supported; the Arduino-specific application is piloted."*

**3. The navigation-card-is-the-boss discipline (Principle 2).** The idea that routing instruction through a printed card reduces the teacher as an authority-target is consistent with the ODD intervention literature (Barker & de Lugt 2022 and the studies within that review) and with the non-human-mediator literature on scaffolded learning. The specific implementation — laminated per-milestone cards with "done when" criteria, stuck protocols, and Claude Code level indicators — is the program's own design and has not been empirically tested as a discrete intervention.

**4. The dual-channel Claude Code integration as an artifact of the workshop's day-to-day operation.** Using Claude Code as both pair programmer and scaffolded tutorial simultaneously is a novel pattern with no published research base. The program is piloting it responsibly: both channels are optional for students, both are supplemented by printed materials, and neither is required for a student to complete the program.

**5. The refusal-of-order protocol (§5.7).** Treating refusal as information rather than defiance, and responding with non-linear entry and just-in-time mini-modules, is consistent with autonomy-supportive teaching literature and with ODD intervention principles but is the program's own synthesis. It has not been discretely tested.

**6. The two-block session structure with movement break (§5.2, Principle 6).** Movement-as-intervention for ADHD has directional support in the verified corpus (Qiu et al. 2023 on non-pharmacological ADHD interventions), but the specific integration of movement breaks into a physical-computing workshop rhythm is the program's design choice, not a directly-tested intervention.

**How the program handles piloted practices.** Every one of these practices is piloted in good faith with an honest acknowledgment that it lacks direct empirical support. The program commits to evaluating them in use via the §7.3 measurement framework, to reporting what works and what doesn't in the end-of-semester teacher reflection, and to revising year-two materials based on what year one actually shows. A funder who wants every practice to be pre-validated before funding is — honestly — a funder who would never fund a novel intervention and should probably fund established clinical programs instead. A funder who is willing to support a principled pilot of a carefully-reasoned novel synthesis is funding exactly what this program is.

### §9.4 What the program's measurement framework cannot detect

§7.3 described the three-instrument lightweight measurement framework (tracking sheet, artifact completion log, teacher reflection) and §7.4 listed what the framework does not measure. §9.4 expands on the latter list and makes the limits explicit.

**The program's measurement framework cannot detect:**

1. **Effects attributable specifically to any single component of the program.** The program has no comparison condition and no controlled isolation of variables. If a student improves across a semester, the program cannot distinguish whether the improvement is due to the three-tier system, the navigation cards, Claude Code integration, the teacher's relational approach, the movement break structure, the student's own maturation, a medication adjustment, a change at home, or a combination of any of these. The tracking sheet shows patterns; it does not identify causes.
2. **Long-term effects beyond the workshop context.** The tracking sheet measures in-workshop behaviour. A student who is more engaged, less avoidant, and less disruptive in the Tuesday-morning Arduino workshop may or may not show the same patterns in their math class on Wednesday, at home on Friday, or in their next grade's classroom a year from now. The program's design does not enable follow-up measurement outside the workshop, and any claim of long-term effect would be unsupported.
3. **Transfer of technical skills to other contexts.** A student who has acquired breadboarding, Arduino IDE fluency, or soldering within the workshop may or may not retain and apply these skills in a different context a year later. The program does not test for retention or transfer.
4. **Academic-subject transfer.** A student who completes the program is not thereby measured as improved in algebra, reading, writing, or any other academic subject. The program makes no transfer claims and does not measure for transfer.
5. **Effects on non-participant peers, family members, or teachers other than the workshop teacher.** The program's measurement is scoped to the workshop. Anything happening outside the workshop is outside the measurement frame.
6. **Population-level generalization.** The program is implemented at a single school with a specific student population taught by a specific teacher. Nothing the program measures can generalize to "EBD students" as a population, "Arduino workshops" as a category, or "differentiated PBL" as a general intervention. Findings from this program apply to this program in this setting with this teacher and these students; any extrapolation beyond that is inferential and should be stated as such.
7. **Causal claims of any kind.** The program is a pilot, not a causal study. Patterns observed can be reported as patterns; they cannot be reported as effects of the program.

### §9.5 Scope and transferability limits

**1. Single-school implementation.** The program is designed for Agourim School, a small special-education school serving students in grades 7–12 with emotional and behavioural challenges. The program's design is specifically tuned to Agourim's student population, its physical workshop space, its token-economy behavioural infrastructure, and its single-teacher staffing model. Transferring the program to a school with a different population, a different behavioural infrastructure, or a different staffing model would require adaptation — potentially substantial adaptation — that this document does not prescribe. Other teachers working with similar populations can use this document as a template and as a set of design principles, but should not treat it as a drop-in curriculum.

**2. Single-teacher implementation.** The program is designed around a single teacher managing three to eight students. A program running with two teachers, with a teacher and a paraprofessional, or with a student-teacher ratio outside the three-to-eight range would run differently than the program as described. §5 (teacher rotation, relational check-ins, between-session maintenance) assumes one adult; co-teaching arrangements would need explicit protocol additions that the program does not currently provide.

**3. Hebrew and English.** The master document and all per-project files are drafted in English. The existing quadcopter project's Hebrew translation (`BUILD_INSTRUCTIONS_STUDENTS_HE.md`) is being updated in parallel during the Project 8 production pass, and the program's ultimate artifact family will include Hebrew versions of student-facing materials (navigation cards, reference cards, HTML tutorials, Claude Code tutorial-channel scaffolds). Teacher-facing materials (master document, per-project source files, troubleshooting crib sheets) may remain English-primary in year one, with Hebrew translation in year two if needed. A school whose teachers do not read English at working level would need a fuller translation than year-one can deliver.

**4. Three-way digital-infrastructure dependency.** The program's scaffolding architecture assumes three pieces of digital infrastructure work together at every session: (a) the shared robotics-workshop Google Drive account (§5.1) as the persistent per-student storage, with all per-student folders organised as subfolders of `G:\My Drive\Arduino_Projects\<student_nickname>\`; (b) **Google Drive for Desktop** on every Windows 11 workshop PC, logged into the shared Workshop account and mounting the account's `My Drive` as the local `G:\My Drive\` directory Claude Code can operate on; and (c) **Claude Code** itself, launched pointed at each student's current project folder (§5.5). If any one of the three breaks during a session, the consequences are graduated rather than catastrophic, but each fallback loses real capacity:

- **If Claude Code is unavailable** (product discontinued, internet outage, provider ToS change): Channel A Level 1 still works because pre-written `.ino` files live in the student's Drive folder and open in the Arduino IDE directly. Channel A Level 2/3 and Channel B become unavailable. The program's fallback is to printed materials and teacher support only, which the dual-channel scaffolding was designed to supplement rather than replace.
- **If Google Drive for Desktop is unavailable** (software bug, Windows update, account misconfiguration): the student's previous work on the shared Drive is still accessible via the Google Drive web interface, but Claude Code cannot read or write the files locally. Fallback: students work on files in the local Downloads folder for that session and the teacher manually syncs them to the shared Drive afterward. This is awkward but not session-breaking.
- **If the shared Workshop Drive itself is unavailable** (Google outage, account lockout, Drive quota exceeded): the per-student persistent workspace is unreachable for the duration of the outage. Fallback: students work on Arduino IDE with sketches saved to the local PC's Documents folder; the teacher collects and manually uploads to the Drive once access is restored. A session run this way loses most of the Claude Code integration capacity but can still complete hardware milestones.
- **If all three break simultaneously**: the session becomes a pure printed-navigation-card-and-Arduino-IDE session with no digital persistence and no AI support. The program's fundamental design still works at this level because the printed navigation cards are the primary instructional medium and the Arduino IDE runs offline; what is lost is the ownership architecture and the Claude Code dual-channel scaffolding. A program running in this mode for more than one or two isolated sessions is a signal that the digital infrastructure needs attention, not a signal that the program should keep running without its infrastructure indefinitely.

The three-way dependency is a real operational risk, not a theoretical one, and the program's year-one pilot should expect at least one or two sessions to fall back to the Arduino-IDE-only mode when something breaks. The teacher is asked to note any fallback sessions in the weekly data collector (§7.3 Instrument 2) so that patterns across fallback incidents can be tracked and addressed.

**5. Hardware supply-chain dependency.** The program's hardware sourcing depends on AliExpress availability and pricing. Major disruptions to AliExpress supply (currency swings, shipping embargos, political events) would affect the program's year-over-year cost and delivery timelines. The budget (§8) includes a 10% contingency line, but a disruption larger than the contingency would require either additional funding or scope adjustment.

### §9.6 What the program is and is not

A clear statement of the program's category helps a reviewer calibrate their expectations.

**The program is:**

- A carefully-designed educational pilot for an underserved population.
- A research-grounded curriculum with nine principles each supported by verified evidence.
- An implementation of differentiated instruction in a project-based learning format, for students with emotional and behavioural challenges, using Arduino and ESP32 hardware.
- A teacher-sustainable workshop that one teacher can run with three to eight students.
- An honestly-reported pilot whose successes and failures will be documented.

**The program is not:**

- A randomized controlled trial. §7.3, §7.4, and §9.4 are explicit about this.
- A clinical or therapeutic intervention. The program supports students who also receive clinical and therapeutic interventions from other providers; it does not replace them. A student with a diagnosed condition continues to need the care their clinicians provide, and the workshop is a complement to that care, not a substitute.
- An IEP goal replacement. A student's Individualized Education Program specifies goals and accommodations the student is entitled to. The workshop does not affect those entitlements and does not count as evidence of IEP goal progress unless the IEP explicitly incorporates workshop participation.
- A trauma-informed intervention in the formal clinical sense. §3.3 on Watson & Astor (2025) critical review established that "trauma-informed" is a label with weaker empirical backing than the popular literature suggests, and the program uses trauma-aware design principles (predictable routine, relational check-ins, cool-down corner) without claiming to be a formal trauma-informed intervention.
- A behavioural intervention comparable to Gersib & Mason (2023)'s meta-analytic effect sizes. The program implements one of Gersib's three relational-moderator components (adult mentoring) and does not claim the meta-analytic effect.
- A guarantee. No educational intervention for this population works for every student. The program is designed so positive outcomes can happen; the teacher's role is to make space for them; the student's role is to show up; and honest reporting is the program's commitment even when outcomes are mixed or disappointing.

### §9.7 How the program handles its limitations

Limitations that are acknowledged but not addressed are decoration. Limitations that are acknowledged and actively handled are part of the design. The program's response to its own limitations is:

1. **Report honestly.** The end-of-semester teacher reflection (§7.2) records what worked and what did not, including failures, refusals, and cases where the program fell short. Year-one reports may include sentences like *"Three students never reached Project 3; for two of them the reason was pattern X and for one it was pattern Y"* — that is the expected honesty level, not a softened marketing version.
2. **Pilot at pilot scale.** The year-one implementation is one cohort at one school. The program does not scale out until the pilot has produced enough honest data to support the scaling. §10 describes the conditions under which year-two and year-three expansion happens; the default path if year-one produces mixed or negative results is revision, not expansion.
3. **Scope claims to evidence.** Every claim in this document is scoped to what its supporting evidence supports. When the evidence is thin, the claim is thin. When the evidence is inferential, the claim is flagged as inferential. When the evidence is absent, the claim is flagged as piloted. This is the Pattern 10 discipline: the paper's own authors' conclusions, not what the program would like to extrapolate from them.
4. **Revise between cohorts.** Year-one outcomes feed into year-two revisions. Year-two outcomes feed into year-three revisions. The program is a learning system that does not pretend to be finished at year one, and the master document will be revised across years as implementation data accumulates.
5. **Acknowledge what is outside the program's scope.** Parental support, clinical care, IEP administration, student housing, medication management, family crisis — all of these matter enormously for student outcomes and are all outside the program's scope. The program does not claim to address any of them and does not pretend that workshop participation is a substitute for the other supports a student may need.
6. **Stop if stopping is the right answer.** If year-one implementation produces clear evidence that the program is not sustainable for the teacher, not beneficial for the students, or not safe (particularly for Project 8), the program stops and reports why. A pilot whose honest lesson is *"this does not work"* is a successful pilot in the sense that matters — it has produced knowledge — and the program commits to that framing even when the news is uncomfortable.

The program is offered in good faith, grounded in verified research, designed around its population, honest about what it is and is not, and committed to learning from whatever year one produces. That is the program's offer; §9 is the boundary around it.

## §10 Implementation Roadmap — Years one through three

This section describes what implementing the program looks like across a three-year horizon: what happens in year one as a first-cohort pilot (§10.1), what happens between years one and two based on what year one produced (§10.2), what year two looks like under a continuation scenario (§10.3), and what year three and beyond look like if the program has matured enough to be documented and shared beyond Agourim (§10.4). A final subsection describes the response plan if year one does not meet its success criteria — the honest "what if this doesn't work" path (§10.5).

The roadmap is deliberately not a prescription for individual students. Students do not walk a three-year arc — a student may participate in the workshop for one semester, for a full year, across multiple years, or not at all in any given year. The three-year horizon describes the **program**'s maturation, not any student's trajectory.

### §10.1 Year one — First-cohort pilot implementation

Year one is a single-cohort pilot at Agourim School. Its primary goals are (a) to verify that the program runs as designed in practice, (b) to produce enough honest data to decide whether year two is warranted and what revisions it needs, and (c) to accumulate the small positive experiences that the super-target commits to (§1).

**Pre-launch phase (approximately 60–90 days before the first session).** The teacher confirms the final hardware list against the per-project BOMs and places procurement orders through AliExpress with the 15–45 day lead time built in. The teacher prints, laminates, and organizes the full artifact family for Projects 1–3 (the artifact families for Projects 4–8 can be printed later, as the cohort approaches each project). The teacher sets up the workshop space with workstations, parts library organization, tool storage, safety equipment, and the cool-down corner (§5.6). The teacher reviews the master document, the per-project files, the principles, and the refusal-of-order protocol. The teacher does **not** attend external training workshops — per Principle 9 and Colbert et al. (2025), the materials are designed to be the training.

**First three sessions.** The first three sessions of the first cohort are the highest-stakes sessions of the year. The first session is almost entirely Arrival, workshop-norms introduction, tool walk-through, and Project 1 Navigation Card 1 (wire one LED and upload the blink sketch). The second session extends Project 1. The third session completes Project 1 for Tier 1 students and begins Project 2 for students who are ready. The teacher is deliberately generous with pacing — no student is pushed to finish Project 1 by session three, and any student who finishes Project 1 with a positive experience in the first three sessions is a program-design success.

**First month.** By the end of the first month (four to six sessions), the teacher should be seeing: (a) most students starting work within 10 minutes of the Work Block beginning (the 5-minute target is for later in the semester, not the first month), (b) most students completing at least one milestone per session, (c) red-card incidents stable or declining, (d) student tier assignments settling into place based on actual in-session behaviour rather than predictions, and (e) the teacher's own between-session preparation time settling at or below 30 minutes per session.

**If the first month is not tracking.** If the tracking sheet after four to six sessions shows most students failing to start within 10 minutes, failing to complete milestones, or accumulating red cards at a rising rate, the teacher reviews the pattern and considers whether specific navigation cards are too long, specific projects are misaligned with the cohort's readiness, or the physical workshop environment is contributing to the difficulty. Year-one expects iteration on the materials during the first semester; the master document's navigation cards are not frozen, and the teacher is explicitly authorized to rewrite a card that is not working for the cohort (as long as the rewrite stays within the nine principles and the three-tier structure).

**First semester (approximately 12–15 sessions).** By the end of the first semester, the expected cohort state is: most students have completed Project 1 at some tier, many have begun or completed Project 2, some have moved to Project 3 or 4, and at least one student has attempted a Tier 2 or Tier 3 project. The teacher has filled out the §5.8 tracking sheet for every session, has noted refusal patterns, has written brief session-log entries (§5.9), and is ready to write the end-of-semester teacher reflection (§7.2).

**End of first semester — teacher reflection and mid-year decisions.** The teacher writes the six-prompt reflection from §7.2. They look at the tracking sheet longitudinally (*"Which students are showing the engagement trajectory we hoped for? Which are not?"*). They identify any navigation cards that consistently caused confusion and plan revisions for the second semester. If any student has been unable to access the workshop at any tier, the teacher considers whether the program needs adaptation for that student or whether the student's needs are better served by a different setting. The mid-year decisions are revisions to materials and rotations, not a program-level continuation decision — that comes at the end of year one.

**Second semester.** Revisions from the mid-year reflection are applied. Students continue through the projects at their own pace. A subset of students may reach Project 4 (the first soldering project) and begin soldering — this is the first project where the baseline safety discipline is actively exercised and is worth treating as a second high-stakes milestone, comparable to the first three sessions. Some students may reach Project 5 or Project 6 by the end of year one; a small number may reach Project 7. **Project 8 is unlikely in year one** — the quadcopter capstone requires a student who has built substantial skills across Projects 1–7, which is difficult to accomplish within a single school year alongside the student's regular academic load.

**End of year one — full reflection and year-two decision.** The teacher writes a more comprehensive end-of-year reflection covering the full year's tracking-sheet data, the artifact-completion log, memorable moments, surprising student trajectories, material revisions made during the year, and the teacher's own sustainability state. This reflection, combined with the §7.5 success criteria, informs the year-two decision described in §10.2.

### §10.2 Between years one and two — the decision point

The end of year one is a decision point, not a default continuation. The program's commitment to honest reporting (§9.7) means that year one's outcomes — whatever they are — are read carefully before year two is planned.

**Three decision scenarios:**

**Scenario A — Year one meets its success criteria cleanly.** If year one meets the §7.5 criteria (workshop ran the full year without teacher burnout, at least two thirds of students completed at least two projects, tracking-sheet trends are the expected direction, no safety incidents), the program continues into year two with a straightforward continuation plan described in §10.3. The between-year work is light: procurement of year-two consumables per §8 year-two budget, refresh of laminated materials that are worn, revision of any navigation cards that the year-one reflection identified as needing work, and preparation for a new cohort (if the cohort composition changes).

**Scenario B — Year one is mixed.** If year one meets some criteria clearly and misses others (for example: the workshop ran the full year, the teacher is sustainable, and most students engaged, but tracking-sheet behavioural trends are flat rather than improving, and no student reached Project 4), the program continues into year two with **targeted revisions** based on the year-one reflection. The revisions are specific to what year one showed, not speculative. Examples of targeted revisions that year one might identify:

- Project 1 navigation cards are too long for some students; split into smaller cards.
- The transition from Project 3 to Project 4 is too steep; add a Project 3.5 "solder practice" mini-project.
- Channel B (Claude Code tutorial) is being underused; the tutorial-channel scaffold for each project needs simplification.
- Tier 2 choice points on Project 6 are overwhelming; reduce from three choices to two.
- The movement break is too early in the session for some students; move it later or make it optional.

Targeted revisions are documented and applied between year one and year two, and year two runs with the revised materials. The three-year horizon lets the program genuinely improve across years rather than freezing year one as canonical.

**Scenario C — Year one fails.** If year one fails against its success criteria — the teacher moves into the struggling burnout profile, most students fail to complete any project, safety incidents occur, or any other fundamental signal that the program is not working — the program does not continue as-is into year two. The teacher and the funding organization review what happened honestly, and the options are:

1. **Major revision and a second pilot year.** If the failure modes are identifiable and addressable (specific projects didn't work, the tracking sheet identifies specific student needs the program did not meet, the teacher identifies specific principles that were hard to implement), the program is substantially revised and year two runs as a second pilot with the revisions. This is a second pilot, not a scaled continuation — the revised program is re-piloted at the same cohort size before any scaling.
2. **Stop and document.** If the failure modes are fundamental — the population is not a good match, the hardware is not appropriate, the super-target is not achievable with this design, the teacher cannot sustain the workshop alongside their other responsibilities — the program stops, and the teacher and the funding organization produce a brief honest write-up of what was tried, what went wrong, and what future attempts might do differently. A stopped pilot that produces honest lessons is not a waste; it is the correct end for a design that did not work as intended. The super-target commits to *small, positive, empowering experiences* — a program that is making students miserable is not achieving the super-target and should not continue.

**The program does not pretend to be a success it is not.** Pretending would harm the students, who deserve a teacher and a curriculum that are honest about what they can offer; it would harm the teacher, who deserves a program that fits their sustainability rather than one they have to compensate for; and it would harm the funding organization, whose future decisions about programs like this one depend on honest data from pilots like this one.

### §10.3 Year two — Continuation under Scenario A or B

If year one meets or mostly meets its success criteria, year two runs as a continuation with the year-two budget from §8.7 (approximately $457–556 for consumables, replacements, and new-cohort materials).

**Cohort composition in year two.** Year two may include some students returning from year one — those who have not yet completed the projects they want to complete or who want to attempt higher-tier work on projects they have already done — plus some new students joining the workshop for the first time. The mix matters because returning students bring skills and can effectively be at Tier 2 or Tier 3 from day one, while new students are at Tier 1 on Project 1. The workshop accommodates this heterogeneity naturally — the three-tier system is designed for exactly this situation — but the teacher's rotation plan for year two is more demanding than year one's because the cohort is more heterogeneous.

**A returning student's second year.** A student who completed Projects 1–3 at Tier 1 in year one may start year two at Tier 2 on Project 2 (building a more ambitious game variant), or may jump to Project 4 at Tier 1, depending on which experience they want. The teacher's conversation with the student at the start of year two is *"What do you want to build this year?"* — not *"Where did we leave off?"* A student who explicitly chooses to rebuild Project 1 in year two because they loved it and want to do it better is a student making a meaningful autonomy-supported choice, and the program honors that choice (Principle 5).

**New Project 4-and-beyond experience.** Year two is the first realistic time a student might attempt Project 8 (the quadcopter capstone). A student who completed Projects 1–6 in year one and is ready for Projects 7 and 8 in year two is the program's design target for the capstone. If no student reaches Project 8 in year two either, that is not a program failure — it is a signal that the time-to-capstone is longer than two years for this population, and year three plans accordingly.

**Year-two measurement and reflection.** The same §7.3 three-instrument framework runs in year two. The end-of-year reflection compares year-two patterns to year-one patterns (the teacher can see trajectories across years for returning students) and identifies any year-two-specific revisions to apply for year three.

**Expansion to a second cohort within Agourim.** If year two goes well and the school identifies demand for a second workshop section (a different grade band, a different time slot, or a different group of students), a second cohort is possible **only** if a second trained teacher is available and only if the second teacher has read the materials thoroughly and had enough observation time in the existing cohort to understand the workshop's actual texture. Adding a second cohort without a second trained teacher — for example, by doubling the first teacher's load — would violate Principle 9 directly and is not considered.

### §10.4 Year three and beyond — Maturation and potential dissemination

Year three is the first point at which the program is mature enough to consider dissemination beyond Agourim. "Dissemination" here is deliberately modest — it does not mean a scale-out program, a commercial curriculum package, or a claim that the program should be adopted widely. It means that the materials, the reflections, and the honest lessons can be shared with other teachers working with similar populations, who may adapt them to their own contexts.

**Year three continuation at Agourim.** Year three runs the program as a mature continuation with light annual revisions. The per-cohort cost is at the year-two or lower level (approximately $500–700). Returning students reach Projects 7 and 8 more consistently, and the cohort experience of Project 8 becomes a meaningful annual event rather than a rare outlier.

**Materials cleanup and documentation.** By year three, the master document and the per-project files have been through two years of implementation revisions and are in a state that reflects what actually works rather than what was initially designed. The year-three version of the master document is worth producing as a "canonical" release — marked as such, dated, and attributable to the cumulative teacher reflections across the pilot years. This canonical release is the artifact most useful to other teachers.

**Optional dissemination activities (only if year-two outcomes support them).**

- **Sharing the materials with teachers at other schools working with similar populations**, as a resource the other teachers can adapt. The sharing mode is document transfer plus an optional conversation; it is not a training workshop or a consulting engagement.
- **Writing up the pilot experience** as a practitioner-facing narrative (not a research paper — the program does not claim research-grade evidence, per §9). The narrative describes what the program tried, what it learned, what worked, what did not, and what a future teacher attempting similar work might consider.
- **Presenting the program informally** at a teacher conference or special-education-practitioner gathering, if an appropriate venue is available and the teacher is interested. Presentation is a reflection, not a sales pitch.

**What the program will NOT do at year three** (even if year two succeeds):

- Claim research-grade evidence for any outcome. The measurement framework does not support that claim and §9.4 is explicit about why.
- Position itself as a replacement for clinical or therapeutic interventions. See §9.6.
- Recommend widespread adoption. The program is one school's design for one school's population and should be adapted by any adopter, not copied.
- Claim credit for broad effects it did not measure. If a student has a transformative experience across three years, the program is happy for the student and notes the experience in the reflection — but the program does not claim that it caused the transformation, because causal attribution is outside the measurement frame (§9.4).

### §10.5 If year one does not meet its success criteria — the honest non-continuation path

This subsection exists because a roadmap that only describes success is a roadmap that has not taken its own honest-limitations section seriously. Year one may not meet its success criteria. The program commits to honest reporting of that outcome and to acting on it responsibly.

**Step 1 — Honest identification of the failure mode.** At the end of year one, if the success criteria are not met, the teacher and the funding organization meet to identify what specifically did not work. Generic statements like *"the workshop was hard"* or *"students struggled"* are not acceptable identifications; the discussion must identify specific failure modes: *"three students refused all projects for pattern X reason,"* *"the teacher's between-session time exceeded 60 minutes per session because of Y factor,"* *"red-card incidents rose through the year due to Z group dynamic."*

**Step 2 — Categorize the failure.** Failure modes fall into three broad categories:

- **Addressable by revision** — the failure mode is specific to a design choice that can be changed (a navigation card is too long, a project is misaligned with the population, a Claude Code channel is being underused). Revision is the response; the program continues after revision as a second pilot year.
- **Addressable by scope change** — the failure mode is related to the scope of the ask (the cohort is too large for one teacher; the hardware is too ambitious; the year is too short). Scope change is the response; the program continues with a smaller cohort, different hardware, or a longer runway.
- **Not addressable by either** — the failure mode is fundamental (the population is not a good match; the teacher cannot sustain the work; the super-target cannot be achieved with this design in this setting). The program stops and documents why.

**Step 3 — Produce a brief honest write-up.** Whether the program continues or stops, the teacher and the funding organization produce a one-page honest write-up describing what was tried, what happened, and what was learned. This write-up is the pilot's deliverable if it ends at year one, and it is more valuable than a glossy success narrative — it is the kind of honest practitioner reflection that future attempts at similar work can learn from.

**Step 4 — If the program stops, stop well.** Stopping a pilot is not a failure of the people who ran it; it is a correct response to evidence. A teacher who runs the year-one pilot honestly and reports that it does not work is a teacher who has done the work the funding organization should want them to do. The program is not designed to self-perpetuate — it is designed to be evaluated and to continue, revise, or stop based on what the evaluation shows. Stopping well means: finishing the year rather than abandoning students mid-year; honoring any commitments made to students about taking home their completed builds; producing the honest write-up; and, where appropriate, helping the students transition to other workshop or enrichment settings that might serve them better.

**Step 5 — Do not hide the failure.** The program's commitment to honest reporting applies to failure outcomes as much as to success outcomes. A failed pilot that is honestly reported contributes to the field's knowledge. A failed pilot that is spun as a success harms the field, harms future funding decisions, and harms students who are then enrolled in programs that do not work for them. The program's default is transparency, and §10.5 exists to make that default explicit for the hardest case.

## Appendix A — Deferred peer collaboration: what the program does not yet do, and why

An earlier version of this program's §5 Methodology included a Collaboration Roles subsection modelled on a Driver / Navigator / Documentarian / Peacekeeper role vocabulary — a pair-and-group-work structure where students share the hardware at a workstation and rotate roles across milestones. That subsection has been **deliberately removed** from the current version of the program, and the decision and its reasoning are documented here honestly so a reviewer, a fellow teacher, or a future version of this program can understand both the choice and the conditions for revisiting it.

### What the current program does instead

In the current version of the program, **every student works on their own project, at their own workstation, at their own tier, at their own pace**. The workshop is a small-group physical setting — three to eight students in the same room, sharing a parts library and tools — but the work is individual. Peer collaboration is not a first-class mechanism. The relationship infrastructure the program relies on is the **teacher–student relationship** (§4 Principle 8), not peer-to-peer collaboration.

### Why peer collaboration was deferred

The decision rests on a specific set of practical and design concerns that apply to Agourim's student population in particular:

1. **Extra complications.** Pair and small-group work introduce coordination costs that a single teacher managing 3–8 students with highly heterogeneous profiles may not be able to absorb. Deciding who pairs with whom, rotating roles at the right moment, catching a dyad that has drifted off-task, and mediating the inevitable friction when one student's pace differs from another's — all of these are additional demands on teacher attention that trade directly against the rotation time Principle 2, Principle 8, and Principle 9 all depend on.

2. **Social dynamics are already a load for this population.** Many of the students this program is built for carry histories of social difficulty — bullying, harassment, group rejection, or quieter patterns of being overlooked. §2 Population Profile is explicit that social comfort varies widely across the student body, and that some students *"find any group dynamic exhausting and need solo workspace by default."* Requiring peer collaboration as a default would demand social performance from students who may not be ready for it, and who may specifically have come to Agourim because mainstream group dynamics did not work for them.

3. **Individual projects preserve the differentiation model.** The three-tier system (§5.4) and the three-level Claude Code access model (§5.5) are designed around a single student's current state. When two students at different tiers work on the same artefact, the lower-tier student risks deferring to the higher-tier student, the higher-tier student risks over-driving, and the match between the scaffolding and the actual student doing the work breaks down. Individual projects preserve the differentiation model cleanly: each student gets exactly the tier and level that matches their current state, without dilution from a partner's different state.

4. **Peer collaboration research is real but the mechanisms are not automatic.** §3.1 reviewed the research on peer-mediated interventions and cooperative learning for students with EBD, and that research is genuinely supportive — Wattanawongwan, Smith & Vannest (2021) in particular document that cooperative learning produces benefits when five specific elements (positive interdependence, individual accountability, face-to-face promotive interaction, social skills, and group processing) are explicitly taught and monitored. The research is not *"put kids in groups and they will learn socially"* — it is *"teach the five elements, monitor the five elements, and positive effects follow."* Teaching and monitoring the five elements is itself a first-class instructional commitment, and the current version of the program has decided to commit its limited teacher-attention budget to the individual-work discipline instead.

5. **Gomez (2019), the closest analogue in the verified literature, used a peer mechanism this program does not implement.** The most directly relevant study in the corpus — a UTSA dissertation examining makerspace learning for middle-school EBD students — found that makerspace participation supported social-emotional skill development specifically through peer interaction opportunities. The program's current design does not use Gomez's mechanism. This is an honest gap, and §9 Honest Limitations restates it as one of the things the program is aware it is not doing.

### What this decision does NOT mean

It does not mean that students in the workshop cannot talk to each other. Students share the same physical room, and when one student notices another student's robot doing something interesting and walks over to look, that is normal workshop life and welcome. Spontaneous verbalisation (see §5.9) is also welcome. What the program does not structurally *require* is a pair-or-group-work format where two or more students depend on each other to complete a shared artefact.

It does not mean that the program has ruled out peer collaboration forever. The explicit framing is **deferral**, not **rejection**. A future version of this program — after the current individual-work version has been evaluated in practice and refined — could reintroduce peer collaboration under specific conditions (see below).

It does not mean that the research on peer collaboration is wrong. §3.1's review of Gersib & Mason (2023), Riden, Kumm & Maggin (2022), and Wattanawongwan et al. (2021) stands as written. The research shows peer-mediated and cooperative-learning interventions can produce positive outcomes for students with EBD when the elements are explicitly taught and monitored. The program's current choice is to invest its limited teacher-attention budget elsewhere, not to dispute the research.

### Conditions for revisiting this decision

The program's current individual-work commitment should be revisited when **any** of the following become true:

- **The program has been running for at least one full implementation year** and the teacher has firsthand evidence about how individual work is actually playing out in the workshop. Premature changes based on speculation are worse than committing to one version long enough to learn from it.
- **A specific student or pair of students is actively asking for collaborative work** and shows signs of being ready for it (shared interest in a project, successful history of individual work, visible social compatibility). Case-by-case flexibility is always available even under the current individual-work default — a teacher who sees two students who genuinely want to build a shared project together can say yes without waiting for a program-level decision.
- **The cohort's group-size, staffing, or adult-support ratio changes** in a direction that would make the teacher-attention budget less constrained (e.g., an assistant teacher, a consistent paraprofessional, or a smaller steady group size than the current 3–8).
- **The program adds a Project 9 or a special unit specifically designed around shared build work** — a category of project where collaboration is the explicit learning goal rather than an overlay on an individual-project structure.

### If peer collaboration is reintroduced, what the roles would look like

For the record, and for whoever eventually revisits this decision: the role vocabulary that was removed from §5 Methodology used four roles inherited from the source quadcopter project's prior documentation. Any future version of this program that reintroduces peer collaboration can start from this vocabulary:

- **Driver.** The one whose hands are on the hardware right now. The Driver holds the soldering iron, turns the screwdriver, plugs in the wire, clicks the Upload button. Everyone else waits.
- **Navigator.** The one who reads the navigation card and guides the Driver. The Navigator does not touch the hardware — they call out the next step, point at the reference diagram, and catch the Driver before a mistake. The Driver–Navigator pairing is the core of the role system.
- **Documentarian.** The one who records what was done — takes a photo after each major step, writes one sentence about what went well, notes any problems that are going on the "next session" list. This role is especially useful for students who are uncomfortable with hands-on work at a given moment but still want to be part of the activity.
- **Peacekeeper.** The one who watches the group's state and calls a pause when needed. *"Someone's frustrated, can we take a minute?"* or *"We've been at this wiring for 10 minutes, let's check the navigation card again."* This is explicitly a social-emotional role, suited to students who are perceptive to group dynamics.

A reintroduced collaboration system would rotate roles every 2–3 milestones so that every student experiences every role across a project, preventing role lock-in and building shared understanding. It would also need the explicit teaching and monitoring of Wattanawongwan et al.'s five cooperative-learning elements (positive interdependence, individual accountability, face-to-face promotive interaction, social skills, group processing) to be treated as first-class instructional work, not as an overlay on top of the individual-work methodology. These are real design commitments, not afterthoughts — which is part of why the current version of the program has chosen to defer them rather than implement them inadequately.

### Why this appendix exists at all

Transparency. A reader who reaches the end of this document should be able to see exactly what the program chose not to do, and why. Hiding the deferred-collaboration decision inside a quiet rewrite of §5 would leave a future reader — or a future version of this program's author — without the reasoning. Surfacing it in a dedicated appendix lets the decision be understood, evaluated, and revisited on its own terms.
