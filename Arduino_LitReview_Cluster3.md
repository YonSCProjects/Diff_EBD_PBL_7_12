#### Taylor, J. C., Allen, L. M., Van, J., & Moohr, M. (2024). The Effects of Project-Based Learning on Student Behavior and Teacher Burnout in an Emotional/Behavioral Support Classroom. *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders*, 32(2). DOI: 10.1177/10634266241235933

**Headline:** PBL using the Buck Institute HQPBL framework produced large effect sizes (BC-SMD >0.80) in on-task behavior for all four middle-school students with EBD and improved teacher/paraprofessional job satisfaction—the only empirical RCT-like study of PBL specifically for EBD students found in the corpus.

**Key findings with quotes:**
- **On-task behavior gains:** "Results indicated that on-task behavior increased substantially for all four participants during the PBL intervention phase" with effect sizes of BC-SMD = 3.81–7.42 (far exceeding Cohen's d = 0.8 threshold for large effects). Visual analysis showed between 32–39 percentage-point level increases. (Taylor et al. 2024)
- **Teacher burnout mitigation:** "Both the teacher and paraprofessional indicated low job satisfaction at baseline" (Positive questions M = 3–4.33 / 5; Negative M = 1–2 / 5); by post-intervention, job satisfaction improved markedly (Positive M = 1.66–2; Negative M = 3.50–4). (Taylor et al. 2024)
- **Sustained inquiry:** "The sustained inquiry component of PBL appeared particularly beneficial for maintaining student motivation and on-task behavior." (Taylor et al. 2024)
- **UDL alignment:** "PBL offers multiple means of engagement, representation, and expression—consistent with Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles—and may be particularly beneficial for students who struggle with traditional didactic instruction." (Taylor et al. 2024)

**Design moves with evidence:**
- HQPBL framework elements: Knowledge/Skills, Challenge/Problem, Sustained Inquiry, Authenticity, Student Voice, Reflection, Critique & Revision, Public Product
- Student Voice: "Students make choices about the products they create, how they work, and how they use their time, guided by the teacher"
- Authentic, student-centered nature reduces stress by providing more meaningful instructional experiences
- Multiple baseline single-case design with high fidelity monitoring

**Arduino / physical computing relevance:** None explicit in this paper; however, the principle of authentic, project-centered problem-solving and student voice applies directly to hardware-driven PBL.

**Effect sizes (if reported):** 
- BC-SMD (analogous to Cohen's d): ES = 1.7030–3.8076 [95% CI], SE = 0.5085 (large effect, p < .01)
- Individual ESs: Eric = 4.70, Stanley = 7.42, Kenneth = 3.89, Kyle = 3.81
- Teacher job satisfaction: Improvement from M = 2–3 to M = 1.66–4 on 5-point scales

**Candidate principle(s):**
1. *Sustained inquiry + authentic context yield on-task gains in EBD.* PBL's depth-over-breadth design counters the restlessness and distraction of ADHD/behavioral students (Taylor et al. 2024).
2. *Student voice + choice reduce external control resistance.* Behavioral/emotional students retain authority over product form, pace, and collaboration (Taylor et al. 2024).
3. *Public product scaffolds effort.* Knowing work will be shared beyond the classroom motivates even disengaged students (Taylor et al. 2024).
4. *Teacher burnout reduction may be a hidden benefit.* When students are engaged in authentic, student-centered work, teachers shift from crisis management to facilitation, improving wellbeing and sustainability (Taylor et al. 2024).

**Headline:** Comprehensive meta-analysis (k=144 studies, 333 effect size outcomes) found that computer-based scaffolding has a consistently strong positive effect on STEM cognitive outcomes (g = 0.46, comparable to or exceeding other ed-tech interventions); scaffolding is effective across learner populations (including underperforming students and those with learning disabilities), contexts (project-based learning showed highest effect, g = 0.71), and assessment levels—fading vs. non-fading makes no difference.

**Key findings with quotes:**
- **Overall effect:** "The overall effect size was g = 0.46 (z = 18.19, p < .01), suggesting that students who receive computer-based scaffolding do better on cognitive tests than students who do not receive scaffolds." (Belland et al. 2017a)
- **Underperforming students benefit most:** "Scaffolding had a significantly higher effect size among underperforming students than among traditional learners, z = 2.29, p < .05." (Belland et al. 2017a)
- **Project-based learning context strongest:** "Scaffolding's effect size was higher when used in the context of project-based learning than when used in the context of [modeling/visualization, problem solving, case-based learning, inquiry-based learning, design-based learning, problem-based learning]" (all p < .01) (Belland et al. 2017a). - **Fading debate settled:** "No significant differences were found between scaffolding with fading, adding, fading/adding, or no change, suggesting the utility of fading is not a decisive factor in scaffolding effectiveness." (Belland et al. 2017a)
- **Generic vs. context-specific:** "No significant difference between generic and context-specific scaffolding was found, suggesting that both can be effective approaches." (Belland et al. 2017a)

**Design moves with evidence:**
- **Scaffolding types included:** Conceptual, metacognitive, strategic, motivation scaffolds (Belland et al. 2017a).
- **Customization logic:** Performance-adapted, self-selected, fixed—no significant differences in effectiveness (Belland et al. 2017a).
- **Scope:** Generic (applies to any problem) vs. context-specific (applies to one domain)—both effective (Belland et al. 2017a).
- **Practical finding:** 82% of successful scaffolding in the literature was context-specific, suggesting real-world practice favors specificity even if generic scaffolds work (Belland et al. 2017a).

**Arduino / physical computing relevance:**
- Study focuses on computer-based scaffolding (intelligent tutoring systems, tutoring software, simulation software).
- Does NOT cover Arduino, robotics, breadboards, or hands-on physical computing.
- Relevant to software scaffolding in coding education but not hardware/circuit design.

**Effect sizes (if reported):**
- Overall: g = 0.46 (p < .01), SE = 0.242
- By context: PBL (g = 0.71, highest), problem-based (g = 0.37), inquiry-based (g = 0.18)
- By population: Underperforming (higher than traditional); adult learners (highest among education levels)
- By assessment: Principles level (higher than concept level)
- Heterogeneity: Q = 1096.96, I² = 69.7%, p < .01 (substantial variability across studies, justifying subgroup analysis)

**Candidate principle(s):**
1. *Project-based learning is the optimal context for scaffolding's effectiveness.* PBL + scaffolds (g = 0.71) outperform problem-based learning (g = 0.37) and problem solving (g = 0.29)—suggests that sustained inquiry, authentic products, and student agency amplify scaffolding benefits (Belland et al. 2017a).
2. *Underperforming and diverse learners benefit most from scaffolding.* Scaffolding is not a generic intervention; it is especially powerful for students furthest from the target learner population (Belland et al. 2017a).
3. *Fading is not necessary for effectiveness.* Persistent scaffolds (non-faded) work as well as faded scaffolds—contradicts traditional assumptions and suggests students regulate their own scaffold use (Belland et al. 2017a).
4. *Metacognitive and strategic scaffolds rival conceptual scaffolds.* Helping students manage their learning process (planning, monitoring, revising) is as effective as providing content hints (Belland et al. 2017a).

**Caveats:**
- Meta-analysis includes studies with varying quality and sample sizes; heterogeneity high (I² = 69.7%).
- Limited subgroup analyses for students with learning disabilities (identified as under-researched area).
- Does not address fidelity of scaffold implementation or teacher training requirements.
- Context of "project-based learning" varies widely across studies; not clear what specific design features drive the high effect.
- No direct comparison of human tutoring + scaffolding vs. computer-based scaffolding alone.
- Scaffolding + PBL interaction effect not fully unpacked—is the effect due to scaffolds, to PBL, or to their combination?

### Top candidate design principles from this cluster

1. **PBL + Sustained Inquiry + Student Voice yield engagement and on-task behavior for EBD students** (cites: Taylor 2024; Hwang & Taylor 2016; Belland 2017a, 2017b).
   - The Buck Institute's eight HQPBL elements (Challenge, Sustained Inquiry, Authenticity, Student Voice, Reflection, Critique & Revision, Public Product) together create conditions where EBD/ADHD students experience agency and see meaningful results, increasing intrinsic motivation and reducing behavioral dysregulation. Particularly important: student choice over products, processes, and pace.

2. **Scaffolding is most effective in PBL contexts (g = 0.71 vs. g = 0.37 in problem-based learning)** (cites: Belland 2017a, 2017b; Boardman & Coleman 2025; Hwang & Taylor 2016).
   - Computer-based and interactional scaffolds (peer collaboration, material supports, teacher modeling) amplify PBL's effect. Scaffolding does not need to be faded; persistent scaffolds work equally well. Underperforming students and those with learning disabilities benefit most.

3. **Tangible, embodied, multi-sensory learning (Concrete → Representational → Abstract) is prerequisite for students with disabilities and ADHD** (cites: Hwang & Taylor 2016; Kuzmina & Romero 2025; Belland 2017a; Israel et al. 2015).
   - Students with disabilities cannot skip the concrete phase. Manipulatives (physical objects, robots, building materials) must precede drawings/visual representations, which precede symbols/equations. Multi-modal output (drawing, building, performing, speaking) allows diverse learners to demonstrate understanding.

4. **Robots as non-threatening mediators of feedback enable ADHD behavior change** (cites: Berrezueta-Guzman 2021a, 2021b; Cibrian et al. 2022; O'Connell et al. 2024).
   - The "emotional distance" of robots vs. human teachers reduces anxiety while maintaining accountability. Real-time sensors + robotic response create closed-loop self-regulation support. "Body doubling" (presence of another entity) helps ADHD students reduce procrastination and increase task initiation.

5. **Computational thinking (CT) is universally accessible with scaffolding, modeling, and peer collaboration** (cites: Israel et al. 2015; Traina 2025; Álvarez Ariza & Hernández 2025).
   - Tile-based programming (Scratch, Etoys, block-based coding) lowers the computational floor without reducing ceiling. Struggling learners (LD, poverty, EBD) thrive in CT when embedded coaching and peer support are present. Teachers initially skeptical become confident advocates when they see diverse learners engage authentically.

6. **Coding education + project-based context supports dual learning: academic + social-emotional** (cites: Traina 2025; Kuzmina & Romero 2025; O'Connell et al. 2024; Díaz-Boladeras et al. 2025).
   - Learning to code provides structure (syntax rules, debugging cycles) and immediate feedback. Collaborative coding projects build communication, problem-solving, and self-regulation. For ADHD students, the concrete feedback loop (code runs or doesn't) provides external accountability structure.

7. **Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles improve access and engagement across diverse learners** (cites: Taylor 2024; Boardman & Coleman 2025; Basham & Marino 2013; Almeqdad et al. 2023).
   - Three principles—Representation (multiple means of taking in information), Expression (multiple means of demonstrating learning), Engagement (multiple means of motivation)—are grounded in neuroscience. Meta-analysis effect size: 3.56 (large). UDL is most effective when implemented as part of professional development and school-wide intervention (not piecemeal).

8. **STEAM (STEM + Arts) lowers affective barriers to abstract content** (cites: Hwang & Taylor 2016; Díaz-Boladeras et al. 2025).
   - Integrating music, visual arts, movement, and creative expression into STEM boosts motivation and self-efficacy for students with prior negative STEM experiences. Arts serve as tangible gateways to abstract concepts (e.g., drumming beats before fractions, drawing before physics diagrams).

9. **Teacher beliefs, problem-solving mindset, and embedded coaching predict implementation fidelity** (cites: Israel et al. 2015; Taylor 2024; Sciacca 2025).
   - When teachers frame barriers as solvable problems (not immutable obstacles), they persist and adapt. In-the-moment coaching during lessons outperforms one-shot professional development workshops. Teacher collaboration (peer mentoring, shared planning) sustains implementation.

10. **Modular, tangible robotics (vs. pre-made robots) enable student agency and creativity** (cites: Kuzmina & Romero 2025; Díaz-Boladeras et al. 2025; Israel et al. 2015).
    - LEGO Mindstorms, Cubelets, and other modular platforms allow students to build, test, iterate, and create novel solutions. Pre-made robots (NAO, Pepper) are engaging but limit student creativity. Modular robotics align with constructionist learning theory and appeal to diverse learners including those with ADHD (spontaneous verbalization, self-regulation strategies observed).
