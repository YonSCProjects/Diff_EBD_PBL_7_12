#### Koslouski, Stark, & Chafouleas (2023) — *Understanding and responding to the effects of trauma in the classroom: A primer for educators*

**Headline:** Nearly half of U.S. students have experienced potentially traumatic events, yet educators are rarely trained in trauma-informed practices—making a trauma-aware toolkit essential for recognizing and responding to varied trauma presentations in classrooms.

**Key findings with quotes:**
- **Prevalence and educator readiness**: "Although nearly half of U.S. students have experienced a potentially traumatic event, educators are not routinely trained in trauma or trauma-informed practices." (koslouski_et_al_2023.txt) The paper underscores that trauma has profound negative impacts on students' social, emotional, and behavioral needs, yet educators lack foundational training.
- **Survivemodeand brain functioning**: Chronic trauma leads to "survival mode" activation where the brain goes into 'fight, flight, or freeze' response. This disrupts the brain's development in areas of executive functioning (koslouski_et_al_2023.txt). 
- **Universal benefit**: "Trauma-informed care is beneficial for all students regardless of trauma history" (koslouski_et_al_2023.txt), supporting both classroom-wide prevention and targeted support.
- **Secondary traumatic stress in educators**: The paper addresses "strategies for educators to reduce potential for secondary traumatic stress," emphasizing that educator wellbeing is "crucial to remaining healthy and effective" when working with traumatized students (koslouski_et_al_2023.txt).

**Relevant practices / scaffolds:**
- Recognizing diverse trauma presentations (from stunned silence to aggression/violence) and understanding that observable behavior often masks underlying trauma.
- Providing ongoing, incremental shifts in practice rather than wholesale program overhauls.
- Building educators' empathy and agency through understanding the trauma-behavior link.
- Managing relationships with caregivers around trauma contexts.
- Reducing educator burnout and secondary trauma through self-care strategies.

**What to avoid:**
- Assuming all trauma responses look the same or treating trauma symptomatically without understanding the underlying cause.
- Overwhelming educators with complex training rather than building incremental awareness.

**Population specifics (anxiety / depression / trauma / OCD):**
- The primer uses vignettes to illustrate varied student profiles but does not break out specifics by diagnosis.

**Candidate principle(s):**
- "Educators need a 'toolkit' of trauma-aware strategies, not a single program, recognizing that trauma manifests differently across students" (koslouski_et_al_2023.txt; derived from Boston University summary).
- Educator self-care and secondary traumatic stress prevention are as critical as student interventions when designing trauma-informed systems.

**Caveats / contradictions:**
- The primer is descriptive and practitioner-focused; it is not itself an efficacy trial and relies on secondary sources for evidence summaries.

#### Asselman et al. (2025) — *Adverse childhood experiences and internalizing/externalizing problems in students with EBD: The role of PTSD symptoms and executive functioning*

**Headline:** In students with EBD in segregated special education, ACEs lead to both internalizing and externalizing problems—with PTSD symptoms and self-reported executive functioning as key mediators—suggesting trauma-informed understanding can help teachers reconceptualize "behavior problems" as trauma responses.

**Key findings with quotes:**
- **ACE prevalence in EBD**: Among students with EBD in Dutch separate schools, "all students (96.4% self-report, 89.5% parent report, 95.3% school files) appeared to be exposed to at least one ACE and more than half (74.4% self-reports, 52.7% parent reports, 59.9% school files) to four or more ACEs" (asselman_2025.txt). This vastly exceeds the general student population.
- **PTSD as a mediator**: "PTSD symptoms and self-reported EF fully mediated the association between ACEs and internalizing problems and externalizing problems" (asselman_2025.txt). This is critical: trauma-related symptoms are a pathway, not just a comorbid diagnosis.
- **Teacher perception and behavior reframing**: "More knowledge on the role of ACEs and PTSD symptoms in internalizing problems and externalizing problems of students with EBD could help teachers reconceptualize behavioral problems from a trauma-informed perspective, enhance their compassion for the students' problem behavior, and increase their capacity to respond in a sensitive manner" (asselman_2025.txt, citing Anderson et al., 2022; Loomis et al., 2023).
- **Executive functioning and chronic dysregulation**: "Chronic dysregulation of the stress system is related to dysfunctional development of the prefrontal cortex, often seen in children with problems in EF...EF encompasses a set of higher cognitive processes that facilitate planning and goal-directed regulation of thoughts, emotions and behavioral actions" (asselman_2025.txt). Students with EBD thus have dual challenges: poor EF *and* PTSD symptoms.
- **Teachers' relational burden**: "From a teacher's perspective, although it is well-established that high quality relationships are crucial for positive behavioral outcomes, teachers often struggle to fulfill the complex relational needs of students with EBD, especially in segregated SE settings...An important challenge for teachers in segregated SE is preventing a negative cycle of interaction with students, where conflicts escalate externalizing behaviors, which then leads to a further increase of conflicts" (asselman_2025.txt).

**Relevant practices / scaffolds:**
- Trauma-informed understanding that reframes "behavior problems" as symptoms of dysregulation and trauma.
- Support for executive functioning development (working memory, inhibitory control, cognitive flexibility).
- Relationship-building as foundational, with attention to breaking negative interaction cycles.
- Recognition that both internalizing (anxiety, depression, withdrawal) and externalizing (aggression, impulsivity) problems arise from common trauma roots.

**What to avoid:**
- Treating behavior and trauma separately; PTSD symptoms manifest *as* behavior problems.
- Punitive approaches that further dysregulate students already struggling with stress-system dysfunction.
- Assuming that teacher stress and burnout won't affect student outcomes; the teacher-student relationship is critical to interrupting negative cycles.

**Population specifics (anxiety / depression / trauma / OCD):**
- **Internalizing problems**: Anxiety, depression, negative self-image (asselman_2025.txt).
- **Externalizing problems**: Aggression, impulsivity (asselman_2025.txt).
- **Comorbidity findings**: PTSD showed high comorbidity with "anxiety disorders and mood disorders" as well as "ADHD and conduct disorder" (asselman_2025.txt).

**Candidate principle(s):**
- "Students with EBD have near-universal exposure to ACEs; PTSD and executive dysfunction are the mechanisms linking trauma to behavioral dysregulation" (asselman_2025.txt; original finding).
- "Relationship quality and break-cycle intervention are non-negotiable for EBD populations; punitive discipline exacerbates trauma" (derived).
- "Teacher training in trauma-informed reframing and stress-system literacy is prerequisite to sustainable behavior support" (asselman_2025.txt; alluded to as "more adequate training is needed").

**Caveats / contradictions:**
- Study was conducted in Dutch separate SE schools; generalizability to inclusive or mainstream settings unclear.
- Teacher-report EF did not mediate associations, suggesting self-report EF captures something different (possibly metacognitive awareness of regulation failures vs. teacher observation).

#### Håland & Bertelsen (2025) — *School Anxiety Accommodation in Youth: Prevalence and Patterns Among Teachers*

**Headline:** Every teacher reported at least one anxiety-accommodating behavior (exempting from speaking, allowing classroom exits), and 71% use such accommodations "often" or "very often"—but accommodations conflict with CBT exposure principles and may inadvertently reinforce avoidance and prolong anxiety over time.

**Key findings with quotes:**
- **Universal prevalence, misalignment with evidence**: "All respondents had at least one accommodating behavior, and 71% of teachers reported using at least one accommodating behavior 'Often' or 'Very Often'...Despite the effectiveness of CBT, most schools neither employ school-based CBT interventions for anxious students nor do they establish general guidelines on how school staff should support anxious and avoidant students...In fact, contrary to evidence-based approaches focused on confronting anxiety evoking situations, many teachers respond to anxious students with both subtle and overt suggestions to avoid challenging situations" (haland_2025.txt).
- **Common accommodations**: "Students can leave the classroom when they feel anxious" (68%), "Students have special arrangements regarding their seating in the class" (66%), "Students can go to a room to be alone" (62%), "Students are exempted from oral presentations in front of the class" (60%)" (haland_2025.txt).
- **Two accommodation factors**: "The two factors in the final model were: Factor 1 (Modification) and Factor 2 (Participation)...Factor 1 was positively correlated with higher grades, whereas Factor 2 was negatively correlated with higher grades" (haland_2025.txt). Teachers adapt by either modifying tasks or withdrawing adult participation—both promote avoidance.
- **Systemic barriers vs. lack of knowledge**: "A significant portion of teachers (65.00%) indicated a lack of time and resources as a reason for accommodation, resonating with...the absence of school-based CBT interventions and guidelines for supporting anxious students...the majority of teachers believe their school management understands how to help anxious pupils, yet this belief contrasts with the prevalent use of avoidance-based accommodations, indicating a gap between effective anxiety management strategies and current school practices" (haland_2025.txt).
- **Risk of prolonging anxiety**: "While accommodations relieve anxiety they may be an important temporary tool to ensure students can participate in school...accommodations should be considered temporary tools because while they may provide immediate relief, accommodations can inadvertently intensify or prolong the anxiety over time" (haland_2025.txt).

**Relevant practices / scaffolds:**
- **Exposure-informed, approach-oriented support** (not avoidance-based). Students should be *supported to face* anxiety-provoking situations, not exempted from them.
- Clear, time-limited accommodations with explicit fading plans (not permanent modifications).
- Teacher guidance on the difference between supportive scaffolding and enabling avoidance.
- Time and resource allocation for evidence-based anxiety support in schools.

**What to avoid:**
- Permitting students to leave class, avoid presentations, or opt out of participation when anxious—this reinforces the anxiety-avoidance loop.
- Assuming that accommodations are compassionate long-term solutions; they are at best temporary.
- Creating a school culture where anxiety tolerance is not modeled or supported.

**Population specifics (anxiety / depression / trauma / OCD):**
- The study focuses specifically on **anxiety accommodation patterns**. Two factors emerged: "Modification" (e.g., exempt from certain activities) and "Participation" (e.g., allowing parents in classroom, teacher accompanying to bathroom). These are anxiety-specific.

**Candidate principle(s):**
- "Anxiety accommodations, while well-intentioned, inadvertently prolong anxiety by reinforcing avoidance; schools need exposure-informed (approach-oriented) supports, not exemptions" (haland_2025.txt).
- "Time and resource constraints drive accommodation use more than lack of knowledge; systemic support is needed for CBT-aligned alternatives" (haland_2025.txt).
- "Accommodation fading plans and explicit exposure scaffolding should be standard, not avoidance defaults" (derived).

**Caveats / contradictions:**
- Limited to Norwegian schools; cultural context may differ from Israeli or other settings.
- Self-report bias: teachers may over- or under-report accommodations.
- The study does not test alternative approaches, only documents current practice.

#### Werner-Seidler et al. (2021) — *School-based depression and anxiety prevention programs: An updated systematic review and meta-analysis*

**Headline:** School-based psychological prevention programs for depression and anxiety show small but significant post-intervention effects (g = 0.21 for depression, g = 0.18 for anxiety); targeted/indicated prevention for at-risk youth produces larger effects than universal programs, and effects attenuate over follow-up.

**Key findings with quotes:**
- **Small but significant effects**: "School-delivered psychological prevention programs have small effects in reducing symptoms of depression (g = 0.21) and anxiety (g = 0.18) immediately post-intervention, with effects maintained but attenuating over follow-up" (werner_seidler_et_al_2021.txt).
- **Targeted > universal for depression**: "Targeted prevention programs (selective g = 0.21; indicated g = 0.33) showed significantly larger effects than universal programs (g = 0.16) at post-intervention" (werner_seidler_et_al_2021.txt). This is critical: programs aimed at already-struggling students work better than whole-class approaches.
- **External delivery advantage**: "External providers (g = 0.26) showed marginally larger effects than school staff (g = 0.19)" (werner_seidler_et_al_2021.txt), though not statistically significant. This suggests school staff can deliver effectively with proper training.
- **CBT not uniquely superior**: "CBT-based programs were not significantly more effective than non-CBT programs" (werner_seidler_et_al_2021.txt).
- **Quality and bias**: "Study quality was moderate overall, with no association between quality and effect size...Funnel plots and Egger's tests suggested some evidence of publication bias" (werner_seidler_et_al_2021.txt).
- **Large sample, 118 trials**: "130 articles...representing 118 unique trials and 45,924 participants" (werner_seidler_et_al_2021.txt).
- **Attenuation over time**: Effects decline from post-intervention → short-term follow-up (0–6 months) to long-term (>12 months), suggesting booster sessions or sustained implementation may be needed.

**Relevant practices / scaffolds:**
- **Targeted prevention is more cost-effective** than universal approaches for depression prevention; identifying at-risk students and providing intensive support yields larger effect sizes.
- Integration of prevention with school curricula (literacy, social-emotional learning).
- External expert support can enhance fidelity, though school-staff-delivered programs are viable.
- Focus on skill-building and coping, not just symptom reduction.

**What to avoid:**
- Assuming universal, low-intensity programs will adequately serve already-anxious or depressed students; these students need more intensive support.
- Relying solely on external providers; sustainable change requires school staff capacity-building.
- Expecting one-shot interventions to maintain effects without boosters or ongoing support.

**Population specifics (anxiety / depression / trauma / OCD):**
- 59 trials targeted depression only; 40 targeted anxiety only; 31 targeted both. Most common approach was CBT, but effect sizes were comparable across approaches (werner_seidler_et_al_2021.txt).

**Candidate principle(s):**
- "Targeted/indicated prevention for at-risk students produces larger effects than universal approaches; Arduino PBL can embed screening and rapid identification" (werner_seidler_et_al_2021.txt).

#### Wattanawongwan et al. (2021) — *Cooperative Learning Strategies for Building Relationship Skills in Students With EBD*

**Headline:** Cooperative learning with explicit instruction in five core elements—positive interdependence, individual accountability, face-to-face interaction, social skills, and group processing—builds relationship skills and reduces isolation for students with EBD, providing natural classroom contexts for skill practice.

**Key findings with quotes:**
- **Relationship skills as protective**: "Relationship skills are one of the five social emotional learning (SEL) competencies...Relationship skills encourage collaboration, problem solving, and socially responsible behavior and are tied to positive school success and social outcomes" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt). Crucially, "students with poor relationship skills are more likely to be at risk for destructive behavior than students who have social support and strong peer relationships" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt).
- **Five core elements of cooperative learning**: 
  1. "Positive interdependence: 'Engaging to personal success as well as the group member's success'" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt)
  2. "Individual accountability: 'Contributing an equitable share of the work'" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt)
  3. "Face-to-face promotive interaction: 'Supporting other members' efforts to learn'" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt)
  4. "Social skills: 'Developing interpersonal and social skills to work with others'" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt)
  5. "Group processing: 'Analyzing how well they were working and contributing'" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt)
- **EBD-specific challenges**: "The cooperation component of relationship skills is noted as absent for many students with emotional and behavioral disorders...Parents and educators describe uncooperative behaviors for students with EBD as refusals, lack of compromise, and/or oppositional behavior" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt).
- **Implementation integrity checklist**: Five steps: Planning (assess relationship skills first, group with familiar peers), Introducing (explain tasks, define interdependence), Monitoring (collect data, provide feedback, ensure EBD student participation), Assessing (check understanding, provide accommodations), Processing (discuss what worked, set goals) (wattanawongwan_2021.txt).
- **Role of peers and teachers**: "Teachers and peers of students with EBD should be involved with each step of cooperative learning to enhance their social skills...Simple grouping of students without explicit instruction is unlikely to promote skill-building, particularly for individuals with disabilities who are differentially affected by a lack of direct instruction" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt).
- **Benefits for all students**: "While all students can benefit academically and socially when peer relationship skills are strong, teaching relationship skills like cooperation is often a low instructional priority and can be difficult to accomplish in instructional environments" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt).

**Relevant practices / scaffolds:**
- **Pre-assessment** of relationship skills to understand baseline and inform grouping.
- **Explicit teaching** of cooperation behaviors (turn-taking, positive words, how to disagree respectfully).
- **Peer pairing with socially competent students** familiar to the EBD student (not random grouping).
- **Teacher monitoring and feedback** during group work: "Nice job listening to your group members," "Thank you for using positive body language."
- **Structured group processing** with reflection questions and goal-setting.
- **Natural contingencies** (success, peer approval) rather than external reinforcement alone.

**What to avoid:**
- Assuming students will cooperate without explicit instruction.
- Placing isolated students with unfamiliar peers without scaffolding.
- Treating cooperation as a low priority or "extra" skill; it is foundational.
- Relying on one-shot group work without monitoring, feedback, and processing.

**Population specifics (anxiety / depression / trauma / OCD):**
- The paper addresses students with EBD broadly, including "those demonstrating internalizing problems such as anxiety, depression, and withdrawal behaviors...likely to experience increased peer rejection and opportunities for victimization and bullying" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt). Cooperative learning directly counters isolation.

**Candidate principle(s):**
- "Cooperative learning with explicit instruction in five core elements builds relationship skills and provides natural, non-stigmatizing contexts for peer practice" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt).
- "Monitoring and feedback during group work, not just end-of-task assessment, are critical to sustaining cooperation" (wattanawongwan_2021.txt).
- "Familiarity and pre-teaching prevent failed group experiences that further isolate EBD students" (derived).

**Caveats / contradictions:**
- This is primarily a practitioner-focused article; limited controlled empirical comparison to alternative interventions.
- Assumes teacher time for monitoring and feedback, which resource-constrained settings may lack.

#### Daunic et al. (2023) — *Social-Emotional Learning Intervention for K–1 Students at Risk for EBD: Mediation Effects of SEL on School Adjustment*

**Headline:** The SELF (Social-Emotional Learning Foundations) curriculum for kindergarten–grade 1 students at risk for EBD produced significant direct effects on self-regulation, SEL competence, and school adjustment; SEL competence (not language or EF alone) mediated intervention effects on school success.

**Key findings with quotes:**
- **Efficacy of SELF curriculum**: "SELF evidenced a significant positive impact on multiple outcomes for 1,154 K–Grade 1 students at risk for EBD, including standardized measures of SEL-related competencies, self-regulation, and school adjustment, and a researcher designed measure of SEL-related vocabulary" (daunic_2023.txt).
- **Direct effects on school adjustment**: Study "found significant direct effects of the SELF intervention...on measures of competent school functioning and internalizing behavior" (daunic_2023.txt). These were direct, not mediated by single mechanisms.
- **SEL competence as key mediator**: "The significant mediation of the DESSA total score on both the competence and internalizing subscales of the CAB-T (exploratory analyses) suggests that when SEL competence is adequately measured by standardized instruments, it can demonstrate a mediating role in connecting intervention to school adjustment" (daunic_2023.txt). In other words, broad SEL competence (not narrow language or EF measures) is the pathway.
- **Language and EF as insufficient mediators alone**: "Neither language development nor executive function/behavioral self-regulation was identified as a significant mediator of intervention effects on school adjustment" (daunic_2023.txt). The paper notes measurement issues: "assessors may have found it difficult to elicit emotional language responses from students without an emotionally loaded context" (daunic_2023.txt).
- **Intervention structure**: "52 kindergarten lessons and 54 first-grade lessons lasting approximately 20 min each...First 16 topics contain lessons using readily available children's storybooks selected for their SEL-related content...First lesson is delivered whole-group...Second and third lessons in each topic are taught in a small-group setting to students identified as at risk for EBD, maximizing opportunities for language interactions and teacher modeling" (daunic_2023.txt).
- **Dialogic reading and self-talk**: "The second lesson uses dialogic reading (DR), an interactive technique to enhance children's language and literacy skills...Teachers expand a child's response by rephrasing, adding information, and repeating the prompt...In the third lesson per topic, students in the small group are asked to demonstrate use of selected SEL vocabulary and apply social-emotional concepts and skills in social problem-solving situations using a progression of strategies focused on regulating emotions and behavior" (daunic_2023.txt).
- **Strategies taught**: "Choices and Consequences, Breathe and Think (BAT), and Steps for Problem Solving...Stop before I say or do something, Think about my choices, Think about the consequences, Make a choice" (daunic_2023.txt).

**Relevant practices / scaffolds:**
- **Whole-group SEL literacy introduction** (storybooks, vocabulary) followed by **small-group intensive skill practice** for at-risk students.
- **Dialogic reading** to deepen comprehension and emotional language use.
- **Explicit, sequential self-regulation strategies** (stop-think-choose) taught and practiced.
- **Integration with existing literacy instruction** so SEL is not a separate "add-on."
- **Small-group instruction maximizes teacher modeling and interaction** for at-risk students.
- **Professional development in SEL foundations and dialogic reading** is essential for teachers.

**What to avoid:**
- Assuming a single language or EF intervention will shift behavior; holistic SEL competence matters.
- Whole-group SEL without intensive small-group practice for at-risk students.
- Passive reading of stories without interactive questioning and application.

**Population specifics (anxiety / depression / trauma / OCD):**
- The study targeted "students at risk for EBD," including those with early externalizing and internalizing behavior problems, not students with diagnosed PTSD or severe trauma.

**Candidate principle(s):**
- "SEL competence (integrating self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, responsible decision-making) is the pathway to school adjustment; narrow focus on language or EF alone is insufficient" (daunic_2023.txt).
- "Whole-group + small-group tiered instruction maximizes both universal learning and intensive support for at-risk students" (daunic_2023.txt).
- "Teacher professional development in SEL and interactive reading techniques is prerequisite" (derived).

**Caveats / contradictions:**
- The SELF curriculum is for K–1; long-term maintenance and effectiveness in later grades is not addressed.
- Missing data at posttest was higher than baseline, reducing power.
- Model fit issues at school level suggest unmeasured classroom/school factors influence outcomes.

#### Flannery et al. (2024) — *Check-In/Check-Out (CICO) in High School: Results From a Small RCT*

**Headline:** A high school-adapted CICO intervention (CICO-HS), with student agency through goal-setting and self-rating plus electronic daily progress reporting, showed meaningful improvements in attendance and teacher-perceived behavior (small-to-moderate effect sizes) and was rated highly acceptable by all stakeholders despite small sample size.

**Key findings with quotes:**
- **Design and adaptations**: "Check-In/Check-Out-High School (CICO-HS) is an adapted version of traditional CICO that preserves the core features of CICO while emphasizing the self-regulation focus of CICO and adapting the intervention to align with the unique contextual features of high schools by adding: (a) an electronic Daily Progress Report (eDPR) to increase user acceptability and overall efficiency, (b) student and teacher training materials to emphasize a student-directed role, (c) a daily CICO protocol that emphasizes student self-regulation skills and self-recruitment of adult feedback, (d) student-directed self-evaluation with feedback on their accuracy, and (e) more efficient home/school communication through automated electronic messaging" (flannery_2024.txt).
- **Efficacy on attendance**: "For unexcused tardies, the mean number of unexcused tardies for students in the treatment group was 23.71 before intervention and 17.43 during intervention...CICO-HS had a medium effect (g = −.73, 95% CI = [−1.74, 0.30]) on unexcused tardies...For unexcused absences, CICO-HS had a small to medium effect as well, with treatment group students showing reduced absences during intervention" (flannery_2024.txt).
- **Efficacy on behavior (teacher-rated)**: Improvements on Behavior Intervention Monitoring Assessment System (BIMAS) across conduct, negative affect, cognitive/attention, and social functioning scales (flannery_2024.txt).
- **Social acceptability**: "CICO-HS coordinators provided the highest mean ratings (M = 5.06), followed by students (M = 5.06), with teachers providing ratings indicating more moderate levels of acceptability (M = 4.36). All groups indicated willingness to use CICO-HS again and perceived it as effective for improving student behaviors" (flannery_2024.txt).
- **Core CICO components preserved**: Daily check-in review of expectations, Daily Progress Report (eDPR) to track behavior, teacher feedback during class, afternoon check-out to evaluate progress, family communication (flannery_2024.txt).
- **Student agency emphasis**: "Students identified and prioritized their behavioral goals and put them into the app...students checked in at the beginning of the day with the CICO coordinator, completed the classroom procedural fidelity components using their eDPR in each of their four classes during the day, and checked out with CICO coordinator at the end of the day" (flannery_2024.txt).

**Relevant practices / scaffolds:**
- **Daily check-in/check-out structure** (morning, multiple class periods, afternoon) provides consistent feedback rhythm.
- **eDPR app for self-rating and teacher feedback** increases engagement and efficiency vs. paper-based systems.
- **Student goal-setting and self-evaluation** boost autonomy and metacognition.
- **Clear behavior expectations taught and reinforced** throughout the day.
- **Weekly home-school communication** (not daily; adjusted for developmental appropriateness).
- **Coordinator role** (coach/mentor, not punitive administrator) is essential.

**What to avoid:**
- CICO should not become a punishment tool or behavior "tracker for getting in trouble."
- Excessive daily family contact can overwhelm parents; weekly is more sustainable.
- Neglecting student buy-in; adolescents need autonomy and respect.

**Population specifics (anxiety / depression / trauma / OCD):**
- The study targeted 14 ninth-grade students nominated for Tier 2 support due to academic engagement struggles. Three had IEPs; two had 504 plans. No trauma-specific diagnosis; focus on school engagement.

**Candidate principle(s):**
- "CICO-HS preserves core CICO features while adding developmentally appropriate autonomy (student goal-setting, self-rating) and technology (eDPR) to increase adolescent engagement" (flannery_2024.txt).
- "Daily feedback loops and clear expectations reduce attendance and behavior problems; weekly family contact is sufficient and sustainable" (flannery_2024.txt).
- "Coordinator role is motivational coaching, not surveillance; student agency is the mechanism" (derived).

**Caveats / contradictions:**
- Small sample size (N=14, 7 per condition); study was underpowered for formal hypothesis testing.
- Single high school, single year; generalizability limited.
- No long-term follow-up beyond 30 school days.

- "Fidelity varies by classroom and teacher; school-level leadership buy-in and coaching are essential" (kittelman_2024.txt).
- "eDPR technology increases efficiency and acceptability; it is a tool, not a replacement for personal feedback" (derived).

**Caveats / contradictions:**
- Single-case design across three students; no control/comparison group.
- Limited to academic engagement; cannot assess behavior or anxiety outcomes.
- Conducted during COVID-era (2019–20 and 2021–22 school years); school climate and attendance patterns may have been affected.

#### Beloyianni & Touloumakos (2024) — *Childhood Posttraumatic Stress and School-Related Behaviors: A Scoping Review*

**Headline:** Childhood PTSD is consistently associated with poor school-related behaviors (cognitive, emotional, social, behavioral); relationships are moderated by individual and environmental mediating factors; but most evidence comes from mixed child/adolescent samples, and school-specific interventions remain understudied.

**Key findings with quotes:**
- **Broad impact on school functioning**: "Childhood PTSD, in turn, is associated with a wide range of poor school-related behaviors, including cognitive, emotional, social, and behavioral functioning" (beloyianni_2024.txt).
- **Core symptoms disrupt school**: "Core symptoms of PTSD such as reexperiencing, hyperarousal, dissociation, sleep problems, attention and memory problems, could substantially interfere with school functioning, and are associated with impaired educational performance over time" (beloyianni_2024.txt; cited from asselman_2025.txt context).
- **Symptom confusion with other disorders**: "Identifying and dealing with the school-related impact of childhood PTSD remains challenging for a variety of reasons, such as the insufficient training in trauma-informed approaches among the school personnel or misdiagnosing PTSD symptoms that might overlap with other childhood disorders, such as anxiety disorders or hyperactivity" (beloyianni_2024.txt).
- **Typologies of traumatic events**: The review categorizes interpersonal (abuse, violence, maltreatment) and non-interpersonal trauma (accidents, natural disasters, medical trauma) (beloyianni_2024.txt).
- **ACEs framework**: Adverse childhood experiences include physical/sexual/emotional abuse, neglect, household dysfunction, and expanding definitions include bullying, poverty, community violence (beloyianni_2024.txt).
- **Moderating factors**: "Results demonstrated a consistent and strong relationship between PTS and various educational outcomes, which seemed to be moderated by a series of mediating factors, both individual and environmental" (beloyianni_2024.txt).

**Relevant practices / scaffolds:**
- **School-based screening for trauma history and PTSD symptoms** to differentiate trauma-related symptoms from ADHD, anxiety, or conduct problems.
- **Teacher training in recognizing PTSD presentations** (reexperiencing, hyperarousal, dissociation, sleep/attention problems).
- **Trauma-informed school policies and practices** that avoid re-traumatization.
- **Mental health services (counseling, trauma-focused CBT)** embedded in schools or referred out.

**What to avoid:**
- Misattributing PTSD symptoms (reexperiencing, hyperarousal) to ADHD or oppositional defiance without trauma assessment.
- School discipline policies that rely on punishment rather than understanding trauma-rooted behavior.
- Assuming all traumatized students will disclose; many hide symptoms.

**Population specifics (anxiety / depression / trauma / OCD):**
- The review focuses specifically on **childhood PTSD and its school manifestations**. PTSD symptoms include intrusion, avoidance, negative mood/cognition, hyperarousal, dissociation (beloyianni_2024.txt). Comorbidity with anxiety, depression, and ADHD is noted.

**Candidate principle(s):**
- "PTSD symptoms closely mimic ADHD and anxiety; trauma-informed screening is prerequisite to accurate identification" (beloyianni_2024.txt).
- "Core PTSD symptoms (reexperiencing, hyperarousal, dissociation) directly impair attention, memory, and emotional regulation—foundational to learning" (beloyianni_2024.txt).
- "School-based trauma-focused CBT and trauma-informed policies are evidence-based but understudied in school populations; more research in typical school settings is needed" (derived).

**Caveats / contradictions:**
- Scoping review, not meta-analysis; no synthesis of effect sizes.
- Most included studies were mixed child/adolescent samples; few school-specific studies.
- Limited evidence on what school-based interventions (beyond clinical trauma-focused CBT) are effective.

### Skim summaries (one paragraph each)

#### Berger (2019) — *Multi-tiered Approaches to Trauma-Informed Care in Schools: A Systematic Review*

This systematic review synthesized 13 published and unpublished studies on multi-tiered, trauma-informed care in schools, identifying programs like HEARTS, CBITS, and TBRI. Findings indicate that multi-tiered programs show promise but are limited by small sample sizes, variable quality, and lack of control groups; all programs emphasized staff training as foundational. The review highlights that the evidence base for Tier 1 (universal) whole-school approaches is particularly weak compared to Tier 2/3, and implementation science (fidelity, adaptation, sustainability) remains understudied. Key recommendations include rigorous RCTs of whole-school approaches, long-term follow-up studies, culturally responsive adaptations, and validated measures of trauma-informed practice implementation.

---

#### Stratford et al. (2020) — *A Scoping Review of School-Based Efforts to Support Students Who Have Experienced Trauma*

This scoping review of 91 publications (2010–2018) found that rigorous evaluations (RCTs and quasi-experimental) are concentrated in Tier 2/3 interventions (especially CBITS, trauma-focused CBT), while Tier 1 (universal) and teacher-delivered approaches lack rigorous evidence despite widespread school adoption. Key findings: most programs include counseling services, skill development, psychoeducation, and parent engagement, but less rigorous publications were more likely to address cultural competence and marginalized populations. The review calls for more rigorous evaluation of whole-school, non-clinical approaches, professional development strategies, and equity-centered research—noting that high-quality evidence should be made accessible to practitioners to avoid inadvertent re-traumatization.

#### Fassl et al. (2025) — *Adolescent Depression in School: Risk Factors and Consequences on School Functioning*

Using a longitudinal representative sample of 1,874 Austrian secondary students, this study identified female students with low SES and immigration background as most burdened by depression (intersectional analysis). Depression was negatively associated with conscientiousness and social inclusion and positively associated with high parental expectations. Notably, depression partially mediated the effects of these factors on test anxiety but not grades, suggesting depressive symptoms exert specific effects on anxiety/worry rather than achievement. The study highlights adolescence as a vulnerable period and calls for mental health education in teacher training, as teachers play a crucial role in identifying psychological issues but often have insufficient knowledge.

#### Gilmour et al. (2022) — *Teaching Students With EBD: Teachers' Burnout Profiles and Classroom Management*

Using latent profile analysis of 102 K–6 teachers with students with or at risk for EBD, this study identified three burnout profiles: "flourishing," "buffered," and "struggling." Burnout profiles were unrelated to teacher type (special vs. general education) but were significantly associated with classroom management skills; teachers in the "struggling" burnout profile received lower ratings on classroom management. The study demonstrates that teacher affect is complex and multidimensional; emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and personal accomplishment co-occur in patterns that influence instructional quality. Implications: teacher support and burnout mitigation are critical for improving classroom management and, ultimately, student outcomes for EBD populations.

### Top candidate design principles from this cluster

1. **Session predictability and ritual**: Daily/weekly check-in-check-out routines with consistent expectations, clear behavior definitions, and teacher feedback reduce anxiety and build trust. The routine itself (not just the content) is calming for dysregulated students (Flannery 2024; Kittelman 2024; derived from CICO evidence base).

2. **Tiered SEL instruction with intensive small-group practice for at-risk students**: Whole-group SEL vocabulary and concepts (storybooks, discussion) combined with small-group dialogic reading and skill practice for identified at-risk students maximizes both universal learning and intensive support (Daunic 2023; Wattanawongwan 2021).

3. **Cooperative learning with explicit teaching and monitoring, not just grouping**: Five core elements (positive interdependence, individual accountability, face-to-face interaction, social skills, group processing) must be explicitly taught, monitored via feedback, and reflected on—isolation and peer rejection are prevented only through intentional, structured practice (Wattanawongwan 2021).

4. **Trauma-informed reframing of "behavior problems" as dysregulation symptoms**: Teachers trained to recognize ACEs, PTSD symptoms, and executive dysfunction can respond with compassion and scaffolding rather than punishment, breaking negative interaction cycles (Asselman 2025; Koslouski 2023).

5. **Exposure-informed, approach-oriented anxiety support, not avoidance accommodation**: Brief scaffolding to help students face feared situations (e.g., speaking in class), combined with explicit fading of support, counteracts the inadvertent anxiety-reinforcement from accommodation (Håland 2025).

6. **Student agency, self-monitoring, and self-evaluation in daily feedback systems**: Adolescents respond to autonomy; self-rating on daily progress reports and student-set goals increase motivation and metacognition more than externally-imposed monitoring (Flannery 2024; Kittelman 2024).

7. **Targeted/indicated prevention for at-risk students produces larger effects than universal-only approaches**: Depression and anxiety prevention show effect sizes of g ≈ 0.33 for indicated programs vs. g ≈ 0.16 for universal; screening and rapid identification are cost-effective (Werner-Seidler 2021).

8. **Educator secondary trauma prevention is non-negotiable**: Teacher burnout disrupts classroom management and student outcomes; secondary traumatic stress reduction and self-care support systems protect teacher retention and student relationships (Koslouski 2023; Gilmour 2022).

9. **Relationship quality and trust as foundational for trauma-informed care**: Safety, predictability, empowerment, voice, and choice—especially in relationships with educators—are the mechanisms through which students develop resilience and emotional regulation (Koslouski 2023; asselman_2025.txt on teacher-student relationships).

10. **Effects attenuate without boosters or ongoing support; integration with core curriculum sustains gains**: One-shot interventions lose efficacy; SEL and depression/anxiety prevention must be embedded in ongoing instruction, with periodic skill review and booster lessons (Werner-Seidler 2021; Daunic 2023).

### Honest caveats about trauma-informed and related approaches

**Watson (2025) — A Critical Review of Empirical Support for Trauma-Informed Approaches in Schools**

While not fully read within token constraints, Watson (2025) is cited in the corpus as a "critical review" that challenges the empirical base of trauma-informed schools. The paper scrutinizes whether trauma-informed components (safety, relationships, strengths-based approaches, understanding/commitment) have strong causal evidence. Key takeaway from literature: much of trauma-informed practice is grounded in theoretical rationale and convergent evidence, but rigorous RCT evidence for whole-school trauma-informed approaches is lacking. Safety, in particular, is often studied as a construct separate from trauma-informed care; evidence for relationship-building interventions (Post et al. 2020) and training (Orapallo et al. 2021) is limited to small, descriptive studies. **Implication for Arduino PBL curriculum**: the curriculum should acknowledge that trauma-informed approaches lack robust empirical validation at the whole-school level; smaller, localized practices (daily check-ins, clear expectations, relationship-building) have stronger evidence.

**Additional caveats from this cluster:**

1. **Publication and quality bias**: Many school-based anxiety/depression/trauma studies rely on pre-post designs without control groups (Werner-Seidler 2021; Stratford 2020); RCT evidence is concentrated in Tier 2/3 (clinical) interventions, not universal approaches. Effect sizes for depression/anxiety prevention are small (g ≈ 0.18–0.21) and attenuate over follow-up.

2. **Accommodation paradox**: Schools intend to help anxious students with accommodations but inadvertently reinforce avoidance, prolonging anxiety (Håland 2025). This is a hard finding: compassionate short-term accommodation can worsen long-term outcomes.

3. **Implementation fidelity is variable**: CICO studies show fidelity ranges of 65–87% (Kittelman 2024; Flannery 2024); some teachers/classrooms are consistently low-fidelity. School buy-in and coaching are non-negotiable, not optional.

4. **Evidence gaps in school-specific contexts**: Most trauma research is in clinical samples, child welfare, or juvenile justice; school-specific trauma interventions remain understudied (Beloyianni 2024; Stratford 2020). Small sample sizes and lack of generalizability limit conclusions.

5. **Teacher training and sustainability**: All multi-tiered trauma-informed programs emphasize staff training, but how training translates to classroom practice, how long effects sustain, and how to prevent trainer-dependent implementation are understudied (Berger 2019).

6. **Measurement challenges**: Assessing "trauma-informed practice" implementation and its direct link to student outcomes is methodologically difficult; many studies use proxy outcomes or indirect measures (Watson 2025 implicit critique; also Daunic 2023 on mediator measurement issues).

### Gaps in this cluster's evidence for Arduino PBL

- **Arduino/Robotics + EBD/Trauma**: No studies in this cluster directly address technology-enhanced learning or maker/robotics projects for trauma-informed education or anxiety/depression intervention. The evidence is based on classroom instruction, group work, and SEL curricula.

- **Small-group, peer-supported project-based learning**: While cooperative learning is well-studied, its combination with hands-on, intrinsically motivating projects (Arduino) in low-resource special education settings is not addressed. Potential gap: do students with EBD and trauma respond differently to "safe failure" in engineering vs. academic tasks?

- **Check-in-check-out in middle school**: CICO evidence focuses on elementary (K–6) and high school (9–12); middle school (7–8) is understudied. Does adolescent development shift the optimal CICO structure?

- **Anxiety accommodation in project-based contexts**: Håland (2025) documents widespread anxiety avoidance accommodation; how do we balance scaffolding for students with social anxiety in group Arduino projects without accommodation? This is a specific, practical gap.

- **Long-term outcomes beyond 12 months**: Most prevention studies do not report maintenance beyond 1 year. Does Arduino PBL (if framed as trauma-informed, SEL-rich, cooperative) sustain engagement and skills beyond a school year?

- **Executive function support for students with complex trauma**: Asselman (2025) shows PTSD and EF deficits mediate internalizing/externalizing problems. What specific EF scaffolds (working memory, inhibition, flexibility) are needed during project-based learning for students with dual diagnoses (e.g., ADHD + trauma)?

- **Equity/cultural responsiveness in trauma-informed schools**: Stratford (2020) notes that less rigorous publications emphasize cultural competence; rigorous studies do not. For an Israeli special education population with diverse cultural/linguistic backgrounds, how do we operationalize trauma-informed care that is culturally responsive?

- **Teacher burnout and sustainability in special education**: Gilmour (2022) links teacher burnout to classroom management; Asselman (2025) emphasizes the toll on EBD teachers. How can Arduino PBL reduce teacher burden (e.g., through peer-supported learning, positive student engagement) while building sustainable practices?
