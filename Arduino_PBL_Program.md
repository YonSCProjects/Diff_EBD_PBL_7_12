# Differentiated Project-Based Arduino Workshop Program for Students with Emotional and Behavioural Disorders

## A research-grounded program for Agourim School, grades 7–12

*Version 0.1 — Phase C draft. 2026-04-10.*
*Author: Yon (robotics and programming teacher, Agourim School), with research synthesis and documentation support from Claude.*

---

## Abstract

This document describes an eight-project differentiated Arduino workshop program for 7th–12th grade students with emotional and behavioural disorders at Agourim School. The program combines three ingredients: differentiated project-based learning with three readiness tiers per project; hands-on Arduino physical computing with ESP32, sensors, motors, and a build-from-scratch tiny quadcopter capstone; and Claude Code as a collaborative AI coding partner rather than a source of copy-paste answers. The design rests on nine research-grounded principles distilled from a deep reading of 65 verified full-text articles spanning EBD behavior interventions, executive function scaffolding, trauma-informed practice, differentiated instruction in secondary settings, project-based learning, robotics in special education, and universal design for learning. The super-target is the creation of small, positive, empowering experiences for students whose confidence in learning was damaged by earlier school experiences that did not meet their needs. The document is written to serve three audiences simultaneously: as a practical program guide for the workshop teacher, as a research-grounded proposal for potential evaluating and funding organizations, and as a resource that other teachers working with similar populations in workshop environments can adopt, adapt, and extend.

## §1 Introduction

### Who this document is for

This document serves three audiences, and each should be able to use it independently.

**First**, it is a **practical program guide for Yon** — the robotics and programming teacher at Agourim School — to run an eight-project Arduino workshop across three school years. Everything from the session rhythm to the per-project task cards is designed to be picked up and used, not just read. A teacher who has read §5 (Methodology) and the relevant chapter of §6 (Projects Overview) should be able to run a session without additional preparation.

**Second**, it is a **research-grounded proposal for potential evaluating and funding organizations**, describing a program that can be evaluated on its merits and supported with budget. The literature review, the honest limitations, the expected-outcomes framework, and the budget ask are all written with an evaluating reviewer in mind. Where the research base is strong, the document says so clearly; where the research base is weaker or where the program extends into territory without direct empirical support, the document says so with equal clarity. Academic integrity and the reviewer's trust both depend on this honesty.

**Third, and equally important**, this document is **a resource for other teachers working with similar populations in workshop environments** — colleagues at Agourim now, teachers at other special-education schools in Israel, and workshop educators elsewhere who face the same challenges. Every design decision in the program is explained, every citation is linked to a real paper, every tier and scaffold is documented in enough detail that a teacher encountering this work for the first time can adopt it, adapt it, and make it their own. The principles in §4 are not Agourim-specific; they are grounded in research on students with emotional and behavioural disorders generally, and they should transfer to other workshop settings with similar student populations. The eight projects in §6 are a working template — a teacher at a different school can substitute a project, reorder the sequence to match their available hardware, or keep the framework and change the content entirely. The hope is that this document becomes something other teachers reach for when designing their own differentiated workshop programs, not something locked to one school.

### The problem this program addresses

The students at the center of this work share a difficult history: **mainstream primary school did not meet their needs**. They arrive at Agourim carrying a wide range of diagnoses — ADHD, oppositional defiant disorder, anxiety, depression, obsessive-compulsive disorder, and post-trauma among the more commonly seen — and an even wider range of strengths, backgrounds, and literacy levels. A few illustrative contrasts, drawn from a much longer list of dimensions along which our students differ: some read fluently while others can barely read or write; some come from supportive, engaged homes while others come from neglect; some are deeply introverted and guard their work carefully while others are extroverted and struggle with impulse control and social boundaries; some have experienced boycotts and harassment in previous school settings that shape how they trust peers and adults, while others carry quieter histories of being overlooked. These examples are illustrative, not exhaustive — no two students at Agourim look alike, and this program is designed to make room for that range.

What the students almost all share is a sense that **school has been a place where they fail, or where they are failed**. Rebuilding trust in learning — one small positive experience at a time — is the super-target of every design choice in this program.

Most of these students benefit from a learning environment that differs substantially from what most secondary curricula assume. They often need it to be **predictable** (routine protects executive function from stress collapse), **visibly scaffolded** (task cards and visual checklists guide work so the teacher is not a verbal bottleneck), **differentiated by readiness** rather than by labeled ability (so students with very different profiles can work on the same project and experience success at their own level), and **structured so a solo teacher** with three to eight students on as many different projects can rotate and support without becoming a single point of failure. Many of them need tasks that produce **tangible, immediate results** — something that lights up, moves, or responds to the world — because abstract academic wins have often not worked for them. And many benefit from **autonomy, choice, and voice**, because an approach that tells these students what to do and when to do it tends to trigger the same power-struggle dynamics that made primary school unbearable for many of them.

### The approach in one paragraph

This program is built from three ingredients that the research supports independently. In the research literature we reviewed during Phase A of this work, we did not find a published study that combined all three for this population — which is not to say no teacher has tried similar combinations in their own classroom, only that we could not locate such work in the verified corpus. **First, differentiated project-based learning:** projects are offered in three tiers — Tier 1 (guided build), Tier 2 (guided design with choice points), Tier 3 (open design) — so students with different readiness levels can work on the same project simultaneously and experience success at their own level, moving between tiers across projects without shame. **Second, hands-on Arduino physical computing:** students wire real circuits, see real hardware respond to their code, and build devices that do things in the world — a robot that follows a line, a car they can drive from their phone, a quadcopter they assembled themselves that actually flies. The physicality is not decoration; it is load-bearing for a population that has often not been served by abstract academic tasks. **Third, Claude Code as a differentiated coding support:** students engage with code at the level that is actually approachable for them today. For students who find coding — or even AI interaction itself — overwhelming, the starting point may simply be uploading a pre-written sketch that matches their wiring: the code just works, the hardware responds, and the student sees the result without having to write or debug anything. Students who are ready for more modify that code with Claude Code's help, making small targeted changes and seeing what happens. And students who are ready to direct the process describe what they are trying to build, show Claude Code their current attempt, and iterate in dialogue. Across all three levels, the AI and the pre-written code are scaffolds the student uses, not obstacles the student has to overcome alone. The eight projects are designed as a progression from a first-session LED circuit through the ESP32 tiny quadcopter capstone, with each project introducing skills that later projects build on. For many students, working through the order as designed is the path of least resistance. But the order is not a rigid gate: some students — particularly those whose school history includes many negative encounters with "do it in this sequence" — will want to skip ahead to a later project, work on projects in a different order, or start from one that happens to grab their interest that day. §5 describes how the program supports this without abandoning the underlying skill progression: prerequisites become explicit skills the student can see and acquire, not boxes to check in sequence, and a student's refusal to follow the prescribed order is treated as information about what they need, not as a failure to be corrected. Every project is built on the same nine research-grounded design principles documented in §4.

### What is novel about this work

In the research literature we reviewed during Phase A, we did not find a published program that combines all three of these ingredients for students with emotional and behavioural disorders. That literature does contain:

- extensive evidence on behavior interventions, executive function scaffolding, and social-emotional learning;
- one empirical study — Taylor, Allen, Van, and Moohr (2024) in the *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders* — of project-based learning specifically implemented in an EBD classroom, with very large on-task effect sizes (BC-SMD = 1.70 to 3.81) and concurrent improvements in teacher job satisfaction;
- evidence from robotics-in-special-education studies that hands-on technological interventions and robot-assisted supports sustain attention and task completion in children with ADHD by providing structured, non-threatening feedback (Berrezueta-Guzmán et al. 2021; Cibrian et al. 2022) — a pattern we describe informally as a "non-human mediator" effect, aggregating across studies rather than citing a unified synthesis of it;
- meta-analytic evidence for scaffolded STEM instruction (Belland et al. 2017: overall effect g = 0.46, with scaffolding producing directionally stronger effects in project-based learning contexts than in problem-based learning, and significantly larger effects for underperforming students than for traditional learners), differentiated instruction in secondary settings (Smale-Jacobse et al. 2019, d = 0.509–0.741), and universal design for learning (Almeqdad et al. 2023);
- a dissertation study of constructionist makerspace learning for middle-school EBD students (Gomez 2019) that is the closest direct analogue to the Arduino workshop in the verified literature.

But the same corpus contained **no published empirical research on Arduino or microcontroller-level physical computing for students with EBD, ADHD, or learning disabilities**. We also found **no research on aerial robotics, drones, or flight-STEM for any special-needs population** — we confirmed this by searching all 78 files in our full-text corpus for any mention of drones, quadcopters, UAVs, or flight, and found only one metaphorical hit in the CAST (2018) UDL guidelines. And we found **no research on AI-collaborative coding tools (such as Claude Code) for neurodivergent students**, likely because this class of tool is too new for a research literature to have developed around it. These absence-claims are scoped to the verified corpus we reviewed in Phase A; we cannot rule out the existence of relevant work outside it.

This program therefore **stands on the shoulders of adjacent research** and extends it responsibly into new territory. §9 of this document, the Honest Limitations section, is dedicated to being explicit about which claims rest on direct empirical evidence and which are inferential extensions — because academic integrity, practical value, and reviewer trust all depend on knowing the difference.

## §2 Population Profile — The Agourim Students

### The school and its role

Agourim is a small Israeli special-education school serving students in grades 7 through 12 who are coping with significant emotional and behavioural difficulties. Many of these students were not served well by mainstream primary education — and, for some, by the mainstream secondary schools they attended before arriving at Agourim. The school's role is academic and developmental. Students at Agourim also receive psychological and therapeutic support through separate channels; the robotics and programming workshop is a place where they learn skills, build things, and — if the program design works — begin to experience school as a place where effort can pay off.

A **school-wide token-economy behavioural program** is integrated across most aspects of daily school activity at Agourim and serves as the school's primary shared framework for positive behavior support. The research this program draws on actually supports this approach: Barker & de Lugt (2022) review empirical classroom studies of interventions for students with oppositional defiant disorder and report strong positive effects of token economies (for example, Rosenberg (1986) — included in Barker & de Lugt's evidence table — produced 82% M PND for on-task behavior and 91% M PND for reducing disruptive behavior in an ODD alternative classroom), and Morsink et al. (2022) review two studies (Carlson et al. 2000; Carlson & Tamm 2000) that explicitly tested whether rewards undermine intrinsic motivation in children with ADHD, with neither study finding that effect. The robotics and programming workshop operates within the school-wide token system rather than running parallel to it. Within the workshop session itself, internal recognition of student effort is additionally **social, immediate, and tangible** — a shared moment when a robot moves, a visible checkmark on a task card, a peer noticing the work — because hardware-based milestones are already inherently tangible and a small-group workshop setting has the capacity for immediate personal acknowledgment in a way larger classrooms may not. The within-workshop recognition mechanism coexists with the school's broader token economy, not in place of it. §4 Principle 5 and §5 Methodology return to both layers with more detail.

The workshop itself is a small-group setting. On any given session, three to eight students from the same mother class attend the workshop at the same time, each working on their own individual project. The teacher is a single adult managing all of them at once, which is why the design requirement of **student independence for at least 10–15 minutes at a time** is not a nice-to-have — it is the basic condition for the workshop to function at all. Almost every design choice in the rest of this document traces back, directly or indirectly, to that one structural fact.

### Heterogeneity is the first fact, not a complication

The thing a reader should know before anything else about the Agourim student body is that **heterogeneity is not a complication of the population; it is the population**. There is no "typical" Agourim student, and this program is designed to start from that heterogeneity rather than treat it as a later adjustment.

What does this heterogeneity look like in practice? §1 gave a short list of illustrative contrasts; §2 goes a little deeper. The examples that follow are still drawn from a much larger set of dimensions along which our students differ, and any description of the population — including this one — is a sketch, not a portrait.

**Diagnostic profiles vary widely, and many students carry more than one.** Among the more commonly seen:
- **ADHD**, with executive function challenges, attention regulation difficulties, and often motor restlessness. Harrison, Soares, Rudzinski & Johnson (2019) found in their Review of Educational Research meta-review that classroom-based interventions for ADHD are moderately effective overall, with self-management and instructional interventions meeting evidence-based standards — which matters because it tells us the intervention surface is the *design of the task and the student's relationship to it*, not the fixing of the student.
- **Oppositional defiant disorder (ODD)**, which Barker & de Lugt (2022) frame as *an interactional disorder* — symptoms arise "within a relationship between the child and another person, within a social context," not from fixed traits. Their review notes that children with ODD "rebel against any attempt to modify or influence their behavior, both positively (e.g., using rewards) or negatively (e.g., using punishments)," because the attempt itself is read as manipulation. This framing shapes how the workshop approaches every student, not only those formally diagnosed.
- **Anxiety** in many forms — generalized, social, school-specific, sometimes panic. Håland & Bertelsen (2025) document how widespread school-based anxiety accommodations are among teachers, and raise the concern that some accommodations can inadvertently reinforce avoidance; §4 Principle 5 and §5 Methodology return to this nuance.
- **Depression**, ranging from low motivation and energy on hard days to clinical depressive episodes. Werner-Seidler et al. (2021) meta-analysis of school-based depression and anxiety prevention programs shows that universal effects are small (g ≈ 0.18–0.21) and targeted effects somewhat larger — a reminder that general-purpose interventions alone are not sufficient.
- **Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)**, which Abramovitch et al. (2024) show produces functional impairment for approximately 90% of affected children and adolescents, with school being the most-affected domain (32–47% of children report academic impact). Colbert et al. (2025) show that school staff knowledge of OCD is limited (46–58% recognize it as a distinct disorder) and that brief written psychoeducation is more effective than long training workshops — both findings are directly relevant to how this program handles OCD-related accommodation.
- **Post-traumatic reactions** arising from a wide range of adverse experiences. Watson & Astor (2025) in their critical review of trauma-informed approaches in schools are explicit that the empirical base is weaker than the field's enthusiasm suggests, and that rigorous evidence concentrates on smaller, localized practices (daily check-ins, clear expectations, relationship-building) rather than whole-school framework adoption. §9 Honest Limitations returns to this caveat.

Many students carry two or three of these profiles at once, sometimes alongside learning disabilities or language differences. Some have formal diagnoses from specialists; some have patterns that everyone around them recognizes but that have not been formally diagnosed. The program responds to the student in front of the teacher, not to the label on a file — the diagnostic information in this profile is context, not a decision tree.

**Literacy and language skills vary widely.** Some of our students read fluently in Hebrew, English, or both; others can barely decode a short paragraph. Some write with ease; others find writing so effortful that they avoid it. This matters for the workshop because traditional curricular materials assume reading as the entry point — our materials cannot.

**Fine motor skills, sensory regulation, and tolerance for frustration vary.** Some students can solder a fine joint on the first attempt; others struggle with the physical precision of threading a wire through a breadboard hole. Some can tolerate a buzzing motor on their desk as background noise; others find it overwhelming.

**Social comfort varies.** Some students actively want group collaboration; some find any group dynamic exhausting and need solo workspace by default. Some arrive carrying histories of bullying or social rejection that shape how they trust peers; others are socially hungry and will work with anyone who lets them.

**Home context varies.** Some students come from supportive, highly engaged homes; others come from homes where educational expectations are low, absent, or actively antagonistic. Some can work on a project over a weekend with parental support; others cannot.

**Prior experience with technology varies.** Some students have been building Arduino projects at home for years and arrive at the workshop already fluent in corners of C++ the teacher has not yet needed. Others have never touched a breadboard and find the workshop's sensory environment — lights, beeps, buzzing motors, wire clippings on the floor — genuinely novel.

These examples — diagnosis, literacy, fine motor, social comfort, home context, tech exposure — are illustrative, not exhaustive. There are many more dimensions along which our students differ, and the program is designed to make room for that range.

### What many of our students share

Despite this heterogeneity, some threads run through the population and shape the program's core design commitments. None of what follows is universal — there are always exceptions — but the patterns are common enough that design choices built around them serve most students well.

**Many arrive at Agourim with damaged trust in school as an institution.** Their primary-school years were, for many, an accumulating sequence of experiences where they tried, were misunderstood, were disciplined for behaviors they could not control, or were quietly left behind. A reasonable conclusion from that history is that school is not a safe place to try hard. The program treats this mistrust as an accurate reading of lived experience, not as a character flaw.

**Capacity fluctuates day to day.** Cumming, Oblath, Qiu, Frazier and colleagues (2024), in their *Remedial and Special Education* 45(2) study of executive function, perceived stress, and academic performance in middle schoolers with and at risk for EBD, showed that perceived stress directly degrades executive function (working memory, cognitive flexibility), which in turn predicts academic performance (β ≈ .61 for language arts, β ≈ .65 for math). EF is not a stable trait in this population; it is a state that rises and falls with what is happening in the student's life outside the workshop. A student who was working independently at Tier 3 yesterday may arrive today unable to follow a two-step task card, not because they have regressed but because their stress budget is spent on something we cannot see. The program must accommodate this without drama — the same student is allowed to be at Tier 3 most of the time AND to drop to Tier 1 on a hard day, without shame and without being relabeled.

**Many are sensitive to perceived coercion.** For ODD-flavored students this sensitivity is most visible, but some version of it runs through much of the population: students with anxiety and trauma histories may read prescriptive, commanding instructions as threat; students with depression may read them as pressure they cannot meet; students with ADHD may read them as a mismatch with their own sense of what is worth attending to. Barker & de Lugt (2022) frame ODD as "best considered to be an interactional disorder" specifically because its symptoms arise in relationships and social contexts; the broader lesson is that the teacher's tone and stance — and the stance of the task cards, the stance of the AI, the stance of the posted routine — matter across the whole population.

**Many are also sensitive to shame.** OCD-related compulsions that would be private elsewhere can become visible in a workshop (a student who needs to recheck a wiring step eleven times, or who cannot begin a new step until the previous one feels "complete enough"). Anxiety-related difficulties with public performance can freeze a student at the moment of presenting their work. Depression can make any invitation to share feel like an exposure. Abramovitch et al. (2024) and Håland & Bertelsen (2025) each document, in their respective populations, how public visibility of symptoms can compound the underlying difficulty. The program's default for this reason is **private competence** — students can always opt out of public demonstration, and no design choice hinges on forcing a student to perform in front of peers.

**And many are hungry for real competence.** Despite — or because of — the experiences that damaged their trust in school, most of our students respond powerfully when they encounter a task they can actually do and where the evidence of success is tangible, visible, and belongs to them. A robot that moves because they wired and coded it is different in kind from a multiple-choice question they got right. Our super-target — *small, positive, empowering experiences* — rests on this hunger, and on the program's commitment to making those experiences genuinely earned.

### Fluctuating capacity is a design constraint, not a problem to solve

Because capacity fluctuates, the program cannot assign a student to a tier or a project and then hold them there. Student state changes across sessions, across weeks, and sometimes within a single session — a student can arrive engaged and drop into shutdown after a twenty-minute phone call with a parent, and the workshop design has to absorb that without making the drop feel like a failure. The program's response is to treat tier and project assignment as **fluid**: students move between tiers of the same project, or between projects entirely, as their state changes, without that movement being treated as regression or promotion. §5 Methodology describes the fluid-assignment logic in more detail. The important thing to understand here is that any statement of the form "Student A is at Tier 2" is a snapshot, not a fixed identity.

### What this profile does NOT tell you

This profile is descriptive, not prescriptive, and it has two important limits.

**First, diagnostic categories are imperfect**. Two students with the same diagnosis may present very differently, and two students with very different diagnoses may need very similar supports on a given day. The program does not match intervention-to-diagnosis; it matches intervention-to-current-state, as read by the teacher in real time and adjusted across the session. The diagnostic information in this profile is a shared vocabulary for discussing the students with other teachers and with reviewers — it is not an instruction manual.

**Second, no profile can substitute for knowing your students.** The teacher's firsthand knowledge of each student — what motivates them, what they are working on outside the workshop, what their week has been like, what their family is dealing with, what small things have gone well for them recently — is irreplaceable. Nothing in this document, and nothing in the research literature it rests on, can replace that knowledge. This profile is a starting point and a common language; it is not a replacement for the teacher's eye.

### How this profile shapes the rest of the document

The population profile is the reason for almost every design choice that follows. Principle 1 (predictable routine) exists because capacity fluctuates with stress. Principle 2 (the task card is the boss) exists because direct verbal instruction tends to be read as coercion across much of the population. Principle 3 (physical-first, then abstract) exists because literacy and tolerance for abstract tasks vary widely. Principle 5 (structured autonomy, across projects as well as within them) exists because many of our students have learned, rationally, that prescribed compliance is not safe for them. Principle 7 (Claude Code as a differentiated coding support with three access levels — including pre-written code upload for students who find AI interaction overwhelming) exists because the AI itself can be a barrier for some students. Principle 8 (relationships are the multiplier) exists because trust in adults is the load-bearing prerequisite for everything else in the workshop. The full research rationale follows in §3; the principles themselves are presented in §4.

### §3.1 EBD population and evidence-based classroom interventions

Students identified with emotional and behavioural disorders (EBD) are at elevated risk for a range of adverse outcomes — poor academic achievement, low high-school graduation rates, weak post-secondary transitions, and fractured social functioning. Mitchell, Kern & Conroy (2019), summarising the state of the field in *Behavioral Disorders*, describe EBD as a category where "students who experience ED are at greater risk for a host of negative long-term outcomes that include poor social, behavioral, and academic achievement in school, which in turn may impact graduation and postsecondary outcomes." Their field recommendations are structural: revise IDEA terminology to an education-based definition, institute universal screening for social-emotional-behavioural concerns, build multi-tiered systems of support (MTSS) that offer preventive intervention before problems consolidate, and integrate service delivery so students have access to counselling, health, and academic supports in a coordinated way. The program described in this document does not implement Mitchell et al.'s field-wide recommendations — a workshop is not a school system — but the principle that **preventive, layered supports outperform reactive, single-layer interventions** is load-bearing for every design decision that follows.

Inclusion of EBD students in general-education settings is supported by policy but the empirical literature is fragmented. Harrison, Soares & Joyce (2019), in their scoping review in the *International Journal of Inclusive Education*, map six themes in the literature — professional experience, strategy effectiveness, policy, influencing factors, teacher training, and teacher knowledge — and identify substantial gaps between policy intent and practice. One practical finding is directly relevant to the program's design: Gage, Kramer II & Ellis (2021), analysing school-climate data from over 350,000 high-school students in Georgia (including more than 5,000 students with or at risk for EBD), found that EBD students reported significantly more negative perceptions of school climate than peers across every measured dimension, **but** EBD students with Individual Education Plan (IEP) services reported more positive climates than EBD students without IEP services in several domains. The mechanism the authors suggest is that formal, visible adult support improves students' sense of belonging — an empirical grounding for the workshop's commitment to relationships as the first-class infrastructure on which everything else rests (§4 Principle 8).

Two bodies of evidence address what actually works in the classroom for this population. For ADHD specifically, Harrison, Soares, Rudzinski & Johnson (2019), in their *Review of Educational Research* meta-review of single-case classroom-based interventions, identified **instructional and self-management interventions** as the two categories meeting What Works Clearinghouse evidence-based standards. Behavioral interventions were also effective, particularly when selected through functional behavior analysis, though more effective when implemented by researchers than by teachers — a reminder that implementation fidelity shapes outcomes as much as the intervention choice itself. Classroom-based interventions for ADHD were, overall, moderately effective.

For self-contained EBD classrooms, Gersib & Mason (2023) conducted a meta-analysis in *Behavioral Disorders* across studies reporting behavior interventions and found a large overall effect size (**g = 0.93, SE = 0.16**). Their moderator analysis is particularly relevant: interventions that included **relational components** — daily parent communication, peer support, adult mentoring — produced significantly stronger effects than interventions without those components (moderator **B = 1.26, SE = 0.15, p < .05**). The practical reading is that *structure alone is not enough*; structure combined with relational supports multiplies the effect. The program's §4 Principle 8 (relationships as the multiplier) rests directly on this finding.

Riden, Kumm & Maggin (2022) conducted a mega-review of 50 systematic reviews and meta-analyses of 20 classroom and behavior management practices for students with or at risk for EBD, identifying eight practices with sufficient evidence: Check-In/Check-Out (CICO), functional behavior assessment-based interventions, group contingency, peer-mediated interventions, self-management, self-regulated strategy development for writing, **token economies**, and video modeling. The list is notable in two ways. First, it spans a range of intervention intensities, supporting the MTSS-style layered model. Second, it includes token economies as an evidence-based practice for EBD — a finding corroborated by Barker & de Lugt (2022) for ODD specifically, and directly relevant to the program's §2 discussion of how the workshop operates within Agourim's school-wide token-economy framework.

Oppositional defiant disorder deserves its own treatment because ODD is the diagnosis most closely tied to the program's "structured autonomy, not forced compliance" principle (§4 Principle 5). Barker & de Lugt (2022), in a review for the *International Journal of Special Education*, frame ODD as fundamentally **an interactional disorder** — symptoms arise within relationships and social contexts, not from fixed traits. They quote Barkley & Benton (2013) as theoretical framing on why direct behavior-modification attempts can backfire ("children with ODD rebel against any attempt to modify or influence their behavior, both positively (e.g., using rewards) or negatively (e.g., using punishments)") — but it is important to note that Barker & de Lugt present this framing as background, not as their own conclusion. Their own empirical review reports that classroom-based interventions — including token economies, group contingencies, and self-monitoring — produce strong positive effects for students with ODD. Rosenberg (1986), included in Barker & de Lugt's evidence table, showed a token economy with rule reminders producing **M PND = 82% for on-task behavior** and **M PND = 91% for reducing disruptive behavior** in an alternative ODD classroom. The theoretical concern about rewards being read as manipulation is real; the empirical evidence for well-structured classroom interventions is also real. Both can be true simultaneously, and the program's design tries to respect both (§4 Principle 5; see also [Phase_B_Reaudit_Log.md](Phase_B_Reaudit_Log.md) for the history of how this nuance was recovered).

OCD is less represented in the EBD literature than ADHD or ODD but is functionally important for the Agourim student population. Abramovitch, Duncan, Schuyler & Geller (2024), in *Journal of Affective Disorders*, report that approximately **90% of children with OCD experience impairment in at least one domain**, with school as the most-affected domain — academic impact is reported by 32–47% of children and parents. Colbert, Powell, Weighall & Gibson-Miller (2025), in *Neurodiversity*, conducted a systematic narrative review of school-staff knowledge of OCD and interventions to improve it; they found that baseline staff knowledge is limited (approximately 46–58% recognition of OCD as a distinct disorder) and that **brief written psychoeducational materials outperform longer workshop formats** for improving staff knowledge. Neither paper prescribes specific workshop design rules for students with OCD — the workshop's choices (private workspace by default, no forced public demo, task cards with clear "done when" criteria) are the program's *inference* from the documented burden, not a direct Abramovitch or Colbert prescription. This distinction was corrected during the Phase C re-audit.

Finally, the research base on teacher well-being in EBD settings matters for program sustainability. Gilmour, Sandilos, Pilny, Schwartz & Wehby (2022), in *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders*, used latent profile analysis to identify three teacher profiles (flourishing, buffered, struggling) among teachers of students with EBD and reported that burnout profile was meaningfully related to observed classroom management quality. The specific directional detail that the program's principle distillation originally attributed to Gilmour — that struggling-profile teachers used fewer effective strategies and gave students less autonomy — appears in parts of the paper we could not fully verify at the abstract level during the Phase C re-audit, and so §9 Honest Limitations flags this particular sub-claim as partially-verified. What is clearly established by the abstract and the Phase B audit triangulation is the higher-order finding that **teacher burnout profile and classroom management quality are linked** — and the practical implication for program design is direct: the workshop must be built so the teacher can run it without burning out, because teacher exhaustion compounds downstream into the students' experience of the workshop. §4 Principle 9 and §8 Budget both carry this constraint.

**Implications for design.** §3.1 supports Principle 8 (relationships as the multiplier — Gersib & Mason 2023), Principle 9 (teacher sustainability as a first-class constraint — Gilmour 2022), and Principle 5 (structured autonomy — Barker & de Lugt 2022's nuanced position on ODD interventions, plus the Riden 2022 mega-review showing token economies as evidence-based for EBD). It also supports the program's commitment to layered preventive supports (Mitchell 2019) and to formal, visible support infrastructure (Gage 2021).

### §3.2 Executive function, perceived stress, and scaffolding

Executive function (EF) — the set of neurocognitive processes that allow goal-directed behavior, including working memory, cognitive flexibility, and inhibitory control — is central to this program's design for one specific reason: **EF in students with or at risk for EBD is not a stable trait but a state that rises and falls with stress**, and the workshop has to be built to protect it rather than to expect it.

The most direct evidence comes from Cumming and colleagues, who published two related studies in *Remedial and Special Education* on the relationship between perceived stress, executive function, and academic outcomes in middle-school students with and at risk for EBD. In their 2024 study of executive function, perceived stress, and academic performance (volume 45, issue 2, pages 85–100; Verification_Log row 69), the authors report that students with or at risk for EBD score significantly lower on EF measures (working memory, cognitive flexibility) than typical peers. Perceived stress is directly and negatively related to EF, and EF in turn mediates academic outcomes — for all students in the sample, higher EF predicted better academic functioning (**β ≈ .61 for language arts, β ≈ .65 for math**, both p < .001). The mediation chain is: stress degrades EF, and degraded EF degrades academic performance. The related 2024 paper by the same research group on stress regulation and emotional and behavioral well-being (volume 46, issue 6, pages 426–441; Verification_Log row P1) extends this framing.

The design implication is that *any program for this population that assumes stable cognitive capacity will fail on the days when the student's stress budget is spent on something outside the workshop*. Students arrive with EF capacity that changes day-to-day, and a program designed around yesterday's performance will be wrong about today's. The workshop's fluid tier assignment (§5 Methodology) and predictable session routine (§4 Principle 1) are both responses to this fact.

External cognitive scaffolds are the program's way of handling EF deficits without requiring EF to be present. Rosen, Boyle, Cariss & Forchelli (2014), in *Learning Disabilities: A Multidisciplinary Journal*, describe a strategic two-column note-taking intervention for middle-school students with learning disabilities that acted as an external cognitive scaffold — *"opening new pathways for struggling students to access executive functions and flexibly deploy cognitive strategies."* The critical finding for program design is that **students do not need intact EF to use an external scaffold**; they can practice EF *through* the scaffold. This inverts the usual teaching sequence, where students are expected to master a skill before being trusted to use it. In the Rosen et al. framing, the scaffold is the training environment — the student develops EF by using the scaffold, not by graduating beyond it. The workshop's task cards (§4 Principle 2) operationalise this principle directly.

Belland, Walker, Kim & Lefler (2017), in *Review of Educational Research*, conducted a meta-analysis of computer-based scaffolding in STEM across 144 studies and reported an overall effect size of **g = 0.46**, with scaffolding producing directionally stronger effects in project-based learning contexts than in problem-based learning (**z = 6.08, p < .01**) and significantly larger effects for **underperforming students** than for traditional learners (**z = 2.29, p < .05**). Their analysis of effective scaffold types includes physical/material supports alongside peer collaboration and teacher modeling. (An earlier draft of this project cited Belland et al. as reporting "g = 0.71 for scaffolding in PBL contexts" — this number does not appear in the paper and was corrected during the Phase C re-audit; see [Phase_B_Reaudit_Log.md](Phase_B_Reaudit_Log.md).) The practical reading of Belland et al. is twofold: scaffolding works especially well in PBL, which is the program's chosen instructional frame; and persistent scaffolding works — the paper does not require that scaffolds be faded, which releases the program from the pressure to treat scaffold removal as a measure of success.

Self-management is the specific intervention category within EF scaffolding that has the strongest classroom evidence base for this population. Del Vecchio, Crosland & Blair (2025), in *Journal of Positive Behavior Interventions*, tested a technology-based self-monitoring intervention (the I-Connect app) combined with reinforcement in three upper-elementary students with or at risk for EBD. Results showed significant increases in on-task behavior for all three participants, with 100% treatment integrity and strong social validity. The practical implication: digital self-monitoring tools are feasible in classroom settings, and combining them with structured reinforcement produces measurable engagement gains. Moohr, Balint-Langel, Taylor & Rizzo (2021), in *Beyond Behavior*, describe three categories of self-regulation strategy for EBD students (self-regulated strategy development, self-monitoring, strategy instruction) and note that 64% of these interventions in their review are delivered in 1:1 or small-group settings — a format the Agourim workshop matches exactly.

Visual schedules are a specific low-cost, high-leverage scaffold worth calling out. Thomas & Karuppali (2022), in *Journal of the Korean Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry*, conducted a systematic review of visual activity schedules (VAS) for children with ADHD aged 5–12 and found that VAS interventions reduced latency to initiate tasks, reduced off-task behavior during transitions, and improved on-task behavior. The mechanism is external cueing: the picture or text tells the student what comes next without the teacher having to say it, which bypasses both verbal working memory load and teacher-student power dynamics. Task cards in the workshop are, among other things, visual activity schedules at the within-task level.

Finally — and this is one of the program's most quantitatively striking findings — physical exercise is among the most effective non-pharmacological EF interventions for ADHD, with effect sizes that exceed cognitive training and EF-specific curricula. Qiu, Liang, Wang, Zhang & Shum (2023), in *Asian Journal of Psychiatry*, conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of 67 studies of non-pharmacological EF interventions for children and adolescents with ADHD. Their headline finding: across six intervention categories (cognitive training, EF-specific curriculum, game-based training, mindfulness, neurofeedback, physical exercise), physical exercise produced **g = 0.900 on inhibitory control** and **g = 1.377 on cognitive flexibility** — the largest effect sizes of any category measured. For comparison, cognitive training produced g = 0.907 on working memory and EF-specific curriculum produced g = 0.532 on planning. The practical implication is unambiguous: *movement breaks are not an optional extra in a workshop for students with ADHD; they are a first-class EF intervention with the largest effect sizes the literature has measured for exactly the deficits this population has*. §4 Principle 6 (movement is medicine) and §5 Methodology's 3–5 minute movement break between work blocks both rest directly on this finding.

**Implications for design.** §3.2 supports Principle 1 (predictable routine protects EF from stress collapse — Cumming 2024), Principle 2 (task card as external scaffold — Rosen 2014), Principle 3 (physical-first CRA — Belland 2017a scaffolding meta-analysis with corrected numbers), Principle 4 (hyper-chunked milestones and visible wins — scaffold persistence per Belland), and Principle 6 (movement is medicine — Qiu 2023). It also informs the workshop's specific commitment to self-monitoring and visual schedules (Thomas & Karuppali 2022, Del Vecchio 2025, Moohr 2021).

### §3.3 Trauma-informed schools — with a first-class caveat

Trauma-informed approaches in schools have become a dominant framework in the last decade, and there are strong theoretical reasons to adopt them for students whose school experiences include early adversity, disrupted attachment, or explicit traumatic events. But the empirical base is weaker than the field's enthusiasm might suggest, and this section presents the honest picture rather than the cheerleading version.

Watson & Astor (2025), in *Review of Education*, published a critical review of empirical support for trauma-informed approaches in schools. **This paper is cited here deliberately and carefully.** An earlier draft of the program's principles cited Watson & Astor as if they had concluded that trauma-informed approaches are well-evidenced; a Phase C re-verification of the source file showed that Watson & Astor's actual conclusion is more nuanced. They endorse the four core components of trauma-informed practice — **understanding trauma, safety, strengths-based approaches, and supportive relationships** — as grounded in convergent evidence, but they specifically caution that *the empirical evidence base for whole-school organisational trauma-informed implementation is more limited than often claimed in the field*. They call for universal inclusion, safety, strengths-based practice, and relationships, and they ask the field to treat rigorous empirical evaluation of whole-school adoption as an open research question. The distinction between "the four components have good convergent support" and "whole-school framework adoption has robust RCT evidence" is the distinction this program takes seriously. The workshop is designed around the four components (safety through predictable routine, relationships through small-group rotation, strengths-based work through tangible competence, and staff understanding of trauma through the §5 Methodology description) without claiming to be a whole-school trauma-informed framework.

Koslouski, Stark & Chafouleas (2023), in *Social and Emotional Learning: Research, Practice, and Policy*, published a primer for educators on understanding and responding to the effects of trauma in the classroom. They describe the neurobiological reality of chronic trauma: under persistent unpredictability or threat, the brain's survival-mode response (fight, flight, or freeze) activates, and this activation disrupts executive function in exactly the areas this program is trying to protect. Their practical framing is that *trauma-informed care is beneficial for all students regardless of trauma history* — because the practices that help students with trauma histories (predictability, relationship-based safety, strengths-based framing, co-regulation) are also good practices for everyone. Koslouski et al. also specifically address secondary traumatic stress in educators, noting that educator well-being is "crucial to remaining healthy and effective" when working with traumatized students. This cluster of findings is the direct empirical basis for the program's Principle 1 (predictable routine) and its commitment to treating teacher sustainability as a first-class constraint (Principle 9).

Asselman, Offerman, Koomen, Bolling, Hoeve & Bosman (2025), in *International Journal of School & Educational Psychology*, examined the relationship between adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) and internalizing/externalizing problems in students with emotional and behavioral disorders, with PTSD symptoms and executive functioning as mediators. Their findings support the mediation model that runs through much of §3.2: adversity → PTSD symptoms → reduced EF → behavioral and emotional outcomes. The paper emphasizes the importance of trauma-informed environments that provide relational safety and co-regulation, reinforcing the same core components Watson & Astor endorse and Koslouski et al. describe.

Two scoping reviews round out the picture. Stratford, Cook, Hanneke, Katz, Seok, Steed, Fulks, Lessans & Temkin (2020), in *School Mental Health*, synthesised the range of intervention approaches being tested in schools — from individual counselling to whole-school framework adoption — and noted that rigorous studies are concentrated in Tier 2 and Tier 3 clinical interventions while universal (Tier 1) whole-school approaches have less rigorous empirical evaluation. Berger (2019), in the same journal, conducted a systematic review of multi-tiered approaches to trauma-informed care in schools and reported that multi-tiered models are theoretically well-grounded but that implementation fidelity varies widely and long-term outcome data are scarce. Beloyianni & Touloumakos (2024), also in *School Mental Health*, published a scoping review of childhood post-traumatic stress and school-related behaviors that synthesises the evidence for how PTSD manifests in school settings — internalizing and externalizing symptoms, EF difficulties, relationship ruptures — and calls for more longitudinal and mechanistic research. All three reviews are consistent with Watson & Astor's caution: the theoretical grounding is solid, the core components have convergent support, but the whole-school, organisational-level research base is not yet strong enough to justify uncritical cheerleading.

**The practical upshot for the program.** The workshop implements the four core trauma-informed components (safety through predictable routine, relationships through small-group teacher rotation, strengths-based framing through tangible hardware success, and staff understanding of trauma through the §5 Methodology description) without claiming to be a whole-school trauma-informed intervention. The claim the program makes, and that §9 Honest Limitations restates, is that *the four components are well-grounded in convergent evidence; the whole-school framework-level evidence is weaker; and the program's contribution is at the workshop level, not the school level*. This is the honest version, and it is the version that can defend itself to a reviewer who reads Watson & Astor's critical review.

**Implications for design.** §3.3 supports Principle 1 (predictable routine — Koslouski 2023, Asselman 2025, Watson & Astor 2025), Principle 5's anxiety-with-trauma sub-rule (safety and predictability first, no forced exposure — Watson & Astor 2025, Koslouski 2023, Asselman 2025), Principle 8 (relationships as the multiplier, including adult relationships as trauma-informed co-regulation — Koslouski 2023, Asselman 2025), and Principle 9 (teacher sustainability as a first-class constraint, drawing on Koslouski 2023's secondary-traumatic-stress framing alongside Gilmour 2022's burnout findings). §3.3 is also the direct source material for §9 Honest Limitations' treatment of trauma-informed approaches.

### §3.4 Differentiated instruction in secondary settings

Differentiated instruction (DI) is the pedagogical approach of proactively adapting content, process, product, learning environment, or learning time to meet students' diverse learning needs — adjusted based on ongoing assessment of each student's readiness, interest, or learning profile. It is the pedagogical frame the workshop rests on, and it has a specific and honest evidence base in secondary education.

Smale-Jacobse, Meijer, Helms-Lorenz & Maulana (2019), in a systematic review published in *Frontiers in Psychology*, synthesised the empirical literature on within-class differentiated instruction in secondary education from 2006 to 2016. After a rigorous search and selection process, only **fourteen papers about twelve unique empirical studies** met their inclusion criteria — an important finding in its own right: despite the prominence of DI in educational discourse, the empirical base in secondary settings is small. Of the twelve studies that met the bar, most showed small-to-moderate positive effects on student achievement. The authors report that **summarised effect sizes across studies range from *d* = +0.509 to +0.741** (omitting an outlier). The studies span a variety of DI operationalisations — teacher trainings for DI, ability grouping and tiering, individualisation, mastery learning, heterogeneous grouping, and remediation in flipped classroom lessons — and the authors explicitly note that the operationalisation heterogeneity makes cross-study comparison difficult.

What Smale-Jacobse et al. do not do is identify a specific mechanism by which DI produces its effects. Their review is descriptive: DI is *associated with* small-to-moderate achievement gains across a small set of secondary studies, but the review does not claim to explain *why* or to endorse a specific causal pathway. The paper concludes with an explicit call for more research: *"These empirical findings give some indication of the possible benefits of differentiated instruction. However, they also point out that there are still severe knowledge gaps. More research is needed before drawing convincing conclusions regarding the effectiveness and value of different approaches to differentiated instruction for secondary school classes."* This honest limitation is load-bearing for how the program presents DI — as a pedagogically coherent approach with directional empirical support in secondary settings, not as a settled intervention with unambiguous evidence.

Tiering by readiness is one specific DI strategy that shows up across studies. The framing the program adopts is that **tiering is not tracking** — students are not assigned fixed ability labels; they are offered the same project at three entry points and move between tiers across projects and even within a single session based on their current state. This framing is consistent with Smale-Jacobse et al.'s description of within-class differentiation as fluid adaptation rather than between-class grouping, though again the direct empirical evidence for this specific fluid-tiering model in a workshop setting is thin. The program cites Smale-Jacobse et al. to support the broader claim that DI has meaningful effects in secondary settings, and scopes the tiering-specific claims as a responsible inference from that broader base.

Two additional papers bear on DI for students with ADHD specifically. Gibbs (2023), in *Emotional and Behavioural Difficulties*, reports on a small-scale qualitative study of Australian teachers and school leaders using DI as responsive teaching for students with ADHD. Teachers in the study used DI effectively to adapt content and process and to maintain safe, secure classroom climates, but they did **not** adjust assessments for students with ADHD — a gap the author identifies as needing further teacher-education work. The practical implication for the program is that differentiation must extend through assessment (not just through content and process), and the workshop's acknowledgement that Tier 1 and Tier 3 projects should be judged against different "done when" criteria reflects this. Ziernwald, Hillmayr & Holzberger (2022), in *Journal of Advanced Academics*, reviewed DI practices for high-achieving students in mixed-ability classrooms along readiness, interest, and learning-profile axes. Their review supports the broader framing that the three dimensions of differentiation (readiness, interest, profile) all matter, and that DI need not be solely remedial — it can also extend and enrich students who arrive at the workshop already fluent in parts of the content.

**Implications for design.** §3.4 supports the tiering structure in §4 Principle 5 (structured autonomy, not forced compliance — three tiers per project, fluid movement between them) and the broader commitment to adapting process, product, and assessment to student readiness. The honest caveat that the empirical base in secondary settings is small (twelve unique studies in Smale-Jacobse et al.) is carried forward to §9 Honest Limitations.

### §3.5 Project-based learning for diverse learners

Project-based learning (PBL) is the instructional format the program is built around. PBL engages students in extended, student-centered investigations to answer a challenging question or solve a real problem, and its strongest feature for students whose school history has been difficult is that it offers **multiple means of engagement, representation, and expression** — consistent with the universal design for learning framework covered in §3.7 — and gives students meaningful voice and choice over their work.

The single most directly relevant study in the verified literature is **Taylor, Allen, Van & Moohr (2024)**, published in *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders*. It is the only empirical classroom study we could locate of PBL specifically implemented with students carrying a formal EBD identification, and its findings are striking. The authors used a multiple-baseline-across-participants single-case design with four middle-school students with EBD in an emotional/behavioral support classroom. The PBL intervention followed the Buck Institute for Education's High Quality PBL (HQPBL) framework's eight essential elements: Knowledge/Understanding/Skill, Challenge/Problem/Question, Sustained Inquiry, Authenticity, Student Voice, Reflection, Critique & Revision, and Public Product.

**On-task behavior results.** Between-case standardised mean difference (BC-SMD), analogous to Cohen's *d* and using a 0.8 threshold for a large effect, **met the criteria for a large effect** with a 95% confidence interval of **[1.70, 3.81]** and a standard error of 0.5085. Individual effect sizes for the four students were Eric = 4.70, Stanley = 7.42, Kenneth = 3.89, Kyle = 3.81 — every student showed a large positive response. Level changes in on-task behavior ranged from approximately +32 to +39 percentage points across the four students, with 96–100% variability resolution and near-complete overlap reduction.

**Teacher and paraprofessional job satisfaction.** Pre-intervention, both the teacher and paraprofessional indicated low job satisfaction on a modified Teacher Job Satisfaction Survey; at the end of the PBL unit, both reported marked improvements. The teacher's positive-question ratings moved from pre-intervention M = 3 to post-intervention M = 1.66, and the paraprofessional's from M = 4.33 to M = 2 (on a 1–5 scale where lower means more positive). The authors interpret this as preliminary evidence that PBL may serve as a protective factor against teacher burnout in EBD settings, because the authentic, student-centered nature of PBL shifts the teacher from crisis management to facilitation.

**Limitations the authors themselves acknowledge.** The paper explicitly names its limitations: single-case design limits generalisability, small sample (n = 4), limited information on fidelity across all eight HQPBL elements, reliance on a brief job-satisfaction measure rather than a validated burnout instrument, and the study was conducted in a single classroom with a single teacher and paraprofessional. These limitations matter for how the program presents Taylor et al. — the effect sizes are genuinely large, and they are the best direct evidence the program has for PBL-in-EBD classroom use, but they come from one small study and cannot carry the weight of a settled evidence base by themselves. §9 Honest Limitations restates this caveat.

The broader PBL literature adds nuance to this picture. Culclasure, Longest & Terry (2019), in *Interdisciplinary Journal of Problem-Based Learning*, published a multi-school study of PBL implementation in three Southeastern U.S. public schools across two academic years. Their finding is worth quoting honestly: *"Findings did not reveal consistent significant differences in the performances of PjBL and non-PjBL demographically matched students on academic and behavioral outcomes. PjBL students did, however, perform better on inventories of social-emotional skills."* In other words, PBL did NOT produce significant academic or behavioral outcome differences in that study — but it DID produce better social-emotional outcomes, and both students and educators reported positive perceptions of PBL's impact. This nuanced finding is important for the program's honesty: PBL is not a settled silver bullet for academic outcomes, but it has specific benefits for social-emotional development that align with the program's super-target of small positive empowering experiences.

Boardman & Coleman (2025), in *Journal of Adolescent & Adult Literacy*, published a mixed-methods study of scaffolding in project-based learning across five inclusive 9th-grade English language arts classes in an urban high school. They used a universal design for learning frame to create the PBL unit and proactively reduced potential barriers. Their practical recommendations for scaffolding in PBL are directly applicable to the workshop: **use a small number of scaffolded materials that connect activities and can be adjusted throughout the unit**, **attend to interactional scaffolds such as peer collaboration**, and **address students' diverse abilities and language backgrounds**. <!-- DRAFT NOTE: Boardman 2025 is paywalled — only the abstract was accessible during Phase A. The practical-implications paragraph is drawn directly from the ERIC abstract and publisher page. Flag in §9 Honest Limitations as abstract-only verification. -->

Sciacca (2025), in a doctoral dissertation at Wilkes University, examined teachers' experiences using project-based learning with students carrying EBD identifications. <!-- DRAFT NOTE: Sciacca 2025 is a ProQuest dissertation — only metadata and abstract were accessible; no full-text read was possible during Phase A. Do not make specific claims about Sciacca's findings beyond what the title and abstract support. Flag in §9 Honest Limitations as abstract-only. --> The dissertation's focus on teacher perspectives complements Taylor et al.'s focus on student outcomes and fills a qualitative gap in the small PBL-in-EBD literature. Together with Taylor et al. (2024), Sciacca (2025) represents the scant but growing empirical footprint of PBL work specifically with EBD students.

Condliffe, Quint, Visher, Bangser, Drohojowska, Saco & Nelson (2017), in an MDRC working paper, conducted a comprehensive literature review of PBL implementation across K–12 settings and identified structural challenges alongside the supports necessary for diverse learners to succeed. Their review emphasises that PBL implementation quality varies widely and that the benefits of PBL appear most reliably when the structural supports — clear driving questions, scaffolded inquiry, formative assessment, and explicit teaching of collaboration skills — are actually in place. The practical upshot is that *PBL is not a pedagogy-in-a-box*: the same PBL label can describe vastly different classroom realities, and outcomes depend on implementation fidelity.

**Implications for design.** §3.5 supports §4 Principle 4 (hyper-chunked milestones with visible wins — Taylor et al.'s "sustained inquiry" combined with frequent visible outcomes), Principle 5 (student voice and choice as core HQPBL elements), Principle 8 (relationships and peer collaboration as interactional scaffolds — Boardman & Coleman 2025), and Principle 9 (teacher sustainability — Taylor et al.'s preliminary job-satisfaction finding). It also frames the program's honest stance: PBL has **directional** support for this population, with strong but single-study effect sizes from Taylor et al. (2024), nuanced results from Culclasure et al. (2019), and a broader body of literature that emphasises implementation fidelity over label adoption.

### §3.6 Robotics and tangible computing for ADHD, EBD, and learning disabilities

Robotics and tangible computing are the hardware frame the program is built on, and the literature on their use with students with ADHD, EBD, and learning disabilities provides the strongest direct evidence the program has for *why* hands-on physical computing matters for this population. The theme is framed around one core idea: **structured, tangible, hands-on feedback — delivered through a system rather than a person — sustains attention, supports self-regulation, and reduces the social-threat dynamics that often derail traditional instruction with these students**.

Berrezueta-Guzmán, Robles-Bykbaev, Pau, Pesántez-Avilés & Martín-Ruiz (2021), in *IEEE Access*, published a literature review of robotic technologies supporting ADHD therapies. Their review is honest about both the promise and the limitations of the field. They classify the reviewed literature by type of therapy supported (attention training, social skills, educational/academic support), type of robot, and associated technologies. The review's headline finding is that **robotic assistants show favorable technical results in supporting attention training and task-completion activities for children with ADHD, but require significant improvements in scalability, human-machine interaction, and data processing to be applied effectively in real-world therapies**. This honesty matters: the program draws on robotic-assistance research for its "non-human mediator" framing, but it does not overclaim — the field is still exploratory, the applications are primarily therapeutic rather than educational, and the gap between laboratory proof-of-concept and classroom-ready deployment is real.

A more direct empirical finding comes from the companion study by Berrezueta-Guzmán, Pau, Martín-Ruiz & Máximo-Bocanegra (2021), also in *IEEE Access*, which presents the assessment of a Robotic Assistant (Atent@) specifically designed to support homework activities for children with ADHD. The study reports that the robotic assistant **positively influenced the homework performance of children with and without ADHD**, with the robot functioning as a monitoring and interaction tool that provides guidance, reminders, and feedback. The mechanism is what the program's Principle 2 calls *non-human mediation*: the robot's guidance is read by the student as instrumental rather than as interpersonal demand, which reduces the social-threat response some students carry into interactions with human teachers.

Cibrian, Lakes, Schuck & Hayes (2022), in *International Journal of Child-Computer Interaction*, reviewed thirty-six articles on emerging technologies (computers, mobile devices, wearables, sensors, virtual and augmented reality, and robotics) that assist and assess self-regulation of behavior in children with ADHD. <!-- DRAFT NOTE: Cibrian 2022 is paywalled — the project's access was via the CHADD research summary and Semantic Scholar metadata, not full text. Flag in §9 Honest Limitations as full-text-not-verified. --> Their review concludes that mobile health devices have shown promising results for assessing emotions, helping with organization, and enhancing self-monitoring, and that sensor-and-wearable technologies *may* detect behaviors and give feedback to support self-regulation. The authors are explicit that the research base is still emerging and that the evidence for most of these technology categories is preliminary rather than settled. For the workshop, Cibrian et al. (2022) provides directional support for the broader claim that **technology-mediated feedback helps ADHD students self-regulate**, while honestly acknowledging that this claim rests on an emerging literature.

Kuzmina & Romero (2025), in *Australian Journal of Learning Difficulties* 30(2), offer one specific observation from a tangential research context that is worth reporting on narrow grounds. Their study used the **CreaCube** task — an assessment platform built around four pre-made Cubelets modular robotic cubes (wheels, battery, sensor, inverter) that children assemble into a working autonomous vehicle — to compare divergent-thinking measures (fluency, flexibility, originality) in fifteen children aged 9–14, seven of whom had a formal ADHD diagnosis. This is **not** the kind of construction the Arduino workshop asks students to do: in CreaCube the components are pre-made, the connection logic is predetermined, and the task is a constrained assembly puzzle rather than an open design-and-build. The authors tested five quantitative hypotheses that children with ADHD would outperform neurotypical peers on divergent-thinking measures, and **all five hypotheses were NOT statistically supported** at the conventional threshold. What makes the paper worth citing at all is one behavioral observation the authors report at line 88 of their manuscript: **five out of seven children with ADHD spontaneously used a think-aloud technique during the task** — verbalizing their actions, hypotheses, and reactions — while **none of the neurotypical children exhibited this behavior**. The authors interpret this pattern in light of Corkum et al.'s (2008) earlier finding that children with ADHD produce more task-relevant external private speech than typically developing peers during problem-solving, framing it as "a productive compensatory strategy for managing cognitive load or enhancing self-regulation." For the Arduino workshop, the narrow but real implication is that *hands-on construction work elicits spontaneous verbalization as a self-regulation behavior in students with ADHD* — which is directly relevant to the workshop's small-group format and to how peer and teacher interactions during construction should be framed (§5 Methodology treats spontaneous verbalization as a feature to be welcomed, not a distraction to be silenced). We do NOT cite Kuzmina & Romero as evidence that modular robotics improves task performance or divergent thinking in children with ADHD — their own quantitative data do not support that claim.

Guneysu Ozgur, Özgür, Asselborn, Johal, Yadollahi, Bruno, Skweres & Dillenbourg (2020), in *Frontiers in Robotics and AI*, report on an iterative design of a tangible robot-assisted handwriting activity for special education. Their work illustrates how hands-on, manipulable robotic artifacts that provide immediate physical feedback support engagement for students in special-education settings, and their iterative-design methodology itself — rapid prototyping, user testing, refinement — is a model for the workshop's commitment to responsive adaptation based on how students actually use the tools.

O'Connell, Banga, Ayissi, Yaminrafie, Ko, Le, Cislowski & Mataric (2024), in the proceedings of the 2024 ACM/IEEE International Conference on Human-Robot Interaction, report on the design and evaluation of a socially assistive robot schoolwork companion for college students with ADHD. Their work extends the non-human-mediator framing to older students and academic contexts: the robot serves as a study companion that provides gentle presence, task reminders, and scaffolded prompts without the social-evaluation weight of a human study partner. The finding is relevant to the program because it shows that the basic mechanism — non-human mediation supporting task persistence in ADHD populations — extends across ages from primary school through college.

Díaz-Boladeras, Claver i Díaz & García-Sánchez (2025), in *Universal Access in the Information Society*, published a scoping review of robots for inclusive classrooms across thirty-three studies. LEGO Mindstorms kits (WeDo, NXT, EV3) were the most-used educational robotics platform, appearing in 13 of the studies. Only two of the robots reviewed were specifically developed for children with special needs; the rest were off-the-shelf educational kits adapted for inclusive use. The practical implication is that **the modular-construction robot paradigm** — students building and programming their own artifacts rather than interacting with pre-made social robots — is the dominant approach in inclusive-classroom robotics research, and it is the approach the Arduino workshop commits to.

Three framing papers complete the picture. Hwang & Taylor (2016), in *Journal of Science Education for Students with Disabilities*, frame the **concrete → representational → abstract (CRA) sequence** as a prerequisite for students with disabilities in STEM: manipulatives first, then diagrams and visual representations, then symbols and equations. The CRA sequence is the direct theoretical basis for §4 Principle 3 (physical-first, then abstract). Israel, Pearson, Tapia, Wherfel & Reese (2015), in *Computers & Education*, conducted a cross-case qualitative analysis across multiple instructional contexts and concluded that *"struggling learners, including students with disabilities and those living in poverty, benefitted from computing education that included scaffolding, modeling, and peer collaboration."* The finding is directly aligned with the program's commitment to scaffolded computational-thinking work as the entry point for every Arduino project. Álvarez Ariza & Hernández Hernández (2025), in *International Journal of Science and Mathematics Education*, published a systematic literature review of research-based interventions and strategies for students with disabilities in STEM and STEAM education, documenting the breadth of approaches but also acknowledging that accessibility and tailored adaptations for students with physical, sensory, or motor impairments remain underdeveloped.

Finally, Gomez (2019), in a doctoral dissertation at the University of Texas at San Antonio (ERIC ED604859), examined the effects of makerspace learning on the social interactions of middle-school students with emotional or behavioral disorders. The study used a single-case experimental design with four middle-school EBD students and reported that makerspace participation — grounded in constructionist principles — supported social-emotional skill development compared to traditional classroom contexts. <!-- DRAFT NOTE: Gomez 2019 is a UTSA dissertation; full text required ProQuest access that was not available during Phase A. **Implications for design.** §3.6 supports §4 Principle 2 (task card and system as non-human mediator — Berrezueta-Guzmán 2021a/b, Cibrian 2022, O'Connell 2024), Principle 3 (physical-first, then abstract / CRA sequence — Hwang & Taylor 2016, Israel et al. 2015), Principle 4 (hyper-chunked milestones with visible wins — Guneysu Ozgur 2020's iterative physical milestones), and §4 Principle 8 (relationships and social-emotional development through making — Gomez 2019). It also frames Project 8's quadcopter capstone as an extension of the modular-construction-robotics paradigm (Díaz-Boladeras 2025) into the aerial-robotics domain that §3.8 will identify as a gap in the literature.

### §3.7 Universal Design for Learning

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) is the pedagogical framework the workshop uses to express what "differentiated access" means in practice. The framework was developed by the Center for Applied Special Technology (CAST) and organises instructional design around three principles — **Representation** (multiple ways of taking in information), **Expression and Action** (multiple ways of demonstrating learning), and **Engagement** (multiple ways of motivating and sustaining learning). These three principles map to three neurologically-grounded networks: recognition, strategic, and affective.

The verified literature on UDL tells two stories at once, and the program reports both honestly.

**The supportive story.** Almeqdad, Alodat, Alquraan, Mohaidat & Al-Makhzoomy (2023), in *Cogent Education*, conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of the effectiveness of UDL in educational settings. Their inclusion criteria selected 13 empirical peer-reviewed pre-post-design studies published between 2015 and 2021 in English and Arabic. The studies spanned six countries, K–12 and higher education, both specific and generic domains, and were directed at teachers or students via professional development programmes or school interventions. **The meta-analytic total effect size was 3.56 — a large effect**, with statistical significance found for one-group studies, student participants, specific-domain studies, and quantitative research designs. The authors' own framing of this finding is important: they note **"considerable heterogeneity was evident"** across studies, and their conclusion is cautious rather than triumphant: *"UDL can improve student learning, especially in particular domains and when applied through professional development initiatives or classroom interventions. More research is required to ensure that UDL is widely applicable and to better understand how to maximize its advantages."* The large meta-analytic effect is real, but it comes from a small and heterogeneous study base, and the authors themselves call for more research before making broad claims.

Basham & Marino (2013), in *TEACHING Exceptional Children*, made the direct case for applying UDL to STEM education for students with disabilities, arguing that UDL's multiple-means framework provides the structural scaffolding needed to open STEM content to learners who cannot access it through a single instructional channel. Their framing is conceptual rather than empirical, but it grounds the program's commitment to UDL in the STEM-specific literature.

Phelan, Maguire & Finnegan (2025), in *European Journal of Education*, conducted a scoping review specifically of UDL principles embedded in subjects in secondary education — the exact level the program operates at. Their review identified that most articles were based in North America, used an intervention study approach, and reported positive results on student engagement post-intervention. They note an interesting methodological pattern: *"while all articles displayed evidence of UDL pedagogical practices, some articles did not specifically identify these practices as UDL. This could suggest that (1) an awareness of UDL as an inclusive pedagogical framework is lacking in current educational research, and/or (2) UDL may be incorporated into teaching practices more frequently than is currently being identified."* In other words, the UDL-in-secondary-education literature may be larger than the label-based literature search suggests, because teachers and researchers use UDL practices without naming them as such.

**The critical story.** Boysen (2024), in *Policy Futures in Education*, published a critical analysis of the research evidence behind CAST's UDL guidelines themselves. Boysen sampled articles that CAST cited on its UDL website as support for three specific UDL guidelines and analysed their relation to choice, learning, and brain function — the three pillars CAST uses to justify the framework. **The findings are blunt:** *"Results indicated that the cited studies provided little evidence for claims about UDL. Most of the studies did not offer a choice to learners or measure learning. None of the studies were related to brain function. Rather than supporting CAST's UDL guidelines about the choice of multiple means of learning, the studies mostly provided support for single, effective instructional techniques."* Boysen's conclusion is that the cited evidence behind CAST's UDL guidelines is **weak**, and that both basic research and implementation research are needed to establish the framework's validity and effectiveness.

These two stories are compatible, and the program presents them together honestly. **Almeqdad et al. (2023) report that when UDL-framework-inspired interventions are implemented and evaluated, they tend to produce positive effects** — a large meta-analytic effect across a small, heterogeneous base. **Boysen (2024) reports that the specific research evidence CAST invokes to justify the guidelines themselves does not cleanly support the framework's neuroscience claims or its choice-and-multiple-means claims** — the implemented practices work, but the framework's theoretical foundations rest on weaker evidence than CAST's presentation suggests. The honest reading is that UDL is **a coherent pedagogical framework with empirically-supported components and a weaker theoretical foundation** — the three principles work as a design checklist even if the neuroscience justification is less solid than the CAST website implies. The workshop uses UDL as a design checklist and a shared vocabulary with other special-education colleagues, not as a neuroscience-of-learning theory.

CAST (2018), *Universal Design for Learning Guidelines version 2.2*, is the canonical guidelines document the program uses operationally. The CAST guidelines themselves are a design reference, not an empirical claim — they are what a teacher consults when designing a lesson, not what a reviewer consults as evidence. The program cites CAST 2018 as the source for the three principles and the nine sub-guidelines that structure how Representation, Expression and Action, and Engagement get operationalised into practice.

**Implications for design.** §3.7 supports the program's use of UDL's three principles as a design checklist for every project. The workshop's three-level Claude Code integration (upload / modify / dialogue) is an Expression and Action differentiation. The task card format and visual milestone system are Representation differentiations. The choice-and-voice design in Principle 5 is an Engagement differentiation. §3.7 also carries forward to §9 Honest Limitations the Boysen (2024) caveat that UDL's theoretical foundation is weaker than the framework's prominence might suggest — the program uses UDL practically without overclaiming its scientific grounding.

### §3.8 What is NOT in the literature

This section is the most important honest caveat in §3. The themes above (§3.1–§3.7) describe what the verified literature *does* say; this section describes what it does *not*. Three structural gaps in the research base are load-bearing for how the program presents itself and for what it can and cannot claim.

**Gap 1: no published empirical research on Arduino or microcontroller-level physical computing for students with EBD, ADHD, or learning disabilities.**

We searched the full-text corpus for mentions of Arduino, ESP32, Raspberry Pi, breadboards, circuit design, electronics pedagogy, and student-built microcontroller projects as intervention contexts for neurodivergent secondary students. The corpus contains substantial research on robotics in special education — but the robotics in that research is almost entirely **pre-made** or **modular platform-based**: LEGO Mindstorms (the most-used platform in Díaz-Boladeras et al.'s 2025 scoping review, appearing in 13 of 33 studies), Cubelets (the platform in Kuzmina & Romero 2025), NAO and Pepper humanoids (reviewed in Papakostas's descriptive review and elsewhere), Bee-Bots, Dash, KASPAR, and similar educational robotics kits. None of the studies in the verified corpus examine students **building** a microcontroller-based project from components — wiring breadboards, writing their own code, soldering connections, debugging hardware-and-software interactions together. This is the gap the program's core technology choice sits in.

The gap is not a rejection of the program's approach — it is an honest acknowledgement that **the evidence the program is extending into Arduino territory comes from adjacent literatures**: LEGO/modular robotics (Díaz-Boladeras 2025), tangible computing (Guneysu Ozgur 2020), makerspace learning (Gomez 2019), computational thinking for diverse learners (Israel et al. 2015), scaffolded STEM meta-analysis (Belland et al. 2017), and hands-on robot-assisted ADHD interventions (Berrezueta-Guzmán 2021a, 2021b; Cibrian et al. 2022). The program's design decisions are grounded in these adjacent evidence bases, and the inferential extension into Arduino is made explicit in §9 Honest Limitations and acknowledged as territory that would benefit from direct empirical evaluation.

**Gap 2: no published research on aerial robotics, drones, or flight-STEM for any special-needs population.**

Project 8 of the program is a build-from-scratch ESP32-based tiny quadcopter capstone. We explicitly searched all 78 files in the full-text corpus for any mention of drones, quadcopters, UAVs, multirotors, aerial robots, flying robots, aircraft, flight (in an educational context), propellers, rotors, or hovering. **The search returned one hit — a metaphorical usage in the CAST (2018) Universal Design for Learning guidelines' scaffolding-as-apprenticeship discussion.** Otherwise, the corpus contains zero substantive research on aerial robotics, drone construction, flight-STEM pedagogy, or quadcopter-based learning for any population — neurotypical or neurodivergent.

Project 8 therefore rests on an **inferential extension** from four adjacent evidence bases: (a) ground robotics for ADHD/EBD (Berrezueta-Guzmán 2021a, 2021b; Cibrian et al. 2022; O'Connell et al. 2024), (b) modular and tangible student-built STEM (Guneysu Ozgur et al. 2020; Israel et al. 2015), (c) scaffolded computational thinking (Belland et al. 2017; Hwang & Taylor 2016), and (d) makerspace learning for EBD (Gomez 2019). The quadcopter itself adds complexity, safety stakes, and motivational weight that the ground-based evidence can only partially inform. §9 Honest Limitations acknowledges this explicitly, and Project 8's design includes dedicated safety protocols that go beyond what the ground-based literature would require. The program does not claim that research directly supports quadcopter PBL for EBD students — it claims that Project 8 is a **responsible extension** from adjacent evidence, and that its outcomes should be evaluated with appropriate humility.

**Gap 3: no published research on AI-collaborative coding tools for neurodivergent students.**

The program uses Claude Code as a first-class differentiation support (§4 Principle 7) with three access levels — pre-written code upload, guided modification with AI help, and free dialogue with the AI. This design is novel, and the verified corpus contains **zero** studies of Claude Code, conversational AI tutors, AI-paired coding, or similar tools specifically for students with EBD, ADHD, OCD, anxiety, or related profiles. The absence is in part structural: Claude Code and tools like it are new enough that a research literature has not yet had time to develop around them, and the empirical work that does exist on AI tutoring for neurodivergent students is preliminary and concentrated on earlier-generation systems.

The program's Claude Code integration is therefore a **piloted practice** rather than an evidence-based intervention. The three-level scaffolding is grounded in adjacent research on external cognitive scaffolds (Rosen, Boyle, Cariss & Forchelli 2014), on the importance of matching support level to student readiness (the broader differentiation literature in §3.4), and on the specific ADHD finding that structured external support can enable — rather than undermine — competence-building (Smith, Langberg, Cusick, Green & Becker 2020). But the specific combination — differentiated AI access levels within an Arduino workshop for EBD students — has no direct empirical precedent. §4 Principle 7 flags this explicitly, and §9 Honest Limitations restates it as one of the program's most significant evidence-thin commitments. The program is willing to pilot Claude Code integration carefully because the theoretical case is coherent and the adjacent evidence is directionally supportive, but it does not overclaim empirical grounding the literature does not provide.

**Two additional gaps worth naming briefly.**

First, **research on small-group project-based learning in special-education settings is thin**. Most PBL studies are whole-class inclusive or 1:1 intervention designs; the program's small-group-of-3-to-8 format matches Taylor et al.'s (2024) single study's scale exactly but is not strongly represented elsewhere. The program's group-size decisions rest on Taylor et al. and on the relational-multiplier finding from Gersib & Mason (2023), plus the practical constraint that Agourim workshops run at this scale regardless of what the literature says.

Second, **research on failure, iteration, and productive struggle in PBL for students with EBD is scarce**. How should debugging failures, project setbacks, or peer criticism be scaffolded for students whose emotional-regulation reserves are limited? The verified corpus does not answer this directly, and the program's "stuck protocol" in §5 Methodology is built from adjacent evidence on self-management (Del Vecchio et al. 2025; Moohr et al. 2021) and from the broader framing that students with ADHD benefit from structured external support (Smith, Langberg et al. 2020) — not from direct empirical work on how to handle project failure for this population.

**The overall honest framing.** The program stands on the shoulders of adjacent research, and §9 Honest Limitations makes this position explicit. Where the literature directly supports a design choice, the program says so and cites it. Where the literature is inferentially extended, the program says so and names the extension. Where the literature is silent or thin, the program acknowledges the gap and describes the design choice as piloted practice requiring careful evaluation rather than as evidence-based intervention. This honesty is the program's defence against a reviewer who knows the literature well — and it is the program's commitment to the academic integrity the evaluator-and-funder audience is entitled to expect.

## §4 Design Principles

§3 reviewed what the research literature says. §4 is the distillation — the nine design principles that every project in the program embodies and that every methodology choice in §5 follows. These principles are the bridge between the evidence and the practice.

Each principle is presented in four parts:

- **Statement** — the principle in one or two sentences.
- **Evidence** — a compact pointer to the §3 themes that support it. §3 carries the full citations; §4 is the summary.
- **Design rule** — the concrete translation into how Arduino projects are built and run.
- **Applied in** — where in §5 Methodology and in the 8 projects (§6) the principle shows up operationally.

A reader who has already read §3 can move through §4 quickly. A reader who jumped straight to §4 (for example, a workshop teacher at another school looking for the adoptable design rules) gets enough context to understand each principle and its justification without having to backtrack. §9 Honest Limitations returns to Principle 7 specifically, because it is the principle with the weakest direct empirical support.

### Principle 1 — Predictable routine is load-bearing for executive function

**Statement.** Every session follows the same rhythm. Routine is not decoration — it is the structural beam that protects executive function from stress-collapse and lets students arrive ready to work with minimum anxiety.

**Evidence.** §3.2 on perceived stress, executive function, and the Cumming et al. (2024) finding that stress directly degrades EF (β ≈ .61 for language arts, β ≈ .65 for math) and EF in turn mediates academic performance. §3.3 on trauma-informed practice, where Koslouski, Stark & Chafouleas (2023) describe the neurobiological survival-mode response that unpredictability triggers, and Watson & Astor (2025) endorse safety and predictability as two of the four well-grounded trauma-informed components. §3.1 on Harrison, Soares, Rudzinski & Johnson's (2019) finding that instructional and self-management interventions — both of which depend on predictable environmental structure — are the two classroom intervention categories meeting What Works Clearinghouse evidence-based standards for ADHD. CICO studies (Flannery et al. 2024; Kittelman et al. 2024) converge from a different angle, showing that predictable daily check-in routines improve on-task behavior in high-school EBD settings.

**Design rule.**
- Every workshop session follows the same seven-phase structure: Arrival & Board Check → Mini-Huddle → Work Block 1 → Movement Break → Work Block 2 → Clean-Up → Close-Out Circle. The structure is described in detail in §5 Methodology.
- No surprises. Any deviation from routine is announced at the Mini-Huddle with an explicit reason.
- Every project's task cards live at predictable physical locations — same place on the workbench, same color-coded clip — so students never have to hunt for their work.
- Every project begins with a predictable recap: "Here's where you left off; here's what you're doing next."

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (session structure), every project in §6 (shared session rhythm), and the "fluctuating capacity" framing in §2 Population Profile that explains why routine is protective rather than restrictive.

### Principle 2 — The task card is the boss, not the teacher

**Statement.** Visual task cards, checklists, and reference sheets guide students through work. The teacher rotates, supports, and celebrates — but does not give verbal multi-step instructions or direct commands. This bypasses the power-struggle dynamics that derail students with ODD-flavored profiles, it bypasses the working-memory load of holding verbal sequences in mind, and it lets students work independently so the teacher can focus on whoever needs help most.

**Evidence.** §3.1 on Barker & de Lugt (2022) framing ODD as an interactional disorder where the specific person–student interaction shapes the response, and on their empirical review showing environmental modifications and self-monitoring produce strong positive effects. §3.2 on Rosen, Boyle, Cariss & Forchelli (2014) showing external cognitive scaffolds open pathways for students with EF deficits to practice EF *through* the scaffold — students don't need intact EF to use the scaffold, and using it is how EF improves. §3.2 also on Thomas & Karuppali's (2022) systematic review of visual activity schedules for ADHD, which reduce task-initiation latency and reduce off-task behavior during transitions. §3.6 on the pattern across multiple robotics studies (Berrezueta-Guzmán et al. 2021a, 2021b; Cibrian et al. 2022) that structured, non-human feedback sustains task engagement for ADHD children — the "non-human mediator" pattern aggregated across constituent studies.

**Design rule.**
- Every step of every project lives on a Task Card (template and rationale in §5 Methodology).
- Teachers never give verbal multi-step instructions. Instead: "Check your next card." Or: "What does the card say the next step is?"
- When a student gets stuck, the teacher points at the card's "Stuck? Try this first" protocol before offering a direct hint.
- Task cards are laminated and placed at each workstation. For the first project in any student's trajectory, the teacher walks them through the card format once and then stops explaining — the card does the rest.
- Corrections come from the hardware, not from the teacher, wherever possible: "Your LED isn't lighting up — check the card. What does the checkpoint say?"

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (Task Card System, Stuck Protocol, Teacher Rotation), and every project in §6 (every milestone is a task card).

### Principle 3 — Physical-first, then abstract (concrete → representational → abstract)

**Statement.** Students build the wiring before they write the code. They see the hardware do something before they abstract it. The concrete → representational → abstract sequence is not optional for students with learning disabilities and attention challenges — they cannot skip the concrete phase.

**Evidence.** §3.6 on Hwang & Taylor (2016) framing the concrete-representational-abstract sequence as a prerequisite for students with disabilities in STEM. §3.6 on Israel, Pearson, Tapia, Wherfel & Reese (2015) showing that struggling learners — including students with disabilities — benefit from computing education that includes explicit scaffolding, modeling, and peer collaboration grounded in concrete experiences. §3.2 on Belland, Walker, Kim & Lefler (2017) meta-analysis showing scaffolded STEM instruction produces an overall effect of g = 0.46, with significantly larger effects for underperforming students (z = 2.29, p < .05) and directionally stronger effects in project-based learning contexts than in problem-based learning. §3.6 on the robotics-in-ADHD studies (Berrezueta-Guzmán et al. 2021a, 2021b; Cibrian et al. 2022) showing hands-on tangible feedback supports self-regulation by giving students the effect of their action before they need to explain it.

**Design rule.**
- Every project begins with a wiring-only milestone: breadboard and wires before any code is written. Students see the circuit and understand what it does physically.
- The first observable outcome of any project is physical — an LED lights, a motor spins, a buzzer sounds. Code comes after the hardware works.
- When students write their first code for a project, they are modifying pre-written scaffold code that already matches their wiring. They never start from a blank editor.
- Code explanations reference the hardware concretely: *"This line turns pin 13 HIGH, which sends voltage to the wire that goes to your LED's long leg."*
- For Project 8 (the quadcopter capstone), the concrete → representational → abstract progression becomes: frame assembly (concrete) → wiring diagram tracing (representational) → Claude-Code-assisted firmware generation (abstract).

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (project sequencing — every project has a wiring-first milestone), every project in §6 (hardware list and first milestone are always physical), and Principle 7's Level 1 (pre-written code upload as the most concrete entry point into the abstraction of code).

### Principle 4 — Hyper-chunked 15-minute milestones with visible wins

**Statement.** Every step of every project produces a concrete, observable outcome within 10–15 minutes. No silent work periods longer than that. No *"and then you'll see the result tomorrow."* Every 15 minutes, something in the real world changes because the student did something — a light turns on, a motor spins, a sensor reads a value, a number on the screen changes. This is the dopamine drip that sustains ADHD engagement, builds momentum for students with depression, and creates evidence of competence for students whose school history has taught them they cannot do things.

**Evidence.** §3.5 on Taylor, Allen, Van & Moohr (2024), the one empirical PBL-in-EBD-classroom study in the verified corpus, which reports very large on-task effect sizes (BC-SMD 95% CI [1.70, 3.81]) in a PBL unit built around sustained inquiry combined with frequent visible milestones. §3.6 on Guneysu Ozgur et al. (2020) showing iterative physical milestones with tangible robot-assisted output support engagement and reduce frustration in special education. §3.6 on Berrezueta-Guzmán et al. (2021) and Cibrian et al. (2022) identifying hands-on tangible feedback as a core mechanism supporting attention span and task-completion rates in children with ADHD. §3.2 on Belland et al. (2017) meta-analysis supporting persistent — rather than faded — scaffolding: students don't need big leaps, they need many small supported wins.

**Design rule.**
- Every task card maps to ≤ 15 minutes of work with an observable "done when" checkpoint.
- Long steps in the Project 8 source material (for example, *"Power Wiring — 15 min"*) are subdivided into 3–5 micro-tasks of 4–8 minutes each, each with its own physical checkpoint.
- No project has a silent middle where students work for 20+ minutes with nothing visible happening. If the natural work unit is that long, it must be broken or scaffolded with an intermediate indicator — a serial-monitor print, an LED status, a buzzer beep — that shows progress.
- Every milestone completion is celebrated with visible progress tracking: a checkmark, a clip moved to the next position, a sticker on the tracking sheet, or just a fist bump when the teacher rotates past.

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (Task Card System with "done when" criteria, simple tracking sheet), and every project in §6 (each project is structured as a sequence of 15-minute micro-milestones per tier).

### Principle 5 — Structured autonomy, not forced compliance

**Statement.** Every project offers at least one genuine choice point, even at Tier 1. *"Do you want a red or blue LED?" "Which sensor — ultrasonic or IR?" "What should we name your robot?"* Choice reduces oppositional resistance, increases intrinsic motivation, and respects student agency. But structure and choice are partners, not opposites: external structure (checklists, scaffolds, reinforcement, pre-built templates) is enabling, not undermining, for ADHD — it creates the safe envelope inside which autonomy becomes possible.

**Autonomy applies across projects, not just within them.** The same logic extends to the order and timing of projects across the program. For students whose school history includes many encounters with prescribed sequences they had no voice in, the refusal to follow the prescribed eight-project order is itself an expression of the very autonomy this principle protects. The program reframes prerequisites as **skills to acquire**, not **projects to complete in sequence**: a student who wants to start with a later project can do so, and the teacher scaffolds the missing skills just-in-time with targeted mini-modules rather than blocking the student at the doorway. A student's refusal to engage with a specific project on a specific day is treated as information about what they need — *"tell me what would make today worth your time"* — not as defiance to be corrected. §5 Methodology contains the detailed teacher guidance for this case.

**Evidence.** §3.5 on Taylor et al. (2024) HQPBL framework explicitly listing Student Voice as one of the eight essential elements and attributing the large on-task effect sizes in an EBD classroom to PBL's choice-and-authenticity design. §3.1 on Billingsley, Thomas & Webber (2018) showing that when students with comorbid learning and emotional/behavioral disabilities chose *how* they received instruction, engagement and task completion improved. §3.1 on Barker & de Lugt (2022) reviewing empirical ODD classroom interventions and supporting structured environmental modifications. §3.1 on Smith, Langberg, Cusick, Green & Becker (2020) showing that adolescents with ADHD have lower intrinsic motivation (d = 0.49) and lower extrinsic motivation (d = 0.43) than peers — but that extrinsic motivation paradoxically supports homework completion and GPA for ADHD students, meaning structured external support is not undermining but enabling. §3.1 on Morsink et al. (2022) reviewing two studies (Carlson et al. 2000; Carlson & Tamm 2000) that explicitly tested whether external rewards undermine intrinsic motivation in children with ADHD and found **no undermining effect**. §3.4 on the broader differentiated-instruction literature (Smale-Jacobse et al. 2019) supporting tiering by readiness in secondary settings.

**Design rule.**
- Every project has at least one genuine choice point at Tier 1 (color, name, one parameter).
- Every project has 2–3 choice points at Tier 2 (component selection, design decision, output behavior).
- Tier 3 of every project is an open-design brief with hard constraints (hardware budget, time, safety) but free creative direction.
- **Recognition within the workshop is immediate and tangible** — the teacher rotating past and acknowledging a working build, a visible checkmark on a task card, the hardware itself responding — rather than bureaucratic and deferred. This is **not** because the research says token economies are bad for this population. The verified research actually supports classroom token economies for students with ODD and ADHD: Barker & de Lugt (2022) include Rosenberg's (1986) study in their evidence table showing a token economy produced M PND = 82% for on-task behavior and M PND = 91% for reducing disruptive behavior in an ODD alternative classroom; Morsink et al.'s (2022) review found no undermining effect of rewards on intrinsic motivation in children with ADHD. The workshop's choice to use social/immediate/tangible recognition is instead grounded in three workshop-specific fits: **(a)** hardware-based milestones are already inherently tangible — a motor spinning is its own reward signal — so layering a token on top adds little; **(b)** a small-group, rotating-teacher setting has capacity for immediate personal acknowledgement in a way larger classrooms may not; **(c)** Agourim already operates a school-wide token-economy behavioural program across most aspects of daily school activity, and the workshop continues to operate within that school-wide system rather than adding a second parallel workshop-level token layer. The within-workshop recognition mechanism described here operates alongside the school-wide token economy, not instead of it.
- **Project order is not a rigid gate.** Prerequisites are skills, not sequence positions. Students may work on the eight projects in any order; when a student skips ahead, the teacher delivers missing prerequisite skills just-in-time via short targeted mini-modules rather than blocking the student at the doorway of the desired project. A student's refusal to do a specific project on a specific day is diagnostic, not defiant — the teacher's first response is *"tell me what would make today worth your time,"* and the conversation itself is the intervention.
- **Population profile sub-rules** (these are adaptive guidance, not diagnosis-based prescriptions):
  - For **OCD-related presentations**: private workspace by default; never forced public demonstration; task cards with clear "done when" criteria to prevent perfectionism spirals; flexible timing with no *"you must finish this before X."* These design choices are inferred from the documented burden of pediatric OCD (Abramovitch et al. 2024 report that approximately 90% of affected children experience impairment in at least one domain, with school as the most-affected domain) and from Colbert et al.'s (2025) finding that school-staff knowledge of OCD-specific accommodations is limited. Neither paper prescribes these specific workshop design rules — they document the problem the design is trying to address.
  - For **anxiety without trauma history**: scaffold approaches to feared tasks rather than permanent avoidance accommodation. Håland & Bertelsen (2025) document that teacher accommodations correlate with increased student avoidance and anxiety severity and warn that accommodations can *"inadvertently intensify or prolong the anxiety over time"* when they interfere with exposure-based treatment principles. Their finding is correlational, not a causal demonstration, but it is strong enough to make long-term avoidance accommodation a design concern worth naming.
  - For **anxiety with trauma history**: safety and predictability first; no forced exposure; use Principle 1 (predictable routine) as the primary anxiety management mechanism, not exposure. Watson & Astor (2025), Koslouski, Stark & Chafouleas (2023), and Asselman et al. (2025) on trauma-informed environments.
  - For **ODD-flavored profiles**: frame tasks as choices or requests, never as commands. *"When you're ready, your next card is here."*
  - For **depression-related profiles**: smallest possible first step; visible tangible results; celebrate micro-progress privately.

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (Tier System, Choice Point Protocol, Refusal-of-Order Subsection), and every project in §6 (Tier 1/2/3 structure with explicit choice points).

### Principle 6 — Movement is medicine for ADHD (not a reward, not a release valve)

**Statement.** Every session includes a structured movement break. This is not an *"optional energy release"* for students who *"can't sit still"* — it is a first-class intervention with the largest effect sizes in the ADHD literature for exactly the deficits this population has.

**Evidence.** §3.2 on Qiu, Liang, Wang, Zhang & Shum (2023) meta-analysis of 67 non-pharmacological EF intervention studies for ADHD finding that **physical exercise produced the largest effect sizes of all intervention types measured**: g = 0.900 on inhibitory control, g = 1.377 on cognitive flexibility. For comparison, cognitive training produced g = 0.907 on working memory and EF-specific curriculum produced g = 0.532 on planning. Physical exercise is not just competitive with other interventions — it is the best single intervention the corpus has measured for ADHD executive function. §3.2 on Cumming et al. (2024, both papers) independently showing that EF (especially cognitive flexibility) degrades under stress, and that movement is a direct stress intervention.

**Design rule.**
- The session structure includes a 3–5 minute Movement Break between Work Block 1 and Work Block 2. This break is **non-negotiable and applies to every session**, even when a student is in flow state. (Especially when a student is in flow state — the break protects the next hour's flow.)
- Movement breaks are structured, not free-for-all: stand up, get water, walk around the room once, stretch for 30 seconds. Structured because unstructured breaks can spike dysregulation for students with ODD-flavored or trauma-related profiles.
- Kinesthetic elements are embedded in task cards where possible. *"Walk to the parts bin and get a 220Ω resistor"* is better than *"reach for the resistor."* Test steps involve standing: *"Stand up, hold the drone, and spin the propeller with your finger — does it turn freely?"*

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (session structure, movement break as mandatory phase), and every project in §6 (test steps involve physical action wherever natural).

### Principle 7 — Claude Code as a differentiated coding support, not a one-size-fits-all tool

**Statement.** Students engage with code at the level that is actually approachable for them today, and that level is allowed to differ across students and across sessions. At the most supported end, a student uploads a pre-written sketch that matches their wiring and watches the hardware respond — coding is not yet a barrier because they are not yet being asked to code. At the middle level, a student modifies a working sketch with Claude Code's help, making small targeted changes and seeing what happens. At the most autonomous end, a student describes what they are trying to build, shows Claude Code their current attempt, and iterates in dialogue. Across all three levels, the AI and the pre-written code are scaffolds the student uses, not obstacles the student has to overcome alone. Critically, students who are ready for dialogue describe the problem before asking for a solution — the AI is a collaborator they direct, not an answer machine they consume.

**Why three levels, not one.** Claude Code itself can be intimidating or overwhelming for some students, particularly those whose prior school experience has trained them to expect AI and computers to be sources of frustration and failure. Forcing a student who is not ready to describe their problem in English into an AI dialogue is the same mistake as forcing a student who cannot yet read fluently to read aloud in front of peers — it turns a scaffold into a source of shame. The three levels mirror the project-tier structure: Tier 1 (pre-written upload) gives students who need maximum support a path to tangible success; Tier 2 (modify with AI help) is the default for most students most of the time; Tier 3 (free dialogue) is for students who have earned confidence in both the code and the AI. Students move between levels fluidly, without shame.

**Evidence.** §3.8 flagged this principle as **the weakest-supported in the program** — there is no direct empirical research in the verified corpus on Claude Code, conversational AI tutors, or AI-paired coding for neurodivergent students, and §9 Honest Limitations restates this explicitly. The principle rests on responsible inference from three adjacent evidence bases: §3.2 on Rosen, Boyle, Cariss & Forchelli (2014), where external cognitive scaffolds enable students to practice EF they don't yet have and where both pre-written code and a well-designed AI prompt scaffold function as external cognitive scaffolds; §3.1 on Morsink et al. (2022), whose review cuts both ways — an AI that gives answers on demand becomes an extrinsic reward system and an AI that asks questions back becomes a scaffold, so dialogue-level AI use requires the student to describe the problem first, but the same logic also argues against forcing AI dialogue on a student who is not ready for it (the pre-written upload level exists precisely to avoid that failure mode); and §3.1 on Smith, Langberg, Cusick, Green & Becker (2020) showing that extrinsic support helps academic outcomes when it structures rather than replaces the student's own work.

**Design rule.**
- Every project provides all three Claude Code interaction levels explicitly: a **pre-written sketch** (Level 1) that students can upload as-is and see the hardware respond; a **partially-filled sketch with guided modification targets** (Level 2) where Claude Code prompt scaffolds walk the student through specific small changes; and **open-ended prompt templates** (Level 3) for students ready to describe new ideas to Claude Code and iterate.
- Task cards for each milestone indicate what Level 1 / 2 / 3 looks like at that step, so students know which entry point is available to them.
- At Level 2 and Level 3, students describe the problem before asking for help. Task cards say: *"Before asking Claude, fill in: (a) what you want to happen, (b) what's currently happening, (c) what you've tried."*
- At Level 2 and Level 3, a quick comprehension check follows any AI interaction: *"After Claude helps you, can you explain in one sentence what changed?"*
- At Level 1, the comprehension check is softer and scoped to observable behavior: *"What does your device do right now that it did not do before you uploaded the code?"*
- Students can move up (Level 1 → 2 → 3) within a session or across sessions, and can also move down without shame. A student having a hard day can drop from Level 3 to Level 1 for that session and return to Level 3 next week.
- For Project 8 (the quadcopter capstone), the quadcopter source material's firmware file is currently an empty stub, with the real firmware living as pseudocode in the project README. This is actually a feature of the three-level model: **Level 1** students upload a simpler pre-written motor-test sketch that proves the electronics work (no stabilization), and **Level 2/3** students use Claude Code to generate the full PID-stabilized `.ino` from the README pseudocode. Even within the most complex project in the program, the three-level model applies.

**Honest caveat.** This is the program's most evidence-thin principle, and §9 Honest Limitations treats it as a **piloted practice** rather than an evidence-based intervention. The three-level differentiated model has no direct precedent in published research — it is the program's own design response to the heterogeneity of the student population, grounded in adjacent evidence but not directly empirically tested. A reviewer who asks *"where is the research that shows this works?"* gets an honest answer: it doesn't exist yet; we are piloting carefully; the design is inferentially grounded; the practice will be evaluated in use.

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (Claude Code integration protocol, three-level access structure), every project in §6 (every project specifies Level 1 / Level 2 / Level 3 entry points), and §9 Honest Limitations (the most prominent caveat in the whole document).

### Principle 8 — The teacher–student relationship is the multiplier

**Statement.** Structure alone does not work. Structure combined with a trustworthy adult presence works. The **teacher–student relationship** is the relational mechanism this program commits to as its first-class infrastructure: consistent adult presence, relational check-ins before task progress, greeting by name, listening without problem-solving, and the visible, formal support that comes from a named adult who knows what each student is working on. The relationship is the soil in which independent work grows, and the program's version of that soil is the teacher's attention — not peer collaboration, at least in the current version of the program (the appendix on deferred peer collaboration explains what the program *does not yet* do and why).

**Evidence.** §3.1 on Gersib & Mason (2023) meta-analysis of behavior interventions in self-contained EBD classrooms, which reported a large overall effect size (g = 0.93, SE = 0.16) with **relational components as the strongest moderator** (B = 1.26, p < .05). Gersib & Mason's relational components include parent communication, peer support, and adult mentoring — this program's version of "relational components" is specifically the **adult mentoring** component (teacher rotation with relational check-ins, consistent caring adult presence in every session, the Arrival and Close-Out rituals). The program does not claim Gersib's full moderator effect for itself — we are implementing one of the three components the moderator describes, and it is honest to say so. §3.1 on Gage, Kramer II & Ellis (2021) analysing school-climate data from 350,000+ high-school students (including 5,000+ with or at risk for EBD) and finding that EBD students with IEP services perceived more positive school climates than EBD students without IEP services in several domains — formal, visible adult support improves sense of belonging, which is specifically the mechanism this principle relies on. §3.3 on Koslouski, Stark & Chafouleas (2023) and Asselman et al. (2025) on trauma-informed environments emphasising relationship quality, trust, and adult co-regulation as the mechanism through which students develop resilience and self-regulation. §3.6 on Gomez (2019) — the closest direct analogue in the verified corpus to the Arduino workshop — found that makerspace participation supported social-emotional skill development for middle-school EBD students; notably, Gomez's mechanism was **peer interaction** during makerspace work, which is specifically a mechanism this program does not implement as a default. This is a genuine gap between Gomez's finding and the program's current design, and §9 Honest Limitations restates it.

**Design rule.**
- Workshop sessions open with **Arrival & Board Check** — greet each student by name, brief one-word check-in — and close with a **Close-Out Circle** where each student says one thing they worked on or got stuck on. These are not optional social filler; they are the relationship infrastructure that Principle 8 rests on.
- Teacher rotation during work blocks includes relational check-ins, not just task progress: *"How are you doing with this?"* before *"Where are you on the card?"* A 5-second relational check-in signals to the student that the teacher sees them as a person first, which is the trust infrastructure on which all task work rests.
- The teacher knows each student's current project, current tier, current task card, and recent history — and shows that knowledge in the first sentence of every rotation. *"Last session you got the LED to blink — how did that feel?"* is a teacher move the program asks for explicitly.
- **Every student works on their own project, at their own workstation, at their own tier, at their own pace.** The workshop is a small-group physical setting (three to eight students in the same room, sharing a parts library and tools) but the work is individual. Peer collaboration is **not** a first-class mechanism in this version of the program — see the appendix for the reasoning and for what revisiting this decision later would look like.
- **Trust accumulates across sessions.** A student who had a hard day last week gets the same greeting this week. A student who refused Project 3 last session gets the same friendly opening line at next session's Arrival phase. The relationship is durable across hard sessions, not contingent on the student having a good day.

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (Arrival & Board Check, Close-Out Circle, Teacher Rotation with Relational Check-ins), every project in §6 (each project is designed for individual student work with teacher rotation), and the appendix on deferred peer collaboration.

### Principle 9 — Teacher sustainability is a first-class design constraint

**Statement.** The program must be designed so one teacher managing 3–8 students can run a session without burning out. Pre-prepared materials. Self-directed task cards. Laminated reference sheets. Clear session structure. Predictable rhythms. If the teacher is exhausted, the whole system fails — and student outcomes collapse first.

**Evidence.** §3.1 on Gilmour, Sandilos, Pilny, Schwartz & Wehby (2022) identifying three teacher profiles (flourishing, buffered, struggling) among teachers of students with EBD and reporting that burnout profile is meaningfully related to observed classroom management quality. §3.5 on Taylor, Allen, Van & Moohr (2024), the only study in the corpus that measured teacher job satisfaction as a PBL outcome, reporting marked improvements from pre-intervention to post-intervention in both teacher and paraprofessional job-satisfaction ratings — preliminary evidence that well-designed PBL can actually *reduce* teacher burden in EBD contexts by shifting the teacher's role from crisis manager to facilitator. §3.3 on Koslouski, Stark & Chafouleas (2023) explicitly addressing secondary traumatic stress in educators and calling educator well-being *"crucial to remaining healthy and effective"* when working with students carrying trauma histories. §3.1 on Colbert et al. (2025) showing that brief written psychoeducation for staff outperforms long workshops — a finding that shapes the kind of implementation support the program is built around.

**Design rule.**
- Every project ships with **pre-prepared teacher materials**: printed task cards ready to laminate, a setup checklist for before students arrive, a rotation plan suggesting which students to start with, a troubleshooting crib sheet.
- No prep work should require the teacher to write from scratch during a session.
- Projects are scoped so they can be run the first time by a teacher who has read the master document once — no additional training required. (Colbert et al.'s finding that brief written materials outperform long workshops directly supports this.)
- The session structure is itself a teacher-sustainability mechanism — predictable rhythms reduce the cognitive load of deciding what happens next.
- The Claude Code prompt scaffolds (Principle 7) are also a teacher-sustainability mechanism — the teacher is not the only debugging resource in the room.
- Budget implication: the program's §8 Budget includes line items for teacher release time, a lightweight between-session check-in protocol, and printable materials — not a heavy training-and-coaching apparatus.

**Applied in.** §5 Methodology (pre-prepared materials, lightweight tracking sheet, troubleshooting crib sheets), every project in §6 (each project file includes a teacher-facing pre-flight checklist), and §8 Budget (explicit line items for teacher-sustainability infrastructure).

## §5 Methodology — How the Workshop Runs

§4 described the nine principles that guide the design. §5 describes how those principles become a workshop that actually runs on a Tuesday morning with three to eight students walking in the door.

This section is operational. A teacher who has read §5 should be able to set up the workshop, run a first session, and know what to do when things go wrong. Every subsection below points back at the principle or principles it implements, so a reader can trace a design choice from "why" (§4) to "how" (§5) without guessing.

§5 is organized into nine subsections:

1. **The workshop setting** — group size, duration, frequency, physical space
2. **Session structure** — the seven-phase rhythm
3. **The Task Card System** — template, rationale, stuck protocol
4. **The three-tier system** — guided build / guided design / open design
5. **Claude Code as a differentiated coding support** — the three-level access model in operation
6. **Safety protocols** — general workshop safety and the Project 8 flight-safety escalation
7. **When a student refuses the prescribed order** — teacher guidance and a short decision tree
8. **Simple tracking sheet** — what the teacher tracks, what they don't
9. **Teacher rotation and between-session maintenance** — running the room and keeping it runnable

**Note on peer collaboration.** An earlier outline of this section included a subsection on peer collaboration roles. The current version of the program commits to **individual work**: every student works on their own project, at their own workstation, at their own tier, at their own pace. Peer collaboration as a structured mechanism is **deferred** — the appendix on deferred peer collaboration describes what that subsection would have contained and the conditions under which the program would revisit this decision.

### §5.1 The workshop setting

**Group size:** three to eight students from the same mother class. The upper bound (eight) is there because above that a single teacher cannot rotate to every student frequently enough to catch the ones who need help before they disengage (Principle 2, Principle 9). The lower bound (three) is a practical floor rather than a pedagogical requirement — students work individually on their own projects, so the workshop does not structurally depend on a minimum number of students to function. Below three, the workshop becomes a series of 1:1 sessions, which is a viable format but loses the benefit of a consistent workshop environment where multiple students are independently working and where the teacher's rotation rhythm is the norm students learn to work inside.

**Session duration:** 60–90 minutes is the target. The seven-phase structure (§5.2) scales to either end — shorter sessions use shorter work blocks, longer sessions add material to the work blocks rather than extending them beyond a student's attention span.

**Session frequency:** ideally two sessions per week per mother class, spaced so students have time to consolidate between sessions but not so far apart that they lose continuity with their project. A once-a-week cadence also works but reduces the program's total contact hours enough that the three-year implementation roadmap (§10) should be stretched proportionally.

**Physical space:** a dedicated workshop room is strongly preferred over shared classroom space. Students need to be able to leave their workstation set up between sessions — a breadboard with wires in progress, a half-built chassis, a task card clipped to the workbench — so that re-entry to the next session takes seconds rather than minutes. The Principle 1 commitment to predictable routine includes predictable physical infrastructure.

**Per-student workstation:** each student has their own workstation, not a shared one. Workstation contents: a laptop or desktop with Arduino IDE and Claude Code access, a breadboard, the student's current task card clipped in a predictable location, a small parts bin for today's components, a red/green signal card pair (see §5.3), and space to keep their in-progress build visible.

**Shared infrastructure:** a parts library organised by component type (resistors sorted by value, LEDs by colour, sensors by function); a set of tools (wire strippers, multimeters, screwdrivers, soldering stations once Project 4 introduces soldering); reference posters (colour-code charts, common wiring diagrams, safety reminders); and a dedicated low-stimulation cool-down corner with a chair and a fidget object for any student who needs to step out briefly without leaving the room.

### §5.2 Session structure — the seven-phase rhythm

The session follows the same seven phases every time. The exact minutes are flexible within a band (a 60-minute session uses shorter work blocks, a 90-minute session uses longer ones), but **the phase order is identical across every session, every project, every tier**. Principle 1 — predictable routine is load-bearing — is implemented here above all other places.

| # | Phase | Minutes (60–90 min session) | What happens | Teacher's role |
|---|-------|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Arrival & Board Check** | 3–5 | Students arrive, greet the teacher, check their personal task board to see where they left off last session and what today's starting point is | Greet each student by name. Brief one-word check-in: *"How are you today?"* A one-word answer is fine; silence is fine too |
| 2 | **Mini-Huddle** | 3–5 | Whole group gathers (standing, central table). Teacher announces: *"Today everyone works on their next step. I'll start with [name], then rotate."* Acknowledge anyone who reached a milestone last session | Set expectations. Name any change to normal routine with an explicit reason |
| 3 | **Independent Work Block 1** | 15–25 | Students work on their project using their task card. Timer visible. | Rotate: spend ~4–5 minutes with each student who needs support. Relational check-in first, task progress second (Principle 8) |
| 4 | **Movement Break** | 3–5 | Everyone stands up, walks to get water, stretches, walks around the room once. Structured, not free-for-all | Required for all. Non-negotiable. Reduces restlessness and anxiety buildup. Principle 6 is load-bearing here |
| 5 | **Independent Work Block 2** | 15–25 | Continue project work. | Rotate to students you did not reach in Block 1. Finish rotations. Start flagging clean-up 2–3 minutes before Block 2 ends |
| 6 | **Clean-Up** | 3–5 | Tools away, workspace tidy, task card returned to its clip. Same routine every time | Consistent. Every student responsible for their own station. Help a student clean up *with* them if they are dysregulated; don't clean up *for* them |
| 7 | **Close-Out Circle** | 3–5 | Each student says one thing: *"Today I worked on ___"* or *"I got stuck on ___."* Students can pass. Thumbs-up/thumbs-down is also a valid answer | Listen. Validate. Do **not** problem-solve at close-out — problems raised here go on the task card for the student to work on next session. Principle 8 infrastructure |

**Why this specific structure matters:**

- **Predictability reduces anxiety** and the need for students to ask the teacher *"what are we doing?"* (Principle 1).
- **Chunked time blocks with a break support ADHD attention spans** (Principle 6).
- **The Movement Break in the middle is non-negotiable** because Principle 6's research finding on physical exercise for ADHD executive function is the strongest effect-size evidence in the whole program's literature review. The break is a first-class intervention, not a concession.
- **The Close-Out Circle builds self-awareness and language for self-monitoring** — naming the work done or the point of stuck is the beginning of the self-management skill Harrison et al. (2019) identified as meeting What Works Clearinghouse evidence-based standards for ADHD.
- **The Arrival Board Check is an antecedent strategy** — it sets the student up to know what to do BEFORE they need to ask the teacher (Principle 2, Principle 5).

**Deviations.** Any deviation from the seven-phase structure is announced at the Mini-Huddle with an explicit reason. *"Today we have 10 extra minutes because the bell is later, so Block 2 will run longer."* Never a surprise. Never *"we're skipping the movement break because we're behind"* — if anything is getting skipped, the movement break is the very last thing to go.

### §5.3 The Task Card System

The Task Card is the most important practical tool in the program. It is how Principle 2 (*the task card is the boss, not the teacher*) becomes a concrete object a student can hold in their hand.

**What a Task Card is.** A single physical sheet — laminated or in a clear sleeve — at each student's workstation. It tells the student five things:

1. What they are building (with a picture).
2. The step they are on right now.
3. Exactly what to do for that step.
4. How they will know when the step is done.
5. What to do if they are stuck, **before** asking the teacher.

**Task Card template** (printable version in the appendix):

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  PROJECT: [name]          STUDENT: [name]        │
│  ────────────────────────────────────────────── │
│                                                   │
│  ★ CURRENT STEP: Step [#] of [total in tier]     │
│                                                   │
│  WHAT TO DO:                                      │
│  □ [clear, concrete action 1]                     │
│  □ [clear, concrete action 2]                     │
│  □ [clear, concrete action 3]                     │
│                                                   │
│  DONE WHEN:                                       │
│  → [observable result — "the LED lights up       │
│    when you press the button"]                    │
│                                                   │
│  CLAUDE CODE LEVEL: [1 / 2 / 3]                  │
│  → Level 1: Upload the sketch at [file]          │
│  → Level 2: Modify this part: [specific target]  │
│  → Level 3: Describe what you want to change     │
│                                                   │
│  STUCK? TRY THIS FIRST:                          │
│  1. Re-read the step above                        │
│  2. Check the reference card in your folder       │
│  3. Look at the example on the demo shelf         │
│  4. Ask a neighbour: "Can you look at this?"      │
│  5. Put the RED card up — teacher will come      │
│                                                   │
│  ☐ STEP COMPLETE → Move clip to next step         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Key design elements:**

- **Chunking into small, concrete steps** reduces EF demand (Principle 1, Principle 3). Each task card covers a maximum of **3–5 sub-actions**, and each sub-action is verb-first and observable ("Connect the red wire from the battery to the switch" — not "Set up the power").
- **The "Done when" criterion** gives a clear finish line (Principle 4). It is always observable — a light on, a motor spinning, a value on the serial monitor. Never "the wiring is correct" (not observable without a test) — always "the LED lights up when you press the button" (observable).
- **The Claude Code Level field** tells the student which of the three levels (Principle 7) is available for this step. Not every step involves code — steps that are pure hardware have this field blank.
- **The "Stuck? Try this first" protocol** is a self-management scaffold (Principle 2). It gives the student a structured sequence of actions to take before escalating to the teacher. This teaches students to problem-solve and reduces teacher interruptions at the same time.
- **The Red / Green signal card** is a non-verbal escalation mechanism. When a student has completed the stuck protocol and still needs help, they put up a red card — the teacher sees it during the next rotation. No calling out, no hand-raising under pressure, no interrupting the teacher's work with another student. This reduces anxiety about asking for help and reduces impulsive blurting.
- **Physical checkbox and clip movement** — each step completion is a small tactile action that tracks progress. Each checkmark is a small dopamine hit (Principle 4).

**How many task cards per project.** It depends on the tier and on the project, but the rule is that no student sees more than the next 2–3 task cards at once. When a student finishes the current card, the teacher replaces it with the next one during rotation. This prevents the overwhelm of seeing "Step 4 of 27" on day one.

**What the task card is NOT.** It is not a script the teacher reads aloud. It is not a worksheet the student fills in with answers. It is not a rubric for grading. It is the student's working tool during the session, and the teacher refers to it instead of giving verbal multi-step instructions.

### §5.4 The three-tier system

Every project in the program is built at three tiers simultaneously. The same project — the same hardware, the same learning goal, the same "the LED lights up" moment of success — can be worked on by a student at Tier 1 (who receives maximum scaffolding) and by a student at Tier 3 (who designs their own version of the project) in the same session.

**Tier 1 — Guided Build.** Maximum predictability, clear steps, frequent success experiences. The student follows a step-by-step build guide with pictures of what each step should look like. Decision points are minimal. Success is visible and concrete: *"it moves," "it lights up," "it follows the line."* Independence level is high — a student at Tier 1 can follow their task card without the teacher for 15+ minutes at a time, which is exactly the independence the teacher needs to rotate. Typical profile: high anxiety, new to the workshop, severe EF deficits, post-trauma recovery, or a student on a hard day who normally works at Tier 2 or Tier 3.

**Tier 2 — Guided Design.** Moderate structure with genuine choice points. The project has a defined goal but the student makes 2–3 design choices along the way. Task cards include decision points: *"Choose ONE: Option A or Option B."* Reference materials are available (photos, short videos, example builds). The student tests, adjusts, and iterates within a constrained design space. Typical profile: students with ADHD who need engagement + novelty, students with ODD-flavored profiles who need meaningful autonomy, students with moderate anxiety who can handle limited ambiguity if supported.

**Tier 3 — Open Design.** Low structure, high autonomy. The student defines the problem and designs the solution. The task card becomes a **project planner** rather than step-by-step instructions — phases like PLAN → BUILD → CODE → TEST → SHOW, with the student filling in the content of each phase. The teacher serves as consultant, not instructor. Independence level is high, but the student needs periodic check-ins to avoid scope creep or frustration. Typical profile: ODD-flavored students who respond strongly to autonomy and respect, high-ability ADHD students who need novelty and complexity to stay engaged, and any student who has mastered Tier 1 and Tier 2 versions of earlier projects.

**Tier assignment is fluid, not fixed.** The program's hardest rule about tiers is that *a student is not a tier*. A student can be at Tier 3 for Project 1, drop to Tier 2 for Project 2 because the hardware is new and they want more scaffolding, jump to Tier 3 for Project 3, and drop to Tier 1 for a single session in the middle of Project 4 because they arrived that day in a state where Tier 3 would not be possible. This movement is not regression, not promotion — it is adaptive response to current state (§2 Population Profile, Principle 1's finding that EF is a state that rises and falls with stress). The teacher never says *"you used to be at Tier 3, why are you at Tier 1 today?"* — the teacher says *"today's card is ready, let's see what you want to work on."*

**How a new student's starting tier is determined.** Start every new student at Tier 1 of their first project, regardless of any prior reputation. Safety and success first. After one or two Tier 1 successes, the teacher asks: *"Do you want to try the next project where you make more of the choices?"* — and the student decides. No forced promotion.

**How to assign tiers across the current group.** Not by diagnosis. A student with severe ADHD might be a Tier 3 builder because they are highly skilled and need the challenge. A student with ODD-flavored presentation might start at Tier 1 because they are new and need to experience success before they trust the workshop enough for autonomy. The teacher's firsthand knowledge of the student — their current state, their recent history, what went well last session — is irreplaceable and no written profile can substitute for it.

**Tier and project order are independent.** A student can be at Tier 1 of Project 3 while another student is at Tier 3 of Project 1. The tier is about the student's current readiness; the project is about which hardware they are working with. §5.8 returns to the question of project order more directly.

### §5.5 Claude Code as a differentiated coding support

Principle 7 is implemented operationally as a **three-level access model** that lives on the task card and in the project files. A student never has to declare their level out loud — the task card shows all three, and the student chooses (or the teacher and student choose together) which entry point is right for today.

**Level 1 — Pre-written sketch upload.** The student opens a pre-prepared `.ino` file that matches their wiring and clicks Upload. The hardware responds. The student has just completed a code-related task without writing, editing, or debugging code. For students who arrive at the workshop with coding trauma, with low English literacy, or with any anxiety about "doing it wrong," this is the path to their first positive experience with code — *something I did made the thing work*.

**Level 2 — Modify with Claude Code's help.** The student opens a partially-complete sketch with one specific thing to change — change the blink rate, change the LED pin, change the threshold distance. The task card tells the student exactly which line or variable to touch, and the Claude Code prompt scaffold says: *"I want to make the LED blink every half second instead of every second. Here is my current code: [paste the line]. How do I change it?"* The student runs the prompt, reviews Claude Code's response, makes the change, tests it, and sees the result.

**Level 3 — Free dialogue with Claude Code.** The student describes what they are trying to build, shows Claude Code their current attempt, and iterates in dialogue. The task card gives the student a **prompt template** — not a prompt — that says: *"Before asking Claude, fill in: (a) what you want to happen, (b) what's currently happening, (c) what you've tried."* The student writes out (a), (b), (c), and then sends the whole thing to Claude Code. This discipline is non-negotiable: the student must describe the problem before asking for a solution. An AI that gives answers on demand becomes an extrinsic reward system that undermines competence (Principle 7's Morsink-based framing); an AI that asks questions back becomes a scaffold.

**Comprehension check at every level.** After each Claude Code interaction (at any level), the student answers one comprehension question on the task card:

- **Level 1:** *"What does your device do right now that it did not do before you uploaded the code?"*
- **Level 2:** *"After Claude helped you, can you explain in one sentence what changed?"*
- **Level 3:** *"Can you say in one sentence what Claude helped you figure out?"*

The point of the comprehension check is not to grade the student. The point is to ensure the student has engaged enough with the code to notice *what* the AI changed, which is the minimum floor for the interaction being a learning experience rather than a copy-paste.

**Level switching is fluid.** A student at Level 3 for Project 6 might be at Level 1 for Project 7 if Project 7 uses a new library they have never seen. A student who is having a hard day can say *"I want to upload Level 1 for everything today"* and the teacher says *"that's fine, let's do that."* The levels are differentiation tools, not achievement badges.

**What the teacher watches for at each level.**

- **At Level 1:** is the student getting a positive experience of the hardware working? If yes, that is success. If the upload is failing or the hardware is not responding, the teacher troubleshoots the *hardware*, not the code.
- **At Level 2:** is the student making the change meaningfully, and do they understand what changed? If the student is mechanically copying Claude Code's output without reading it, the teacher gently redirects: *"Before you paste, can you tell me what Claude is suggesting?"*
- **At Level 3:** is the student describing the problem before asking, and are they iterating (not just accepting the first response)? If a student is pasting Claude Code's output without review, the teacher redirects to the comprehension check before the student moves on.

### §5.6 Safety protocols

The workshop has a baseline safety discipline that applies across all eight projects, plus a dedicated escalation for Project 8 (the quadcopter capstone) because flight carries safety stakes that ground projects do not.

**Baseline safety (Projects 1–7).**

- **Eye protection** when wire-clipping, soldering, or running any motor at speed. Safety glasses live at each workstation. The teacher wears them too — modeling, not exception.
- **Soldering discipline** (introduced in Project 4, reinforced in Projects 5–7): hot iron on its stand whenever not in the hand; damp sponge for cleaning the tip; never solder over a lap or near skin; always solder on a heat-resistant mat; hair and loose sleeves tied back.
- **Battery discipline**: LiPo batteries live in a fireproof storage bag (a sand bucket is also acceptable) when not installed in a device; never charge a LiPo unattended; never puncture, crush, or short-circuit a LiPo; damaged LiPos are discharged in saltwater and recycled, not thrown in regular trash.
- **Power-off discipline**: never modify wiring on a powered device; disconnect the battery before changing components; test with a multimeter before first power-up.
- **Tool discipline**: wire strippers are not pliers, pliers are not hammers, screwdrivers are not pry bars. Use the right tool, or put the wrong tool down and ask for the right one.
- **Parts cleanup**: clipped wire ends go into a dedicated bin, not on the floor where someone will step on them.

These are taught at Project 1 as general habits, reinforced at Project 4 when soldering is introduced, and reinforced again at every project that adds a new safety surface.

**Project 8 escalation — flight safety.**

Project 8 adds a safety envelope that Projects 1–7 do not have. A tiny quadcopter with exposed propellers rotating at ~50,000 RPM can cut fingers, scratch eyes, and damage equipment. The program treats Project 8's safety protocol as first-class and non-negotiable, and the full detail lives in the Project 8 file (§6 and the per-project file). The baseline rules are:

- **Propeller guards are mandatory** for the initial test and practice phases. Guards can be removed only for final performance flights, and only with explicit teacher supervision.
- **Eye protection is mandatory during any flight test**, including bench thrust tests, not just free flight.
- **Tethered flight is mandatory for Tier 1 and Tier 2 first flights**. A fishing line or kite string attached to a fixed anchor prevents the drone from flying away or hitting a student who is too close.
- **One at a time**. Only one student arms a drone at any moment. All other students are at least 2 metres back and behind a visible line marked on the floor.
- **Defined indoor flight zone**: a 3-metre-diameter clear circle in an empty classroom corner or a gym corner. Spectators (including teachers) stand outside the circle during flight.
- **Pre-flight checklist** (the teacher runs this with each student before each flight): battery voltage is above 3.7 V, propellers are tight and undamaged, wiring is not snagged, the flight zone is clear, eye protection is on, the student is calm and ready.
- **LiPo fire emergency procedure**: sand bucket or fireproof bag within reach, never use water on a LiPo fire, fireproof mat under the charging station.
- **Medical alert protocol**: if a student has an anxiety spike or freezes during their first flight, disarm the drone immediately, put it down, walk the student to the cool-down corner, and try again another session. Never push a student to complete a first flight when they are not ready.

**Why Project 8 gets this escalation.** The risk is physical, not only psychological. A panicked student in the flight zone with an armed drone is a student at risk of injury. The program's commitment to the super-target of *small, positive, empowering experiences* requires that those experiences not come with a bandage — Project 8's safety protocol is the infrastructure that lets the empowering experience of flight actually happen without the shadow of physical harm.

### §5.7 When a student refuses the prescribed order

Some students — particularly those whose school history includes many negative encounters with *"do it in this sequence"* — will not work through the eight projects in the prescribed order. They will want to start with Project 8 because drones are exciting. They will refuse Project 3 because the example robot reminds them of a previous failure. They will insist on building a camera-equipped car in Project 1 because a camera is what they actually care about.

The program treats refusal-of-order as **information, not defiance**. A student who refuses today is telling the teacher something real — what they are and are not ready for, what excites them, what they need right now. The teacher's first response is conversational, not corrective: *"Tell me what would make today worth your time."*

**Four tools for responding to refusal.**

1. **Skills-based prerequisites, not project-based prerequisites.** The eight projects are sequenced because each introduces skills later projects build on — soldering in Project 4, I2C in Project 6, camera streaming in Project 7 — not because the order is intrinsically meaningful. A student who wants to start with Project 8 needs the skills Project 8 uses, not the prior project numbers themselves. When a student wants to jump ahead, the teacher identifies the specific skills the target project needs and delivers them as short, targeted mini-modules ("here is the 15 minutes of soldering practice you need before we wire the motors") rather than blocking the student at the doorway.

2. **Non-linear entry.** A student can start wherever they want. If they insist on Project 5, the teacher opens Project 5's task card set and says *"here is what you would be doing today; here are the things you will need to learn along the way that earlier projects would normally teach; here is the first task card."* The student decides whether the trade-off is worth it — and often, a student who insisted on Project 5 will voluntarily go back to Project 1 or 2 after seeing the skills gap, because the teacher gave them the choice rather than imposing it.

3. **Just-in-time scaffolding for skipped prerequisites.** The teacher prepares a small set of 15-minute "mini-module" task cards covering the discrete skills that normally accrue across Projects 1–7: one for soldering, one for reading a schematic, one for the serial monitor, one for I2C basics, one for motor-driver wiring, one for the Arduino IDE file structure. When a student skips ahead, the teacher delivers the specific mini-module they need at the moment they need it. No prerequisite is "ahead" if the student needs it now.

4. **Refusal as diagnostic.** A student who refuses a specific project on a specific day is telling the teacher something — maybe the hardware looks like something they failed with before, maybe they had a fight at home, maybe the social dynamic in today's group is wrong, maybe the teacher's enthusiasm sounded like pressure. The teacher's response is to ask what would make today worth the student's time, and then to act on whatever the student says — even if the answer is *"I want to sort the resistor bin today"* or *"I want to take apart last week's build and look at how I did the wiring."* Those are both legitimate answers, and both are activities that keep the student in the workshop, engaged with hardware, and building trust with the teacher for next session.

**Decision tree for the first 60 seconds after a refusal:**

1. **Is the student safe and stable?** If not, safety first. Walk with them to the cool-down corner, sit with them briefly. Whatever you were going to do about the project waits.
2. **Has the student said *why*?** If they have, thank them and take the answer seriously. If they have not, ask once: *"Tell me what would make today worth your time."* If they shrug or go silent, that is also an answer — offer the lowest-effort Level 1 option and move on. Do not interrogate.
3. **Is the refusal a one-off or part of a pattern?** A one-off gets a flexible response (an alternative activity, a different project, a quieter workstation). A pattern — three refusals in a row of the same project, or refusals of every project in a row — is a conversation with the student outside of session time, not a within-session fix.
4. **Has the teacher been giving this student enough quiet time?** Sometimes refusal is a protest against feeling watched. Give the student the alternative, step away, rotate to other students, and come back in 10 minutes.

**What the teacher does not do.** Argue about the order. Insist that the prescribed sequence is for the student's own good. Make the student feel that refusing has cost them something. Bring consequences or remove access to later projects. None of these responses work for this population, and several of them actively make the next refusal more likely.

**What the teacher does do.** Document the refusal on the tracking sheet (§5.9), note the conversation, and check in with the student at the next session's Arrival phase. Refusals are data, and the pattern across refusals is more useful than any single refusal.

### §5.8 Simple tracking sheet

The teacher tracks three things per student per session, and nothing else. The goal is a sheet so simple that filling it in takes less than 30 seconds per student and the teacher can actually do it during the Close-Out Circle rather than at home in the evening.

| Student | Started work within 5 minutes? (Y/N) | Completed at least 1 milestone today? (Y/N) | Red card or behavioural incident? (Y/N + one-line note) |
|---|---|---|---|

**Why these three and only these three.**

- **Started within 5 minutes** is the single best proxy for whether the student is experiencing the workshop as a safe place to try. A student who consistently takes 15 minutes to start is telling the teacher something.
- **Completed at least 1 milestone** is the single best proxy for whether the student's tier and task card are matched to their current state. A student who never completes a milestone is at the wrong tier, or the milestones are too big, or something else is blocking them.
- **Red card or incident** is the single best proxy for whether the student's regulation was stable during the session. One-line notes ("panicked during the wiring," "refused Project 3 again," "did well, wanted more time") build a pattern across sessions.

**What the teacher does not track.** Academic performance. Behavioural points. Participation grades. Skill mastery rubrics. None of these are necessary for running the workshop, and every additional measurement is a task that cuts into rotation time without producing actionable information.

**What the teacher does with the tracking data.** Reviews it weekly (10 minutes), notices patterns (*"Student X has not completed a milestone in three sessions — task cards are too big"*), and adjusts — at the tier level, the task card level, or the conversation level. The tracking sheet is not a reporting instrument; it is a feedback signal to the teacher.

### §5.9 Teacher rotation and between-session maintenance

**Rotation during the session.** The teacher's rotation plan for each session is: start with the 2–3 students most likely to need help today (based on last session's tracking sheet), finish rotations with the students who are typically fine, and reserve 2–3 minutes of the movement break to check the task-card queue for any student who finished their current card during Block 1.

A student at Tier 1 typically needs a rotation every 10–15 minutes (short check-in, confirm the next card, move on). A student at Tier 3 typically needs a rotation every 20–30 minutes (longer consultation, help with a design decision, push back on scope creep). The teacher plans the rotation around these different cadences rather than rotating to everyone equally.

**Relational check-in before task progress.** Principle 8 in action. The first thing the teacher says during a rotation is not *"where are you?"* — it is *"how's it going?"* A 5-second relational check-in signals to the student that the teacher sees them as a person first, which is the trust infrastructure on which all task work rests.

**Between-session maintenance (10–15 minutes after each session).**

- Review the tracking sheet. Note patterns.
- Rotate task cards — remove completed cards, add the next card for each student, confirm the Claude Code level for the next step.
- Replenish parts bins. Components that ran out get restocked from the parts library.
- Note any equipment that failed or was damaged, and flag it for repair before next session.
- Write one sentence in a session log about what went well and what was hard. The log is a self-care tool for the teacher as much as a pedagogical tool — it prevents the teacher from carrying the hard session home in their head without processing.

**Spontaneous verbalisation is welcome.** A student who thinks out loud while building — talking to the hardware, narrating their wiring, asking questions of the air — is using a self-regulation behaviour that research has linked to ADHD problem-solving (§3.6 on Kuzmina & Romero 2025's narrow qualitative observation, framed through Corkum et al. 2008's earlier finding on task-relevant external private speech). The teacher does not shush this behaviour. If it is disruptive to a neighbour, the teacher may gently suggest working in a different corner of the room for that session, but the verbalisation itself is a feature, not a bug.

**Between-session maintenance that does NOT happen.** Rewriting task cards from scratch. Designing new projects in response to a single student's feedback. Evaluating whether the program is "working" based on one session. The program is designed so the teacher's between-session time is short, focused, and sustainable (Principle 9), not open-ended.

## Appendix A — Deferred peer collaboration: what the program does not yet do, and why

<!-- DRAFT NOTE: This appendix is placed here for now because §6–§11 have not been drafted yet. During the final scrub pass, Appendix A will be relocated to the actual Appendices section at the end of the document, after §11 References. -->

An earlier version of this program's §5 Methodology included a Collaboration Roles subsection modelled on a Driver / Navigator / Documentarian / Peacekeeper role vocabulary — a pair-and-group-work structure where students share the hardware at a workstation and rotate roles across milestones. That subsection has been **deliberately removed** from the current version of the program, and the decision and its reasoning are documented here honestly so a reviewer, a fellow teacher, or a future version of this program can understand both the choice and the conditions for revisiting it.

### What the current program does instead

In the current version of the program, **every student works on their own project, at their own workstation, at their own tier, at their own pace**. The workshop is a small-group physical setting — three to eight students in the same room, sharing a parts library and tools — but the work is individual. Peer collaboration is not a first-class mechanism. The relationship infrastructure the program relies on is the **teacher–student relationship** (§4 Principle 8), not peer-to-peer collaboration.

### Why peer collaboration was deferred

The decision rests on a specific set of practical and design concerns that apply to Agourim's student population in particular:

1. **Extra complications.** Pair and small-group work introduce coordination costs that a single teacher managing 3–8 students with highly heterogeneous profiles may not be able to absorb. Deciding who pairs with whom, rotating roles at the right moment, catching a dyad that has drifted off-task, and mediating the inevitable friction when one student's pace differs from another's — all of these are additional demands on teacher attention that trade directly against the rotation time Principle 2, Principle 8, and Principle 9 all depend on.

2. **Social dynamics are already a load for this population.** Many of the students this program is built for carry histories of social difficulty — bullying, harassment, group rejection, or quieter patterns of being overlooked. §2 Population Profile is explicit that social comfort varies widely across the student body, and that some students *"find any group dynamic exhausting and need solo workspace by default."* Requiring peer collaboration as a default would demand social performance from students who may not be ready for it, and who may specifically have come to Agourim because mainstream group dynamics did not work for them.

3. **Individual projects preserve the differentiation model.** The three-tier system (§5.4) and the three-level Claude Code access model (§5.5) are designed around a single student's current state. When two students at different tiers work on the same artefact, the lower-tier student risks deferring to the higher-tier student, the higher-tier student risks over-driving, and the match between the scaffolding and the actual student doing the work breaks down. Individual projects preserve the differentiation model cleanly: each student gets exactly the tier and level that matches their current state, without dilution from a partner's different state.

4. **Peer collaboration research is real but the mechanisms are not automatic.** §3.1 reviewed the research on peer-mediated interventions and cooperative learning for students with EBD, and that research is genuinely supportive — Wattanawongwan, Smith & Vannest (2021) in particular document that cooperative learning produces benefits when five specific elements (positive interdependence, individual accountability, face-to-face promotive interaction, social skills, and group processing) are explicitly taught and monitored. The research is not *"put kids in groups and they will learn socially"* — it is *"teach the five elements, monitor the five elements, and positive effects follow."* Teaching and monitoring the five elements is itself a first-class instructional commitment, and the current version of the program has decided to commit its limited teacher-attention budget to the individual-work discipline instead.

5. **Gomez (2019), the closest analogue in the verified literature, used a peer mechanism this program does not implement.** The most directly relevant study in the corpus — a UTSA dissertation examining makerspace learning for middle-school EBD students — found that makerspace participation supported social-emotional skill development specifically through peer interaction opportunities. The program's current design does not use Gomez's mechanism. This is an honest gap, and §9 Honest Limitations restates it as one of the things the program is aware it is not doing.

### What this decision does NOT mean

It does not mean that students in the workshop cannot talk to each other. Students share the same physical room, and when one student notices another student's robot doing something interesting and walks over to look, that is normal workshop life and welcome. Spontaneous verbalisation (see §5.9) is also welcome. What the program does not structurally *require* is a pair-or-group-work format where two or more students depend on each other to complete a shared artefact.

It does not mean that the program has ruled out peer collaboration forever. The explicit framing is **deferral**, not **rejection**. A future version of this program — after the current individual-work version has been evaluated in practice and refined — could reintroduce peer collaboration under specific conditions (see below).

It does not mean that the research on peer collaboration is wrong. §3.1's review of Gersib & Mason (2023), Riden, Kumm & Maggin (2022), and Wattanawongwan et al. (2021) stands as written. The research shows peer-mediated and cooperative-learning interventions can produce positive outcomes for students with EBD when the elements are explicitly taught and monitored. The program's current choice is to invest its limited teacher-attention budget elsewhere, not to dispute the research.

### Conditions for revisiting this decision

The program's current individual-work commitment should be revisited when **any** of the following become true:

- **The program has been running for at least one full implementation year** and the teacher has firsthand evidence about how individual work is actually playing out in the workshop. Premature changes based on speculation are worse than committing to one version long enough to learn from it.
- **A specific student or pair of students is actively asking for collaborative work** and shows signs of being ready for it (shared interest in a project, successful history of individual work, visible social compatibility). Case-by-case flexibility is always available even under the current individual-work default — a teacher who sees two students who genuinely want to build a shared project together can say yes without waiting for a program-level decision.
- **The cohort's group-size, staffing, or adult-support ratio changes** in a direction that would make the teacher-attention budget less constrained (e.g., an assistant teacher, a consistent paraprofessional, or a smaller steady group size than the current 3–8).
- **The program adds a Project 9 or a special unit specifically designed around shared build work** — a category of project where collaboration is the explicit learning goal rather than an overlay on an individual-project structure.

### If peer collaboration is reintroduced, what the roles would look like

For the record, and for whoever eventually revisits this decision: the role vocabulary that was removed from §5 Methodology used four roles inherited from the source quadcopter project's prior documentation. Any future version of this program that reintroduces peer collaboration can start from this vocabulary:

- **Driver.** The one whose hands are on the hardware right now. The Driver holds the soldering iron, turns the screwdriver, plugs in the wire, clicks the Upload button. Everyone else waits.
- **Navigator.** The one who reads the task card and guides the Driver. The Navigator does not touch the hardware — they call out the next step, point at the reference diagram, and catch the Driver before a mistake. The Driver–Navigator pairing is the core of the role system.
- **Documentarian.** The one who records what was done — takes a photo after each major step, writes one sentence about what went well, notes any problems that are going on the "next session" list. This role is especially useful for students who are uncomfortable with hands-on work at a given moment but still want to be part of the activity.
- **Peacekeeper.** The one who watches the group's state and calls a pause when needed. *"Someone's frustrated, can we take a minute?"* or *"We've been at this wiring for 10 minutes, let's check the task card again."* This is explicitly a social-emotional role, suited to students who are perceptive to group dynamics.

A reintroduced collaboration system would rotate roles every 2–3 milestones so that every student experiences every role across a project, preventing role lock-in and building shared understanding. It would also need the explicit teaching and monitoring of Wattanawongwan et al.'s five cooperative-learning elements (positive interdependence, individual accountability, face-to-face promotive interaction, social skills, group processing) to be treated as first-class instructional work, not as an overlay on top of the individual-work methodology. These are real design commitments, not afterthoughts — which is part of why the current version of the program has chosen to defer them rather than implement them inadequately.

### Why this appendix exists at all

Transparency. A reader who reaches the end of this document should be able to see exactly what the program chose not to do, and why. Hiding the deferred-collaboration decision inside a quiet rewrite of §5 would leave a future reader — or a future version of this program's author — without the reasoning. Surfacing it in a dedicated appendix lets the decision be understood, evaluated, and revisited on its own terms.
