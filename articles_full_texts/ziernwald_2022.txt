ARTICLE: Promoting High-Achieving Students Through Differentiated Instruction in Mixed-Ability Classrooms—A Systematic Review

AUTHORS: Lisa Ziernwald; Delia Hillmayr; Doris Holzberger

JOURNAL: Journal for the Education of the Gifted (Sage)
DATE: November 14, 2022
DOI: 10.1177/1932202X221112931

ABSTRACT:
Promoting high-achieving students plays an important role in the school context. Hence, one promising support measure within the mixed-ability classroom is differentiated instruction (DI).

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK: DI AND ITS ROLE FOR HIGH-ACHIEVING STUDENTS

The construct of "differentiation" includes DI during a lesson as well as student assessment, evaluation, philosophical aspects, and more general principles (cf. Smale-Jacobse et al., 2019; Tomlinson, 2014). The systematic review focused on DI in mixed-ability classrooms, which contains two main aspects: (a) instructional practices and (b) organizational aspects (Smale-Jacobse et al., 2019). DI covers various instructional practices such as tiered tasks, scaffolds, and adaptive learning materials. The organizational aspect contains structures for DI, such as within-class grouping arrangements (Deunk et al., 2018; Puzio et al., 2020; Smale-Jacobse et al., 2019; Tomlinson, 2014). Another construct that considers the needs of individual students and provides corresponding learning opportunities for each student is individualization. In the current systematic review, individualization is included as a part of DI as it can be seen as an extreme type of DI, where instructional practices are not adapted for student subgroups (e.g., high-achievers) but for the individual student (Bohl et al., 2012; Dumont, 2018; Smale-Jacobse et al., 2019).

RESEARCH ON IMPACT OF DI ON STUDENTS' OUTCOMES:

Motivational-affective characteristics are strongly represented (Heller et al., 2005; Stoeger, 2009). Previous research syntheses showed that enrichment and acceleration for high-achieving students could have differential effects on academic achievement and motivational-affective characteristics (Kim, 2016; Steenbergen-Hu & Moon, 2011). The current study summarized different motivational and emotional constructs—including motivational beliefs and attitudes—under the term "motivational-affective characteristics."

CONDITIONS FOR IMPLEMENTING DI:

Research has found that DI has only been implemented to a very small extent in practice (Reis & Renzulli, 2010). Studies from the 1990s found that DI is not used regularly for high-achieving students (Archambault et al., 1993; Matthews, 1992; Westberg et al., 1993). Westberg et al. (1993) observed high-achieving students' instruction in 46 grades 3 and 4 mixed-ability classrooms and found that 84% of high-achieving students' instructional activities across five subjects included no DI.

Key factors for implementation:
- Teachers' beliefs and expectations were related to the (intended) use of different instructional practices (Bondie et al., 2019; Gebauer & McElvany, 2017; Trivette et al., 2012)
- Barriers: time constraints, lack of teacher competence (Dixon et al., 2014)
- Facilitators: colleagues, mentors, control in decision-making (Bondie et al., 2019; Puzio et al., 2015)

PRESENT STUDY:

The review focused only on high-achieving students (not underachieving students), investigated primary and secondary education settings, and examined DI in mixed-ability classrooms specifically. The study investigated:
1. Impact of DI on high-achieving students' outcomes
2. How teachers and high-achieving students evaluate DI's utility for promoting high-achieving students
3. Barriers and facilitators while implementing DI

METHOD - STUDY SEARCH:

The search employed a snowball search strategy and screened included journal articles' reference lists. Inclusion criteria focused on studies of DI in mixed-ability classrooms with high-achieving students, covering primary and secondary education.
