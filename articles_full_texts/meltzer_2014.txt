ABSTRACT

Students with learning disabilities have been reported to have difficulty in a number of different executive function processes that affect their academic performance (Singer & Bashir, 1999). Executive function difficulties for students with learning disabilities have been implicated as the reason why these students struggle with complex academic tasks such as reading, writing, and note-taking (Denckla, 2007). This mixed-methods study explored the extent to which a strategic note-taking intervention encouraged more efficient employment of executive function in middle school students with learning disabilities. This paper draws on interviews, students' notes, and pre- and post-intervention assessments to present case studies of three student participants. Results indicate that the strategic note-taking intervention served as a scaffold, opening up new pathways for struggling students to access executive functions and flexibly deploy cognitive strategies. Importantly, "how" each student did so and "what" each student learned about her/himself as a learner in the process were dependent on the specific challenges presented by her/his learning disability. These cases shed light on the potential for cognitive scaffolding to help students actively improve their own executive functioning in complex tasks such as note-taking.

KEY FINDINGS

1. Strategic note-taking intervention as scaffold for executive function:
The study examined a strategic note-taking intervention (using a two-column structured note-taking approach) in a 7th grade science class. Three middle school students with learning disabilities were selected as case study participants.

2. Mixed-methods design:
Pre- and post-intervention assessments were used alongside qualitative interviews and analysis of students' actual notes to provide a rich picture of how each student engaged with the scaffolding.

3. Results:
- The note-taking scaffold opened new pathways for students to access and deploy executive function strategies
- Results were individualized: each student's response to the scaffold depended on their specific learning disability profile
- The scaffold helped students improve working memory, organization, and planning processes needed for note-taking
- Post-intervention, students showed improved ability to identify key information, organize notes, and use notes to support recall

4. Implications:
- Cognitive scaffolding can help students with learning disabilities actively improve their own executive functioning
- Scaffolds must be adapted to individual student needs and specific challenges presented by their learning disability
- Teachers can use structured academic tasks to provide executive function scaffolding without explicit EF instruction
- Note-taking represents a complex academic task that naturally requires multiple EF processes simultaneously
