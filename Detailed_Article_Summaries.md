## 1. Smale-Jacobse et al. (2019)

### **"Differentiated Instruction in Secondary Education: A Systematic Review of Research Evidence"**
- **Journal:** *Frontiers in Psychology*, 10, 2366
- **Citations:** 771 ⭐ (highest-cited article in this review)
- **Full text:** Open access — [Frontiers.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2019.02366/full)

### Research Question
What is the current research evidence on differentiated instruction (DI) within regular secondary education classrooms, and what effects does DI have on student cognitive outcomes?

### Method
- Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines
- Searched Web of Science, ERIC, PsycINFO
- Included peer-reviewed empirical studies from 2006–2018 with quantitative student outcome data
- Final sample: **14 papers** covering **12 distinct studies**
- Quality rated using NAECC/CEC standards

### Key Findings

#### 1. DI Approaches Identified in Secondary Education
The review identified the following operationalizations of DI in secondary settings:

| DI Approach | Description | # Studies |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Ability grouping** | Students grouped by current performance level within the same class | 4 |
| **Tiered instruction** | Multiple tiers of scaffolding, complexity, or depth | 3 |
| **Individualized instruction** | Personalized learning paths, adaptive software | 2 |
| **Mastery learning** | Progress only when competence is demonstrated | 1 |
| **Heterogeneous grouping** | Mixed-ability groups with structured roles | 1 |
| **Flipped classroom + remediation** | Pre-class video, class time for differentiated remediation | 1 |

#### 2. Effect Sizes
- **Overall mean effect**: d = **+0.741** (moderate-to-large) including outlier; d = **+0.509** (small-to-moderate) excluding one outlier study
- The positive effects indicate that DI generally improves student achievement compared to traditional whole-class instruction
- **Most beneficial for low-achieving students** — students who are behind their peers benefited most from DI
- Effect sizes varied considerably across operationalizations

#### 3. Critical Gaps Identified
- ⚠️ **Very limited empirical evidence** exists for DI specifically in secondary education (only 12 studies in 12 years)
- Most DI research focuses on elementary education
- Nearly all studies used **cognitive/academic outcomes** as measures; almost none measured **behavioral, social-emotional, or motivational outcomes**
- Teachers reported **substantial difficulty** implementing DI, requiring significant training and support
- Most studies focused on math and science; few covered language arts or social studies

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** This is the gold-standard review showing that DI works in secondary settings (effect size ~0.5), particularly for low-achieving students. However, it reveals a critical gap: no studies examined DI effects on students with EBD specifically, and no studies measured behavioral or emotional outcomes. This gap is precisely what the current research aims to address.

**Key takeaways for practice:**
- Within-class ability grouping and tiered instruction are the most-studied DI approaches
- Low-achieving students (which EBD students often are) benefit most from DI
- Teachers need structured training and ongoing support to implement DI effectively
- Combining DI with project structures (like PBL) could address the engagement gap that whole-class DI alone may miss

## 2. Mitchell, Kern & Conroy (2019)

### **"Supporting Students with Emotional or Behavioral Disorders: State of the Field"**
- **Journal:** *Behavioral Disorders*, 44(2), 70–84
- **Citations:** 346 ⭐
- **Access:** SAGE (paywall; abstract available)

### Overview
This is the definitive state-of-the-field review for students with Emotional Disturbance (ED) — one of the 13 IDEA disability categories. The authors provide historical context and current data to highlight contemporary issues.

### Key Findings

#### 1. Population Characteristics
- ED is one of the disability categories under IDEA for which students receive special education services
- Students with ED are at **significantly greater risk** for:
  - Poor social, behavioral, and academic achievement in school
  - Lower graduation rates
  - Poor postsecondary outcomes (employment, independent living, further education)
- ED is frequently comorbid with ADHD, anxiety, depression, trauma, and learning disabilities

#### 2. Disciplinary Challenges
- Students with ED experience **disproportionately high rates** of disciplinary events (suspensions, expulsions)
- Disciplinary removal further reduces academic engagement and worsens outcomes

#### 3. Recommended Reforms (based on evidence)
The authors recommend four major changes:

1. **Revised terminology and eligibility criteria** — move to a more comprehensive education-based definition rather than the current exclusionary medical model
2. **Universal screening** — implement regular screening for social, emotional, and behavioral problems at the school level
3. **Multi-tiered systems of support (MTSS)** — deliver preventive interventions at Tier 1 (universal), Tier 2 (targeted), and Tier 3 (intensive) to address social, emotional, and behavioral needs
4. **Integrated service delivery** — allow greater access to related services (counseling, mental health) to enhance the benefits of special education

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** Establishes that EBD students have the worst outcomes of any disability category and that current service delivery is insufficient. The call for MTSS and integrated delivery models supports the argument that innovative instructional approaches like PBL combined with DI could serve as a Tier 1/Tier 2 intervention.

**Key takeaways for practice:**
- The EBD population is heterogeneous (ADHD, anxiety, depression, trauma, ODD/CD, OCD)
- One-size-fits-all instruction fails this population comprehensively
- Differentiated approaches must address both academic AND social-emotional-behavioral needs simultaneously
- Any intervention must account for the high rates of school discipline and disengagement in this population

## 3. Taylor, Allen et al. (2024)

### **"The Effects of Project-Based Learning on Student Behavior and Teacher Burnout in an Emotional/Behavioral Support Classroom"**
- **Journal:** *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders*, SAGE, 2024
- **Citations:** 32
- **Access:** SAGE (paywall)

### Overview
This is the **most directly relevant** empirical study for this research. It examines PBL implementation specifically in an emotional/behavioral support classroom.

### Key Findings

- Teaching is one of the most stressful occupations in the US; this is **especially true for EBD teachers** who have a higher risk of burnout than other special education teachers
- PBL was implemented in an emotional/behavioral support classroom setting
- The study found evidence that PBL can:
  - **Support positive prosocial behavior** in students with EBD
  - **Increase teacher feelings of efficacy** in managing the classroom
  - **Reduce teacher burnout** associated with EBD instruction
- PBL provided a structured framework that gave students agency while maintaining behavioral expectations
- The study aligns with evidence that student engagement reduces disruptive behavior

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** This is the first empirical study to directly examine PBL in an EBD classroom. It provides preliminary evidence that PBL is feasible and beneficial for students with emotional and behavioral disorders, addressing both student behavior and teacher sustainability.

**Key takeaways for practice:**
- PBL can serve as both an instructional approach AND a behavioral intervention for EBD students
- Teacher burnout is a critical variable — any effective intervention must also be sustainable for teachers
- The combination of student agency (choice, voice) within structured projects creates a productive balance for EBD students
- More research is needed with larger samples and controlled designs

## 4. Culclasure et al. (2019)

### **"Project-Based Learning (PjBL) in Three Southeastern Public Schools: Academic, Behavioral, and Social-Emotional Outcomes"**
- **Journal:** *Interdisciplinary Journal of Problem-Based Learning*, 13(2)
- **Citations:** 119
- **Access:** Open access via Purdue University

### Overview
This is the largest empirical evaluation of PBL outcomes that includes behavioral AND social-emotional measures alongside academic outcomes.

### Key Findings

- Study evaluated the New Tech Network (NTN) model of PBL across three southeastern US public schools
- Federal Investing in Innovation (i3) grant funded the evaluation (2015–2017)
- NTN ninth graders **outperformed** demographically similar control students on end-of-year assessments
- The study measured three outcome domains:
  1. **Academic outcomes** — positive effect on standardized test scores
  2. **Behavioral outcomes** — reduction in disciplinary referrals and absenteeism
  3. **Social-emotional outcomes** — improvements in self-reported SEL skills including self-management and relationship skills

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** Demonstrates that PBL can simultaneously improve academic, behavioral, AND social-emotional outcomes — the three domains most impacted in students with EBD. The NTN model provides a proven framework that could be adapted with differential instruction for EBD populations.

**Key takeaways for practice:**
- PBL's effects extend beyond academics to behavior and SEL
- Ninth grade (transition to high school) is a critical period — PBL can buffer this transition
- The NTN model provides structured scaffolding that could be further differentiated for EBD students
- Whole-school PBL models create a culture of engagement that reduces behavioral incidents

## 5. Harrison, Soares & Joyce (2019)

### **"Inclusion of Students with Emotional and Behavioural Disorders in General Education Settings: A Scoping Review of Research in the US"**
- **Journal:** *International Journal of Inclusive Education*, 23(12)
- **Citations:** 60
- **Access:** Taylor & Francis (paywall)

### Overview
Comprehensive scoping review examining the research on including EBD students in general education settings in the United States.

### Key Findings

- Reviews the efficacy of both academic and behavioral interventions for students with or at risk for EBD in inclusive settings
- Identifies a significant gap between the **inclusion mandate** under IDEA and actual **evidence-based inclusive practices**
- Most interventions studied were behavior-focused; **fewer studies examined academic instruction** for EBD students in inclusive settings
- Teacher preparation and ongoing support emerged as critical factors for successful inclusion
- Peer-mediated strategies and collaborative instructional approaches showed promise

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** Confirms that EBD students are increasingly placed in inclusive settings but that teachers lack evidence-based tools for instructing them effectively. PBL with DI could fill this gap by providing authentic, engaging instruction that also addresses behavioral needs.

**Key takeaways for practice:**
- Inclusive placement alone is insufficient — specific instructional strategies are needed
- Teacher training must address both academic instruction AND behavioral management
- Collaborative and peer-mediated approaches (natural components of PBL) show promise for EBD inclusion
- The scoping review reveals a need for intervention studies that combine academic and behavioral approaches

## 6. Billingsley, Thomas & Webber (2018)

### **"Effects of Student Choice of Instructional Method on the Learning Outcomes of Students with Comorbid Learning and Emotional/Behavioral Disabilities"**
- **Journal:** *Learning Disability Quarterly*, 41(4), 213–226
- **Citations:** 44
- **Access:** SAGE (paywall; abstract retrieved)

### Research Question
Do learning preferences of secondary students with comorbid LD and ED predict the instructional method with which they best learn mathematics?

### Method
- Exploratory study with secondary students dually identified with LD and ED
- Single-subject alternating treatments design
- Compared three instructional methods:
  1. **Direct instruction (teacher-led)**
  2. **Computer-assisted instruction (CAI)**
  3. **Combination of both**
- Students completed a preference survey before AND after instruction

### Key Findings

- Most students were able to **accurately predict** the instructional condition under which they would learn best
- Students could also identify, **in hindsight**, the condition that provided the best learning outcome
- **Student choice** aligned with actual performance — when students chose their preferred method, outcomes improved
- The combination of direct teach + CAI was effective for some students, suggesting that variety matters

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** Provides empirical evidence that **student choice** — a core principle of both DI and PBL — actually works for students with comorbid LD/EBD. Students with these disabilities can self-advocate for their learning preferences and benefit from having options.

**Key takeaways for practice:**
- Choice is not just a motivational strategy — it's an empirically supported DI approach for EBD/LD students
- Students with EBD can reliably self-assess their learning preferences
- PBL naturally offers multiple pathways (research, build, create, present) — this study supports letting EBD students choose their method
- Combining technology-based and teacher-led instruction may be optimal

## 7. Harrison et al. (2019)

### **"Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorders and Classroom-Based Interventions: Evidence-Based Status, Effectiveness, and Moderators of Effects in Single-Case Design Research"**
- **Journal:** *Review of Educational Research* (SAGE), 89(4)
- **Citations:** 100+
- **Access:** SAGE (paywall)

### Overview
Systematic review of classroom-based interventions for students with ADHD.

### Key Findings

- Classroom interventions for ADHD are **moderately effective** overall
- **Instructional interventions** (modifying how content is taught) met evidence-based standards
- **Self-management interventions** (teaching students to monitor their own behavior) also met evidence-based standards
- **Antecedent-based strategies** (modifying the environment or task before problems occur) showed strong effects
- Interventions were more effective when:
  - They were **matched to the function of the behavior**
  - They offered **choice and autonomy**
  - They involved **structured peer interaction**

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** Identifies exactly which types of classroom strategies work for ADHD — instructional modification, self-management, and antecedent strategies. All three can be embedded within a DI-enhanced PBL framework.

**Key takeaways for DI-PBL design for ADHD:**

| Evidence-Based Strategy | How to Embed in PBL |
|------------------------|---------------------|
| Self-management training | Daily check-in/check-out during projects; self-monitoring checklists |
| Antecedent modification | Provide visual project timelines; chunk tasks; reduce distractions during work time |
| Instructional choice | Let students choose project topics, presentation methods, team roles |
| Structured peer interaction | Assign clear roles in PBL teams; use peer tutoring within groups |
| Immediate feedback | Use rubrics for ongoing self-assessment; daily brief conferences |

## 8. Cumming et al. (2024)

### **"Perceived Stress, Executive Function, and Stress Regulation: Implications for Middle Schoolers' Emotional and Behavioral Well-being"**
- **Journal:** *Remedial and Special Education* (SAGE), 46(6), 426–441, 2024
- **Citations:** New publication
- **Access:** SAGE

### Overview
Examines the critical link between executive function (EF), perceived stress, and emotional/behavioral outcomes in middle school students.

### Key Findings

- Executive function deficits are **highly prevalent** in students with EBD, ADHD, anxiety, and trauma
- Perceived stress significantly mediates the relationship between EF deficits and behavioral/emotional problems
- Students with poor **stress regulation** show:
  - More externalizing behaviors (aggression, defiance, disruption)
  - More internalizing symptoms (anxiety, depression, withdrawal)
  - Lower academic engagement
- EF interventions (planning, organization, cognitive flexibility) can buffer stress effects

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** Demonstrates that executive function is the underlying mechanism connecting ADHD, anxiety, trauma, and behavioral problems. DI within PBL must explicitly scaffold EF skills — planning, organizing, prioritizing, and self-monitoring — rather than assuming students possess them.

**Key takeaways for DI-PBL design:**
- Build **explicit EF scaffolds** into every project phase (checklists, graphic organizers, progress trackers)
- Reduce cognitive load by **chunking** large projects into manageable steps
- Teach **stress regulation strategies** (breathing, movement breaks) as part of the PBL routine
- **Predictable structure** within PBL reduces anxiety; flexibility within structure supports ADHD

## 9. Watson et al. (2025)

### **"A Critical Review of Empirical Support for Trauma-Informed Approaches in Schools and a Call for Conceptual, Empirical and Practice Integration"**
- **Journal:** *Review of Education* (Wiley/BERA), 2025
- **Citations:** New publication
- **Access:** Wiley

### Overview
The most current and rigorous review of trauma-informed approaches (TIA) in schools across the US, UK, Australia, and Canada.

### Key Findings

- Interest in trauma-informed school approaches has **surged** since 2015
- However, the **empirical evidence base remains limited**:
  - Most studies are descriptive or qualitative
  - Few controlled trials exist
  - Outcome measures vary widely
- Whole-school TIA models often lack **clear operational definitions**
- The review calls for **integration** of trauma-informed practice with existing frameworks including:
  - Differentiated instruction
  - Social-emotional learning (SEL)
  - Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS)
  - Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** Validates the approach of integrating trauma-informed principles into DI and PBL rather than treating trauma-informed care as a standalone initiative. The review supports embedding trauma awareness into everyday instructional design.

**Key trauma-informed principles to embed in DI-PBL:**
1. **Safety** — predictable routines, consistent expectations, safe physical space
2. **Trustworthiness** — transparent project expectations, follow through on promises
3. **Choice** — offer options within project constraints (naturally aligned with DI)
4. **Collaboration** — peer support, shared responsibility (naturally aligned with PBL)
5. **Empowerment** — student voice in project design, strength-based feedback

### **"Trauma-Informed Teaching and IEPs: Strategies for Building Student Resilience"**
- **Publisher:** Book, 2022
- **Citations:** 21
- **Access:** Published book

### Overview
Bridges trauma-informed teaching with the IEP process, offering practical strategies for differentiation in inclusive classrooms.

### Key Themes

- Many students with EBD have experienced adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) that shape their behavior
- Traditional behavioral approaches (consequence-based) often **re-traumatize** rather than help
- **Resilience-building** through:
  - **Predictable structure** — reduces hypervigilance
  - **Sensory-aware environments** — reduces sensory overload (relevant for ADHD, anxiety, PTSD)
  - **Strength-based IEP goals** — focus on what students CAN do
  - **Differentiated assessment** — flexible deadlines, alternative demonstration methods
- Integration with IEP development: trauma-aware goals and accommodations

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** Provides the practical link between trauma-informed care and IEP-based differentiation. For students with EBD who have IEPs, the book offers specific strategies that can be embedded into PBL project design.

**Key takeaways for DI-PBL design:**
- Write IEP-aligned project modifications that account for trauma
- Provide **multiple ways to demonstrate learning** (presentation alternatives for anxious students)
- Build in **predictable project routines** (same team meeting structure daily)
- Use **strength-based assessment rubrics** that highlight growth

## 11. Boardman (2025)

### **"High School Students' Experiences With Scaffolding in Project-Based Learning"**
- **Journal:** *Journal of Adolescent & Adult Literacy* (Wiley/ILA), 2025
- **Citations:** New publication
- **Access:** Wiley

### Overview
Examines how scaffolding functions within PBL for high school students with diverse abilities, including students with disabilities.

### Key Findings

- Effective PBL scaffolding involves:
  1. A **small number of connected scaffolded materials** (not overwhelming amounts of worksheets)
  2. **Interactional scaffolds** (peer collaboration, teacher conferencing)
  3. Attending to students' **diverse abilities and language backgrounds**
- Students reported that too much scaffolding felt controlling; too little felt overwhelming
- The ideal balance: **"structured freedom"** — clear expectations with room for personal expression
- Peer scaffolding was as important as teacher scaffolding

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** Defines the practical scaffolding approach needed to make PBL accessible for students with EBD. The concept of "structured freedom" is exactly what students with ADHD, anxiety, and trauma need — predictability with autonomy.

**Key takeaways for DI-PBL design:**
- Design **3–5 key scaffolding tools** per project (not 15)
- Prioritize **peer interaction** as a scaffold (structured peer check-ins)
- Balance structure (timelines, milestones, templates) with freedom (topic choice, method choice)
- Different students need different scaffold levels — this IS differentiation

## 12. Sciacca (2025)

### **"Experiences of Teachers Using Project-Based Learning with Students with Emotional and Behavioral Disorders"**
- **Type:** Doctoral Dissertation, ProQuest, 2025
- **Citations:** 1
- **Access:** ProQuest

### Overview
The most recent qualitative study directly investigating teacher experiences implementing PBL with EBD students.

### Key Findings

- Teachers reported using PBL with EBD students as a way to:
  - Increase **student engagement** and reduce off-task behavior
  - Provide **meaningful, real-world connections** that motivate students who have historically disengaged from school
  - Create opportunities for **positive social interaction** within structured teams
- Challenges identified:
  - Managing group dynamics when students have conflicting behavioral needs
  - Adapting projects for students with severe executive function deficits
  - Balancing content coverage with project depth
- Teachers emphasized the importance of:
  - **High-leverage practices** (explicit instruction, feedback, structured routines)
  - **Behavioral practices** integrated into the PBL framework (not separate)
  - **Differentiation** across content, process, and product within projects

### Implications for This Research

> **Direct relevance:** Provides the teacher perspective on what actually happens when PBL meets EBD in real classrooms. Confirms that PBL + DI is feasible but requires intentional design, behavioral integration, and teacher preparation.

## Cross-Cutting Implications

### Integrated Framework: DI-PBL for Students with EBD

Based on the synthesis of all 12 articles, the following framework emerges:

### 1. CORE PRINCIPLES

| Principle | Evidence Base | Application |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Differentiation by readiness** | Smale-Jacobse (2019): d = +0.509 | Tiered project tasks, scaffolded materials |
| **Student choice** | Billingsley (2018): LD/EBD students predict own best method | Choice of topic, method, product, team role |
| **Executive function scaffolding** | Cumming (2025): EF mediates stress → behavior | Checklists, visual timers, chunked deadlines |
| **Trauma-informed design** | Watson (2025), Koslouski et al. (2023): integrate with DI | Safety, predictability, empowerment within PBL |
| **ADHD-specific supports** | Harrison (2019): self-management + antecedent strategies | Self-monitoring tools, reduced transitions, immediate feedback |
| **Behavioral integration** | Taylor (2024): PBL reduces problem behavior | Embed behavioral expectations INTO project norms |

### 2. PROJECT DESIGN RECOMMENDATIONS

#### For Students with **ADHD**:
- High-interest, hands-on project components
- Short, clear task segments with built-in movement breaks
- Visual project trackers (Kanban boards)
- Daily check-in/check-out on project progress
- Choice in how to demonstrate learning

#### For Students with **Anxiety / Social Anxiety**:
- Predictable project schedules and routines
- Low-pressure presentation alternatives (recorded video, written report, small-group share)
- Gradual increase in collaboration requirements
- Clear rubrics shared upfront to reduce uncertainty
- Safe failure: revision opportunities built into timeline

#### For Students with **ODD / Conduct Challenges**:
- High autonomy within clear boundaries
- Student voice in project topic selection and team formation
- Leadership roles within projects
- Positive reinforcement linked to project milestones
- Avoid power struggles; offer genuine choices

#### For Students with **Depression / Low Motivation**:
- Meaningful, real-world project connections
- Strength-based entry points (build on what students CAN do)
- Smaller, achievable milestones to build momentum
- Peer support structures
- Flexible pacing without punitive consequences

#### For Students with **Post-Trauma / PTSD**:
- Trauma-sensitive classroom environment (safe space, minimal sensory overwhelm)
- Consistent, predictable routines within each project phase
- Adult check-ins (brief, daily)
- Avoid triggering content without warning and alternative pathways
- Relationship-first approach — trust before academic demand

#### For Students with **OCD**:
- Flexible deadlines (reduce time pressure)
- Alternative assessment methods (reduce perfectionism triggers)
- Structured transitions between project phases
- Allow accommodations for repetitive behaviors without drawing attention
- Minimize performance-based anxiety through formative (not summative-heavy) assessment

### 3. RESEARCH GAPS IDENTIFIED

Based on this review, the following gaps remain:

1. **No controlled studies** of DI within PBL specifically for secondary EBD students
2. **Smale-Jacobse (2019)** found only 12 DI studies in all of secondary education — none focused on EBD
3. **Taylor (2024)** is the only empirical study of PBL in EBD classrooms — sample size was small
4. **Watson (2025)** found limited empirical evidence for trauma-informed approaches in schools
5. **No studies** examined the intersection of DI + PBL + specific disorders (ADHD, ODD, anxiety, OCD)
6. **Teacher preparation** for implementing DI-PBL with EBD populations is under-studied
7. **Long-term outcomes** (beyond one year) of PBL/DI for EBD students are unknown

### 4. SUGGESTED STRUCTURE: DI-PBL UNIT FOR EBD STUDENTS

```
PHASE 1: LAUNCH (1–2 days)
├── Hook: Real-world problem/driving question (high interest)
├── Student interest survey (DI: differentiate by interest)
├── Establish project norms (trauma-informed: safety, predictability)
└── Form flexible teams (balance needs, assign clear roles)

PHASE 2: BUILD KNOWLEDGE (3–5 days)
├── Mini-lessons (tiered by readiness)
├── Station rotations (DI: process differentiation)
├── Self-selected research (DI: choice + autonomy)
├── Daily check-in/check-out (ADHD support + self-monitoring)
└── EF scaffolds: graphic organizers, checklists, visual timers

PHASE 3: DEVELOP & CREATE (5–7 days)
├── Work sessions with structured milestones (chunking)
├── Peer conferences (interactional scaffolding)
├── Teacher conferencing (formative feedback)
├── Product choice menu (DI: by learning profile)
│   ├── Option A: Build/construct
│   ├── Option B: Design/create digitally
│   ├── Option C: Write/document
│   └── Option D: Perform/present
└── Built-in revision time (reduces anxiety, supports perfectionism)

PHASE 4: PRESENT & REFLECT (2–3 days)
├── Multiple presentation formats (DI: by profile)
│   ├── Public presentation (for those comfortable)
│   ├── Small-group share (for anxious students)
│   └── Recorded/written submission (for severe anxiety/OCD)
├── Self-assessment using rubric (self-management)
├── Peer feedback (structured, positive-first)
└── Individual reflection (growth-focused)
```
